      <p>The <b>Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <b>SADR</b>; also <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a> with <b>Saharawi</b>; <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">الجمهورية العربية الصحراوية الديمقراطية</span>‎ <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">al-Jumhūrīyah al-'Arabīyah aṣ-Ṣaḥrāwīyah ad-Dīmuqrāṭīyah</i></i> <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">República Árabe Saharaui Democrática</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition" title="List of states with limited recognition">partially recognized <i>de facto</i> sovereign state</a> located in the western <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, which claims the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_list_of_Non-Self-Governing_Territories" title="United Nations list of Non-Self-Governing Territories">non-self-governing territory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a>, but controls only <a href="/wiki/Free_Zone_(region)" title="Free Zone (region)">the easternmost one-fifth of that territory</a>. Between 1884 and 1975, Western Sahara was known as <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Sahara" title="Spanish Sahara">Spanish Sahara</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colony" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish colony">Spanish colony</a> (later an <a href="/wiki/Overseas_province" title="Overseas province">overseas province</a>). The SADR is one of the two African states in which <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> is a significant language, the other being <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>. </p>
      <p>The SADR was proclaimed by the <a href="/wiki/Polisario_Front" title="Polisario Front">Polisario Front</a> (a former socialist liberation force which has since reformed its ideological and political views) on February 27, 1976, in <a href="/wiki/Bir_Lehlou" title="Bir Lehlou">Bir Lehlou</a>, Western Sahara. The SADR government controls about 20–25% of the <a href="/wiki/Territory_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Territory (country subdivision)">territory</a> it claims.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It calls the territories under its control the <i>Liberated Territories</i> or the <i><a href="/wiki/Free_Zone_(region)" title="Free Zone (region)">Free Zone</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> controls and administers the rest of the disputed territory, and calls these lands its <a href="/wiki/Southern_Provinces" title="Southern Provinces">Southern Provinces</a>. The SADR government considers the Moroccan-held territory to be occupied territory, while Morocco considers the much smaller SADR-held territory to be a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_zone" title="Buffer zone">buffer zone</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The claimed <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a> of the SADR is former Western Sahara capital <a href="/wiki/Laayoune" title="Laayoune">El-Aaiún</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Temporary_capital" title="Temporary capital">temporary capital</a> moved from Bir Lehlou to <a href="/wiki/Tifariti" title="Tifariti">Tifariti</a> in 2008. The seat of government is located in the <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_refugee_camps" title="Sahrawi refugee camps">Sahrawi refugee camps</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tindouf" title="Tindouf">Tindouf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>. </p>
      <p>The SADR maintains diplomatic relations with 31 UN states, but is a full member of the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The name <i>Sahrawi</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanization of the Arabic word</a> Ṣaḥrāwī صحراوي, meaning "Inhabitant of <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">the Desert</a>". The word Ṣaḥrāwī صحراوي then is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> word Ṣaḥrā' (صحراء), meaning desert. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Western_Sahara" title="History of Western Sahara">History of Western Sahara</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Following the evacuation of the Spanish as a consequence of the Moroccan <a href="/wiki/Green_March" title="Green March">Green March</a>), Spain, Morocco, and <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> signed the <a href="/wiki/Madrid_Accords" title="Madrid Accords">Madrid Accords</a> on November 14, 1975, six days before <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Franco</a> died. Morocco and Mauritania responded by annexing the territory of Western Sahara. On 26 February 1976, Spain informed the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> (UN) that as of that date it had terminated its presence in Western Sahara and relinquished its responsibilities, which left the region devoid of any Administering Power.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Neither Morocco nor Mauritania gained international recognition, and war ensued with the independence-seeking <a href="/wiki/Polisario_Front" title="Polisario Front">Polisario Front</a>. The UN considers the Polisario Front to be the legitimate representative of the <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_people" title="Sahrawi people">Sahrawi people</a>, and maintains that the people of Western Sahara have a right to "<a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a> and independence".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The creation of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic was proclaimed on February 27, 1976, as the Polisario declared the need for a new entity to fill what they considered a political void left by the departing Spanish colonizers. While the claimed capital is the former Western Sahara capital El-Aaiún (which is in Moroccan-controlled territory), the proclamation was made in the <a href="/wiki/Government-in-exile" class="mw-redirect" title="Government-in-exile">government-in-exile</a>'s provisional capital, <a href="/wiki/Bir_Lehlou" title="Bir Lehlou">Bir Lehlou</a>, which remained in Polisario-held territory under the 1991 <a href="/wiki/Ceasefire" title="Ceasefire">ceasefire</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Settlement_Plan" title="Settlement Plan">Settlement Plan</a>). On February 27, 2008, the provisional capital was formally moved to <a href="/wiki/Tifariti" title="Tifariti">Tifariti</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Day-to-day business, however, is conducted in the <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_refugee_camps" title="Sahrawi refugee camps">Sahrawi refugee camps</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tindouf_Province" title="Tindouf Province">Tindouf Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, which house most of the Sahrawi exile community. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitution">Constitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Constitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Constitution of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">Constitution of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic</a>
      </div> 
      <p>A new 1999 Constitution of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic took a form similar to the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentarism" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentarism">parliamentary</a> constitutions of many European states, but with some paragraphs suspended until the achievement of "full independence". Among key points, the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> is constitutionally the Secretary General of the Polisario Front during what is referred to as the "pre-independence phase", with provision in the constitution that on independence, Polisario is supposed to be dismantled or separated completely from the government structure. Provisions are detailed for a transitory phase beginning with independence, in which the present SADR is supposed to act as Western Sahara's government, ending with a constitutional reform and eventual establishment of a state along the lines specified in the constitution. </p>
      <p>The broad guidelines laid down in the constitution for an eventual Western Saharan state include eventual multi-party democracy with a <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market economy</a>. The constitution also defines Sahrawis as a Muslim, African and Arab people.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Constitution also declares a commitment to the principles of <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> and to the concept of a <a href="/wiki/Arab_Maghreb_Union" title="Arab Maghreb Union">Greater Maghreb</a>, as a regional variant of <a href="/wiki/Pan-Arabism" title="Pan-Arabism">Pan-Arabism</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_structure">Government structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Government structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>Since August 1982, the highest office of the republic has been the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="President of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">President of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic</a>, a post held by the secretary-general of the Polisario Front, presently <a href="/wiki/Brahim_Ghali" title="Brahim Ghali">Brahim Ghali</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Zunes_2010_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zunes_2010-12">[12]</a></sup> who appoints the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Prime Minister of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">Prime Minister</a>, presently <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_Wali_Akeik" title="Mohamed Wali Akeik">Mohamed Wali Akeik</a>. The SADR's government structure consists of a Council of Ministers (a cabinet led by the Prime Minister), a judicial branch (with judges appointed by the President) and the parliamentary <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_National_Council" title="Sahrawi National Council">Sahrawi National Council</a> (SNC; the present <a href="/wiki/Speaker_(politics)" title="Speaker (politics)">speaker</a> is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kathri_Aduh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kathri Aduh (page does not exist)">Kathri Aduh</a>). Since its inception in 1976, the various <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Constitution of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">constitutional revisions</a> have transformed the republic from an <i>ad hoc</i> managerial structure into something approaching an actual governing apparatus. From the late 1980s the parliament began to take steps to institute a <a href="/wiki/Division_of_powers" class="mw-redirect" title="Division of powers">division of powers</a> and to disentangle the republic's structures from those of the Polisario Front, although without clear effect to date. </p>
      <p>Its various ministries are responsible for a variety of services and functions. The <a href="/wiki/Judiciary" title="Judiciary">judiciary</a>, complete with trial courts, <a href="/wiki/Appeals_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Appeals court">appeals courts</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">supreme court</a>, operates in the same areas. As a <a href="/wiki/Government_in_exile" title="Government in exile">government-in-exile</a>, many branches of government do not fully function, and has affected the constitutional roles of the institutions. Institutions parallel to government structures also have arisen within the Polisario Front, which is fused with the SADR's governing apparatus, and with operational competences overlapping between these party and governmental institutions and offices. A 2012 report mentioned the existence of the Sahrawi Bar Association.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2016, the bar association (going by the name Union of Sahrawi Lawyers) issued a report calling for the implementation of political and civil rights.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Unfortunately, there is no clear indication as to how certain demographic groups, such as women, have fared in the legal field. </p>
      <p>The SNC is weak in its legislative role, having been instituted as a mainly consultative and <a href="/wiki/Consensus" class="mw-redirect" title="Consensus">consensus</a>-building institution, but it has strengthened its theoretical legislative and controlling powers during later constitutional revisions. Among other things, it has added a ban on the <a href="/wiki/Death_penalty" class="mw-redirect" title="Death penalty">death penalty</a> to the constitution, and brought down the government in 1999 through a <a href="/wiki/Vote_of_no-confidence" class="mw-redirect" title="Vote of no-confidence">vote of no-confidence</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Area_of_authority">Area of authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Area of authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The SADR acted as a government administration in the <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_refugee_camps" title="Sahrawi refugee camps">Sahrawi refugee camps</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Tindouf_Province" title="Tindouf Province">Tindouf Province</a> of western Algeria. It is headquartered in Camp Rabouni, south of <a href="/wiki/Tindouf" title="Tindouf">Tindouf</a>, although some official events have taken place in <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Western_Sahara" title="List of cities in Western Sahara">towns</a> in the Free Zone, including the provisional capitals, first <a href="/wiki/Bir_Lehlou" title="Bir Lehlou">Bir Lehlou</a> until 2008, then Tifariti. The government of the SADR claims sovereignty over all of the Western Sahara territory, but has control only within the Free Zone. Several foreign <a href="/wiki/Aid" title="Aid">aid</a> agencies, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Refugees" title="United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees">United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organizations</a>, are continually active in the camps. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_recognition_and_membership">International recognition and membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International recognition and membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Western_Sahara" title="Political status of Western Sahara">Political status of Western Sahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_recognition_of_the_Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="International recognition of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">International recognition of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_the_Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Foreign relations of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">Foreign relations of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic</a>
      </div> 
      <p>As of November 2020, the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic has been recognized by 85 states. Of these, 45 have since "frozen" or "withdrawn" recognition for a number of reasons. A total of 40 UN states maintain diplomatic relations with the SADR, while a further 7 also recognise the state. <a href="/wiki/List_of_diplomatic_missions_of_the_Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="List of diplomatic missions of the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">Sahrawi embassies exist in 18 states</a>. </p>
      <p>Although it is not recognised by the UN, the SADR has held full membership of the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> (AU, formerly the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_of_African_Unity" title="Organisation of African Unity">Organisation of African Unity</a>, OAU) since 1982. Morocco withdrew from the OAU in protest during 1984, and from the time of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>'s admittance to the OAU in 1994 was the only African UN member not also a member of the AU, until it was readmitted on 30 January 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-worldbulletin_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-worldbulletin-15">[15]</a></sup> The SADR participates as a guest in meetings of the <a href="/wiki/16th_Summit_of_the_Non-Aligned_Movement" title="16th Summit of the Non-Aligned Movement">Non-Aligned Movement</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Asian%E2%80%93African_Strategic_Partnership" title="New Asian–African Strategic Partnership">New Asian–African Strategic Partnership</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NAASP_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAASP-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Asian-African_Strategic_Partnership_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Asian-African_Strategic_Partnership-19">[19]</a></sup> over Moroccan objections to SADR participation.<sup id="cite_ref-Asian-African_Strategic_Partnership_2_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Asian-African_Strategic_Partnership_2-20">[20]</a></sup> On the other hand, Morocco's claim to Western Sahara is supported by the <a href="/wiki/Arab_League" title="Arab League">Arab League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Arabic_1999_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arabic_1999-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Arabic_1998_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arabic_1998-22">[22]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The SADR also participated in a conference of the Permanent Conference of Political Parties of the Latin American and the Caribbean (<a href="/wiki/COPPPAL" title="COPPPAL">COPPPAL</a>) in 2006;<sup id="cite_ref-Permanent_Conference_of_Political_Parties_of_the_Latin_American_and_the_Caribbean_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Permanent_Conference_of_Political_Parties_of_the_Latin_American_and_the_Caribbean-23">[23]</a></sup> the SADR ambassador to Nicaragua participated in the opening conference of the <a href="/wiki/Central_American_Parliament" title="Central American Parliament">Central American Parliament</a> in 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and a SADR delegation participated in the meeting of COPPPAL and <a href="/wiki/International_Conference_of_Asian_Political_Parties" title="International Conference of Asian Political Parties">International Conference of Asian Political Parties</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On 27 February 2011, the 35th anniversary of the proclamation of SADR was held in Tifariti, Western Sahara. Delegations, including parliamentarians, ambassadors, NGOs and activists from many countries participated in this event.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The SADR is not a member of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_League" title="Arab League">Arab League</a>, nor of the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Maghreb_Union" title="Arab Maghreb Union">Arab Maghreb Union</a>, both of which include Morocco as a full member. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_Western_Sahara_Authority">Proposed Western Sahara Authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Proposed Western Sahara Authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Baker_Plan" title="Baker Plan">Baker Plan</a> created by <a href="/wiki/James_Baker" title="James Baker">James Baker</a>, former UN Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a>'s personal envoy to Western Sahara, the SADR would have been replaced with a five-year transitional <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Sahara Authority">Western Sahara Authority</a> (WSA), a non-sovereign <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_administrative_division" title="Autonomous administrative division">autonomous region</a> supervised by Morocco, to be followed by a referendum on independence. It was endorsed by the UN in 2003. However, as Morocco has declined to participate, the plan appears dead.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>In April 2007, the government of Morocco suggested that a self-governing entity, through the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Advisory_Council_for_Saharan_Affairs" title="Royal Advisory Council for Saharan Affairs">Royal Advisory Council for Saharan Affairs</a> (CORCAS), should govern the territory with some degree of autonomy for Western Sahara. The project was presented to the UN Security Council in mid-April 2007. A stalemate over the Moroccan proposal led the UN, in an April 2007 "Report of the UN Secretary-General", to ask the parties to enter into direct and unconditional negotiations to reach a mutually accepted political solution.<sup id="cite_ref-UN_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UN-28">[28]</a></sup> </p>
