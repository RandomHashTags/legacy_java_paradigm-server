Its <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Croatia" title="List of cities in Croatia">largest city</a> is <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a>. It was one of the republics of the Socialist Federal Republic of <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>. It became independent in 1991. It joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> on 1 July 2013. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In earliest times, the land now known as Croatia was inhabited by seafaring pirates known to the Romans as the Dalmatian Tribes (Such as the Histri, Liburni and the Delmatae), until the Romans formally conquered and annexed the territory in 9AD, calling it the province of "Dalmatia" named after these tribes. During the more than 400 years of Roman rule, the province of Dalmatia would become <a href="/wiki/Romanized" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanized">Romanized</a>: with the Roman invaders mixing with the natives and their descendants adopting a Roman lifestyle even after the collapse of the Roman state in 476 AD. </p>
      <p>In the early seventh century AD, pagan <a href="/wiki/West_Slavs" title="West Slavs">West Slavs</a> named the <a href="/wiki/Croats" title="Croats">Croats</a> (where Croatia gets its name) came to live in the former province of Dalmatia when they conquered the people already living there. However, they would soon be influenced by <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> Christian missionaries who completed the process of <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianization</a> in the early 9th century. From the time of the crowning of Tomislav in 925 as the first King of Croatia, Croatia would become its own independent Catholic kingdom until 1102, when a Hungarian prince inherited the Croatian throne. The Hungarians would rule as Kings of Croatia until the early modern period, when the last Hungarian king of Croatia Louis II was killed at the Battle of Mohacs in 1526 and the kingdom chose the Austrian Archduke Ferdinand as king the next year. </p>
      <p>Croatia would remain as a part of the empire of <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> under Ferdinand's descendants until 1918. In 1918 it became a part of Kingdom of Slovenians, Croats and Serbs, later named <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> which was occupied in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. After a short war with Italy a <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascist dictatorship</a> formed the <a href="/wiki/Independent_State_of_Croatia" title="Independent State of Croatia">Independent State of Croatia</a> in 1941, but it was not independent from the control of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Jasenovac_concentration_camp" title="Jasenovac concentration camp">Jasenovac concentration camp</a>). </p>
      <p>In 1945, Croatia became a part of new Yugoslavia (Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia) which was non-aligned after breaking connections with Eastern Block. It collapsed in 1991 as initially Slovenia, than Croatia, later other republics claimed higher independence and transition to parliamentary democracies. War with Yugoslavia made a strong impact on society in economic, political and cultural terms. Civil society and media that came out of <a href="/wiki/Arkzin" title="Arkzin">anti-war struggle in Croatia</a> grew against nationalism, media and civil freedoms, later also corruption. </p>
      <p>The country's democratic development around 2000, made it possible for Croatia to finally join Slovenia as only the second republic to become the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> member on 1 July 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In recent decades Croatia is increasingly popular tourists destination, especially coastal regions of <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">Istria</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Geography">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Geography">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Hr-map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Hr-map.png/220px-Hr-map.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fc/Hr-map.png 1.5x" data-file-width="328" data-file-height="353"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

         </div>A map of Croatia
        </div>

      <p>Croatia is located in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Europe" title="Southeast Europe">Southeast Europe</a>, bordering <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> to the northeast, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a> to the south-east, <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">Montenegro</a> to the south-east, the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a> to the south-west and <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a> to the northwest. It lies mostly between latitudes <a href="/wiki/42nd_parallel_north" title="42nd parallel north">42°</a> and <a href="/wiki/47th_parallel_north" title="47th parallel north">47° N</a> and longitudes <a href="/wiki/13th_meridian_east" title="13th meridian east">13°</a> and <a href="/wiki/20th_meridian_east" title="20th meridian east">20° E</a>. Part of the territory in the extreme south surrounding <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a> is a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Practical_exclave&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Practical exclave (not yet started)">practical exclave</a> connected to the rest of the mainland by <a href="/wiki/Territorial_waters" title="Territorial waters">territorial waters</a>, but separated on land by a short coastline strip belonging to Bosnia and Herzegovina around <a href="/wiki/Neum" title="Neum">Neum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Croatia is divided into 21 <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_Croatia" title="Counties of Croatia">counties</a>. </p>
      <p>Croatia is the 127th largest country in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-CIA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIA-6">[6]</a></sup> The highest point is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinara&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dinara (not yet started)">Dinara</a> peak at 1,831 metres (6,007 feet). Thousands of islands are part of Croatia. 48 have people living there year round. The largest islands are <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cres&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cres (not yet started)">Cres</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Krk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Krk (not yet started)">Krk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CIA_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIA-6">[6]</a></sup> Major rivers are the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sava&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sava (not yet started)">Sava</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Drava&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Drava (not yet started)">Drava</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kupa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kupa (not yet started)">Kupa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>. </p>
      <p>There are many deep caves in Croatia. 49 of which are deeper than 250&nbsp;m (820.21&nbsp;ft). Croatia's most famous lakes are the <a href="/wiki/Plitvice_Lakes_National_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Plitvice Lakes National Park">Plitvice lakes</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Climate">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Change section: Climate">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Most of Croatia has a moderately warm and rainy <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental climate</a>. Average temperature ranges between −3&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (27&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>) (in January) and 18&nbsp;°C (64&nbsp;°F) (in July). The coldest parts of the country are <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lika&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lika (not yet started)">Lika</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gorski_Kotar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gorski Kotar (not yet started)">Gorski Kotar</a>. The warmest are at the Adriatic coast. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Biodiversity">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Change section: Biodiversity">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>There are several <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a> in Croatia. The coastline, forests, mountains, and rivers give Croatia diverse <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>. There are more than a thousand <a href="/wiki/Endemic" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemic">endemic</a> species. </p>
      <p>Croatia is home to the only known aquatic cave <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a>—the <a href="/wiki/Olm" title="Olm">olm</a>. </p>
      <p>There are 444 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Protected_areas_of_Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Protected areas of Croatia (not yet started)">protected areas of Croatia</a>. Those include eight <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_national_parks_of_Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="List of national parks of Croatia (not yet started)">national parks</a>, two strict reserves, and ten <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nature_park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nature park (not yet started)">nature parks</a>. The oldest <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national park</a> in Croatia is the Plitvice Lakes National Park, a <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Sites_in_Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="World Heritage Sites in Croatia (not yet started)">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Politics">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Change section: Politics">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Croatia adopted its <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes-Elections2-HRV1990_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-Elections2-HRV1990-7">[7]</a></sup> It <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_independence" title="Declaration of independence">declared independence</a> from <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> on 8 October 1991. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_Croatia" title="President of Croatia">President of the Republic</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a>. The President is directly elected to a five-year term. The Constitution limits the President to a maximum of two terms. <a href="/wiki/Zoran_Milanovi%C4%87" title="Zoran Milanović">Zoran Milanović</a> became president in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Croatia" title="Prime Minister of Croatia">Prime Minister of Croatia</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a>. Since 2016, the prime minister of the government is <a href="/wiki/Andrej_Plenkovi%C4%87" title="Andrej Plenković">Andrej Plenković</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Economy">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Change section: Economy">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>Croatia is one of the richest countries of the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Balkan Peninsula">Balkan Peninsula</a> and of the former <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>'s countries. But Croatia had also the highest cost prices of the whole <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>. The average monthly salary/wages in Croatia standing on 739 euro or nearly $1000 USD.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The retirement age for men is 65 years and for women 60 years.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The health care enjoys relative strong protection for the country's inhabitants. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Education">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Change section: Education">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The education is free and required until the child reaches the age of 15. Many choose to continue their studies in high school until the age of 18.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Tourism">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Change section: Tourism">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>After the war that devastated the country, Croatia has rebuilt its infrastructure, revitalizing its image as a Mediterranean country with crystal clear waters, medieval cities that mix western and eastern architecture, respect for the environment and traditions with the arrival of tourism. <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a> has become the most characteristic symbol of this new Croatia that is strongly committed to European tourism. It is developing a type of tourism based on sustainability, in which culture, gastronomy and nature coexist with the visitor, offering them the experience of discovering Croatia as part of it. </p>
      <p>There are many reasons to visit Croatia. Its six UNESCO World Heritage sites&nbsp;: Dubrovnik's Old Town (undoubtedly one of the most beautiful cities in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>), the Diocletian's Palace in Split , the historic core of Trogir , the Cathedral of St. James of Sibenik , the Plitvice Lakes National Park , or the Episcopal Ensemble of the Euphrasian Basilica in the Historic Center of Porec (Istria) . We could name the wonders of Croatia one by one, but we prefer that you go into our Guide and discover for yourself each town, each city and every corner. </p>
      <p>The nature is another attractive source of tourism in Croatia. Its eight National Parks and ten Natural Parks with their varied fauna and flora show all their splendor, with a coastline that winds from the Istria Peninsula to the south of Dalmatia, leaving beautiful coves, beaches, and cliffs. The islands, such as Hvar or Korcula , in Dalmatia or Rab and Losinj in Kvarner Bay , which are sometimes grouped together in archipelagos as interesting as those of Brijuni or Kornati, they host millions of tourists who seek peace or the possibility of practicing nudism, so associated with Croatia, in its crystalline waters. More than a thousand islands and islets that make up the Adriatic Coast, a paradise for those who love sailing and seek to cross the Adriatic by Sailboat . </p>
      <p>The variety of the tourist offer in Croatia goes beyond the beautiful cities Dubrovnik, <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a> or Split.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It is an ideal country to spend your holidays, practicing adventure sports, hiking , scuba diving , traveling through its islands by sailboat , enjoying its gastronomy or its excellent wines and getting lost with routes in the magical corners that history has left in its streets. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nightlife">Nightlife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Nightlife">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Change section: Nightlife">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>How do you imagine the nightlife in Croatia? beer is cheap, and the bartenders shake and prepare the latest cocktails while the DJ's mix the latest in the world, from Slavic music to soul, rock, electronic, jazz, international and beyond. </p>
      <p>It's just that, after a day of tanning on the beach, there is nothing better than going out to explore the nightlife of the city, going from Irish pubs to big parties, cocktail bars, rock clubs, and discos, just to start. </p>
      <p>To find the best places, you just have to follow the Croatian migration patterns. When everyone is working hard during the winter, the nightlife is found in big cities like Zagreb, Split, Rijeka, and Zadar.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>When the weather starts to heat up, the inhabitants of the big cities rush to the clubs, bars, and discos of the islands and beaches, especially in Hvar and the Pag Islands. </p>
      <p>So what time does the fun start? In the big cities, the most avant-garde music, art, and fashion are served in multipurpose cafes/bars/discos, which open from noon and remain open until midnight. </p>
