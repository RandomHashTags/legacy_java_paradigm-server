      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Chile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>On January 28, 1915, <a href="/wiki/President_of_Chile" title="President of Chile">President of Chile</a> <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Barros_Luco" title="Ramón Barros Luco">Ramón Barros Luco</a> promulgated law 2,977,<sup id="cite_ref-law_2977_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_2977-5">[5]</a></sup> which arranged all the information insofar about holidays celebrated in Chile. It established or kept the following holidays: </p> 
      <ol>
       <li>Sundays year-round.</li> 
       <li>January 1 (<a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Circumcision_of_Christ" title="Feast of the Circumcision of Christ">Feast of the Circumcision of Christ</a> at the time; <a href="/wiki/New_Year" title="New Year">New Year</a> in the late 20th and 21st centuries), June 29 (<a href="/wiki/Feast_of_Saints_Peter_and_Paul" title="Feast of Saints Peter and Paul">Saint Peter and Saint Paul</a>), August 15 (<a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">Assumption of Mary</a>), December 8 (<a href="/wiki/Immaculate_Conception" title="Immaculate Conception">Immaculate Conception</a>), December 25 (<a href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>), and the floating holidays of the <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascension of Jesus Christ">Ascension of Jesus Christ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_(feast)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corpus Christi (feast)">Corpus Christi</a>.</li> 
       <li>Fridays and Saturdays during <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">Holy Week</a>.</li> 
       <li>September 18, Establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Government_Junta_of_Chile_(1810)" title="Government Junta of Chile (1810)">First Government Junta</a> in 1810.</li> 
       <li>September 19 and May 21, in celebration of the glories of the Army and the Navy.</li> 
       <li>The day of election of electors of the President of the Republic.</li>
      </ol> 
      <p>The same law abolished, by omission, four religious holidays: <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">Epiphany</a> ("Adoración de los Santos Reyes", January 6), <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a> ("Carnaval", mobile), <a href="/wiki/Annunciation" title="Annunciation">Annunciation</a> ("Anunciación del Señor", march 25) and <a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_the_Theotokos" class="mw-redirect" title="Nativity of the Theotokos">Nativity of the Virgin Mary</a> ("Natividad de la Virgen", September 8). It also shortened the <a href="/wiki/Fiestas_Patrias_(Chile)" title="Fiestas Patrias (Chile)">national independence holiday</a> to two days (until 1914, they occupied September 18, 19 and 20). </p>
      <p>On November 14, 1921, President <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Alessandri_Palma" class="mw-redirect" title="Arturo Alessandri Palma">Arturo Alessandri Palma</a> promulgated Law 3,810,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> marking October 12 (<a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day" title="Columbus Day">Columbus Day</a>) as holiday. </p>
      <p>On April 30, 1931, President <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_del_Campo" title="Carlos Ibáñez del Campo">Carlos Ibáñez del Campo</a> promulgated the "Decree Enforceable as Law" ("Decreto con Fuerza de Ley") 130, marking May 1 (<a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a>) as holiday. </p>
      <p>In 1932, Provisional President <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Blanche" title="Bartolomé Blanche">Bartolomé Blanche</a> reinstated the September 20 holiday,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which existed until 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1968, the holidays of <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_Saints_Peter_and_Paul" title="Feast of Saints Peter and Paul">Saint Peter and Saint Paul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascension of Jesus Christ">Ascension of Jesus Christ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_(feast)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corpus Christi (feast)">Corpus Christi</a> were eliminated by Article 144 of Law 16,840.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Catholic Church agreed to celebrate them on the nearest Sunday. </p>
      <p>September 11 (the day of the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_coup_of_1973" class="mw-redirect" title="Chilean coup of 1973">military coup in 1973</a>) was established as a holiday (<i>Día de la Liberación Nacional</i>, "Day of National Liberation") by the military regime in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1998 it was replaced by the <i>Día de la Unidad Nacional</i> ("Day of National Unity"),<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> to be observed the first Monday in September. In March 2002 the latter was abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The holiday on June 29 (<a href="/wiki/Feast_of_Saints_Peter_and_Paul" title="Feast of Saints Peter and Paul">Saint Peter and Saint Paul</a>) was reestablished in September 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1987, during a visit by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_(feast)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corpus Christi (feast)">Corpus Christi</a> was reestablished<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> (although the Church continued to observe the holiday on the nearest Sunday<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>). </p>
      <p>On March 10, 2000, Law 19,668<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> moved the <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_Saints_Peter_and_Paul" title="Feast of Saints Peter and Paul">Saint Peter and Saint Paul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day" title="Columbus Day">Columbus Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_(feast)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corpus Christi (feast)">Corpus Christi</a> holidays to the preceding Monday, if they were to fall on a Tuesday, Wednesday or Thursday, or to the following Monday, if they were to fall on a Friday. Additionally, the designation of <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day" title="Columbus Day">Columbus Day</a> was changed from <i>Aniversario del Descubrimiento de América</i> to <i>Día del Descubrimiento de Dos Mundos</i> ("Discovery of Two Worlds' Day"), an obvious corruption from the then-recent ubiquitous "Encuentro de dos mundos" ("The Meeting of Two Worlds") slogan. <sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On January 6, 2007, Law 20,148<sup id="cite_ref-law_20148_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_20148-2">[2]</a></sup> replaced <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_(feast)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corpus Christi (feast)">Corpus Christi</a> with <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Mount_Carmel" title="Our Lady of Mount Carmel">Our Lady of Mount Carmel</a>, to be observed July 16, starting that year. </p>
      <p>On September 14, 2007, Law 20,215.<sup id="cite_ref-law_20215_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_20215-22">[22]</a></sup> declared holidays all September 17s falling on a Monday and all September 20s falling on a Friday. </p>
      <p>On October 11, 2008, Law 20,299<sup id="cite_ref-law_20299_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_20299-4">[4]</a></sup> established <a href="/wiki/Reformation_Day" title="Reformation Day">Reformation Day</a> on October 31 as a national holiday, starting that year. This holiday is moved to the preceding Friday if it falls on a Tuesday, or to the following Friday if it falls on a Wednesday. </p>
      <p>On April 30, 2013, Law 20,663 established the regional <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Arica" title="Battle of Arica">Battle of Arica</a> holiday, to be observed only in the <a href="/wiki/Arica_and_Parinacota" class="mw-redirect" title="Arica and Parinacota">Arica and Parinacota</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-law_20663_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_20663-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On December 30, 2016, Law 20,983<sup id="cite_ref-law_20983_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_20983-24">[24]</a></sup> declared as holidays those days that are Monday January 2 or Friday September 17. </p>
      <p>Besides yearly holidays, since 1901 presidential elections are declared holidays, as mandated by successive versions of the General Elections law (Law 18,700<sup id="cite_ref-law_18,700_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_18,700-25">[25]</a></sup> being the current one; see laws 1,464,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> 1,752,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> 2,977,<sup id="cite_ref-law_2977_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_2977-5">[5]</a></sup> decree-law 542,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> 6,250,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> 6,834, 9,334 and 12,891). Since the late 20th century, parliamentary and municipal elections are also public holidays (see Law 18,700<sup id="cite_ref-law_18,700_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_18,700-25">[25]</a></sup>). Censuses, held every ten years, are also declared holidays since 1982; that year's census and 1992's were so due to ad-hoc laws;<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> censuses taken from 1992 onwards are declared holidays due to a reform in the Census law.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> (This did not occur in 2012, where the census was carried out in the space of two months, using a different methodology.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>) </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning_of_the_January_1_holiday">Meaning of the January 1 holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Chile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meaning of the January 1 holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Shortly after Chile's independence, an 1824 (government-approved) Church decree<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> reduced the amount of religious holidays, enumerating and the surviving holidays, including the <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Circumcision_of_Christ" title="Feast of the Circumcision of Christ">Feast of the Circumcision of Christ</a>, observed on January 1. Law 2,977 (passed in 1915, still in vigour) lists this holiday by date only, without using any name, but does treat it as religious one.<sup id="cite_ref-law_2977_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law_2977-5">[5]</a></sup> However, nowadays (as of 2009) it's widely celebrated as the (civic) New Year holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (February 2010)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup> As a matter of fact, there aren't any known regulations later than 1915 that use the "Circumcision of Christ" name, while "New Year" is used by numerous regulations of all types (laws (19.925<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>), decree-laws (<i>decretos ley</i>) (1.299,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> 1.613<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and 2,408<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>), decrees enforceable as law (<i>decretos con fuerza de ley</i>) (D.F.L 338 of 1960<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Ministerial_decree" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministerial decree">ministerial decrees</a> and resolutions (decree 6234 of 1929 of the Public Education Ministry<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> (the oldest such regulation), decree 83 of 1985 of the Labor Ministry,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> decree 48 of 2007 of the Labor Ministry,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> et cetera) and municipal decrees and ordinances (decree 1316 of 2004 of the Las Condes Municipality,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> decree 23 of 2006 of the Padre Las Casas Municipality,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> decree 23 of 2009 of the San Carlos Municipality,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> et cetera). </p>
