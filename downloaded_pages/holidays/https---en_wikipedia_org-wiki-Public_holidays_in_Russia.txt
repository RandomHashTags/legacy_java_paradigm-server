      <p>The following is the list of <b>official public holidays recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Russia" title="Government of Russia">Government of Russia</a></b>. On these days, government offices, <a href="/wiki/Embassy" class="mw-redirect" title="Embassy">embassies</a> and some shops, are closed. If the date of observance falls on a <a href="/wiki/Workweek_and_weekend#Russia" title="Workweek and weekend">weekend</a>, the following Monday will be a day off in lieu of the holiday. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_National_Holidays">Major National Holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Major National Holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Year_Holiday">New Year Holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: New Year Holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In addition to <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">New Year's Day</a> (Новый год <i>Novy god</i>) on 1 January, 2–5 January are public holidays as well,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-laboru112holidays_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laboru112holidays-2">[2]</a></sup> called New Year holiday (Новогодние каникулы <i>Novogodniye kanikuly</i>). The holiday includes January 6 and 8, with Christmas being January 7, declared as non-working days by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Russian_labour_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Russian labour law (page does not exist)">law</a>. Until 2005, only 1 and 2 January were public holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthodox_Christmas_day">Orthodox Christmas day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orthodox Christmas day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Christmas_in_Russia" title="Christmas in Russia">Christmas in Russia</a>
      </div>
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Further information: <a href="/wiki/Christmas_traditions" title="Christmas traditions">Christmas traditions</a>
      </div> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Christmas_in_Russia" title="Christmas in Russia">Christmas in Russia</a> (Рождество Христово <i>Rozhdestvo Khristovo</i>) is celebrated on 25 December <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a> which falls on January 7th <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> and commemorates the birth of Jesus Christ. The public holiday was re-established in 1991, following the decades of <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Religion in the Soviet Union">suppression of religion</a> and <a href="/wiki/State_atheism" title="State atheism">state atheism</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defender_of_the_Fatherland_Day">Defender of the Fatherland Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Defender of the Fatherland Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Defender_of_the_Fatherland_Day" title="Defender of the Fatherland Day">Defender of the Fatherland Day</a> (<i>День защитника Отечествa</i> or <i>Den zashchitnika Otechestva</i>) is celebrated on 23 February and is dedicated to veterans and members of the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed Forces of the Russian Federation">Armed Forces of the Russian Federation</a>, though it is often colloquially known as Men's Day (День Мужчин, <i>Den' Muzhchin</i>) and commonly treated as a celebration of all men. The holiday was established in 1918. </p> 
      <h3><span id="International_Women.27s_Day"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Women's_Day">International Women's Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International Women's Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On the eve of World War I campaigning for peace, Russian women observed their first <a href="/wiki/International_Women%27s_Day" title="International Women's Day">International Women's Day</a> on the last Sunday in February 1913. In 1913 following discussions, International Women's Day was transferred to 8 March and has remained the global date for <a href="/wiki/International_Women%27s_Day" title="International Women's Day">International Women's Day</a> ever since. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spring_and_Labour_day">Spring and Labour day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spring and Labour day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Further information: <a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day#Russia" title="International Workers' Day">International Workers' Day §&nbsp;Russia</a>
      </div> 
      <p>In the former Soviet Union, 1 May was <a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">International Workers' Day</a> and was celebrated with huge parades in cities like <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>. Though the celebrations are low-key nowadays, several groups march on that day to protest grievances the workers have. Since 1992, May Day is officially called "The Day of Spring and Labour". </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Victory_Day">Victory Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Victory Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>May 9, Russia celebrates the victory over <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, while remembering those who died in order to achieve it. On 9 May 1945 (by <a href="/wiki/Moscow_time" class="mw-redirect" title="Moscow time">Moscow time</a>) the German military surrendered to the Soviet Union and the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies of World War II</a> in Berlin (Karlshorst). <a href="/wiki/Victory_Day_(May_9)" class="mw-redirect" title="Victory Day (May 9)">Victory Day</a> (День Победы <i>Den Pobedy</i>) is by far one of the biggest Russian holidays. It commemorates those who died in World War II and pays tribute to survivors and veterans. Flowers and wreaths are laid on wartime graves and special parties and concerts are organized for veterans. In the evening there is a firework display. A huge ground and air military parade, hosted by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="President of the Russian Federation">President of the Russian Federation</a>, is annually organized in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> on <a href="/wiki/Red_Square" title="Red Square">Red Square</a>. Similar ground, air and marine (if it is possible) parades are organized in several other Russian cities (which are <a href="/wiki/Hero_City" title="Hero City">Hero Cities</a> or have <a href="/wiki/Military_districts_of_Russia" title="Military districts of Russia">military district</a> or <a href="/wiki/Russian_Navy" title="Russian Navy">fleet</a> headquarters primarily). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia_Day">Russia Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Russia Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><b><a href="/wiki/Russia_Day" title="Russia Day">Russia Day</a></b> (День России <i>Den Rossii</i>) National Day, celebrated on June 12. On this day, in 1991, Russian parliament formally declared Russian sovereignty from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. The holiday was officially established in 1992. Initially it was named <b>Day of the Adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_State_Sovereignty_of_the_Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic" title="Declaration of State Sovereignty of the Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic">Declaration of Sovereignty of the Russian Federation</a></b>, on 1 February 2002 it was officially renamed to Russia Day (in 1998 <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a> offered this name socially). In Russian society there is a misconception that this holiday is also called "Russia's Independence Day", but it never has had such a name in official documents. According to a survey by <a href="/wiki/Levada_Center" title="Levada Center">Levada Center</a> in May 2009, 44% of respondents named the holiday as "Independence Day of Russia". </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unity_Day">Unity Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Unity Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Unity_Day_(Russia)" title="Unity Day (Russia)">Unity Day</a> (День народного единства <i>Den narodnogo edinstva</i>) was first celebrated on November 4, 2005, commemorates the popular uprising led by <a href="/wiki/Kuzma_Minin" title="Kuzma Minin">Kuzma Minin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Pozharsky" title="Dmitry Pozharsky">Dmitry Pozharsky</a> which ejected the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish</a> invaders from Moscow in November 1612, and more generally the end of the <a href="/wiki/Time_of_Troubles" title="Time of Troubles">Time of Troubles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Muscovite_War_(1605%E2%80%931618)" title="Polish–Muscovite War (1605–1618)">foreign intervention in Russia</a>. The event was marked by a public holiday which was held in Russia on October 22 (<a href="/wiki/Old_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Style">Old Style</a>) from 1649 till 1917. Its name alludes to the idea that all the classes of Russian society willingly united to preserve Russian statehood when its demise seemed inevitable, even though there was neither Tsar nor Patriarch to guide them. Most observers view this as an attempted replacement to counter <a href="/wiki/Communist" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist">Communist</a> demonstrations on November 7 holiday, which marked the anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a>. National Unity Day is also known as Consolidation Day (as an alternative translation), which people in Russia celebrate on November 3–4. </p>
