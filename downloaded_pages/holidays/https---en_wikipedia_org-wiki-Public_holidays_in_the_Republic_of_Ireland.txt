   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Public holidays in the Republic of Ireland</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>

      <p> These are the <b>public holidays</b> observed in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">Public holidays</a> in Ireland (as in other countries) may commemorate a special day or other event, such as <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick%27s_Day" title="Saint Patrick's Day">Saint Patrick's Day</a> or Christmas Day. On public holidays (sometimes incorrectly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holidays</a> – a colloquialism), most businesses and schools close. Other services, for example, public transport, still operate but often with reduced schedules. </p>
      <p>The nine public holidays in Ireland each year are as follows: </p>

      <p>Note that where a public holiday falls on a Saturday or a Sunday, or possibly coincides with another public holiday, it is generally observed (as a day off work) on the next available weekday, even though the public holiday itself does not move. In such cases, an employee is entitled to at least one of the following (as chosen by the employer): a day off within a month, an additional day's paid annual leave or an additional day's pay. The usual practice is, however, to award a day off on the next available weekday. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> <a href="/wiki/Bank_Holidays_Act_1871" title="Bank Holidays Act 1871">Bank Holidays Act 1871</a> established the first Bank holidays in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. The Act designated four Bank holidays in Ireland: Easter Monday; <a href="/wiki/Whit_Monday" title="Whit Monday">Whit Monday</a>; St. Stephen's Day and the first Monday in August. As <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a> and Christmas Day were traditional days of rest and Christian worship (as were Sundays), therefore it was felt unnecessary to include them in the Act as they were already recognised as common law holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-TUC_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TUC-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1903, Saint Patrick's Day became an official public holiday in Ireland. This was due to the Bank Holiday (Ireland) Act 1903, an Act of the United Kingdom Parliament introduced by the Irish <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> <a href="/wiki/James_O%27Mara" title="James O'Mara">James O'Mara</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-omara_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-omara-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1939, the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a> passed the Holidays (Employees) Act 1939 which designated the public holidays as Christmas Day; St. Stephen's Day; St. Patrick's Day; Easter Monday; Whit Monday and the first Monday in August.<sup id="cite_ref-Hol_act_1939_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hol_act_1939-8">[8]</a></sup> The Holidays (Employees) Act 1973 replaced the Whit Monday holiday with the first Monday in June.<sup id="cite_ref-Hol_act_1973_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hol_act_1973-5">[5]</a></sup> New Year's Day was not listed in the Act but was added by <a href="/wiki/Statutory_Instrument" class="mw-redirect" title="Statutory Instrument">Statutory Instrument</a> in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-New_year_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_year-9">[9]</a></sup> The October Holiday was added in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-Oct_hol_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oct_hol-6">[6]</a></sup> The first Monday in May (commonly known as May Day) was added in 1993 and first observed in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-MayDay_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MayDay-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1997 the Oireachtas passed the Organisation of Working Time Act. This Act, among other things, transposed European Union directives on working times into Irish law. Schedule 2 of the Act specifies the nine public holidays to which employees in Ireland are entitled to receive time off work, time in-lieu or holiday pay depending on their terms of employment.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>

      <ul>
       <li>In Ireland the academic year in <a href="/wiki/Primary_school" title="Primary school">primary schools</a> lasts from late August to late June.</li> 
       <li>The academic year is composed of 183 schooldays and schools are not open in July or August (though for flexibility school may open for the last two/three days of August).</li> 
       <li>The first mid-term break is always the last week of October (also called the <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> break).</li> 
       <li>Most Catholic schools previously closed for the <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Immaculate_Conception" title="Feast of the Immaculate Conception">Feast of the Immaculate Conception</a> on 8 December, but however this has stopped.</li> 
       <li>The Christmas break lasts from the last school day before 23 December to the first weekday after 6 January (17–21 days).</li> 
       <li>The second mid-term break is a minimum of two days to a maximum of five days duration taken in the third week of February (also called the Shrove break).</li> 
       <li>The Easter break consists of a week before Easter to the 2nd Monday after Easter (10 school days or 16 days inclusive).</li> 
       <li>In the last term holidays are flexible and are generally arranged around the public holidays in May and June.</li>
      </ul> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_schools">Secondary schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Secondary schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <ul>
       <li>In Ireland the academic year in <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">secondary schools</a> is composed of 167 school days and lasts from late August to early June.</li> 
       <li>The first mid-term break begins on the last weekend before 31 October and lasts for one week.</li> 
       <li>Many Catholic schools used to close for the Feast of the Immaculate Conception on 8 December but this however has stopped nationwide.</li> 
       <li>The Christmas break lasts from the last school day before 23 December to the first weekday after 6 January (10 school days or 15–18 days inclusive).</li> 
       <li>The second mid-term break begins on the last school day in the second week of February and lasts for one week.</li> 
       <li>The Easter break consists of a week before Easter to the second Monday after Easter (10 school days or 16 days inclusive).</li> 
       <li>The school year ends on the Friday before the June public holiday.</li> 
       <li>The state examinations (the <a href="/wiki/Junior_Certificate" title="Junior Certificate">Junior</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leaving_Certificate_(Ireland)" title="Leaving Certificate (Ireland)">Leaving Certificate</a> examinations) begin the Wednesday after the June Holiday.</li>
      </ul>
