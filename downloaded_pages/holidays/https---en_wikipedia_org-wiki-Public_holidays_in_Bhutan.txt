   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Public holidays in Bhutan</h1>

      <p><b>Public holidays in Bhutan</b> consist of both <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">national holidays</a> and local festivals or <i><a href="/wiki/Tshechu" title="Tshechu">tshechu</a></i>s. While national holidays are observed throughout <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, tsechus are only observed in their areas.<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> Bhutan uses its own calendar,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">lunisolar</a> <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_calendar" title="Tibetan calendar">Tibetan calendar</a>. Because it is a lunisolar calendar, dates of some national holidays and most tshechus change from year to year. For example, the new year, <a href="/wiki/Losar" title="Losar">Losar</a>, generally falls between February and March. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_holidays">National holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: National holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> has sixteen <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holidays</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> Bhutanese holidays are rooted in the <a href="/wiki/Drukpa_Lineage" title="Drukpa Lineage">Drukpa Lineage</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kagyu" title="Kagyu">Kagyu Buddhism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wangchuck" title="House of Wangchuck">House of Wangchuck</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_calendar" title="Tibetan calendar">Tibetan calendar</a>. Even secular holidays, however, have a measure of religious overtone, as religious choreography and blessings mark these auspicious days.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-3">[3]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Winter_solstice">Winter solstice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Winter solstice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Nyinlong or Nyilong (<a href="/wiki/Dzongkha_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Dzongkha language">Dzongkha</a>: <span lang="dz">ཉིན་ལོང་</span>, <a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie</a>: <i>nyin long</i> "return of the sun"),<sup id="cite_ref-Kuensel1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuensel1-4">[4]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a> celebration, is a public holiday falling on 2 January every year.<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mark1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mark1-5">[5]</a></sup> Nyinlog is considered the most auspicious day of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-Kuensel1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuensel1-4">[4]</a></sup> It is celebrated like new year among some western Bhutan, though more so in the central and eastern regions, where the shortest day of the year is marked with archery and feasting.<sup id="cite_ref-Mark1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mark1-5">[5]</a></sup> Farmers, on the other hand, may feel some chagrin as the solstice signals longer and longer work days ahead.<sup id="cite_ref-FW_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FW-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_Day_of_Offering">Traditional Day of Offering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Traditional Day of Offering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The Traditional Day of Offering<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Dzongkha" title="Dzongkha">Dzongkha</a>: <i>buelwa phuewi nyim</i>) is a holiday usually falling in January or February, on the 1st day of the 12th month of the calendar.<sup id="cite_ref-Kuensel2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuensel2-7">[7]</a></sup> The main purpose behind this holiday is to give thanks to <a href="/wiki/Ngawang_Namgyal" title="Ngawang Namgyal">Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal</a>, the founder of <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BJ_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BJ-8">[8]</a></sup> It also focuses on charity, particularly feeding others, and recreation.<sup id="cite_ref-FW_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FW-6">[6]</a></sup> The day is celebrated with feasting and traditional sports, including <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digor_(sports)" title="Digor (sports)">digor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Khuru_(sport)" title="Khuru (sport)">khuru</a> (<a href="/wiki/Darts" title="Darts">darts</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Kuensel2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuensel2-7">[7]</a></sup> This holiday may have originally begun as a Bhutanese new year celebration.<sup id="cite_ref-Kuensel3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuensel3-9">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Losar">Losar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Losar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Losar" title="Losar">Losar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dzongkha" title="Dzongkha">Dzongkha</a> <span lang="dz" title="Dzongkha-language text">ལོ་གསར་</span>, <a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie</a>: <i>lo gsar</i>), the New Year, is celebrated between February and March, officially on the 1st month, 1st day of the calendar.<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> Festivities last 15 days, ahead of which people spend much time preparing food and alcohol and cleaning their homes of old and unused objects.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In Bhutan, different communities celebrate Losar at slightly different times and refer to the holiday by particular local names.<sup id="cite_ref-Kuensel3_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuensel3-9">[9]</a></sup> The common Losar greeting is "<i>tashi delek</i>." </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birthday_of_King_Jigme_Khesar_Namgyel_Wangchuck">Birthday of King Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Birthday of King Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>February 21–23 are holidays commemorating the birth anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Jigme_Khesar_Namgyel_Wangchuck" title="Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck">Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck</a> the 5th and current <a href="/wiki/Druk_Gyalpo" title="Druk Gyalpo">Druk Gyalpo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zhabdrung_Kuchoe">Zhabdrung Kuchoe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Zhabdrung Kuchoe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Shabdrung Kurchoe<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> marks the passing of Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal in 1651 at <a href="/wiki/Punakha_Dzong" title="Punakha Dzong">Punakha Dzong</a>. It generally falls in April or May (4th month, 10th day of the calendar). The holiday is a national day of mourning.<sup id="cite_ref-SS_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS-12">[12]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birthday_of_King_Jigme_Dorji_Wangchuck">Birthday of King Jigme Dorji Wangchuck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Birthday of King Jigme Dorji Wangchuck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>May 2 is the birth anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Jigme_Dorji_Wangchuck" title="Jigme Dorji Wangchuck">Jigme Dorji Wangchuck</a> the 3rd <a href="/wiki/Druk_Gyalpo" title="Druk Gyalpo">Druk Gyalpo</a>, who began Bhutan's first steps toward modernization (b. 1928, <a href="/wiki/Thruepang_Palace" title="Thruepang Palace">Thruepang Palace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trongsa" title="Trongsa">Trongsa</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SS_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS-12">[12]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coronation_of_King_Jigme_Singye_Wangchuck">Coronation of King Jigme Singye Wangchuck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Coronation of King Jigme Singye Wangchuck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>June 2 is the coronation day of the <a href="/wiki/Jigme_Singye_Wangchuck" title="Jigme Singye Wangchuck">Jigme Singye Wangchuck</a>, the 4th <a href="/wiki/Druk_Gyalpo" title="Druk Gyalpo">Druk Gyalpo</a> (1974).<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> It also doubles as Social Forestry Day, where children plant trees.<sup id="cite_ref-SS_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS-12">[12]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parinirvana_of_Buddha">Parinirvana of Buddha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Parinirvana of Buddha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>June 15 is <a href="/wiki/Parinirvana_Day" title="Parinirvana Day">Parinirvana Day</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> a public holiday commemorating the <a href="/wiki/Nirvana" title="Nirvana">nirvana</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birthday_of_Guru_Rinpoche">Birthday of Guru Rinpoche</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Birthday of Guru Rinpoche">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>July 10 marks the birth anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Guru_Rinpoche" class="mw-redirect" title="Guru Rinpoche">Guru Rinpoche</a> (also known as Padmasambhava),<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> the saint credited with introducing Buddhism to Bhutan in the 8th century AD. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Sermon_of_Buddha">First Sermon of Buddha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: First Sermon of Buddha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>August 3 marks the first sermon of Gautama Buddha at <a href="/wiki/Sarnath" title="Sarnath">Sarnath</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blessed_Rainy_Day">Blessed Rainy Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Blessed Rainy Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Blessed_Rainy_Day" title="Blessed Rainy Day">Blessed Rainy Day</a><sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Dzongkha" title="Dzongkha">Dzongkha</a>: <i>thruebab</i>) generally falls in September, and is held on an auspicious day during <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Monsoon season">monsoon season</a>. The traditional holiday has not always been a public holiday, but was rather recently reinstated to official status.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The event is marked by feasting, drinking alcohol, playing traditional sports, and <a href="/wiki/Ritual_purification" title="Ritual purification">purification through washing</a>. In some parts of <a href="/wiki/Pemagatshel" class="mw-redirect" title="Pemagatshel">Pemagatshel</a>, the traditional celebrations are more intense than those of even <a href="/wiki/Losar" title="Losar">Losar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dashain">Dashain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dashain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Dashain" title="Dashain">Dashain</a>, the main <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepalese</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> festival, falls on October 6.<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> During this holiday, houses are cleaned and replastered, gifts are exchanged, and families gather.<sup id="cite_ref-FW_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FW-6">[6]</a></sup> Dashain has been a public holiday in Bhutan.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coronation_of_King_Jigme_Khesar_Namgyel_Wangchuck">Coronation of King Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Coronation of King Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>November 1 marks the coronation day of <a href="/wiki/Jigme_Khesar_Namgyel_Wangchuck" title="Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck">Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck</a>, the 5th and current <a href="/wiki/Druk_Gyalpo" title="Druk Gyalpo">Druk Gyalpo</a> (2008).<sup id="cite_ref-FW_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FW-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birthday_of_King_Jigme_Singye_Wangchuck">Birthday of King Jigme Singye Wangchuck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Birthday of King Jigme Singye Wangchuck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>November 11 marks the birth anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Jigme_Singye_Wangchuck" title="Jigme Singye Wangchuck">Jigme Singye Wangchuck</a>, the 4th <a href="/wiki/Druk_Gyalpo" title="Druk Gyalpo">Druk Gyalpo</a> (b. 1955, <a href="/wiki/Dechencholing_Palace" title="Dechencholing Palace">Dechencholing Palace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thimphu" title="Thimphu">Thimphu</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-FW_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FW-6">[6]</a></sup> The holiday is also called <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Bhutan" title="Constitution of Bhutan">Constitution Day</a>; under this king and at his behest, the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Bhutan" title="Constitution of Bhutan">Constitution of Bhutan</a> was enacted. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lhabab_Duchen">Lhabab Duchen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Lhabab Duchen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Lhabab_Duchen" title="Lhabab Duchen">Lhabab Duchen</a> is a public holiday<sup id="cite_ref-RCSC_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCSC-1">[1]</a></sup> generally falling in November (on the 9th month, 22nd day of the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_calendar" title="Tibetan calendar">Tibetan calendar</a>). Lhabab Duchen marks <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Gautama Buddha</a>'s return to earth after attaining Nirvana. In the eastern regions of Bhutan, the holiday is a popular occasion for performing Lhasoel, which are religious offerings in the form of <i><a href="/wiki/Ara_(drink)" title="Ara (drink)">ara</a></i> (traditional wine), blessings, and supplications.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Day">National Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Bhutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: National Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="National Day">National Day</a> of Bhutan is December 17. The date marks the coronation of <a href="/wiki/Ugyen_Wangchuck" title="Ugyen Wangchuck">Ugyen Wangchuck</a> as the first <a href="/wiki/Druk_Gyalpo" title="Druk Gyalpo">Druk Gyalpo</a> of modern Bhutan.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Celebrations are held at <a href="/wiki/Changlimithang_Stadium" title="Changlimithang Stadium">Changlimithang Stadium</a>, and include a public address by the Druk Gyalpo and a procession including a statue of Ugyen Wangchuck to honor the first Druk Gyalpo and the independent Bhutanese nation.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/Tsechu" class="mw-redirect" title="Tsechu">tsechus</a>, or festivals, take place for up to five days each at different locales across <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>. These usually feature large-scale pageantry and costumes, allegorical dances, <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archery</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Bhutan" title="Music of Bhutan">music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide-3">[3]</a></sup> These festivals are centuries-old traditions functioning not only as links to the past, but attract large numbers of tourists.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p>
