      <p><b>Public holidays in Canada</b>, known as <i>statutory holidays</i>, <i>stat holidays</i>, or simply <i>stats</i>, consist of a variety of cultural, nationalistic, and religious <a href="/wiki/Holidays" class="mw-redirect" title="Holidays">holidays</a> that are legislated in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">federal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">provincial and territorial</a> levels. While many of these holidays are honoured and acknowledged nationwide, provincial and territorial legislation varies in regard to which are officially recognized. </p>
      <p>There are five nationwide statutory holidays,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and six additional holidays for federal employees.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Each of the 13 provinces and territories observes a number of holidays in addition to the nationwide days, but each varies in regard to which are legislated as either statutory, optional, or not at all. Currently, the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a> have the most legislated holidays in comparison to other provinces and territories, with 12 days off. </p>
      <p>Many public and private employers, as well as <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Canada" title="Education in Canada">school systems</a>, provide additional days off <a href="/wiki/Christmas_and_holiday_season" title="Christmas and holiday season">around the end of December</a>, often including at least a full or half-day on December 24 (<a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a>) or December 31 (<a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year's Eve">New Year's Eve</a>), or in some cases the entire week between Christmas and New Year.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> While not officially legislated in any capacity, internationally notable cultural holidays such as <a href="/wiki/Valentine%27s_Day" title="Valentine's Day">Valentine's Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Patrick%27s_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Patrick's Day">St. Patrick's Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mother%27s_Day" title="Mother's Day">Mother's Day</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Father%27s_Day" title="Father's Day">Father's Day</a> are traditionally observed by Canadians as part of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian culture">Canadian culture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statutory_holidays">Statutory holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Statutory holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>A statutory holiday (also known as "stats" or "general" or "public" holiday) in Canada is legislated either through the federal, or a provincial or territorial government.<sup id="cite_ref-workrights_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-workrights-6">[6]</a></sup> Most workers, public and private, are entitled to take the day off with regular pay. However, some employers may require employees to work on such a holiday, but the employee must either receive a day off in lieu of the holiday or must be paid at a premium rate – usually 1½ (known as "time and a half") or twice (known as "double time") the regular pay for their time worked that day, in addition to the holiday pay<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (except for high technology workers in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In most provinces, when a statutory holiday falls on a normal day off (generally a weekend), the following work day is considered a statutory holiday. <a href="/wiki/Statistics_Canada" title="Statistics Canada">Statistics Canada</a> shows an average of 11 paid statutory holidays per year in regard to all firms and corporations operating within the province.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statutory_holidays_for_federal_employees">Statutory holidays for federal employees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Statutory holidays for federal employees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In addition to the nationwide holidays listed above, the following holidays are mandated by federal legislation for federally regulated employees. All banks and post offices commemorate these holidays, and they are statutory in some provinces and territories. </p> 
      <table class="wikitable sortable plainrowheaders"> 
       <tbody>
        <tr> 
         <th style="width:12%;">Date </th> 
         <th style="width:15%;">English name </th> 
         <th style="width:15%;">French name </th> 
         <th>Remarks </th>
        </tr> 
        <tr valign="top"> 
         <td>In lieu of Good Friday (Stat Holiday), Monday after <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> Day</td> 
         <td><a href="/wiki/Easter_Monday" title="Easter Monday">Easter Monday</a></td> 
         <td><i>Lundi de Pâques</i></td> 
         <td>Variable date between March 23 and April 26. Celebrates the <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">resurrection of Jesus</a>. <p>Not a statutory holiday in any province or territory; however, in Quebec employers must give either Good Friday or Easter Monday as a statutory holiday, though most give both days. </p><p>Banks remain open (legally they cannot close for more than three consecutive days except in emergencies<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>), but employees often receive a "floating" paid day off to be taken on or near the holiday. </p><p>This is not one of the nine "General Holidays" as defined by the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Labour_Code" title="Canada Labour Code">Canada Labour Code</a> – Part III. As such, there is no legal requirement for private sector employers in federally regulated industries to provide Easter Monday as a paid holiday to employees.       <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newfoundland_and_Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Newfoundland and Labrador">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <ul>
       <li>Five nationwide and one provincial statutory holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Thanksgiving is not a statutory holiday.       </ul> 
      <p><b>Provincial statutory</b> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Memorial Day (July 1)</li>

       <li><a href="/wiki/Orange_walk" title="Orange walk">Orangemen's Day</a> (July 12)</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>These have not been observed as statutory holidays since 1992. They are, however, observed by the provincial government. Unlike most other provinces, there is no province-wide holiday on the first Monday in August. It may be seen as redundant due to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_St._John%27s_Regatta" title="Royal St. John's Regatta">Royal St. John's Regatta</a>, which is observed as a civic holiday in St. John's on the first Wednesday in August (or, in case of poor weather, the next suitable day thereafter). <a href="/wiki/Harbour_Grace" title="Harbour Grace">Harbour Grace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labrador_City" title="Labrador City">Labrador City</a> have a similar holiday for their regatta in late July. All other municipalities are entitled to designate one day a year as a civic holiday, however many do not take advantage of this. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nova_Scotia">Nova Scotia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nova Scotia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Five nationwide holidays plus two provincial holidays. Victoria Day, Thanksgiving, and Boxing Day are not statutory holidays but most businesses and retail are closed Boxing Day. Most statutory holidays can be substituted for a mutually agreeable alternative paid day off in lieu or employers can require employees to work at a premium rate of pay. Several types of employment, including workplaces covered by a collective agreement, are exempt from provincial rules governing statutory holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><b>Provincial statutory</b> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Heritage Day – This holiday is held on the third Monday of February since 2015, and celebrates notable people, events and locations from the province's history. In 2015, Heritage Day celebrated <a href="/wiki/Black_Nova_Scotian" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Nova Scotian">Black Nova Scotian</a> civil rights activist and businesswoman <a href="/wiki/Viola_Desmond" title="Viola Desmond">Viola Desmond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Remembrance Day – November 11; this holiday has been governed separately from all other public holidays in Nova Scotia since 1981:<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> it is illegal for any person to offer any goods or real property for sale on this date, or to accept or offer employment in exchange for gain or reward. There are special exemptions for workers who are employed in certain categories but an alternative day off with pay must be offered in lieu.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p><b>Optional</b> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Natal Day – first Monday in August; not a statutory holiday but a common day off in Halifax Regional Municipality.</li>
      </ul>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quebec">Quebec</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Quebec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In Quebec, there are five nationwide and three provincial statutory holidays. Remembrance Day and Boxing Day are not statutory holidays, and there is no civic holiday in August. Many details of employment law are different in Quebec. The official statutory holidays are:<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>January 1 (New Year’s Day)</li> 
       <li>Good Friday or Easter Monday at the employer’s choice</li> 
       <li>Monday preceding May 25 (<a href="/wiki/National_Patriots%27_Day" title="National Patriots' Day">National Patriots' Day</a>)</li> 
       <li>June 24 (<a href="/wiki/National_Holiday_(Quebec)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Holiday (Quebec)">National Holiday</a>)</li> 
       <li>July 1. If this date falls on a Sunday: July 2 (<a href="/wiki/Canada_Day" title="Canada Day">Canada Day</a>)</li> 
       <li>First Monday in September (<a href="/wiki/Labour_Day" title="Labour Day">Labour Day</a>)</li> 
       <li>Second Monday in October (<a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(Canada)" title="Thanksgiving (Canada)">Thanksgiving</a>)</li> 
       <li>December 25 (Christmas Day).</li>
      </ul> 
      <p><b>Optional</b> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Construction_Holiday_(Quebec)" title="Construction Holiday (Quebec)">Construction Holiday</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Vacances de la construction</i>) takes place during the last two weeks of July and also the last two weeks of December for Christmas holidays. While it applies officially only to the construction industry, many other Quebecers arrange to take their vacations during these two weeks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.       <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Saskatchewan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Five nationwide and five provincial statutory holidays. </p>
      <p><b>Provincial statutory</b> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Family Day – third Monday in February</li> 
       <li>Victoria Day – Monday preceding May 25</li> 
       <li>Saskatchewan Day – first Monday in August. Celebration of Saskatchewan history and culture similar to Canada Day.</li> 
       <li>Thanksgiving – second Monday in October</li> 
       <li>Remembrance Day – November 11</li>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yukon">Yukon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Yukon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Five nationwide and four territorial statutory holidays. In addition, Easter Monday, Boxing Day, and Heritage Day are statutory for public service workers. Many employers give their employees days off that may not be statutory holidays in the particular province, particularly Boxing Day.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><b>Territorial statutory</b> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Victoria Day – Monday preceding May 25</li> 
       <li>Discovery Day – third Monday in August</li> 
       <li>Thanksgiving – second Monday in October</li> 
       <li>Remembrance Day – November 11</li> 
       <li>National Aboriginal Day – June 21 since 2017<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
      </ul>

      <ul>
       <li>Heritage Day – Friday before the last Sunday in February<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> – optional for non-public service workers</li>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipal_holidays">Municipal holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Municipal holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Some municipalities also have local statutory holidays. For instance, the morning of the <a href="/wiki/Calgary_Stampede" title="Calgary Stampede">Stampede Parade</a> is often given as a half-day holiday in the city of <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>. In Ontario, the August Civic Holiday is not defined provincially, but by each municipality. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civic_holidays">Civic holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Civic holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In Canada, there are two definitions of the term "<a href="/wiki/Civic_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Civic holiday">civic holiday</a>": </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_definition">Legal definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Legal definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>By law, a civic holiday is defined as any holiday which is legally recognized and for which employers are obliged to offer holiday pay. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="August_Civic_Holiday">August Civic Holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: August Civic Holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Civic_Holiday" title="Civic Holiday">Civic Holiday</a>
      </div>

      <p>In parts of Canada, the term "Civic Holiday" is a generic name referring to the annual holiday on the first Monday of August. However, this definition is far from uniform nationwide as Quebec, Newfoundland, and Yukon do not recognize it at all (in the Yukon the civic holiday is celebrated instead on the third Monday of August as <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Day" title="Discovery Day">Discovery Day</a>). Five other provinces (Ontario, Alberta, Manitoba, Nova Scotia, and Prince Edward Island) do not oblige employers to offer holiday pay on this day, thus not making it a civic holiday in the legal sense. No universal name is recognized for this holiday – the official name varies between the provinces and even between municipalities within Ontario. In Manitoba, Saskatchewan, British Columbia, New Brunswick, Nunavut and the Northwest Territories, it is a statutory holiday. </p>
      <p>The Civic Holiday is meant to replace a city's birthday, also known as Natal Day. Instead of each city and town having a separate birthday celebration and day off, the August Civic Holiday is observed. For example, the Halifax Regional Municipality is made up of the former cities of Halifax and Dartmouth and the town of Bedford. Each of these places used to hold civic birthday celebrations on different days. Many people lived in one jurisdiction but worked in another. This meant significant confusion arose as to which day a person would be excused from work. </p>
      <p>This holiday is commonly referred to as "August Long Weekend" but this is not a government term. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_holidays">Proposed holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Proposed holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The other leading candidate for a new holiday is a weekend in February to celebrate the anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Canada" title="Flag of Canada">Canadian flag</a>, or more likely a general "Heritage Day". February 15 is already designated as <a href="/wiki/National_Flag_of_Canada_Day" title="National Flag of Canada Day">Flag Day</a>, but this is simply a day of commemoration, not a statutory holiday. </p>
      <p>In the province of Nova Scotia, which has relatively few days off, a bill has been introduced for a new holiday for the third Monday in February, to start in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In April 2014, a private member's bill to make Remembrance Day a legal holiday and give it the same status as Canada Day was introduced to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons</a>. Bill C-597 passed second reading in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons</a> by a margin of 258 to 2; however, it did not become law.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2001, members of the 14th Legislative Assembly of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a> passed the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Aboriginal_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="National Aboriginal Day">National Aboriginal Day</a> Act</i>, making it the first jurisdiction in Canada to recognize this day as a formal statutory holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Holidays_occurring_on_non-work_days">Holidays occurring on non-work days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Holidays occurring on non-work days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>For federally regulated workers, if a holiday occurs on a day that is normally not worked, then "...another day off with pay will be provided".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>When New Year's Day, Canada Day, Remembrance Day, Christmas Day or Boxing Day falls on a Saturday or Sunday which a federally regulated worker would not normally work, they are entitled to a holiday with pay on the working day immediately before or after the holiday. If one of the other holidays falls on a weekend, then the employer must add a holiday with pay to their employees’ annual vacation or give them a paid day off at another mutually convenient time. </p>
