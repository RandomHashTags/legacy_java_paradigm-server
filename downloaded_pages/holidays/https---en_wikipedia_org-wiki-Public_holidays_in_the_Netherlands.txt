   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Public holidays in the Netherlands</h1>

      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> has the second lowest number of public holidays in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Holiday" title="Holiday">holidays</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> are: </p>

      <p>Saint Nicholas' Eve (the eve of <a href="/wiki/Sinterklaas" title="Sinterklaas">Sinterklaas</a>, also called <i>Sinterklaasavond</i> or <i>Pakjesavond</i>) on 5 December is not a national holiday, however it is widely celebrated. While Saint Nicholas's traditional <i>name day</i> is on 6 December, it is Saint Nicholas' Eve the day before which is the focus of celebrations in the Netherlands. </p>
      <p>In the South of the Netherlands carnival is celebrated. Though not an official holiday, many people in the South take the week off to celebrate. Schools schedule their spring holiday at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-Rijksoverheid_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rijksoverheid-5">[5]</a></sup> There has been some debate over whether the Islamic holiday of <i><a href="/wiki/Eid_ul-Fitr" class="mw-redirect" title="Eid ul-Fitr">Eid ul-Fitr</a></i> (<i>Suikerfeest</i> in Dutch) should be a national holiday. This was met by opposition from right-wing political parties such as the <a href="/wiki/Partij_voor_de_Vrijheid" class="mw-redirect" title="Partij voor de Vrijheid">PVV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reformed_Political_Party" title="Reformed Political Party">SGP</a>. For now, <i>Eid ul-Fitr</i> is not an official national holiday, but it usually justifies a day off for Islamic employees. Those opposed to this proposition say that there are enough national holidays as it is. Schools are allowed to give additional days off anyway.<sup id="cite_ref-Rijksoverheid_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rijksoverheid-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
