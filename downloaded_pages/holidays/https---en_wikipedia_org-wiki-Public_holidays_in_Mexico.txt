      <p>In Mexico there are three major kinds of public holidays: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><b>Statutory holiday</b>: Holidays observed all around Mexico. Employees are entitled to a day off with regular pay and schools (public and private) are closed for the day.</li> 
       <li><b>Civic holiday</b>: These holidays are observed nationwide, but employees are not entitled to a day off with pay and schools (public and private) still continue.</li> 
       <li><b>Festivities</b>: These are traditional holidays to honor religious events, such as <a href="/wiki/Carnival_in_Mexico" title="Carnival in Mexico">Carnival</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week_in_Mexico" title="Holy Week in Mexico">Holy Week</a>, <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, etc. or public celebrations, such as <a href="/wiki/Mother%27s_Day" title="Mother's Day">Mother's Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Father%27s_Day" title="Father's Day">Father's Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valentine%27s_Day" title="Valentine's Day">Valentine's Day</a>, etc.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p><i>Dia de la Independence</i> or <i>Anniversario de la Independence</i>, September 16, commemorates <a href="/wiki/Mexican_War_of_Independence" title="Mexican War of Independence">Mexico's independence from Spain</a> and is the most important patriotic statutory holiday.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Parades are held and many schools are closed. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statutory_holidays">Statutory holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Mexico&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Statutory holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><b>Statutory holidays</b> (referred as "<i>feriados</i>" or "<i>días de asueto</i>" in Mexico) are legislated through the federal government and ruled by the Federal Labor Law (<i>Ley Federal del Trabajo</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Most workers, public and private, are entitled to take the day off with regular pay. However, some employers may require employees to work on such a holiday, but the employee must be paid: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>the regular pay for the statutory holiday if no work is performed by the employee, and</li> 
       <li>the regular pay and two additional daily salary rates if work is performed by the employee, for a total of triple the usual rate.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>When a statutory holiday falls on a Sunday, Monday is considered a statutory holiday; if a statutory holiday falls on Saturday, Friday will be considered a statutory holiday. </p>

People must not believe anything that other people say nor let them borrow any amount of money. If any person has fallen victim of the joke, the person pulling the joke will say <i>¡Inocente palomita...!</i>, literally meaning 'Innocent little dove..!!!'          <td width="10%" align="center">December 31 </td> 
         <td width="10%" align="center">New Year's Eve </td> 
         <td width="10%">Víspera de Año Nuevo </td> 
         <td>Mexicans celebrate New Year's Eve or locally known as <i>Año Nuevo</i>, by downing a grape with each of the twelve chimes of the bell during the midnight countdown, while making a wish with each one. Mexican families decorate homes and parties, during New Year's, with colors such as red, to encourage an overall improvement of lifestyle and love, yellow to encourage blessings of improved employment conditions, green to improve financial circumstances and white to improved health. Mexican sweet bread is baked with a coin or charm hidden in the dough. When the bread is served, the recipient whose slice contains the coin or charm is believed to be blessed with good luck in the new year. Another tradition is making a list of all the bad or unhappy events from the current year; before midnight, this list is thrown into a fire, symbolizing the removal of negative energy from the new year.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.topics-mag.com">[1]</a> At the same time, thanks is expressed for all the good things had during the year that is coming to its end so that they will continue to be had in the new year.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Mexicans celebrate by having a dinner with their families, the traditional meal being turkey and <a href="/wiki/Mole_sauce" title="Mole sauce">mole</a>, a tradition which has now spanned worldwide. Those who want to party generally go out afterwards, to local parties or night clubs. If you're in Mexico, you can still enjoy festivities in the street. In <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> there is a huge street festival on New Year's Eve; celebrations center around the Zocalo, the city's main square.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <p>One can expect a lot of firecrackers, fireworks and sparklers being fired. At midnight there is a lot of noise and everyone shouts: "Feliz año nuevo!" People embrace, make noise, set off <a href="/wiki/Firecracker" title="Firecracker">firecrackers</a>, and sing. </p> </td>
