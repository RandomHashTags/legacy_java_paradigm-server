   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Public holidays in Malaysia</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">

    </div>

      <p><b>Public holidays in Malaysia</b> are regulated at both <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Federated_state" title="Federated state">state</a> levels, mainly based on a list of federal holidays observed nationwide plus a few additional holidays observed by each individual <a href="/wiki/States_and_federal_territories_of_Malaysia" title="States and federal territories of Malaysia">state and federal territory</a>. The public holidays are a mix of secular holidays celebrating the nation and its history, and selected traditional holidays of the various ethnic and religious groups that make up the country. </p>
      <p>The legislation governing public holidays in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> includes the Holidays Act 1951 (Act 369) in <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Malaysia" title="Peninsular Malaysia">Peninsular Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labuan" title="Labuan">Labuan</a>, the Holidays Ordinance (Sabah Cap. 56) in <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a> and the Public Holidays Ordinance (Sarawak Cap. 8) in <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a>. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Workweek_and_weekend" title="Workweek and weekend">workweek and weekend</a> varies between states, with most states and federal territories observing a Saturday–Sunday weekend, while <a href="/wiki/Johor" title="Johor">Johor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kedah" title="Kedah">Kedah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kelantan" title="Kelantan">Kelantan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terengganu" title="Terengganu">Terengganu</a> observe a Friday–Saturday weekend, though in Johor many private businesses and banks observe the Saturday–Sunday weekend due to close business ties with <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In states and territories with a Saturday–Sunday weekend, a public holiday that falls on Sunday is substituted by a holiday on Monday (or the next working day if Monday itself is a public holiday). In Johor and Kedah, a public holiday that falls on Friday is replaced by Sunday or the next working day, while in Kelantan and Terengganu, a public holiday that falls on Saturday is replaced by Sunday or the next working day. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span id="Federal_.28national.29_holidays"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_(national)_holidays">Federal (national) holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Federal (national) holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Federal public holidays are fixed by the federal government and are observed nationwide with some exceptions. They are: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Mawlid" title="Mawlid">Muhammad's Birthday</a> (Maulidur Rasul)</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Hari_Merdeka" class="mw-redirect" title="Hari Merdeka">Hari Merdeka</a> (National Day)</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a> <small>(one day in Kelantan and Terengganu, two days in rest of the country)</small></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Vesak" title="Vesak">Wesak Day</a></li> 
       <li>The <a href="/wiki/Yang_di-Pertuan_Agong" title="Yang di-Pertuan Agong">Yang di-Pertuan Agong</a>'s (Official) Birthday (King's Birthday)</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Eid_al-Fitr" title="Eid al-Fitr">Hari Raya Puasa</a> (Aidilfitri) <small>(two days)</small></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Eid_al-Adha" title="Eid al-Adha">Hari Raya Qurban</a> (Aidiladha) <small>(two days in Kelantan and Terengganu, one day in rest of the country)</small></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Deepavali" class="mw-redirect" title="Deepavali">Deepavali</a> <small>(except Sarawak)</small></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Labour_Day" title="Labour Day">Labour Day</a></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Awal_Muharram" class="mw-redirect" title="Awal Muharram">Awal Muharram</a></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Malaysia_Day" title="Malaysia Day">Malaysia Day</a></li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Each state and federal territory observes 14 days of federal holidays, except Sarawak which observes 13 days. </p>
      <p>Although the second day of Chinese New Year is not listed as a federal holiday in Kelantan and Terengganu, the holiday is gazetted as a state holiday in both states, effectively making it a nationwide holiday. Additionally, the second day of Hari Raya Qurban is gazetted as a state holiday in Kedah and Perlis. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_holidays">State holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: State holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In addition to the federal public holidays, each state may gazette a number of state public holidays to be observed in the state. For the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Territories_(Malaysia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Territories (Malaysia)">federal territories</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Malaysia" title="Prime Minister of Malaysia">Prime Minister</a> is in charge of designating the territorial public holidays to be observed in each federal territory. </p>
      <p>In every state, the (official) birthday of the state <a href="/wiki/Monarchies_of_Malaysia" title="Monarchies of Malaysia">ruler</a> or <a href="/wiki/Yang_di-Pertua_Negeri" title="Yang di-Pertua Negeri">governor</a> is celebrated as a public holiday. In the federal territories, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Territory_Day" title="Federal Territory Day">Federal Territory Day</a> is celebrated instead. </p>
      <p>The most widespread state holiday is <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">New Year's Day</a> which is observed in eight states and all three federal territories, followed by <a href="/wiki/Muhammad%27s_first_revelation" title="Muhammad's first revelation">Nuzul al-Quran</a> in seven states and all three federal territories, and <a href="/wiki/Thaipusam" title="Thaipusam">Thaipusam</a> in five states and two federal territories. </p>
      <p>As of 2020, each state and federal territory has designated four to six state public holidays, bringing the total number of (federal and state) public holidays to 20 days in Sabah and Terengganu, 19 days in Labuan, Penang and Sarawak and 18 days in the rest of the country. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holidays_by_declaration">Holidays by declaration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Holidays by declaration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In Peninsular Malaysia and Labuan, under Section 8 of the Holidays Act 1951, the Prime Minister may declare any day to be observed as a public holiday in the whole of Peninsular Malaysia and Labuan, or in one of the federal territories, or in one of the states after consultation with the relevant state government. The declared holiday must be observed by all employers as a paid holiday. </p>
      <p>Public holidays had been declared on the polling day for a <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_Malaysia" title="Elections in Malaysia">general election</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-GE14_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GE14-3">[3]</a></sup> on the day of the <a href="/wiki/Installation_of_the_Yang_di-Pertuan_Agong" title="Installation of the Yang di-Pertuan Agong">installation of the Yang di-Pertuan Agong</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Agong15_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Agong15-4">[4]</a></sup> as well as after international sporting events to celebrate the achievements of Malaysian athletes.<sup id="cite_ref-SEAGames_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEAGames-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>At the state level, the state government may declare occasional holiday (<i>cuti peristiwa</i>) for events such as the installation of the state ruler,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> after major achievements in sporting events,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> or even to provide an extra holiday but unable to officially gazette it (such as the annual holiday declaration for Thaipusam in Kedah).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Unlike holidays declared under Section 8 of the Holidays Act 1951, observance of occasional state holidays by private businesses and organizations is voluntary, while government offices and schools (except for nationwide exams) are closed. </p>
      <p>In Sabah and Sarawak, the power to declare any day as a public holiday rests with the state governor (in practice, exercised on the advice of the state government) in accordance with the states' respective Holidays Ordinances. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entitlement_in_employment_law">Entitlement in employment law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Entitlement in employment law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In Peninsular Malaysia and Labuan, employees whose employment is covered by the Employment Act 1955 are entitled to 11 paid public holidays a year. Five of the holidays are fixed by law: National Day, Yang di-Pertuan Agong's Birthday, birthday of the ruler or governor of the state (Federal Territory Day in the federal territories) where the employee is contracted to work, Labour Day and Malaysia Day. The remaining six paid holidays are chosen by the employer from the gazetted public holidays, with notice provided to employees before the start of each calendar year. In addition, any public holiday declared under Section 8 of the Holidays Act 1951 is to be observed as a paid holiday. </p>
      <p>Should an employee be required to work on a paid holiday, the employee may be given another day off, or compensated at two times their ordinary wages in addition to holiday pay. <a href="/wiki/Overtime" title="Overtime">Overtime</a> work done on a paid holiday is to be compensated at three times the hourly rate of pay (or three times the ordinary rate per piece for piece-rated employees). </p>
      <p>Employment in Sabah and Sarawak is regulated by the Labour Ordinance of the respective states. Employees in Sabah are entitled to 14 paid public holidays a year while those in Sarawak are entitled to 16 days, with four fixed holidays on National Day, Yang di-Pertuan Agong's Birthday, the State Governor's Birthday and Labour Day. The provisions on compensation for work done on paid holidays are identical to the Employment Act 1955. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_holidays">Types of holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types of holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Malaysia has one of the highest numbers of public holidays in the world, ranking number seven in the top ten countries after <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and Hong Kong. Some holidays are federally gazetted public holidays and some are public holidays observed by individual states. Other festivals are observed by particular ethnic or religion groups, but are not public holidays. The main holy days of each major religion are public holidays, taking place on either the western calendar or religious ones. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secular">Secular</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Secular">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The most widespread holiday is the "<a href="/wiki/Hari_Merdeka" class="mw-redirect" title="Hari Merdeka">Hari Kebangsaan</a>" (National Day), otherwise known as "<a href="/wiki/Hari_Merdeka" class="mw-redirect" title="Hari Merdeka">Hari Merdeka</a>" (Independence Day) on 31 August commemorating the independence of the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Malaya" title="Federation of Malaya">Federation of Malaya</a>. This, as well as Labour Day (1 May), the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Malaysia" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Malaysia">King</a>'s birthday (9 September) and some other festivals are major national public holidays. Federal Territory day is celebrated in the three Federal territories. <a href="/wiki/Malaysia_Day" title="Malaysia Day">Malaysia Day</a>, held on 16 September to commemorate the formation of Malaysia, became a nationwide holiday in 2010. Before that it was celebrated only in <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a>. New Year's Day is also observed as a public holiday in all Malaysian states, except for Johor, Kedah, Kelantan, Perlis, and Terengganu. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_and_ethnic">Religious and ethnic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Religious and ethnic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Muslim_holidays" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim holidays">Muslim holidays</a> are highly prominent in Malaysia. The most important of these is <a href="/wiki/Eid_ul-Fitr" class="mw-redirect" title="Eid ul-Fitr">Hari Raya Puasa</a> (also called <i>Hari Raya Aidilfitri</i>) which is the <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a> translation of <a href="/wiki/Eid_al-Fitr" title="Eid al-Fitr">Eid al-Fitr</a>. It is generally a festival honoured by the <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> worldwide marking the end of <a href="/wiki/Ramadan" title="Ramadan">Ramadan</a> will the fasting month. In addition to Hari Raya Puasa, they also celebrate <a href="/wiki/Hari_Raya_Aidiladha" class="mw-redirect" title="Hari Raya Aidiladha">Hari Raya Aidiladha</a> (also called <i>Hari Raya Haji</i> referring to its occurrence after the culmination of the annual <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a> or <i>Hari Raya Qurban</i>), Awal Muharram (<a href="/wiki/Islamic_New_Year" title="Islamic New Year">Islamic New Year</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Mawlid" title="Mawlid">Maulidur Rasul</a> (Birthday of Muhammad). </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese" title="Malaysian Chinese">Malaysian Chinese</a> typically hold the same festivals observed by Chinese around the world. <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a> is the most prominent, lasting for 15-days and ending with <i><a href="/wiki/Lantern_Festival" title="Lantern Festival">Chap Goh Mei</a></i> (十五瞑). Other festivals celebrated by Chinese are the <a href="/wiki/Qingming_Festival" title="Qingming Festival">Qingming Festival</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dragon_Boat_Festival" title="Dragon Boat Festival">Dragon Boat Festival</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Autumn_Festival" title="Mid-Autumn Festival">Mid-Autumn Festival</a>. </p>
      <p>Malaysian Indians of the Hindu faith celebrate <a href="/wiki/Deepavali" class="mw-redirect" title="Deepavali">Deepavali</a>, the festival of light,<sup id="cite_ref-Marshall_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall-24">[24]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Thaipusam" title="Thaipusam">Thaipusam</a> is a celebration in which Hindu pilgrims from all over the country meet at the <a href="/wiki/Batu_Caves" title="Batu Caves">Batu Caves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The most important Sikh festival is the Sikh new year or <a href="/wiki/Vaisakhi" title="Vaisakhi">Vaisakhi</a> festival. Other important days are <a href="/wiki/Lohri" title="Lohri">Lodi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gurpurab" class="mw-redirect" title="Gurpurab">Gurpurab</a>. Other Indian and <a href="/wiki/Indochinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Indochinese">Indochinese</a> communities observe their new year celebrations at around the same time, such as <a href="/wiki/Pohela_Boishakh" class="mw-redirect" title="Pohela Boishakh">Pohela Boishakh</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bengali_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Bengali people">Bengalis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Songkran_(Thailand)" title="Songkran (Thailand)">Songkran</a> (water festival) of the Thais. People in the northern states do celebrate the Thai festival of <a href="/wiki/Loy_Kratong" class="mw-redirect" title="Loy Kratong">Loy Kratong</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Vesak" title="Vesak">Wesak</a> (Malay for Vesak), the <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist festival">Buddhist festival</a> commemorating <a href="/wiki/Buddha" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>'s birth, is a public holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-Marshall_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall-24">[24]</a></sup> Malaysia's Christian community observes most of the holidays observed by Christians elsewhere, most notably Christmas<sup id="cite_ref-Marshall_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall-24">[24]</a></sup> and Easter. Good Friday, however, is only a public holiday in the two Bornean states. The harvest festivals of <a href="/wiki/Gawai_Dayak" title="Gawai Dayak">Gawai</a> in Sarawak and <a href="/wiki/Kaamatan" title="Kaamatan">Kaamatan</a> in Sabah are also important for East Malaysians.<sup id="cite_ref-goto1_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goto1-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>New Year's Day, Chinese New Year, and the start of the Islamic calendar are all public holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-Marshall_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall-24">[24]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Participation">Participation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Malaysia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Participation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Despite most of the festivals being identified with a particular ethnic or religious group, festivities are often participated in by all Malaysians. One example of this is the celebration of <i>Kongsi Raya</i> which is used when Hari Raya Puasa and Chinese New Year coincide. The term <i>Kongsi Raya</i> (which means "sharing the celebration" in <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>) was coined because of the similarity between the word <i>kongsi</i> and the Chinese New Year greeting of <i>Gong xi fa cai</i>. Similarly, the portmanteau <i>Deepa Raya</i> was coined when Hari Raya Puasa and Deepavali coincided.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A practice known as "open house" (<i>rumah terbuka</i>) is common during the festivities, especially during Hari Raya Aidilfitri, Deepavali, Chinese New Year and Christmas. Open house means that all well-wishers are received and that everyone regardless of background is invited to attend.<sup id="cite_ref-matic1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matic1-29">[29]</a></sup> Open houses are normally held at the home of the host and food are also prepared by the host, however, there are also open houses held at larger public venues especially when hosted by government agencies or corporations. Also during the festivities, most Malaysians would take the time off work or school to return to their hometowns to celebrate the festivities with their extended relatives. This practice is commonly known as <i>balik kampung</i> and usually causes traffic jams on most highways in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> </p>
