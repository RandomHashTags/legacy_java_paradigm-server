   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Public holidays in Japan</h1>

      <p><b>Public holidays in Japan</b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">国民の祝日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">kokumin no shukujitsu</i>)</span> were established by the Public Holiday Law<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">国民の祝日に関する法律</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Kokumin no Shukujitsu ni Kansuru Hōritsu</i>)</span> of 1948 (as amended). A provision of the law establishes that when a national holiday falls on a Sunday, the next working day shall become a public holiday, known as <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">furikae kyūjitsu</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">振替休日</span>, "compensatory public holiday", literally "substitute holiday")</span>. Additionally, any day that falls between two other national holidays shall also become a holiday, known as <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">kokumin no kyūjitsu</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">国民の休日</span>, literally "citizens' holiday")</span>. May 4, sandwiched between <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Memorial_Day" title="Constitution Memorial Day">Constitution Memorial Day</a> on May 3 and <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Day" title="Children's Day">Children's Day</a> on May 5, was an annual example of such a holiday until it was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Greenery_Day" title="Greenery Day">Greenery Day</a> in 2007. </p>
      <p>Prior to Japan's adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> in 1873, the dates of holidays were based on the traditional <a href="/wiki/Chinese_lunisolar_calendar" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese lunisolar calendar">Chinese lunisolar calendar</a>. Thus, New Year's Day, for example, was celebrated at the beginning of spring, as it is in modern China, <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_South_Korea" title="Public holidays in South Korea">Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Vietnam" title="Public holidays in Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. Japan has 16 national, government-recognized holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

         <td>January 1 </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1948, as a day to celebrate the new year. New Year's Day marks the beginning of Japan's most important holiday season, the New Year season<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">正月</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Shōgatsu</i>)</span>, which generally refers to the first one, three or seven days of the year. Although not prescribed by law, many workplaces are closed from December 29 to January 3. Prior to 1948, New Year's Day was a national holiday on which the imperial worship ceremony is known as <i>Shihō-hai</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">四方拝</span>)</span> took place. </td>

         <td>Second Monday of January </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1948 as a day to congratulate and encourage people who have reached the age of maturity (20) during the year. Cities and towns throughout the nation hold ceremonies for these people. Originally held on January 15, in 2000 it was changed to the second Monday of January in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Happy_Monday_System" title="Happy Monday System">Happy Monday System</a>. </td>

         <td>February 11 </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1966 (and first held in 1967) as a day to reflect on the establishment of the nation and to nurture a love for the country. From 1872 to 1948, February 11 was known as <i>Kigen-setsu</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">紀元節</span>)</span>, a holiday commemorating the day on which—according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Nihon_Shoki" title="Nihon Shoki">Nihon Shoki</a></i>—<a href="/wiki/Emperor_Jimmu" title="Emperor Jimmu">Emperor Jimmu</a> is said to have acceded the throne in <a href="/wiki/660_BC" title="660 BC">660 BCE</a>. </td>

         <td><a href="/wiki/The_Emperor%27s_Birthday" title="The Emperor's Birthday">The Emperor's Birthday</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">天皇誕生日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Tennō Tanjōbi</i>)</span> </td> 
         <td>February 23 </td> 
         <td>The birthday of the reigning emperor has been a national holiday since 1868. Originally known as <i>Tenchō-setsu</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">天長節</span>)</span>, it was renamed <i>Tennō tanjōbi</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">天皇誕生日</span>)</span> in 1948. It is currently celebrated on February 23; <a href="/wiki/Naruhito" title="Naruhito">Emperor Naruhito</a> was born on this day in 1960. Prior to the abdication of Emperor Akihito on April 30th 2019, this holiday was celebrated on December 23, and it was not celebrated in 2019. </td> 
         <td><sup id="cite_ref-japan-guide_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-guide-2">[2]</a></sup> </td>

         <td>Around March 20<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1948 as a day for the admiration of nature and the love of living things. Prior to 1948, the vernal equinox was an imperial ancestor worship festival called <i>Shunki kōrei-sai</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">春季皇霊祭</span>)</span>. </td>

         <td><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dwa_Day" title="Shōwa Day">Shōwa Day</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">昭和の日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Shōwa no Hi</i>)</span> </td> 
         <td>April 29 </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 2007 as a day to reflect on the events of the <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dwa_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Shōwa period">Shōwa period</a>. As the birthday of <a href="/wiki/Hirohito" title="Hirohito">Hirohito</a>, officially known as Emperor Shōwa, April 29 was originally celebrated as a holiday during his lifetime. Hirohito was born on this day in 1901. (See "The Emperor's Birthday" above.) After the death of Hirohito in 1989, the date continued to be a holiday under the new name "Greenery Day". (See also below.) In 2007, Greenery Day was moved to May 4, and April 29 took the name "Shōwa Day" in honor of the late Emperor. Shōwa Day marks the start of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Week_(Japan)" title="Golden Week (Japan)">Golden Week</a> holiday period. </td> 
         <td><sup id="cite_ref-japan-guide_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-guide-2">[2]</a></sup> </td>

         <td>May 3 </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1948, to commemorate the day on which Japan's postwar <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Japan" title="Constitution of Japan">constitution</a> took effect. Constitution Memorial Day falls during <a href="/wiki/Golden_Week_(Japan)" title="Golden Week (Japan)">Golden Week</a>. </td> 
         <td><sup id="cite_ref-japan-guide_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-guide-2">[2]</a></sup> </td>

         <td>May 4 </td> 
         <td>This national holiday is celebrated as a day to commune with nature and be grateful for its blessings. Originally established in 1989 and held annually on April 29 (the late Shōwa Emperor's birthday), in 2007 Greenery Day was moved to May 4, and April 29 was renamed "Shōwa Day" (see above.) Greenery Day falls during <a href="/wiki/Golden_Week_(Japan)" title="Golden Week (Japan)">Golden Week</a>. (From 1985 to 2006, May 4 was a <i>kokumin no kyūjitsu</i> holiday.) </td> 
         <td><sup id="cite_ref-japan-guide_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-guide-2">[2]</a></sup> </td>
        </tr> 
        <tr> 
         <td><a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Day_(Japan)" title="Children's Day (Japan)">Children's Day</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">こどもの日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Kodomo no Hi</i>)</span> </td> 
         <td>May 5 </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1948, as a day on which to esteem the personalities of children and plan for their happiness. It is on this day that the Japanese equivalent of the <a href="/wiki/Dragon_Boat_Festival" title="Dragon Boat Festival">Dragon Boat Festival</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">端午の節句</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Tango no Sekku</i>)</span> is held. On this day, and for some time before it, families who have a boy in their home may fly <i><a href="/wiki/Koinobori" title="Koinobori">koinobori</a></i> and decorate their homes with armor or samurai dolls. Children's Day marks the end of <a href="/wiki/Golden_Week_(Japan)" title="Golden Week (Japan)">Golden Week</a>. </td> 
         <td><sup id="cite_ref-japan-guide_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-guide-2">[2]</a></sup> </td>
        </tr> 
        <tr> 
         <td><a href="/wiki/Marine_Day" title="Marine Day">Marine Day</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">海の日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Umi no Hi</i>)</span> </td> 
         <td>Third Monday of July </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1995 (first held in 1996) as a day of gratitude for the blessings of the oceans and hoping for the prosperity of Japan. Originally held on July 20, the holiday was changed to be celebrated on the third Monday of July in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Happy_Monday_System" title="Happy Monday System">Happy Monday System</a> starting in 2003. </td> 
         <td> </td>
        </tr>

         <td><a href="/wiki/Mountain_Day#Mountain_Day_in_Japan" title="Mountain Day">Mountain Day</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">山の日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Yama no Hi</i>)</span> </td> 
         <td>August 11 </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 2014 (and first held in 2016), as a day on which to appreciate Japan's mountains. It is intended to coincide with the vacation time usually given during the <a href="/wiki/Bon_Festival" title="Bon Festival">Bon Festival</a> held in mid-August. </td> 
         <td><sup id="cite_ref-japan-guide_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-guide-2">[2]</a></sup> </td>

         <td>Third Monday of September </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1966 as a day to respect the elderly and celebrate a long life. Originally held on September 15, it originated as a renaming of Old Folks' Day<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">老人の日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Rōjin no hi</i>)</span>. In 2003, it was changed to the third Monday of September in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Happy_Monday_System" title="Happy Monday System">Happy Monday System</a>. </td> 
         <td><sup id="cite_ref-japan-guide_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japan-guide-2">[2]</a></sup> </td>
        </tr>

         <td><a href="/wiki/Autumnal_Equinox_Day" title="Autumnal Equinox Day">Autumnal Equinox Day</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">秋分の日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Shūbun no Hi</i>)</span> </td> 
         <td>Around September 23<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[b]</a></sup> </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1948 as a day on which to honor one's ancestors and remember the dead. Prior to 1948, the autumnal equinox was an imperial ancestor worship festival called <i>Shūki kōrei-sai</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">秋季皇霊祭</span>)</span>. </td>

         <td>Second Monday of October </td> 
         <td>This national holiday was established in 1966 as a day on which to enjoy sports and cultivate a healthy mind and body. Originally held on October 10 to commemorate the anniversary of the opening ceremony of the 1964 <a href="/wiki/1964_Summer_Olympics" title="1964 Summer Olympics">Tokyo Olympics</a>, in 2000 it was changed to the second Monday of October in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Happy_Monday_System" title="Happy Monday System">Happy Monday System</a>. </td>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_of_imperial_mourning_and_celebration">Events of imperial mourning and celebration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Japan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Events of imperial mourning and celebration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In addition to the annual holidays listed above, certain events of celebration or mourning related to the imperial family are also treated as national holidays in the year in which they occur. </p>
      <p>There have been six instances of such holidays since the introduction of the Public Holiday Law: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>April 10, 1959: Marriage of Crown Prince Akihito</li> 
       <li>February 24, 1989: <a href="/wiki/Death_and_funeral_of_Hirohito" title="Death and funeral of Hirohito">State Funeral</a> of Emperor Shōwa</li> 
       <li>November 12, 1990: Official <a href="/wiki/Enthronement_of_the_Japanese_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Enthronement of the Japanese Emperor">Enthronement Ceremony</a> of Emperor Heisei (Akihito)</li> 
       <li>June 9, 1993: Marriage of Crown Prince Naruhito</li> 
       <li>April 30, 2019: <a href="/wiki/Japanese_imperial_transition,_2019" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese imperial transition, 2019">Abdication of Emperor Heisei</a></li> 
       <li>October 22, 2019: Official <a href="/wiki/Enthronement_of_the_Japanese_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Enthronement of the Japanese Emperor">Enthronement Ceremony</a> of current Emperor (Naruhito)</li>
      </ul> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_changes">Recent changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Japan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Beginning in 2000, Japan implemented the <a href="/wiki/Happy_Monday_System" title="Happy Monday System">Happy Monday System</a>, which moved a number of national holidays to Monday in order to obtain a long weekend. </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><b>Coming-of-Age Day</b>: January 15 → 2nd Monday of January, starting in 2000.</li> 
       <li><b>Marine Day</b>: July 20 → 3rd Monday of July, starting in 2003.</li> 
       <li><b>Respect for the Aged Day</b>: September 15 → 3rd Monday of September, starting in 2003.</li> 
       <li><b>Health and Sports Day</b>: October 10 → 2nd Monday of October, starting in 2000.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>In 2006, the country added <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dwa_Day" title="Shōwa Day">Shōwa Day</a>, a new national holiday, in place of <a href="/wiki/Greenery_Day" title="Greenery Day">Greenery Day</a> on April 29, and to move Greenery Day to May 4. These changes took effect in 2007. </p>
      <p>In 2014, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Councillors" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Councillors">House of Councillors</a> decided to add Mountain Day<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">山の日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Yama no Hi</i>)</span> to the Japanese calendar on August 11, after lobbying by the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Alpine_Club" title="Japanese Alpine Club">Japanese Alpine Club</a>. It is intended to coincide with the <a href="/wiki/Bon_Festival" title="Bon Festival">Bon Festival</a> vacation time, giving Japanese people an opportunity to appreciate Japan's mountains.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>With the <a href="/wiki/2019_Japanese_imperial_transition" title="2019 Japanese imperial transition">Japanese imperial transition</a>, the <a href="/wiki/The_Emperor%27s_Birthday" title="The Emperor's Birthday">Emperor's Birthday</a> was moved from December 23 to February 23 (the respective birthdays of <a href="/wiki/Akihito" title="Akihito">Emperor Emeritus Akihito</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naruhito" title="Naruhito">Emperor Naruhito</a>). Due to Akihito's 2019 birthday being after his abdication but Naruhito's before his accession, this holiday was not celebrated in 2019. </p>
      <p>As special arrangement for the <a href="/wiki/2020_Summer_Olympics" title="2020 Summer Olympics">2020 Summer Olympics</a>, the 2020 dates for Marine Day, Sports Day, and Mountain Day were moved to July 23, July 24, and August 10 respectively. With the Olympics and Paralympics <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Japan" title="COVID-19 pandemic in Japan">postponed until 2021 due to the COVID-19 pandemic</a>, the government left this change in place for 2020 and passed an amendment to the Olympic and Paralympic Special Measures Act to make a corresponding change to the holidays in 2021, moving them to July 22, July 23, and August 9 respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-2021_changes_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2021_changes-14">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jiji_2021_holiday_movement_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jiji_2021_holiday_movement-12">[7]</a></sup> </p>
