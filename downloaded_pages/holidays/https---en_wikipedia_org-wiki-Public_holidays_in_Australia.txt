   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Public holidays in Australia</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

      <p> <b>Public holidays in Australia</b> refer to the <a href="/wiki/Holidays" class="mw-redirect" title="Holidays">holidays</a> recognised in law in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Although they are declared on a <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_Australia" title="States and territories of Australia">state and territory</a> basis, they comprise a mixture of nationally celebrated days and holidays exclusive to the individual jurisdictions. </p>
      <p>Public holidays function as non-working days, with workers generally receiving full <a href="/wiki/Paid_leave" class="mw-redirect" title="Paid leave">paid leave</a> independently of <a href="/wiki/Annual_leave" title="Annual leave">annual leave</a>. Those working on public holidays receive additional penalty rates of pay.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Where they fall on a weekend, public holidays are generally declared <i><a href="/wiki/In_lieu" class="mw-redirect" title="In lieu">in lieu</a></i> for the following Monday. </p>
      <p>Statutory holidays in Australia are based on varying religious, cultural and civic observations. <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> celebrations, namely <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, are some of the most significant ones observed. A <a href="/wiki/Labour_day" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour day">labour day</a> is observed in each state and territory, although it is varied in date. There are two significant <a href="/wiki/National_day" title="National day">national days</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia_Day" title="Australia Day">Australia Day</a> (26 January) and <a href="/wiki/Anzac_Day" title="Anzac Day">Anzac Day</a> (25 April), which are nationwide public holidays. </p>
      <p>When a public holiday occurs on a Friday or Monday, the three-day period is colloquially known as a "<a href="/wiki/Long_weekend" title="Long weekend">long weekend</a>". </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_public_holidays">Nature of public holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nature of public holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Traditionally, Australians in employment (whether in the <a href="/wiki/Public_sector" title="Public sector">public</a> or <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private sector</a>) have had the right to take a public holiday off work with regular pay. In recent years this tradition has changed somewhat. For example, <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">businesses</a> that normally open on a public holiday may request employees to work on that day. Employers can deny employees a holiday only on reasonable <a href="/wiki/Business_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Business culture">business</a> grounds. </p>
      <p>From 2006, <a href="/wiki/WorkChoices" title="WorkChoices">WorkChoices</a> entirely eliminated the entitlement to penalty rates in many workplaces; however since the implementation of the <a href="/wiki/Fair_Work_Act_2009" title="Fair Work Act 2009">Fair Work Act 2009</a> and the modern awards in 2010, most public-holiday penalty rates have increased dramatically.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As of 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> employees generally receive pay at a penalty rate—usually 2.5 times (known as "double time and a half") the base rate of pay—when they work on a public holiday. </p>
      <p>Besides designating days as public holidays, Australian authorities also designate some of these days as restricted trading days. </p>
      <p>Public holidays are determined by a combination of: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>statutes, with specific gazetting of public holidays</li> 
       <li>industrial awards and agreements</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>If a standard public holiday falls on a weekend, a substitute public holiday will sometimes be observed on the first non-weekend day (usually Monday) after the weekend, whether by virtue of the public holiday legislation or by <i>ad hoc</i> proclamation. Workers required to work on a public holiday or substituted public holiday will usually be entitled to remuneration at a holiday penalty rate. </p>
      <p>All states have their own public holidays in addition to national public holidays, and in some states public holidays, such as <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Cup_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Melbourne Cup Day">Melbourne Cup Day</a>, are provided<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> on a local basis. </p>
      <p>Alcohol licences in several states prevent sale of alcohol on certain public holidays, such as <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a>. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Substitute_holidays_for_holidays_falling_on_a_weekend">Substitute holidays for holidays falling on a weekend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Substitute holidays for holidays falling on a weekend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <p>Holidays that always fall on a particular day of the week are not listed in this table. Prior to 2008, Victorian law only specified substitute holidays for New Year and Boxing Day, and only if they fell on a Sunday.<sup id="cite_ref-vic-law_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vic-law-28">[28]</a></sup> From 2008, Victorian law specifies the substitute holidays in the table above.<sup id="cite_ref-vic-bill_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vic-bill-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Since <a href="/wiki/Easter_Monday" title="Easter Monday">Easter Monday</a> can occur <a href="/wiki/Easter#Date" title="Easter">as late as 26 April</a> it is possible for the Easter Monday holiday to coincide with <a href="/wiki/Anzac_Day" title="Anzac Day">Anzac Day</a>, as occurred in 2011. State Acts do not give a provision to separate the days when this occurs, so no additional public holiday is given by law. However an extra day is usually proclaimed by the minister, so as to have a steady number of public holidays each year.<sup id="cite_ref-nt-2011_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nt-2011-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sa-2011_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sa-2011-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tas-2011_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tas-2011-31">[31]</a></sup> In the year 2038, Anzac Day will coincide with Easter Sunday. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia_Day">Australia Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Australia Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Nationally, <a href="/wiki/Australia_Day" title="Australia Day">Australia Day</a> was originally celebrated on 30 July 1915.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Recorded celebrations of the 26 January date back to 1808 in Australia, and in 1818, Governor <a href="/wiki/Lachlan_Macquarie" title="Lachlan Macquarie">Lachlan Macquarie</a> held the first official celebration of Australia Day.<sup id="cite_ref-nsw-hist_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nsw-hist-33">[33]</a></sup> 26 January was chosen because it is the day of the establishment of the first British settlement at <a href="/wiki/Port_Jackson" title="Port Jackson">Port Jackson</a> by Captain <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Phillip" title="Arthur Phillip">Arthur Phillip</a> in 1788.<sup id="cite_ref-NADCHistory_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NADCHistory-34">[34]</a></sup> It was made a public holiday in New South Wales in 1836, and Victoria adopted the day as a public holiday in 1931. The 26 January commenced to be recognised by all states and territories as Australia Day in 1946. </p>
      <p>Australia Day has been celebrated as a national public holiday on 26 January since 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Since 1960, the winner of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_of_the_Year" title="Australian of the Year">Australian of the Year</a> award is announced by the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Australia" title="Prime Minister of Australia">Prime Minister</a> on the eve of Australia Day (25 Jan). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labour_Day">Labour Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Labour Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Labour Day commemorates the achievements of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_labour_movement" title="Australian labour movement">Australian labour movement</a>. The celebration of Labour Day has its origins in the <a href="/wiki/Eight-hour_day" title="Eight-hour day">eight-hour day</a> movement, which advocated eight hours for work, eight hours for recreation, and eight hours for rest. On 21 April 1856 Stonemasons and building workers on building sites around <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, Australia, stopped work and marched from the University of Melbourne to Parliament House to achieve an eight-hour day. Their direct action protest was a success, and they are noted as the first organised workers in the world to achieve an eight-hour day with no loss of pay, which subsequently inspired the celebration of Labour Day and May Day. In Tasmania the public holiday is called Eight Hours Day and in the Northern Territory it is called May Day. </p>
      <p>The Labour Day public holiday varies considerably between the various <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_Australia" title="States and territories of Australia">states and territories</a>. It is the first Monday in October in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, it is the first Monday in March. In both <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, it is the second Monday in March. In the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a><sup id="cite_ref-pubholqld_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pubholqld-3">[3]</a></sup> it is the first Monday in May. More than 80 countries celebrate Labor Day. Labor Day is a long weekend. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Easter">Easter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Easter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The days of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> vary each year depending on the day determined by the <a href="/wiki/Western_Christian" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Christian">Western Christian</a> calendar. Until 1994 Easter Tuesday was a Bank Holiday in Victoria (it retains this status partially in Tasmania). The day after <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a> and before <a href="/wiki/Easter_Sunday" class="mw-redirect" title="Easter Sunday">Easter Sunday</a> is traditionally known as <a href="/wiki/Holy_Saturday" title="Holy Saturday">Holy Saturday</a>. However, the states where that day is a public holiday use different terminology – it is officially gazetted as "Easter Saturday" in the ACT, New South Wales, and the Northern Territory;<sup id="cite_ref-actsat_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-actsat-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nswsat_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nswsat-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ntsat_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ntsat-6">[6]</a></sup> as "the day after Good Friday" in Queensland and South Australia;<sup id="cite_ref-qldsat_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qldsat-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sasat_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sasat-8">[8]</a></sup> and as "Saturday before Easter Sunday" in Victoria.<sup id="cite_ref-vicsat_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vicsat-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ANZAC_Day">ANZAC Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: ANZAC Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/ANZAC_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="ANZAC Day">ANZAC Day</a> is a day on which the country remembers those citizens who fell fighting or who served the country in wars. ANZAC Day is commemorated on 25 April every year. The tradition began to remember the <a href="/wiki/Australian_and_New_Zealand_Army_Corps" title="Australian and New Zealand Army Corps">Australian and New Zealand Army Corps</a> (ANZAC) soldiers who <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gallipoli" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Gallipoli">landed</a> at <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli" title="Gallipoli">Gallipoli</a> in Turkey during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. </p>
      <p>ANZAC Day commemoration features marches by veterans and by solemn "Dawn Services", a tradition started in <a href="/wiki/Albany,_Western_Australia" title="Albany, Western Australia">Albany</a>, Western Australia on 25 April 1923 and now held at <a href="/wiki/War_memorials" class="mw-redirect" title="War memorials">war memorials</a> around the country, accompanied by thoughts of those lost at war to the ceremonial sounds of <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Last Post">The Last Post</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Bugle_(instrument)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bugle (instrument)">bugle</a>. The fourth stanza of <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Binyon" title="Laurence Binyon">Laurence Binyon</a>'s poem <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/For_the_Fallen" class="extiw" title="s:For the Fallen">For the Fallen</a> (known as the "<a href="/wiki/Ode_of_Remembrance" class="mw-redirect" title="Ode of Remembrance">Ode of Remembrance</a>") is often recited. </p> 
      <h3><span id="Queen.27s_Birthday"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Queen's_Birthday">Queen's Birthday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Queen's Birthday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In all <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_Australia" title="States and territories of Australia">states and territories</a> except Queensland<sup id="cite_ref-pubholqld_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pubholqld-3">[3]</a></sup> and Western Australia, <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Birthday" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen's Birthday">Queen's Birthday</a> is observed on the second Monday in June. Because Western Australia celebrates <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia_Day" title="Western Australia Day">Western Australia Day</a> (formerly Foundation Day) on the first Monday in June, the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Western_Australia" title="Governor of Western Australia">Governor of Western Australia</a> proclaims the day on which the state will observe the Queen's Birthday, based on school terms and the <a href="/wiki/Perth_Royal_Show" title="Perth Royal Show">Perth Royal Show</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> There is no firm rule to determine this date before it is proclaimed, though it is typically the last Monday of September or the first Monday of October: in 2011 the Queen's Birthday holiday in Western Australia was moved from Monday, 3 October 2011 to Friday, 28 October 2011 to coincide with the Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting (CHOGM), which was held in Perth.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In Queensland, it is celebrated on the 1st Monday in October.<sup id="cite_ref-pubholqld_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pubholqld-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The day has been celebrated since 1788, when <a href="/wiki/Governors_of_New_South_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Governors of New South Wales">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Phillip" title="Arthur Phillip">Arthur Phillip</a> declared a holiday to mark the birthday of <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George III of the United Kingdom">King George III</a>. Until 1936 it was held on the actual birthday of the Monarch, but after the death of <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">King George V</a>, it was decided to keep the date at mid-year. </p>
      <p>On that day the "Queen's Birthday honours list" is released naming new members of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Australia" title="Order of Australia">Order of Australia</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Australian_honours_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian honours system">Australian honours</a>. This occurs on the date observed in the Eastern States, not the date observed in Western Australia. </p>
      <p>The Queen's Birthday weekend and <a href="/wiki/Empire_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Empire Day">Empire Day</a>, 24 May, were long the traditional times for public <a href="/wiki/Firework" class="mw-redirect" title="Firework">fireworks</a> displays in Australia. Although they still occur, the tradition has recently been overshadowed by larger New Year's Eve fireworks, as the sale of fireworks to the public was banned by the states in the 1980s, and in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">ACT</a> as of 24 August 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christmas_Day">Christmas Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Christmas Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Christmas is observed on 25 December each year to commemorate the birth of Jesus. In Australia, it was introduced with British settlement in 1788 as the cultural norms were transferred to the new colonies. Though a Christian religious festival, it does not breach the constitution's separation of Church and State provision, because it is declared under State law, which is not subject to the provision. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boxing_Day">Boxing Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Boxing Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day" title="Boxing Day">Boxing Day</a> is on the day after Christmas, i.e. 26 December each year, except in South Australia. In South Australia, the first otherwise working day after Christmas is a public holiday called <a href="/wiki/Proclamation_Day_(South_Australia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Proclamation Day (South Australia)">Proclamation Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Boxing Day is noted for the start of the post-Christmas sale season. The day has also become a significant sporting day. Melbourne hosts the <a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day_Test" title="Boxing Day Test">Boxing Day Test match</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_to_Hobart_Yacht_Race" title="Sydney to Hobart Yacht Race">Sydney to Hobart Yacht Race</a> also starts on this day. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_holidays_by_state">Public holidays by state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Public holidays by state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Queensland">Queensland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Queensland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The days are set in the "Holidays Act 1983".<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Most public holidays include a second public holiday on a week-day if they happen to fall on Saturday or Sunday. In which case, both days are public holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-QldPublicHolidays_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QldPublicHolidays-42">[42]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>New Year's Day: 1 January, and if 1 January is a Saturday or Sunday, the following Monday.</li> 
       <li>Australia Day: 26 January, and if 26 January is a Saturday or Sunday, the following Monday.</li> 
       <li>Good Friday: on the date it is publicly observed, always a Friday.</li> 
       <li>The day after Good Friday: Always a Saturday, one day after Good Friday.</li> 
       <li>Easter Monday: The next Monday after Good Friday.</li> 
       <li>ANZAC Day: 25 April, and if 25 April is a Sunday, 26 April.</li> 
       <li>Labour Day ("May Day"): 1st Monday in May.<sup id="cite_ref-pubholqld_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pubholqld-3">[3]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Birthday of the Sovereign: 1st Monday in October.<sup id="cite_ref-pubholqld_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pubholqld-3">[3]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Christmas Day: 25 December.</li> 
       <li>Boxing Day: 26 December.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>If Christmas Day (25 December) is a Saturday or Sunday, then 27 December is also a public holiday. If Boxing day (26 December) is a Saturday or Sunday, then 28 December is also a public holiday. </p>
      <p>Because of the variable days of Easter, Anzac day could fall on an Easter holiday. When ANZAC falls on Saturday, there is no week day public holiday. In such situations it is generally expected that the minister will proclaim extra public holidays on week-days to ensure every year has the same number of public holidays on week-days. </p>
      <p>The minister of the state may proclaim and adjustments or additions, such as the date of the <a href="/wiki/Ekka" title="Ekka">Brisbane Ekka Show</a> day holiday. This day has historically always been proclaimed for the second Wednesday in August, except if there are 5 Wednesday's in August, in which case the third Wednesday in August.<sup id="cite_ref-QldPublicHolidays_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QldPublicHolidays-42">[42]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_South_Wales">New South Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: New South Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Public holidays generally follow the national pattern, but special cases are resolved by the State Government and advised by proclamation. Details of future holidays can be found on the NSW Industrial Relations website. Public holidays are regulated by the New South Wales <i>Public Holidays Act 2010 No 115</i>, which supersedes the <i>Banks and Bank Holidays Act 1912 No 43</i>. </p>
      <p>The first Monday in August is a Bank Holiday, during which banks and financial institutions are closed.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Australian_Capital_Territory_.26_Jervis_Bay_Territory"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_Capital_Territory_&amp;_Jervis_Bay_Territory">Australian Capital Territory &amp; Jervis Bay Territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Australian Capital Territory &amp; Jervis Bay Territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Most New South Wales public holidays are public holidays in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a>, with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Canberra_Day" title="Canberra Day">Canberra Day</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Victoria">Victoria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Victoria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Public holidays in Victoria are regulated by the Victorian <i>Public Holidays Act</i> 1993. </p>
      <p>Victorian employees fall under the <a href="/wiki/Workchoices" class="mw-redirect" title="Workchoices">Workchoices</a> system either as coming within the Commonwealth constitutional power (called "constitutional corporation employees") or because of Victoria's referral of its legislative powers to the Commonwealth for particular workplace relations matters. </p>
      <p>Employee entitlements to public holidays and additional pay depend on whether they are covered by a federal award or agreement. </p>
      <p>Employees not covered by a federal award or agreement are entitled to public holidays under the Victorian <i>Public Holidays Act</i> 1993. Also, all permanent employees not covered by a federal award or agreement who would normally work on a public holiday (or a substitute public holiday) are entitled to the holiday without loss of pay. Their employers are not required to provide additional payment if they work on a public holiday, but this does not exclude the possibility of employees and employers negotiating for additional pay. </p>
      <p>Employees who are covered by a federal award or agreement are entitled to public holidays as provided by the relevant federal award or agreement and the <i>Public Holidays Act</i> 1993. Many federal awards and agreements also provide for additional penalty rates for work performed on a public holiday. </p>
      <p>Restricted <a href="/wiki/Shopping_hours" title="Shopping hours">shop trading laws</a> apply to Good Friday, Christmas Day and before 1&nbsp;pm on Anzac Day. On these days only exempted businesses are permitted to open for trading.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> All public holidays and substitute public holidays are <a href="/wiki/Bank_holidays" class="mw-redirect" title="Bank holidays">bank holidays</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In August 2015, the day before the <a href="/wiki/AFL_Grand_Final" title="AFL Grand Final">AFL Grand Final</a>, as well as Easter Sunday, were gazetted as Public Holidays within Victoria. This date of the holiday is as gazetted by the Victorian Government and cannot be accurately predicted. In 2019, the Victorian Parliament legislated the AFL Grand Final public holiday by amending the <i>Public Holidays Act 1993 (Vic)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> </p>

See the list at <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110807092429/http://www.vic.gov.au/about-victoria-tourism/holidays-daylight-saving/public-holidays/victorian-non-metropolitan-public-holiday-dates.html">[1]</a>. </p>
      <p>Melbourne Show Day used to be observed on the Thursday in the last full week of September as a half-day public holiday—later changing to full day—until 1994 (abolished by the state government).<sup id="cite_ref-ShowDay_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ShowDay-48">[48]</a></sup> Easter Tuesday was observed as a Bank Holiday in Victoria until 1994 (also abolished by the state government). </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Penalty_rates">Penalty rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Penalty rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Overtime" title="Overtime">Overtime</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Penalty rates are the rates of pay which an employee is paid higher than their standard base rate for working at times or on days, such as public holidays, which are outside the normal working week.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> They were introduced in 1947 for workers working on the <a href="/wiki/Sabbath" title="Sabbath">Sabbath</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-spectator.com.au_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spectator.com.au-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-smh.com.au_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smh.com.au-53">[53]</a></sup> as most workers were Christian, while today, these rates of pay are set by the <a href="/wiki/Fair_Work_Commission" title="Fair Work Commission">Fair Work Commission</a>. </p>
