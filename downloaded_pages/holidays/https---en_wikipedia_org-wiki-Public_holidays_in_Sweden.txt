      <p><b>Public holidays in Sweden</b> (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">helgdagar</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> are established by acts of Parliament (the <i><a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliament of Sweden">Riksdag</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-public-holiday-law_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-public-holiday-law-1">[1]</a></sup> The official holidays can be divided into Christian and non-Christian holidays. The Christian holidays are <i><a href="/wiki/Yule" title="Yule">jul</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>). <i>trettondedag jul</i> (<a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(Christian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Epiphany (Christian)">Epiphany</a>), <i>påsk</i> (<a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>), <i>Kristi himmelsfärds dag</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ascension_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascension Day">Ascension Day</a>), <i>pingstdagen</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a>) and <i>alla helgons dag</i> (<a href="/wiki/All_Saints%27_Day" title="All Saints' Day">All Saints' Day</a>). The non-Christian holidays are: <i>nyårsdagen</i> (<a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">New Year's Day</a>), <i>första maj</i> (<a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">International Workers' Day</a>), <i>Sveriges nationaldag</i> (<a href="/wiki/National_Day_of_Sweden" title="National Day of Sweden">National Day</a>) and <i>midsommar</i> (<a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">Midsummer</a>); Midsummer is, however, officially also a Christian holiday to celebrate John the Baptist's birthday. </p>
      <p>In addition to this, all Sundays are official holidays but they are not as important as the <i>main</i> holidays. The names of the Sundays follow the liturgical calendar and they should be categorized as Christian holidays. <a href="/wiki/Easter_Sunday" class="mw-redirect" title="Easter Sunday">Easter Sunday</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a> are always on Sundays, but they are seen more like <i>main</i> holidays than ordinary Sundays. When the standard working week in Sweden was reduced to 40 hours by the <a href="/wiki/Riksdag" title="Riksdag">Riksdag</a>, all Saturdays became <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a> public holidays. <a href="/wiki/Holy_Saturday" title="Holy Saturday">Holy Saturday</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midsummer%27s_Eve" class="mw-redirect" title="Midsummer's Eve">Midsummer's Eve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year's Eve">New Year's Eve</a> are also de facto holidays. </p>
      <p>Part of the Swedish tradition is the celebration of <i>Lucia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucy_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Lucy Day">Saint Lucia Day</a>). She is the only saint to be celebrated in Lutheran Sweden (as well as those parts of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, where Swedish influence has historically been prominent). The celebration, which, however, is not a public holiday, always takes place on 13 December and retains many pre-Christian traditions. The same is also true for many holidays in Sweden. </p>
      <p>In Sweden, a public holiday is sometimes referred to as <i>röd dag</i> (red day), as it is printed in red in most calendars. It is quite common for some businesses to close at noon the day before certain holidays, and also if a holiday falls on a Tuesday or a Thursday, Swedes will commonly take off the <i>klämdag</i> (squashed in days, or squeeze day) that falls between the holiday and the weekend. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tradition">Tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In Swedish tradition many holidays have their main celebrations not on the <i>Day</i> but on the <i>Eve</i> of the holiday, meaning one day earlier. This is especially significant on Christmas Eve and Midsummer Eve, but also on New Year's Eve, however in this case not really unique. Christmas Eve, Midsummer Eve and New Year's Eve might very well be the single most important holidays during the entire year for the Swedish. These days are however only de facto holidays. There are also de facto half-day holidays (with some variation depending on employer): <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Night_(holiday)" title="Twelfth Night (holiday)">Twelfth Night</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maundy_Thursday" title="Maundy Thursday">Maundy Thursday</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walpurgis_Night" title="Walpurgis Night">Walpurgis Night</a>, the day before Ascension Day and the day before All Saints's Day. </p>
      <p>The Swedish calendar also provides for special <a href="/wiki/Flag_days_in_Sweden" title="Flag days in Sweden">flag days</a>. Flag days are in some cases official holidays or the birthdays and namedays for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_family_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal family of Sweden">Royal family</a> and <i>informal</i> holidays like <i><a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_Day" title="Gustavus Adolphus Day">Gustavus Adolphus Day</a></i> (November 6) or the Nobel Day (December 10). There is no formal connection between flag days and holiday. Many flag days are ordinary <a href="/wiki/Working_time" title="Working time">workdays</a>. </p>
      <p>The official <a href="/wiki/National_holiday_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="National holiday of Sweden">National holiday of Sweden</a> is celebrated on June 6, a status which it was finally granted in 2005, removing <a href="/wiki/Whit_Monday" title="Whit Monday">Whit Monday</a> as a public holiday. The <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Sweden" title="Name days in Sweden">Name days in Sweden</a> calendar is also denoted. It has a long history, originally a calendar of saints, some names have stuck throughout centuries while others have been modernized. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Several_observances_at_once">Several observances at once</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Several observances at once">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>There are instances where official holidays, de facto half days, official flagdays and other observances clash and several celebrations may run concurrently. One such case is the April 30 which is immediately followed by May 1. April 30 is a de facto half day because it is the Walpurgis Night and the main day for celebrations to the arrival of the spring season. The following day is actually Walpurgis Day; however, in the calendar it is primarily denoted as May Day, or Labor Day. This means that depending on your sympathies you may either celebrate it as May Day or as Walpurgis Day. In addition to this April 30 is also the <a href="/wiki/Carl_XVI_Gustaf_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl XVI Gustaf of Sweden">king's</a> birthday and an official flag day. Also May 1 is an official flag day by virtue of May Day <i>or</i> Walpurgis day. If either day would fall on a Sunday that day would also in that respect be an official holiday and a Christian holiday, as one of the Sundays following Easter. </p>
      <p>In 2008, due to the unusually early Easter, Ascension Day occurred on 1 May. This was the first time this happened since May Day became a public holiday in 1939. The next time these holidays overlap is in 2160. The next time Ascension Day will coincide with Walpurgis Night on April 30 (which is the earliest possible day) is in 2285. </p>

      <p>The day before an official holiday is in most cases treated as a <i>de facto</i> holiday in two variants, full day and half day. </p>

      <h4><span id="Permanent_kl.C3.A4mdag_.28squeeze_day.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_klämdag_(squeeze_day)">Permanent <i lang="sv" title="Swedish-language text">klämdag</i> (squeeze day)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Permanent klämdag (squeeze day)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>Days between a holiday and a weekend are in Swedish called <i lang="sv" title="Swedish-language text">klämdagar</i> (squeeze days). These may arise at different holidays, but there is one permanent <i lang="sv" title="Swedish-language text">klämdag</i> every year. Many people are off work on <i lang="sv" title="Swedish-language text">klämdagar</i> getting <a href="/wiki/Long_weekend" title="Long weekend">long weekends</a>. In some cases employers treat some of these days as de facto holidays; in other cases people may use some form of leave (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Vacation" title="Vacation">vacation</a>). </p>

      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Christmas_week_and_the_first_days_of_January">Christmas week and the first days of January</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_holidays_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Christmas week and the first days of January">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>During Christmas week and the days before and after Epiphany many Swedes are off from work, combining holidays, de facto holidays and other forms of leave (e.g. vacation). It is in fact quite common to leave work before Christmas Eve and then not come back to work until around January 10 (after the weekend after Epiphany). Most people however work at least some of these days. For instance, in 2019, Christmas Eve falls on a Tuesday, with Christmas Day falling on a Wednesday and St. Stephen's Day on a Thursday. Thus, some employees are automatically allowed the day off (as they are <i lang="sv" title="Swedish-language text">klämdagar</i>) on the Monday before Christmas Eve (December 23) and the Friday after St. Stephen's Day (December 27), while others chose to take those days off as vacation. The same goes for the Monday before New Year's Eve (December 30). However, the Thursday and Friday after New Years Day (January 2 and 3 of 2020) are not considered <i lang="sv" title="Swedish-language text">klämdagar</i>, since they are two workdays falling next to each other. Thus, if people wanted those days off, they would have had to take them as vacation. As most people in Sweden are not required to work on Saturdays or Sundays, people could in fact work their last day on December 20, having taken two or five vacation days, and then not have to return to work until January 7 (the Tuesday after Epiphany). </p>
