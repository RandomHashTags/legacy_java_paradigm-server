      <p>The Tongan archipelago has been inhabited for perhaps 3000 years, since settlement in late <a href="/wiki/Lapita" class="mw-redirect" title="Lapita">Lapita</a> times. The <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> of its inhabitants has surely changed greatly over this long time period. Before the arrival of European explorers in the late 17th and early 18th centuries, the Tongans were in frequent contact with their nearest Oceanic neighbors, <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a>. In the 19th century, with the arrival of Western traders and missionaries, Tongan culture changed dramatically. Some old beliefs and habits were thrown away and others adopted. Some accommodations made in the 19th century and early 20th century are now being challenged by changing Western civilization. Hence <b>Tongan culture</b> is far from a unified or monolithic affair, and Tongans themselves may differ strongly as to what it is "Tongan" to do, or not do. </p>
      <p>Contemporary Tongans often have strong ties to overseas lands. They may have been migrant workers in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, or have lived and traveled in New Zealand, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Many Tongans now live overseas, in a Tongan <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a>, and send home remittances to family members (often aged) who prefer to remain in <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>. Tongans themselves often have to operate in two different contexts, which they often call <i>anga fakatonga</i>, the traditional Tongan way, and <i>anga fakapālangi</i>, the Western way. A culturally adept Tongan learns both sets of rules and when to switch between them. </p>
      <p>Any description of Tongan culture that limits itself to what Tongans see as <i>anga fakatonga</i> would give a seriously distorted view of what people actually do, in Tonga, or in diaspora, because accommodations are so often made to <i>anga fakapālangi</i>. The following account tries to give both the idealized and the on-the-ground versions of <i>Tongan culture</i>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Livelihood">Livelihood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Livelihood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Traditionally, fishing and farming have accounted for the livelihood of a majority of Tongans. The main food crops include Sweet Potatoes, Bananas, Yucca, Taro and giant Taro to name a few. Cash crops include <a href="/wiki/Squash_(plant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Squash (plant)">squash</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pumpkins" class="mw-redirect" title="Pumpkins">pumpkins</a>, which have in recent years replaced <a href="/wiki/Bananas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bananas">bananas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copra" title="Copra">copra</a> as the largest agricultural exports. <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">Vanilla</a> is another important cash crop.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinship">Kinship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kinship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">

      <p>Men and women hold unique roles in Tongan society. A man usually holds the power and is considered the head of his household. However, a man has an obligation to care for his sister and her children. So in Tongan families one's maternal uncle, or Fa'etangata, is considered lower in ranking because of his obligation to take care of the needs of his sister and her children. In contrast, one's paternal aunt is the highest-ranking member and is referred to as mehekitanga or Fahu. Men hold power and women hold rank. In ancient Tonga one would inherit titles, land and people from their mother; after Christianity this was changed to mainly inheriting from one's father. Until recently it was Tapu (taboo) for any male that has passed puberty to be in the same room with his sister or girl cousins alone. This was done under the notion of respect or faka'apa'apa. The recent introduction of western ideas and culture has slowly made this taboo vary widely amongst Tongan families. At one time it was inappropriate for a brother and sister to even watch television together if the characters were to kiss or even flirt on screen. That is mostly relaxed in the modern age, but there are still families that will separate the boys and girls when watching TV or movies. The notion of faka'apa'apa is still strong but the meaning of it is slowly changing especially amongst the youth of the Tongan diaspora. Other key members of Tongan kinship are the 'Ulumotu'a or the oldest male in one's extended family on your father's side, they are usually called upon to be in charge of funerals and other family events. They do not outrank a Fahu but they have power to direct events. All of your maternal aunts are called your mothers, likewise your paternal uncles are your fathers. Your cousins on either side are called your brothers or sisters. Similarly if you are female, you are mother to your sister's children and if you are a male, your brother's children will refer to you as a father. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Male_circumcision">Male circumcision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Male circumcision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In post-contact Tonga, newly pubescent males were kamu (<i>tefe</i>), or circumcised by cutting one slit in the foreskin, on the underside of the penis. This is a Christian practice of biblical context. Afterwards, the family held a feast for the new "man". <a href="/wiki/Circumcision" title="Circumcision">Circumcision</a> is still practiced, but it is now done informally. Sometimes it is done at home, with relatives present. More commonly a boy, or a group of boys, go to the hospital, where the operation is done under sanitary conditions. </p> 
      <h3><span id="First_menstruation_.28Menarche.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_menstruation_(Menarche)">First menstruation (Menarche)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First menstruation (Menarche)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In pre-contact Tonga, a girl's first menstruation was celebrated by a feast. This practice continued up until the mid-20th century, at which point it fell out of favor. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tongan_funerals" title="Tongan funerals">Tongan funerals</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Contemporary <a href="/wiki/Funerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Funerals">funerals</a> are large, well-attended occasions, even for Tongans who are not wealthy. Relatives gather, often traveling long distances to do so. Large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> are contributed, then distributed to the crowds during and after the funeral. Funeral practices are a mix of introduced <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> rites and customs (such as a wake and a <a href="/wiki/Christian_burial" title="Christian burial">Christian burial</a>), and older indigenous customs that survive from pre-contact times. For instance, mourners wear black (a Western custom) but also wrap mats (<a href="/wiki/Ta%27ovala" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta'ovala">ta'ovala</a>) around their waist. The type and size of the mat proclaim the mourner's relationship to the deceased. </p>
      <p>Tongan families do not necessarily compete to put on the largest, grandest funeral possible, but they do strive to show respect for the deceased by doing all that is customary. This can put great strain on the resources of the immediate family and even the extended family. Sometimes the funeral is called a <i>fakamasiva</i>, an occasion that leads to poverty. </p>

      <p>Violent <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> is limited, but increasing, and public perception associates this with returns of ethnic Tongans who have been raised overseas. A few notable cases involve young men raised since infancy in the US, whose family neglected to obtain citizenship for them and who were deported on involvement with the American justice system. At this moment<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2019)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> crime increases faster than the police force and are expected to remain a serious problem for the years to come. Increasing <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a> has also increased the gap between the rich and the poor, leading to more and more <a href="/wiki/Burglaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Burglaries">burglaries</a>. </p>
      <p>At this moment most <a href="/wiki/Prisons" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisons">prisons</a> in Tonga still abide with the old laissez-faire attitude. Usually having no fences, no iron bars and so forth, that makes it very easy for the inmates to escape. This system may be required to change, adapting for the influx of foreign born/raised <a href="/wiki/Criminals" class="mw-redirect" title="Criminals">criminals</a> who may treat such a system with contempt, alternatively a minimum/maximum security prison system may need to be developed placing escapists or repeat offenders into closed prisons, but for the moment the jailors can trust on the goodwill of the inmates. Some are glad to be in prison, not to be bothered by demanding family members. There is no social stigma on being in prison (although that may change now too), but then of course it also does not serve as a deterrent against crimes. </p>
      <p>More troublesome are the youth offenders "schoolboys who want to have money to show off" and are apprehended in burglaries. As there are no juvenile prisons, they are to be locked up in the main prisons together with hardened criminals. For a while it was tried to confine them on <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tau_(Tongatapu)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tau (Tongatapu) (page does not exist)">Tau</a>, a small island offshore <a href="/wiki/Tongatapu" title="Tongatapu">Tongatapu</a> but that was not ideal either. </p>
      <p>In the 1990s Chinese immigration caused resentment among the native Tongan population (especially those from <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, who bought a Tongan passport to get away before the Beijing takeover). Much violent crime nowadays is directed against these Chinese. </p>
      <p>There is however a rise in violence among Tongans that have left Tonga. On March 7, 1987, a 15-year-old was fatally shot in the Disneyland parking lot. The incident began as an early morning confrontation between rival Samoan and Tongan gang members before escalating into a brawl. Another participant was convicted of second-degree murder, but the conviction was subsequently overturned by a state appellate court. </p>

      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_poetry_and_short_stories">Modern poetry and short stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Modern poetry and short stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>The genre of short stories in Tonga is most associated with <a href="/wiki/%27Epeli_Hau%27ofa" class="mw-redirect" title="'Epeli Hau'ofa">'Epeli Hau'ofa</a>, whose most popular collection of stories, <i>Tales of the Tikong</i>, was published in 1973. <a href="/wiki/Konai_Helu_Thaman" title="Konai Helu Thaman">Konai Helu Thaman</a> was one of the country's earliest published poets.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Traditional_women.27s_crafts"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_women's_crafts">Traditional women's crafts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Traditional women's crafts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Tapa.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Tapa.jpg/220px-Tapa.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Tapa.jpg/330px-Tapa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Tapa.jpg/440px-Tapa.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="806"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Tapa.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Tongan <i>tapa</i>
        </div>
       </div>

      <p>In pre-contact Tonga, women did not do the cooking (cooking in an earth oven was hard, hot work, the province of men) or work in the fields. They raised children, gathered shellfish on the reef, and made <i>koloa</i>, barkcloth and mats, which were a traditional form of wealth exchanged at marriages and other ceremonial occasions. An industrious woman thus raised the social status of her household. Her family also slept soundly, on the piles of mats and barkcloth that were the traditional bedding. On sunny days, these were spread on the grass to air, which prolonged their life. </p>
      <p>Among the typical <i>koloa</i> are: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Bark cloth, or <a href="/wiki/Tapa_cloth" title="Tapa cloth">tapa</a> (but it is called <i>ngatu</i> in Tonga).</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Mat" title="Mat">Mats</a>.</li> 
       <li>Waist mats, called <a href="/wiki/Ta%27ovala" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta'ovala">taʻovala</a>.</li> 
       <li>Waist girdles, called <a href="/wiki/Kiekie_(clothing)" title="Kiekie (clothing)">kiekie</a>.</li> 
       <li>And any other type of traditional (dance) clothing.</li>

      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mat-weaving">Mat-weaving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mat-weaving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>Woven mats serve a variety of purposes, from the ordinary to the ceremonial. Many woven mats are passed down from generation to generation, acquiring greater status with the passage of time. It is in fact a collection of these mats in the palace that forms the true crown jewels of Tonga. These royal mats are displayed only on high state occasions such as the death of a member of the royal family or the coronation of a monarch. </p> 
      <h3><span id="Traditional_men.27s_crafts"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_men's_crafts">Traditional men's crafts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Traditional men's crafts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wood_carving">Wood carving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Wood carving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

      <p>Before Western contact, many objects of daily use were made of carved <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>: food bowls, head rests (<i>kali</i>), war clubs and <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">spears</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cult" title="Cult">cult</a> images. Tongan craftsmen were skilled at inlaying pearl-shell and ivory in wood, and Tongan war clubs were treasured items in the neighboring archipelago of <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>. </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canoe-building">Canoe-building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Canoe-building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>Tongan craftsman were also adept at building <a href="/wiki/Canoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Canoes">canoes</a>. Many canoes for daily use were simple <i>pōpaos</i>, dug-out canoes, shaped from a single log with fire and adze and outfitted with a single outrigger. Due to a dearth of large trees suitable for building large war canoes, these canoes were often imported from Fiji. </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_architecture">Traditional architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Traditional architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>The tradition Tongan <i><a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Samoa" title="Architecture of Samoa">fale</a></i> consisted of a curved roof (branches lashed with <a href="/wiki/Sennit" title="Sennit">sennit</a> rope, or <i>kafa</i>, thatched with woven palm leaves) resting on pillars made of tree trunks. Woven screens filled in the area between the ground and the edge of the roof. The traditional design was extremely well adapted to surviving hurricanes. If the winds threatened to shred the walls and overturn the roof, the inhabitants could chop down the pillars, so that the roof fell directly onto the ground. Because the roof was curved, like a limpet shell, the wind tended to flow over it smoothly. The inhabitants could ride out the storm in relative safety. </p>
      <p>There are many surviving examples of Tongan stone architecture, notably the <a href="/wiki/Ha%27amonga_%27a_Maui" class="mw-redirect" title="Ha'amonga 'a Maui">Haʻamonga ʻa Maui</a> and mound tombs (<a href="/wiki/Langi_(burial)" class="mw-redirect" title="Langi (burial)">langi</a>) near <a href="/wiki/Lapaha" class="mw-redirect" title="Lapaha">Lapaha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tongatapu" title="Tongatapu">Tongatapu</a>. And so several on other islands. Archaeologists have dated them hundreds to a thousand years old. </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tattooing">Tattooing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Tattooing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>Tongan males were often heavily tattooed. In <a href="/wiki/Captain_Cook" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain Cook">Captain Cook</a>'s time only the <a href="/wiki/Tu%27i_Tonga" class="mw-redirect" title="Tu'i Tonga">Tuʻi Tonga</a> (king) was not: because he was too high ranked for anybody to touch him. Later it became the habit that a young Tuʻi Tonga went to <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a> to be tattooed there. </p>
      <p>The practice of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tatatau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tatatau (page does not exist)">Tātatau</a> disappeared under heavy missionary disapproval, but was never completely suppressed. It is still very common for men (less so, but still some for women), to be decorated with some small tattoos. Nevertheless, tattoos shows one's strength. <a href="/wiki/Tattoos" class="mw-redirect" title="Tattoos">Tattoos</a> also tell a story. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestication_of_Western_arts_and_crafts">Domestication of Western arts and crafts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Domestication of Western arts and crafts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_textile_arts">Western textile arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Western textile arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>Tonga has evolved its own version of Western-style clothing, consisting of a long <a href="/wiki/Tupenu" title="Tupenu">tupenu</a>, or sarong, for women, and a short tupenu for men. Women cover the tupenu with a <i>kofu</i>, or Western-style dress; men top the tupenu either with a T-shirt, a Western casual shirt, or on formal occasions, a dress shirt and a suit coat. Preachers in some Methodist sects still wear long <a href="/wiki/Frock_coat" title="Frock coat">frock coats</a>, a style that has not been current in the West for more than a hundred years. These coats must be tailored locally. </p>
      <p>Tongan outfits are often assembled from used Western clothing (for the top) mixed with a length of cloth purchased locally for the tupenu. Used clothing can be found for sale at local markets, or can be purchased overseas and mailed home by relatives. </p>
      <p>Some women have learned to sew and own sewing machines (often antique treadle machines). They do simple home-sewing of shirts, kofu, and school uniforms. </p>
      <p>Nukuʻalofa, the capital, supports several tailoring shops. They tailor tupenu and suitcoats for Tongan men, and matching tupenu and kofu for Tongan women. The women's outfits may be decorated with simple blockprint patterns on the hems. </p>
      <p>There is also some local production of knit jerseys by Tongans operating imported sergers. They produce on speculation and sell at the Nukuʻalofa market. </p>
      <p>Women who attend the Wesleyan Methodist girls' school, Queen Sālote College, are taught several Western handicrafts, such as embroidery and crochet. They learn to make embroidered pillowcases and bed coverings or crocheted lace <a href="/wiki/Tablecloth" title="Tablecloth">tablecloths</a>, bedcovers, and lace trim. However, Western-style handicrafts such as these have not become widely popular outside the school setting. They require expensive imported materials that can only be purchased in major towns. Village women are much more likely to turn their efforts to weaving mats or beating barkcloth, which can be done with free local material. </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting">Painting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Painting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>A few Tongan village churches are decorated with freehand murals or decorations done in house paint, which may mix crosses, flowers, and traditional barkcloth motifs. The practice is uncommon and the execution is always crude. </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coral_and_tortoise-shell_jewelry">Coral and tortoise-shell jewelry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Coral and tortoise-shell jewelry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>In the 1970s there was a small factory near Nukuʻalofa that made simple jewelry from coral and tortoise-shell for sale to Western tourists. It is not clear if this factory is still operating. The government may have protected sea-turtles and corals (as has been done in most other countries) and ended this line of manufacture. </p>

      <p>Scholars know relatively little about the music of Tonga as it existed before Tonga was encountered by European explorers. Early visitors, such as <a href="/wiki/Captain_Cook" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain Cook">Captain Cook</a> and the invaluable <a href="/wiki/William_Mariner_(writer)" title="William Mariner (writer)">William Mariner</a>, note only the singing and drumming during traditional dance performances. Scholars can assume the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Lali_(drum)" title="Lali (drum)">lali</a> or <a href="/wiki/Slit-gong" class="mw-redirect" title="Slit-gong">slit-gong</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nose_flute" title="Nose flute">nose flute</a>, as these survived to later times. Traditional songs, passed down over the generations, are still sung at chiefly ceremonies. Some ancient dances are still performed, such as <a href="/wiki/Ula_(dance)" title="Ula (dance)">ula</a>, <a href="/wiki/%27otuhaka" class="mw-redirect" title="'otuhaka">ʻotuhaka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Me%27etu%27upaki" class="mw-redirect" title="Me'etu'upaki">meʻetuʻupaki</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_music">Church music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Church music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Methodists" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodists">Methodists</a> were known for their extensive use of hymns in their emotional services. True to their tradition, the early missionaries introduced hymn-singing to their congregations. These early hymns—still sung today in some of the Methodist sects, such as the <a href="/wiki/Free_Church_of_Tonga" title="Free Church of Tonga">Free Church of Tonga</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Tonga" title="Church of Tonga">Church of Tonga</a> - have Tongan tunes and simple, short Tongan lyrics. There is a special <a href="/wiki/Tongan_music_notation" title="Tongan music notation">Tongan music notation</a> for these, and other, musics. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_music">Traditional music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Traditional music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Traditional music is preserved in the set pieces performed at royal and noble weddings and funerals, and in the song sung during the traditional ceremony of apology, the lou-ifi. <a href="/wiki/Radio_Tonga" title="Radio Tonga">Radio Tonga</a> begins each day's broadcast with a recording from Honourable Veʻehala, a nobleman and celebrated virtuoso of the nose flute. This music is not popular music; it is a cherished heirloom, preserved by specialists and taught as needed for special occasions. </p>

      <p>In former times, there was only one main meal, a midday meal cooked in an earth oven. Villagers would rise, eat some leftover food from the previous day's meal, and set out to work in the fields, <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, gathering <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, etc. The results of the morning's work would be cooked by the men, and served to the assembled household. The remnants would be placed in a basket suspended from a tree. This food is served as an end-of-the-day snack as well as the next day's breakfast. Food past its prime was given to the pigs. </p>
      <p>The diet consisted mainly of <a href="/wiki/Taro" title="Taro">taro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxalis_tuberosa" title="Oxalis tuberosa">yams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bananas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bananas">bananas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coconuts" class="mw-redirect" title="Coconuts">coconuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> baked in leaves; shellfish were usually served raw, as a <a href="/wiki/Relish" title="Relish">relish</a>. The liquid from the center of coconuts was commonly drunk, and the soft "spoon meat" of young coconuts much relished. Baked <a href="/wiki/Breadfruit" title="Breadfruit">breadfruit</a> was eaten in season. <a href="/wiki/Pigs" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigs">Pigs</a> were killed and cooked only on special occasions, such as <a href="/wiki/Weddings" class="mw-redirect" title="Weddings">weddings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Funerals">funerals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feasts" class="mw-redirect" title="Feasts">feasts</a> honoring a visiting chief, and the like. Tongans also ate <a href="/wiki/Chickens" class="mw-redirect" title="Chickens">chickens</a>. </p>
      <p>Food could be stored by feeding it to pigs. Pre-contact Tongans also built elevated storehouses for yams. Yams would keep only a few months. Hence a household's main security was generous distribution of food to relatives and neighbors, who were thus put under an obligation to share in their turn. </p>
      <p>Many new foods were introduced in the 19th and early 20th centuries, following Western contacts and settlements. The <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a> plant was one such introduction; it is called <i>manioke</i> in Tongan. While it lacks the prestige of the yam, it is an easy plant to grow and a common crop. Introduced <a href="/wiki/Watermelons" class="mw-redirect" title="Watermelons">watermelons</a> became popular. They were eaten either by themselves, or pulped and mixed with <a href="/wiki/Coconut_milk" title="Coconut milk">coconut milk</a>, forming a popular drink called <i><a href="/wiki/%27otai" title="'otai">'otai</a></i>. Other fruits, such as <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">oranges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">limes</a>, became popular. Tongans also adopted <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">onions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scallion" title="Scallion">green onions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carrot" title="Carrot">carrots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, and other common vegetables. In the last few decades, Tongan farmers with access to large tracts of land have engaged in commercial farming of <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">pumpkins</a> and other easily shipped vegetables as cash crops. </p>
      <p>Tongans now consume large quantities of imported <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>. One dish that uses both is <i>topai</i> (doughboys), flour and water worked into a paste and dropped into a kettle of boiling water, then served with a <a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">syrup</a> of sugar and coconut milk. Topai are a common funeral food, being easily prepared for hundreds of mourners. </p>
      <p>There are now bakeries in the larger cities. The most popular loaves are soft, white, and bland. There are also local soft drink bottlers, who make various local varieties of <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soda</a>. A Tongan who might once have breakfasted on bits of cooked <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a> and yam from a hanging basket may now have <a href="/wiki/White_bread" title="White bread">white bread</a> and soda for breakfast. </p>
      <p>Purchased prepared foods have also made great headway, even in remote villages. Canned <a href="/wiki/Cornbeef" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornbeef">cornbeef</a> is a great favorite. It is eaten straight from the can, or mixed with coconut milk and onions, wrapped in leaves, and baked in the earth oven. Tongans also eat canned fish, such as <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>. In villages or towns with refrigeration, cheap frozen "<a href="/wiki/Mutton_flaps" title="Mutton flaps">mutton flaps</a>" imported from New Zealand are popular. Tongans also eat the common South Pacific "ship's biscuit", hard plain <a href="/wiki/Cracker_(food)" title="Cracker (food)">crackers</a> once a shipboard staple. These crackers are called <i>mā pakupaku</i>. </p>
      <p>Tongans no longer make an earth oven every day. Most daily cooking is done by women, who cook in battered pots over open fires in the village, in wood-burning stoves in some households, and on gas or electric ranges in some of the larger towns. The meal schedule has also changed, to more Westernized breakfast, light lunch, and heavy dinner. Tongans say that the old schedule is unworkable when household members have Western-style jobs, or attend schools at some distance from home; such family members cannot come home to eat, then have a doze after a heavy mid-day meal. </p>
      <p>As well as drinking soda, Tongans now drink <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>. Usually this is of the cheapest variety, and served with tinned <a href="/wiki/Condensed_milk" title="Condensed milk">condensed milk</a>. </p>
      <p>Some men drink <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a>. Sometimes this is imported Australian or New Zealand <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>; more often it is home-brew, <i><a href="/wiki/Hopi" title="Hopi">hopi</a></i>, made with <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, sugar or mashed <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>. Imported drinks are sold only to Tongans who have liquor permits, which require a visit to a government office, and limit the amount of alcohol which can be purchased. There are no such formalities with <i>hopi</i>. Drinking is usually done secretively; a group of men gather and drink until they are drunk. Such gatherings sometimes result in drunken quarrels and assaults. </p>

      <p>The drinking of <a href="/wiki/Kava" title="Kava">kava</a> by men at kava clubs is somewhat equal to drinking beers in the bar in western cultures. However formal kava drinking is an important and intrinsic part of Tonga culture. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tongan_cuisine_and_health">Tongan cuisine and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Tongan cuisine and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Tonga is notable for its high obesity rates with over 90% of the population being overweight. Consequently, many Tongan islanders have an increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Heart_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a> and other obesity related diseases which place the nation's health service under considerable strain.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Much of this is related to the nation's cultural love of food and eating as well as the modern influx of cheap and high-fat content <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Corned_beef" title="Corned beef">corned beef</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lamb_(meat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lamb (meat)">lamb</a> belly remaining firm favourites in Tongan cuisine. Despite being a highly obese population, there is little stigma attached to being overweight as one might find in many Western civilizations. Like a great number of South Pacific cultures, large bodies are often revered,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The reason for this is unclear. (April 2014)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup> though there is growing acknowledgment of the health risks involved. </p>

      <p>Tongan men wear a <a href="/wiki/Tupenu" title="Tupenu">tupenu</a>, a cloth that is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Sarong" title="Sarong">sarong</a>, which is wrapped around the waist. It should be long enough to cover the knees or the shins of the legs. In daily life, any <a href="/wiki/Shirt" title="Shirt">shirt</a> (T-shirt, jersey, woven shirt) will do to top the tupenu. Usually shirts are used clothing imported from overseas. Some men will go shirtless working on their plantations, but by law they are not allowed to go shirtless in public. </p>
      <p>On formal occasions a <a href="/wiki/Ta%27ovala" class="mw-redirect" title="Ta'ovala">taʻovala</a>, a woven mat, is worn over the tupenu. It is wrapped around the waist and secured with a <a href="/wiki/Coir" title="Coir">kafa</a> rope. The tupenu may be smartly tailored and have a matching suit jacket. If a man cannot afford to have a suit tailored to fit him, he will buy a used Western <a href="/wiki/Jacket" title="Jacket">jacket</a>, or wear a threadbare jacket inherited from an older relative. </p>
      <p>Women too wear a tupenu, but a long one which should reach to the ankles. They sometimes wear a shorter tupenu for working in the house or picking shellfish on the reef. The tupenu is usually topped with a <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kofu_(clothing)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kofu (clothing) (page does not exist)">kofu</a></i>, or dress. This may be sewn to order, or it may be an imported used dress. Sometimes women wear blouses or jerseys. </p>
      <p>On formal occasions women too wear a taʻovala, or more often a <a href="/wiki/Kiekie_(clothing)" title="Kiekie (clothing)">kiekie</a>, a string skirt attached to a waistband. It is lighter and cooler than a mat. Kiekie are made from many different materials, from the traditional (pandanus leaves, as used in mats) to the innovative (unspooled magnetic tape from tape cassettes). </p>
      <p>Huge taʻovala are worn at <a href="/wiki/Funerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Funerals">funerals</a>. </p>
      <p>The largest Methodist church holds a yearly celebration for the women of the congregation. Churches hold special church services to which women wear white clothing. All the Methodist churches have adopted the Western custom of women wearing <a href="/wiki/Hat" title="Hat">hats</a> to church. Only women who have been admitted to the congregation can wear hats; those denied admittance (because they are still young, or because they are considered to be living immoral lives) are only "inquirers" and go hatless. </p>
      <p>More and more Tongan men are abandoning the traditional tupenu for <a href="/wiki/Trousers" title="Trousers">trousers</a>, at least when it comes to working in the fields. Women can be innovative in terms of color and cut within the context of the traditional kofu/tupenu combination. </p>

      <p><a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">Rugby union</a> is the national sport in Tonga. The nation has a <a href="/wiki/Tonga_national_rugby_union_team" title="Tonga national rugby union team">national rugby union team</a>, which played in the <a href="/wiki/1987_Rugby_Union_World_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="1987 Rugby Union World Cup">1987</a>, <a href="/wiki/1995_Rugby_Union_World_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="1995 Rugby Union World Cup">1995</a>, <a href="/wiki/1999_Rugby_Union_World_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="1999 Rugby Union World Cup">1999</a>, <a href="/wiki/2003_Rugby_Union_World_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="2003 Rugby Union World Cup">2003</a>, 2007 <a href="/wiki/Rugby_World_Cup" title="Rugby World Cup">Rugby World Cup</a> and the 2015 competitions. Though Tongans are passionate rugby followers, the small population base means that internationally, Tongan rugby continually struggles. Often, young talent emigrate to countries which offer greater prospects of individual success such as <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Some notable rugby union players of Tongan descent include <a href="/wiki/Jonah_Lomu" title="Jonah Lomu">Jonah Lomu</a> (played for the All Blacks) and <a href="/wiki/Toutai_Kefu" title="Toutai Kefu">Toutai Kefu</a> (plays for Australian Wallaby). </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">Rugby league</a> is a popular sport enjoyed by Tongans. <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">Football</a> has a following, while <a href="/wiki/Judo" title="Judo">judo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surfing" title="Surfing">surfing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> have gained popularity in recent years. </p>

      <p>The king and the majority of the royal family are members of the <a href="/wiki/Free_Wesleyan_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Wesleyan Church">Free Wesleyan Church</a> (<a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a>) which claims some 40,000 adherents in the country. There are four other Methodist denominations in the country, as well as a number of (much smaller) <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostal</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelicalist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Evangelicalist (page does not exist)">Evangelicalist</a> congregations. <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> and each have a strong presence in the country as well. There is a small <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist Church</a> group, an <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglican</a> church, and a few adherents of the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith_in_Tonga" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahá'í Faith in Tonga">Bahá'í Faith in Tonga</a>. There are even some Tongan <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Muslims</a>. </p>
      <p>Tongans are ardent churchgoers. Methodist services usually follow a call and response structure. Singing in the church is often done <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a>. Although church attends primarily to the spiritual needs of the population, it also functions as the primary social hub. As consequence people who go to a church of another denomination are absolutely not <a href="/wiki/Shunning" title="Shunning">shunned</a>. </p>
      <p>Sunday in Tonga is celebrated as a day for rest and worship; strict <a href="/wiki/Sabbath_in_Christianity" title="Sabbath in Christianity">Sabbatarianism</a> is enshrined in the constitution. No trade is allowed on Sunday, except essential services, after special approval by the minister of police. Lawbreakers risk a fine or imprisonment. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Holidays">Public Holidays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Public Holidays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Some Tongan holidays are based upon the Christian faith, which include <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> with its preceding Friday and following Monday and both <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> days (but not Pentecost, Ascuncion, etc.). Tonga also celebrates <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">New Year's Day</a> on January 1 and <a href="/wiki/Anzac_Day" title="Anzac Day">Anzac Day</a> on April 25. Tonga's unique holidays are: Emancipation day on June 4, Crownprince's birthday on July 12, the King's official birth- and coronation day on August (which is celebrated with the consumption of African cuisine),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> National Tonga day on November 4 (formerly known as Constitution day) and King <a href="/wiki/George_Tupou_I" title="George Tupou I">George Tupou I</a>'s installation as <a href="/wiki/Tu%27i_Kanokupolu" class="mw-redirect" title="Tu'i Kanokupolu">Tuʻi Kanokupolu</a> on December 4, often misnamed as his birthday. Since the promulgation of new holidays on December 6, 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> May 4 and July 4 are no longer public holidays. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Culture_of_Tonga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Popular Tongan festivals included (new scheme still to be established since the change of public holidays): </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Heilala Festival Week (around 8 July)</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Vava%27u" class="mw-redirect" title="Vava'u">Vavaʻu</a> Festival Week (around 8 May)</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Ha%27apai" class="mw-redirect" title="Ha'apai">Haʻapai</a> Tourism Festival (around 8 June)</li> 
       <li>Royal Agricultural and Industrial Show (triennial, August - September)</li>
