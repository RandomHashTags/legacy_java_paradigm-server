      <p><b>National DNA Day</b> is a United States holiday <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_secular_observances#April" title="List of minor secular observances">celebrated</a> on April 25. It commemorates the day in 1953 when <a href="/wiki/James_D._Watson" class="mw-redirect" title="James D. Watson">James Watson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Francis Crick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkins" title="Maurice Wilkins">Maurice Wilkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Franklin" title="Rosalind Franklin">Rosalind Franklin</a> and colleagues published papers in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> on the structure of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Franklin1953_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Franklin1953-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wilkins1953_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkins1953-3">[3]</a></sup> Furthermore, in early April 2003 it was declared that the <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a> was very close to complete, and "the remaining tiny gaps were considered too costly to fill."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, DNA Day was first celebrated on April 25, 2003, by proclamation of both the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, they only declared a one-time celebration, not an annual holiday. Every year from 2003 onward, annual DNA Day celebrations have been organized by the <a href="/wiki/National_Human_Genome_Research_Institute" title="National Human Genome Research Institute">National Human Genome Research Institute</a> (NHGRI), starting as early as April 23 in 2010, April 15 in 2011<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and April 20 in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> April 25 has since been declared "International DNA Day"<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and "World DNA Day"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> by several groups. <a href="/wiki/Genealogical_DNA_test" title="Genealogical DNA test">Genealogical DNA testing</a> companies and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_genealogy" title="Genetic genealogy">genetic genealogy</a> publishers run annual sales around DNA Day, seeking interest from the public and promoting their services.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>
