      <p><b>German Unity Day</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de"><b>Tag der Deutschen Einheit</b></i>) is the <a href="/wiki/National_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="National Day">National Day</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, celebrated on 3 October as a public holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It commemorates <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a> in 1990 when the Federal Republic of Germany (<a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>) and the German Democratic Republic (<a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>) were unified, so that for the first time since 1945 there existed a single German state. German Unity Day on 3 October has been the <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Germany" title="Public holidays in Germany">German National Holiday</a> since 1990, when the reunification was formally completed. </p>
      <p>An alternative choice to commemorate the reunification could have been the day the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a> came down: 9 November 1989, which coincided with the anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%9319" class="mw-redirect" title="German Revolution of 1918–19">proclamation of the German Republic</a> in 1918, and the defeat of <a href="/wiki/Beer_Hall_Putsch" title="Beer Hall Putsch">Hitler's first coup</a> in 1923. However, 9 November was also the anniversary of the first large-scale Nazi-led <a href="/wiki/Pogrom" title="Pogrom">pogroms</a> against <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jews</a> in 1938 (<i><a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a></i>), so the day was considered inappropriate as a national holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (See <a href="/wiki/November_9_in_German_history" title="November 9 in German history">November 9 in German history</a>.) Therefore, 3 October 1990, the day of the formal reunification, was chosen instead and replaced the "Day of German Unity" on 17 June, the national holiday of the <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a> from 1954. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Germany has historically associated various dates with its nationhood and unity. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_Germany">Imperial Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Imperial Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Before 1871, in the area where the single state of Germany now exists, different kingdoms and principalities existed. After the <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">unification of Germany</a>, and the Founding of the Empire 1871, there was still no common national holiday. The <a href="/wiki/Sedantag" title="Sedantag">Sedantag</a> was, however, celebrated every year on 2 September, recalling <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sedan" title="Battle of Sedan">the decisive victory</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> on 2 September 1870.<sup id="cite_ref-Schellack_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schellack-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Before the Empire was founded in 1872, there were calls for a national holiday, and there were three suggestions. No decision was made. Until 1873, the Sedantag was moved to 18 January or the day of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Frankfurt_(1871)" title="Treaty of Frankfurt (1871)">Frankfurt Treaty</a> (10 May 1871). The Sedantag would soon also be celebrated at the universities and in many German cities. It never occurred to them to think about "Empire Parade" or "Emperor's Birthday". Some Culture Ministers of the states, especially in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, decided that the Sedantag would be an official festival in schools. Upon many suggestions, the date of the Emperor's proclamation on 18 January would be established as day of remembrance. Emperor Wilhelm I declined this: "This was also the day of the first Prussian coronation of the king, which should not fall into the shadow of a united German holiday."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weimar_Republic">Weimar Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weimar Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On 31 July 1919, the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Constitution" title="Weimar Constitution">Weimar Constitution</a> would be accepted in its form by the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_National_Assembly" title="Weimar National Assembly">Weimar National Congress</a>. In memorial of this "Hour of birth of democracy", 11 August was created as Constitution Day, because the President of the Empire, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Ebert" title="Friedrich Ebert">Friedrich Ebert</a>, signed the constitution on this day.<sup id="cite_ref-Schellack_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schellack-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Socialism">National Socialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National Socialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Shortly after the Nazis took power in 1933 (the so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Machtergreifung" class="mw-redirect" title="Machtergreifung">Machtergreifung</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a> (1 May) was established as a national holiday in the <i><a href="/wiki/German_Reich" title="German Reich">German Reich</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It was already celebrated as a "<a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">Day of the Labor Movement</a>" since 1890, and also was part of the tradition for the May dance commemorating the <a href="/wiki/Walpurgis_Night" title="Walpurgis Night">Walpurgis Night</a>. Immediately after the establishment of the holiday in 1933, the Nazis banned trade unions on 2 May 1933 and occupied their buildings as offices for the Nazi Movement. On 1 March 1939, Hitler declared <a href="/wiki/November_9_in_German_history" title="November 9 in German history">9 November</a> (the day of the failed <a href="/wiki/Beer_Hall_Putsch" title="Beer Hall Putsch">Beer Hall Putsch</a> in 1923) as the "Memorial Day for the movement" as the national holiday. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_Republic_of_Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Federal Republic of Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>From 1954 to 1990, 17 June was an official holiday in the Federal Republic of Germany to commemorate the <a href="/wiki/East_German_uprising_of_1953" title="East German uprising of 1953">East German uprising of 1953</a>, even with the name "Day of the German Unity".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Since 1963, it was proclaimed by the President of the Federal Republic as "National Day of Memorial of the German People". However by the mid-1960s as hope faded that the two Germanies would ever be re-united, this date became more of a holiday and day of recreation than a day to consider national unity.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the year 1990, the "Day of German Unity" was celebrated twice, on this date and on 3 October.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_Democratic_Republic">German Democratic Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: German Democratic Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In East Germany, the Founding Day in 1949 was celebrated on 7 October as <a href="/wiki/Republic_Day_(East_Germany)" title="Republic Day (East Germany)">Day of the Republic</a>, until the 40th anniversary in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span id="Decision_for_GDR.27s_unity_with_the_Federal_Republic"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Decision_for_GDR's_unity_with_the_Federal_Republic">Decision for GDR's unity with the Federal Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Decision for GDR's unity with the Federal Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The motive for setting the date of 3 October as the possible Day of Unity was decided by the <a href="/wiki/Volkskammer" title="Volkskammer">Volkskammer</a> (GDR Congress) on the impending economical and political collapse of the GDR. The Helsinki Conference was set for 2 October, at which the foreign ministers would be informed of the results of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Final_Settlement_with_Respect_to_Germany" title="Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany">Two-plus-Four talks</a>. </p>
      <p>At the beginning of July, the governments of both German states decided on the schedule: elections in the GDR would be held on 14 October, and a common election for the entire country on <a href="/wiki/1990_German_federal_election" title="1990 German federal election">2 December</a>. </p>
      <p>The decision on the date was finally made on 22 August by the GDR's Minister-President, <a href="/wiki/Lothar_de_Maizi%C3%A8re" title="Lothar de Maizière">Lothar de Maizière</a>, at a special session of the Volkskammer, which began at 9 p.m. After a heated debate, the President of the Volkskammer, <a href="/wiki/Sabine_Bergmann-Pohl" title="Sabine Bergmann-Pohl">Sabine Bergmann-Pohl</a>, announced the results at 2:30&nbsp;a.m. on 23 August: </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>The Volkskammer decides on the accession of the GDR to the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Germany according to Article 23 of the Basic Laws effective as of 3 October 1990. In the matter Nr. 201 there have been 363 votes. There were no invalid votes. 294 deputies have voted 'yes.' (Strong applause from CDU/DA, DSU, FDP, partly SPD and the deputies standing up in their seats.) 62 deputies have voted 'no', and 7 people abstained. This is a historic event. Ladies and Gentlemen, I believe that we have not made an easy decision, but today we have acted within our responsibilities of the voting rights of the citizens of the GDR. I thank everybody that this result was made possible by a consensus across party lines.</p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Gregor_Gysi" title="Gregor Gysi">Gregor Gysi</a>, Chairman of the SED-PDS, was visibly moved and made a personal statement: "Madame President! The Parliament has no more and no less decided on the downfall of the German Democratic Republic as of 3 October 1990".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (Jubilant cheers from the CDU/DA, DSU and SPD.) </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempt_to_change_the_date_of_national_holiday">Attempt to change the date of national holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Attempt to change the date of national holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On 3 November 2004, the Federal Chancellor, <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der" title="Gerhard Schröder">Gerhard Schröder</a>, suggested that the "Day of the German Unity" be celebrated on a Sunday, for economic reasons. Instead of 3 October, the National Reunification should be celebrated on the first Sunday of October. This suggestion received a lot of criticism from many sides, amongst them from Federal President <a href="/wiki/Horst_K%C3%B6hler" title="Horst Köhler">Horst Köhler</a> as well as the President of the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Thierse" title="Wolfgang Thierse">Wolfgang Thierse</a>. The demand worried a part of the population because of discontent for increased working hours would be seen as a provocation and devaluing the national holiday. In addition, fixing the Unity Day on the first Sunday of October would have meant that it would sometimes fall on 7 October, which happens to have been the national day of East Germany; this date would thus have been seen as commemorating the division of Germany rather than the reunification. The idea was dropped after a short but angry debate.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The Day of German Unity is celebrated each year with a ceremonial act and a citizens' festival (<i><i lang="de" title="German-language text">Bürgerfest</i></i>). </p>
      <p>The celebrations are hosted by a major city, usually the state capital, in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German state</a> presiding over the <a href="/wiki/Bundesrat_of_Germany" title="Bundesrat of Germany">Bundesrat</a> in the respective year (a sequence determined by the Königstein Agreement<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>). After <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a> in 2011, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a> was the second non-state capital to host the celebrations in 2015; however, both cities bear a significance for German history (Bonn as former capital of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Parliament" title="Frankfurt Parliament">Frankfurt Parliament</a> of 1848–49). </p>

      <p>In addition, various celebrations are held in the federal capital <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, mainly based on the <a href="/wiki/Stra%C3%9Fe_des_17._Juni" title="Straße des 17. Juni">Straße des 17. Juni</a> and around the <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg_Gate" title="Brandenburg Gate">Brandenburg Gate</a>. State capitals and also other cities often have additional festivities. Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Oktoberfest" title="Oktoberfest">Oktoberfest</a> <a href="/wiki/Beer_festival" title="Beer festival">beer festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, which traditionally runs until the first Sunday in October, now runs until 3 October, if the Sunday in question falls on the first or second day of October. The celebrations in the host city always includes a festival and fireworks show. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zipfelbund:_compass_communities">Zipfelbund: compass communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Unity_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Zipfelbund: compass communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>At the 1999 Day of German Unity celebration in <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden" title="Wiesbaden">Wiesbaden</a> the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zipfelbund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Zipfelbund (page does not exist)">Zipfelbund</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&nbsp;[<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zipfelbund" class="extiw" title="de:Zipfelbund">de</a>]</span> (Compass Confederation) was formalised. The Zipfelbund are the four communities at the cardinal <a href="/wiki/Points_of_the_compass#Compass_point_names" title="Points of the compass">compass points</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> of Germany: North – <a href="/wiki/List,_Schleswig-Holstein" class="mw-redirect" title="List, Schleswig-Holstein">List</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Sylt" title="Sylt">Sylt</a>, West – <a href="/wiki/Selfkant" title="Selfkant">Selfkant</a>, South – <a href="/wiki/Oberstdorf" title="Oberstdorf">Oberstdorf</a> and East – <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6rlitz" title="Görlitz">Görlitz</a>. Together, they always participate in the respective annual celebration to represent the <a href="/wiki/Oder%E2%80%93Neisse_line#Division_of_cities" title="Oder–Neisse line">modern borders</a> of Germany. </p>
