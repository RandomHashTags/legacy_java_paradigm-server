      <p><b>Labor Day</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Federal_holiday_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal holiday in the United States">federal holiday in the United States</a> celebrated on the first Monday in September to honor and recognize the <a href="/wiki/Labor_history_of_the_United_States" title="Labor history of the United States">American labor movement</a> and the works and contributions of <a href="/wiki/Workforce" title="Workforce">laborers</a> to the development and achievements of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is the Monday of the <a href="/wiki/Long_weekend" title="Long weekend">long weekend</a> known as <b>Labor Day Weekend</b>. </p>
      <p>Beginning in the late 19th century, as the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labor_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor movement">labor movements</a> grew, trade unionists proposed that a day be set aside to celebrate labor. "Labor Day" was promoted by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Labor_Union" title="Central Labor Union">Central Labor Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Labor" title="Knights of Labor">Knights of Labor</a>, which organized the first parade in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. In 1887, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> was the first state of the United States to make it an official <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a>. By the time it became an official <a href="/wiki/Federal_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal holiday">federal holiday</a> in 1894, thirty <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states in the United States</a> officially celebrated Labor Day.<sup id="cite_ref-Bridgemens1921_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridgemens1921-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Labour_Day#Canada" title="Labour Day">Canada's Labour Day</a> is also celebrated on the first Monday of September. More than 80 countries celebrate <a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">International Workers' Day</a> on May 1, the ancient European holiday of <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a>. May Day was chosen by the <a href="/wiki/Second_International" title="Second International">Second International</a> of <a href="/wiki/Socialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist">socialist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communist" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist">communist</a> parties to commemorate the <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_affair" title="Haymarket affair">Haymarket affair</a> which occurred in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> on May 4, 1886.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Beginning in the late 19th century, as the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labor_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor movement">labor movements</a> grew, different groups of trade unionists chose a variety of days on which to celebrate labor. In the United States, a September holiday called Labor Day was first proposed in the early 1880s. Alternate stories of the event's origination exist. </p>
      <p>According to one early history of Labor Day, the event originated in connection with a General Assembly of the <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Labor" title="Knights of Labor">Knights of Labor</a> convened in New York City in September 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-CinciTrib_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CinciTrib-7">[7]</a></sup> In connection with this clandestine Knights assembly, a public parade of various labor organizations was held on September 5 under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Labor_Union" title="Central Labor Union">Central Labor Union</a> (CLU) of New York.<sup id="cite_ref-CinciTrib_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CinciTrib-7">[7]</a></sup> Secretary of the CLU <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Maguire_(labor_activist)" title="Matthew Maguire (labor activist)">Matthew Maguire</a> is credited for first proposing that a national Labor Day holiday subsequently be held on the first Monday of each September in the aftermath of this successful public demonstration.<sup id="cite_ref-labor_dept_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-labor_dept-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Descendants of two men with similar last names claim their great-grandfather was the true father of the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Peter-j-mcguire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/84/Peter-j-mcguire.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="265" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="265"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Peter-j-mcguire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>P. J. McGuire, Vice President of the American Federation of Labor, is frequently credited as the father of Labor Day in the United States.
        </div>

      </div> 
      <p>An alternative thesis maintains that the idea of Labor Day was the brainchild of <a href="/wiki/Peter_J._McGuire" title="Peter J. McGuire">Peter J. McGuire</a>, a vice president of the <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Labor" title="American Federation of Labor">American Federation of Labor</a>, who, after a visit to <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> where he saw parades celebrating labor that May,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> had put forward the initial proposal in the spring of 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-Bridgemens1921_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridgemens1921-4">[4]</a></sup> According to McGuire, on May 8, 1882, he made a proposition to the fledgling Central Labor Union in New York City that a day be set aside for a "general holiday for the laboring classes".<sup id="cite_ref-McGuire_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGuire-12">[12]</a></sup> According to McGuire he further recommended that the event should begin with a street parade as a public demonstration of organized labor's solidarity and strength, with the march followed by a picnic, to which participating local unions could sell tickets as a fundraiser.<sup id="cite_ref-McGuire_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGuire-12">[12]</a></sup> According to McGuire he suggested the first Monday in September as an ideal date for such a public celebration, owing to optimum weather and the date's place on the calendar, sitting midway between the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_of_July" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth of July">Fourth of July</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">Thanksgiving</a> public holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-McGuire_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGuire-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Labor Day picnics and other public gatherings frequently featured speeches by prominent labor leaders.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1909, the American Federation of Labor convention designated the Sunday preceding Labor Day as "Labor Sunday", to be dedicated to the spiritual and educational aspects of the labor movement.<sup id="cite_ref-labor_dept_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-labor_dept-8">[8]</a></sup> This secondary date failed to gain significant traction in popular culture, although some churches continue to acknowledge it.<sup id="cite_ref-Mental_Floss_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mental_Floss-14">[14]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_recognition">Legal recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legal recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The popularity of the event spread across the country. In 1887, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> became the first state of the United States to make Labor Day an official <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a>. By 1894, thirty <a href="/wiki/U.S._states" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. states">U.S. states</a> were already officially celebrating Labor Day. In that year, Congress passed a bill recognizing the first Monday of September as Labor Day and making it an official <a href="/wiki/Federal_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Federal holidays in the United States">federal holiday</a>. President Grover Cleveland signed the bill into law on June 28.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bridgemens1921_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridgemens1921-4">[4]</a></sup> The federal law, however, only made it a holiday for federal workers. As late as the 1930s, unions were encouraging workers to strike to make sure they got the day off.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> All U.S. states, the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">District of Columbia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">United States territories</a> have subsequently made Labor Day a statutory holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_Day_vs._May_Day">Labor Day vs. May Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Labor Day vs. May Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>The date of May 1 (an ancient European folk holiday known as <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a>) emerged in 1886 as an alternative holiday for the celebration of labor, later becoming known as <a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">International Workers' Day</a>. The date had its origins at the 1885 convention of the American Federation of Labor, which passed a resolution calling for adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Eight-hour_day" title="Eight-hour day">eight-hour day</a> effective May 1, 1886.<sup id="cite_ref-Foner19_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foner19-18">[18]</a></sup> While negotiation was envisioned for achievement of the shortened work day, use of the strike to enforce this demand was recognized, with May 1 advocated as a date for coordinated strike action.<sup id="cite_ref-Foner19_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foner19-18">[18]</a></sup> The proximity of the date to the bloody <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_affair" title="Haymarket affair">Haymarket affair</a> of May 4, 1886, further accentuated May First's radical reputation.<sup id="cite_ref-Mental_Floss_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mental_Floss-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>There was disagreement among labor unions at this time about when a holiday celebrating workers should be, with some advocating for continued emphasis of the September march-and-picnic date while others sought the designation of the more politically charged date of May 1. Conservative <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> President <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> was one of those concerned that a labor holiday on May 1 would tend to become a commemoration of the <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_affair" title="Haymarket affair">Haymarket affair</a> and would strengthen <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_States" title="History of the socialist movement in the United States">socialist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anarchism_in_the_United_States" title="Anarchism in the United States">anarchist</a> movements that backed the May 1 commemoration around the globe.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1887, he publicly supported the September Labor Day holiday as a less inflammatory alternative,<sup id="cite_ref-progressive_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-progressive-20">[20]</a></sup> formally adopting the date as a United States federal holiday through a law that he signed in 1894.<sup id="cite_ref-labor_dept_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-labor_dept-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Since the mid-1950s, the United States has celebrated <a href="/wiki/Loyalty_Day" title="Loyalty Day">Loyalty Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Law_Day_(United_States)" title="Law Day (United States)">Law Day</a> on May 1. Unlike Labor Day, both are not legal public holidays (in that non-essential government agencies and most businesses do not shut down to celebrate them) and therefore have remained relatively obscure. Loyalty Day is formally celebrated in a few cities, while some <a href="/wiki/Bar_association" title="Bar association">bar associations</a> hold Law Day events to celebrate the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unofficial_end_of_summer">Unofficial end of summer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Unofficial end of summer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Labor Day is called the "unofficial end of summer"<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> because it marks the end of the cultural summer season. Many take their two-week <a href="/wiki/Vacation" title="Vacation">vacations</a> during the two weeks ending Labor Day weekend.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Many fall activities, such as school and sports, begin about this time. </p>
      <p>In the United States, many school districts resume classes around the Labor Day holiday weekend (see <a href="/wiki/First_day_of_school" title="First day of school">First day of school</a>). Some begin the week before, making Labor Day weekend the first three-day weekend of the school calendar, while others return the Tuesday following Labor Day. Many districts across the Midwest are opting to begin school after Labor Day.<sup id="cite_ref-CharlesSenter2008_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CharlesSenter2008-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">amusement park</a> industry has successfully lobbied for legislation requiring most school districts in the state to have their first day of school after Labor Day, in order to give families another weekend to visit amusement parks in the state. The relevant statute has been nicknamed the "<a href="/wiki/Kings_Dominion" title="Kings Dominion">Kings Dominion</a> law" after one such park.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This law was repealed in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_State_Fair" title="Minnesota State Fair">State Fair</a> ends on Labor Day. Under state law, public schools normally do not begin until after the holiday. One reason given for this timing was to allow time for schoolchildren to show <a href="/wiki/4-H" title="4-H">4-H</a> projects at the Fair.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In U.S. sports, Labor Day weekend marks the beginning of many fall sports. <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">National Collegiate Athletic Association</a> (NCAA) teams usually play their first games that weekend,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL) traditionally play their <a href="/wiki/NFL_Kickoff_Game" title="NFL Kickoff Game">kickoff game</a> the Thursday following Labor Day.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Southern_500" title="Southern 500">Southern 500</a> <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> auto race has been held on Labor Day weekend at <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Raceway" title="Darlington Raceway">Darlington Raceway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Darlington,_South_Carolina" title="Darlington, South Carolina">Darlington, South Carolina</a> from 1950 to 2003 and since 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Oil_Raceway_at_Indianapolis" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucas Oil Raceway at Indianapolis">Indianapolis Raceway Park</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hot_Rod_Association" title="National Hot Rod Association">National Hot Rod Association</a> hold their finals of the <a href="/wiki/NHRA_U.S._Nationals" title="NHRA U.S. Nationals">NHRA U.S. Nationals</a> drag race that weekend.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Labor Day is the middle point between weeks one and two of the <a href="/wiki/US_Open_(tennis)" title="US Open (tennis)">U.S. Open Tennis Championships</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Flushing_Meadows" class="mw-redirect" title="Flushing Meadows">Flushing Meadows</a>, New York.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In fashion, Labor Day is (or was) considered the last day when it is acceptable to wear white<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Seersucker" title="Seersucker">seersucker</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>There are numerous events and activities organized in major cities. For example, New York offers the <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day_Carnival" title="Labor Day Carnival">Labor Day Carnival</a>, and fireworks over Coney Island.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In Washington, one popular event is the Labor Day Concert at the U.S. Capitol featuring the National Symphony Orchestra with free attendance.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_Day_sales">Labor Day sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Labor Day sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>To take advantage of large numbers of potential customers with time to shop, Labor Day has become an important weekend for <a href="/wiki/Discounts_and_allowances" title="Discounts and allowances">discounts and allowances</a> by many retailers in the United States, especially for <a href="/wiki/Back_to_school_(marketing)" title="Back to school (marketing)">back-to-school sales</a>. Some retailers claim it is one of the largest sale dates of the year, second only to the Christmas season's <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(shopping)" title="Black Friday (shopping)">Black Friday</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> </p>
