      <p><b>Boxing Day</b> is a holiday celebrated the day after <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>, occurring on the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Days_of_Christmas" title="Twelve Days of Christmas">second day</a> of <a href="/wiki/Christmastide" title="Christmastide">Christmastide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Robb1984_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robb1984-1">[1]</a></sup> Though it originated as a holiday to give gifts to the poor, today Boxing Day is primarily known as a shopping holiday. It originated in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and is celebrated in a number of countries that previously formed part of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. Boxing Day is on 26 December, although the attached <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a> or <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a> may take place either on that day or one or two days later (if necessary to ensure it falls on a weekday). Boxing Day is also concurrent with the Christian holiday <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen%27s_Day" title="Saint Stephen's Day">Saint Stephen's Day</a>. </p>
      <p>In parts of Europe, such as <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Czechia" class="mw-redirect" title="Czechia">Czechia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a><sup id="cite_ref-slovakia-second-christmas-day_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slovakia-second-christmas-day-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, 26 December is celebrated as a second Christmas Day.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_Christmas_Day_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_Christmas_Day-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>There are competing theories for the origins of the term, none of which is definitive.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The European tradition of giving money and other gifts to those in need, or in service positions, has been dated to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, but the exact origin is unknown. It is sometimes believed to be in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Alms_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Alms box">Alms Box</a> placed in the <a href="/wiki/Narthex" title="Narthex">narthex</a> of Christian churches to collect donations to the poor. The tradition may come from a custom in the late Roman/early Christian era wherein alms boxes placed in churches were used to collect special offerings tied to the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen%27s_Day" title="Saint Stephen's Day">Feast of Saint Stephen</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which, in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christian Churches</a>, falls on the same day as Boxing Day, the second day of <a href="/wiki/Christmastide" title="Christmastide">Christmastide</a>. On this day, it is customary in some localities for the alms boxes to be opened and distributed to the poor.<sup id="cite_ref-Robb1984_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robb1984-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FaustSach2002_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FaustSach2002-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> gives the earliest attestations from Britain in the 1830s, defining it as "the first weekday after Christmas day, observed as a holiday on which postmen, errand boys, and servants of various kinds expect to receive a Christmas box".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The term "Christmas box" dates back to the 17th century, and among other things meant: </p> 
      <blockquote>
       <p>A present or gratuity given at Christmas: in Great Britain, usually confined to gratuities given to those who are supposed to have a vague claim upon the donor for services rendered to him as one of the general public by whom they are employed and paid, or as a customer of their legal employer; the undefined theory being that as they have done offices for this person, for which he has not directly paid them, some direct acknowledgement is becoming at Christmas.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>In Britain, it was a custom for tradesmen to collect "Christmas boxes" of money or presents on the first weekday after Christmas as thanks for good service throughout the year.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">Samuel Pepys</a>' diary entry for 19 December 1663.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This custom is linked to an older British tradition where the servants of the wealthy were allowed the next day to visit their families since they would have had to serve their masters on Christmas Day. The employers would give each servant a box to take home containing gifts, bonuses, and sometimes leftover food. Until the late 20th century there continued to be a tradition among many in the UK to give a Christmas gift, usually cash, to vendors, although not on Boxing Day as many would not work on that day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>In South Africa, vendors who normally have little interaction with those they serve are accustomed to knock on the vendors' doors to ask for a "Christmas box", being a small cash donation, in the weeks before or after Christmas. This practice has become controversial and some municipalities have banned their staff from asking for Christmas boxes.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Date">Date</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Date">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Boxing Day is traditionally celebrated on 26 December, the day after Christmas Day, though many people hold—and there is documentary assertion—that it would not fall on a Sunday (Sunday being the day of worship), and consequently Monday 27 December would be Boxing Day.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen%27s_Day" title="Saint Stephen's Day">Saint Stephen's Day</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Religious_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious holiday">religious holiday</a>, also falls on 26 December.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_by_country">Status by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Status by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a></b>, Boxing Day is a public holiday in all jurisdictions except the state of <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>, where a public holiday known as <a href="/wiki/Proclamation_Day_(South_Australia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Proclamation Day (South Australia)">Proclamation Day</a> is celebrated on the first weekday after Christmas Day or the Christmas Day holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Every year a <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">test cricket</a> match is played at the <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Cricket_Ground" title="Melbourne Cricket Ground">Melbourne Cricket Ground</a> starting on Boxing day. </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a></b>, Boxing Day (French: <i lang="fr">le Lendemain de Noël</i>) is a federal statutory holiday. Government offices, banks and postal services are closed. In <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, it is a provincial statutory holiday. </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a></b>, despite the <a href="/wiki/Handover_of_Hong_Kong" title="Handover of Hong Kong">transfer of sovereignty</a> from the UK to China in 1997, Boxing Day is a general holiday as the first weekday after Christmas.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a></b>, when the entire island <a href="/wiki/History_of_Ireland_(1801%E2%80%931922)" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Ireland (1801–1922)">was part of</a> the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bank_Holidays_Act_1871" title="Bank Holidays Act 1871">Bank Holidays Act 1871</a> established the feast day of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen" title="Saint Stephen">Saint Stephen</a> as a non-moveable public holiday on 26 December.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Following <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partition in 1920</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> reverted to the British name, Boxing Day. In East <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">Donegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inishowen" title="Inishowen">Inishowen</a>, the day is also popularly known as Boxing Day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a></b>, Boxing Day is a statutory holiday. On these holidays, people who must work receive 1 1/2 times their salaries and a day in lieu is provided to employees who work.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a></b>, Boxing Day is a public holiday for working people and students. When it falls on a Saturday or Sunday, there is always a holiday on Monday.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a></b>, Boxing Day has been specified as an additional bank holiday since 1974,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Royal_proclamation" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal proclamation">royal proclamation</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Banking_and_Financial_Dealings_Act_1971" class="mw-redirect" title="Banking and Financial Dealings Act 1971">Banking and Financial Dealings Act 1971</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a></b>, Boxing Day was a public holiday for working people and students; when it fell on a Saturday or Sunday, there was a holiday on Monday. However, in recent years this tradition has ceased in Singapore.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a></b>, the <a href="/wiki/Day_of_Goodwill" class="mw-redirect" title="Day of Goodwill">Day of Goodwill</a>, which falls on 26 December, is a public holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a></b>, Boxing Day is a public holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the <b><a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a></b>, 26 December (unless it is a Sunday) has been a <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a> since 1871. When 26 December falls on a Saturday, the associated public holiday is on the following Monday. When 26 December falls on a Sunday, the public holiday is the following Tuesday, Monday being the public holiday associated with <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the British overseas territory of <b><a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a></b>, the costumed <a href="/wiki/Gombey" title="Gombey">Gombey</a> dancers perform throughout the mid-Atlantic island on Boxing Day, a tradition believed to date back to the 18th century when slaves were permitted to gather at Christmastime.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In <b><a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a></b>, Governor <a href="/wiki/William_F._Weld" class="mw-redirect" title="William F. Weld">William F. Weld</a> declared every 26 December is Boxing Day, in response to the efforts of a coalition of British citizens to "transport the English tradition to the United States",<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> but not an employee holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shopping">Shopping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shopping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Boxing_Day_at_the_Toronto_Eaton_Centre_(cropped).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Boxing_Day_at_the_Toronto_Eaton_Centre_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Boxing_Day_at_the_Toronto_Eaton_Centre_%28cropped%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Boxing_Day_at_the_Toronto_Eaton_Centre_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Boxing_Day_at_the_Toronto_Eaton_Centre_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Boxing_Day_at_the_Toronto_Eaton_Centre_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Boxing_Day_at_the_Toronto_Eaton_Centre_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3130" data-file-height="2136"></a>

      <p>In the UK,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Canada,<sup id="cite_ref-ctv.ca_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctv.ca-32">[32]</a></sup> Australia,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Trinidad and Tobago, and New Zealand,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Boxing Day is primarily known as a shopping holiday. Boxing Day sales are common and shops often allow dramatic price reductions. For many merchants, Boxing Day has become the day of the year with the greatest revenue. In the UK in 2009 it was estimated that up to 12 million shoppers appeared at the sales (a rise of almost 20% compared to 2008, although this was also affected by the fact that the <a href="/wiki/VAT" class="mw-redirect" title="VAT">VAT</a> was about to revert to 17.5% from 1 January, following the temporary reduction to 15%).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Many retailers open very early (typically 5&nbsp;am or even earlier) and offer <a href="/wiki/Doorbuster" title="Doorbuster">doorbuster</a> deals and <a href="/wiki/Loss_leader" title="Loss leader">loss leaders</a> to draw people to their stores. It is not uncommon for long queues to form early in the morning of 26 December, hours before the opening of shops holding the big sales, especially at <a href="/wiki/Big-box_store" title="Big-box store">big-box consumer electronics</a> retailers.<sup id="cite_ref-ctv.ca_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctv.ca-32">[32]</a></sup> Many stores have a limited quantity of big draw or deeply discounted items.<sup id="cite_ref-toronto.ctv.ca_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toronto.ctv.ca-36">[36]</a></sup> Because of the shoulder-to-shoulder crowds, many choose to stay at home and avoid the hectic shopping experience. Local media often covers the event, mentioning how early the shoppers began queuing up, and showing video of shoppers queuing and later leaving with their purchased items.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Many retailers have implemented practices aimed at managing large numbers of shoppers. They may limit entrances, restrict the number of patrons in a store at a time, provide tickets to people at the head of the queue to guarantee them a hot ticket item, or canvass queued-up shoppers to inform them of inventory limitations.<sup id="cite_ref-toronto.ctv.ca_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toronto.ctv.ca-36">[36]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In some areas of Canada, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ontario" title="Northern Ontario">Northern Ontario</a>, most retailers are prohibited from opening on Boxing Day, either by provincial law or by municipal <a href="/wiki/Bylaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Bylaw">bylaw</a>, or by informal agreement among major retailers, in order to provide a day of relaxation following Christmas Day. In these areas, sales otherwise scheduled for 26 December are moved to the 27th.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The city council of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">Greater Sudbury</a>, Ontario, which was the largest city in Canada to maintain this restriction as of the early 2010s, formally repealed its store hours bylaw on 9 December 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>While Boxing Day is 26 December, many retailers will run the sales for several days before or after 26 December, often up to New Year's Eve, branding it as "<a href="/wiki/Boxing_Week" title="Boxing Week">Boxing Week</a>". Notably, in <a href="/wiki/Late_2000s_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="Late 2000s recession">the recession of late 2008</a>, a record number of retailers held early promotions due to a weak economy.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2009, many retailers with both online and <a href="/wiki/High_Street" title="High Street">High Street</a> stores launched their online sales on Christmas Eve and their High Street sales on Boxing Day.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The tradition has been compared to the U.S. phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(shopping)" title="Black Friday (shopping)">Black Friday</a> – the Friday following the <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(United_States)" title="Thanksgiving (United States)">Thanksgiving</a> holiday in late-November – which is usually considered the first day of the Christmas shopping season. In the late-2000s, when the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">Canadian</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">U.S. dollar</a> were near parity, Canadian retailers began to hold Black Friday promotions to attract consumers who would otherwise travel across the border to visit U.S. stores. This has lessened the appeal of Boxing Day in Canada somewhat, as it was overtaken by Black Friday in terms of sales by 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the 2010s, many British retailers also began to import the Black Friday tradition, led primarily by retailers with American ownership such as <a href="/wiki/Amazon_(company)" title="Amazon (company)">Amazon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a> (the latter owned by the U.S.-based <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a>). In 2015, British retail sales in November overtook sales in December for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In 2019, a retail analysis firm estimated that there was a 9.8% drop in British store traffic on Boxing Day in comparison to 2018 (the largest year-over-year drop since 2010), citing several factors, such as the weather, the increased prominence of <a href="/wiki/Online_shopping" title="Online shopping">online shopping</a>, uncertainties in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/2019_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2019 United Kingdom general election">general election</a>, and the growing prominence of Black Friday sales.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Boxing Day sales are not a prominent tradition in the United States, although many retailers often begin after-Christmas sales that day. It is typically the earliest starting day after Christmas for people to return unwanted gifts for exchanges or refunds, and to redeem <a href="/wiki/Gift_card" title="Gift card">gift cards</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>

      <p>In the United Kingdom, it is traditional for all top-tier <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> leagues in England, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Premiership" title="Scottish Premiership">Scottish Premiership</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/NIFL_Premiership" title="NIFL Premiership">NIFL Premiership</a> – and the lower ones, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby</a> leagues, to hold a full programme of <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> matches on Boxing Day. Originally, matches on Boxing Day were played against <a href="/wiki/Local_derby" class="mw-redirect" title="Local derby">local rivals</a> to avoid teams and their fans having to travel a long distance to an away game on the day after Christmas Day. Prior to the formation of leagues, a number of traditional <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> fixtures took place on Boxing Day each year, notably <a href="/wiki/Llanelli_RFC" title="Llanelli RFC">Llanelli</a> v <a href="/wiki/London_Welsh_RFC" title="London Welsh RFC">London Welsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Tigers" title="Leicester Tigers">Leicester</a> v <a href="/wiki/Barbarian_F.C." title="Barbarian F.C.">The Barbarians</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>In Italy, Boxing Day football was played for the first time in the <a href="/wiki/2018%E2%80%9319_Serie_A" title="2018–19 Serie A">2018/19 Serie A season</a>. The experiment was successful, with Italian stadiums 69% full on average – more than any other matchday in December 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a>, festive fixtures were a staple of the traditional winter season. Since the transition to a summer season in the 1990s, no formal fixtures are now arranged on Boxing Day but some clubs, such as <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Trinity" title="Wakefield Trinity">Wakefield Trinity</a>, arrange a traditional <a href="/wiki/Local_derby" class="mw-redirect" title="Local derby">local derby</a> friendly fixture instead. </p>
      <p>In Australia, New Zealand and South Africa, <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test cricket</a> matches are played on Boxing Day. For more details see <a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day_Test" title="Boxing Day Test">Boxing Day Test</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Australia, the first day of the <a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day_Test" title="Boxing Day Test">Boxing Day Test</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> and the start of the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_to_Hobart_Yacht_Race" title="Sydney to Hobart Yacht Race">Sydney to Hobart Yacht Race</a> are on Boxing Day.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In horse racing, there is the <a href="/wiki/King_George_VI_Chase" title="King George VI Chase">King George VI Chase</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kempton_Park_Racecourse" title="Kempton Park Racecourse">Kempton Park Racecourse</a> in Surrey, England. It is the second most prestigious <a href="/wiki/National_Hunt_racing" title="National Hunt racing">chase</a> in Britain, after the <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Gold_Cup" title="Cheltenham Gold Cup">Cheltenham Gold Cup</a>. In addition to the prestigious race at Kempton, in Britain, it is usually the day with the highest number of racing meetings of the year, with eight in 2016, in addition to three more in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In Barbados, the final day of horse racing is held on Boxing Day at The Historic Garrison Savannah, a UNESCO world heritage site. This tradition has been going on for decades in this former British colony. </p>
      <p>Boxing Day is one of the main days in the hunting calendar for <a href="/wiki/List_of_foxhound_packs_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of foxhound packs of the United Kingdom">hunts</a> in the UK and US, with most hunts (both mounted foxhound or harrier packs and foot packs of beagles or bassets) holding meets, often in town or village centres.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Several <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> contests are associated with the day. The <a href="/wiki/IIHF_World_U20_Championship" title="IIHF World U20 Championship">IIHF World U20 Championship</a> typically begins on 26 December, while the <a href="/wiki/Spengler_Cup" title="Spengler Cup">Spengler Cup</a> also begins on 23 December in <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">Davos</a>, Switzerland; the Spengler Cup competition includes <a href="/wiki/HC_Davos" title="HC Davos">HC Davos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada_men%27s_national_ice_hockey_team" title="Canada men's national ice hockey team">Team Canada</a>, and other top European Hockey teams. The <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> traditionally had close to a full slate of games (10 were played in 2011<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>), following the league-wide days off given for Christmas Eve and Christmas Day. However, the 2013 collective bargaining agreement (which followed <a href="/wiki/2012-13_NHL_lockout" class="mw-redirect" title="2012-13 NHL lockout">a lock-out</a>) extended the league mandate of Christmas Eve and Christmas Day off to include Boxing Day, except when it falls on a Saturday, in which case the league can choose to make 23 December a league-wide off day instead for that year.<sup id="cite_ref-NHL_CBA_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHL_CBA-54">[54]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In some African <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> nations, particularly Ghana, Uganda, Malawi, Zambia and Tanzania, <a href="/wiki/Professional_boxing" title="Professional boxing">professional boxing</a> contests are held on Boxing Day. This practice has also been followed for decades in Guyana and Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A notable tradition in Sweden is <i><i lang="sv" title="Swedish-language text"><a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen%27s_Day_bandy" title="Saint Stephen's Day bandy">Annandagsbandy</a></i></i>, which formerly marked the start of the <a href="/wiki/Bandy" title="Bandy">bandy</a> season and always draws large crowds. Games traditionally begin at 1:15&nbsp;pm.<sup id="cite_ref-annandagsbandy_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-annandagsbandy-56">[56]</a></sup> </p>
