      <p><b>April Fools' Day</b> or <b>April Fool's Day</b> is an annual custom on <a href="/wiki/April_1" title="April 1">April 1</a> consisting of <a href="/wiki/Practical_joke" title="Practical joke">practical jokes</a> and hoaxes. Jokesters often expose their actions by shouting "April Fool!" at the recipient. Mass media can be involved in these pranks, which may be revealed as such the following day. The day is not a public holiday in any country except <a href="/wiki/Odessa" title="Odessa">Odessa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, where the first of April is an official city holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The custom of setting aside a day for playing harmless pranks upon one's neighbour has been relatively common in the world historically.<sup id="cite_ref-harp_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harp-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Washing_of_the_Lions.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Washing_of_the_Lions.jpg/220px-Washing_of_the_Lions.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/Washing_of_the_Lions.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="201"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Washing_of_the_Lions.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>An 1857 ticket to "Washing the Lions" at the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a> in London. No such event ever took place.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>A disputed association between April 1 and foolishness is in <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canterbury_Tales" title="The Canterbury Tales">The Canterbury Tales</a></i> (1392).<sup id="cite_ref-Time_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-3">[3]</a></sup> In the "<a href="/wiki/Nun%27s_Priest%27s_Tale" class="mw-redirect" title="Nun's Priest's Tale">Nun's Priest's Tale</a>", a vain cock Chauntecleer is tricked by a fox on <i>Syn March bigan thritty dayes and two</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Readers apparently understood this line to mean "32 March", i.e. April 1.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="What source suggests that non-modern-day readers interpreted it in this way? (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, it is not clear that Chaucer was referencing April 1, since the text of the "Nun's Priest's Tale" also states that the story takes place on the day when the sun is <i>in the signe of Taurus had y-runne Twenty degrees and one</i>, which cannot be April 1. Modern scholars believe that there is a copying error in the extant manuscripts and that Chaucer actually wrote, <i>Syn March was gon</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> If so, the passage would have originally meant 32 days after March, i.e. 2 May,<sup id="cite_ref-Hoax_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoax-7">[7]</a></sup> the anniversary of the engagement of King <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II of England</a> to <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Bohemia" title="Anne of Bohemia">Anne of Bohemia</a>, which took place in 1381. </p>
      <p>In 1508, French poet <a href="/wiki/Eloy_d%27Amerval" title="Eloy d'Amerval">Eloy d'Amerval</a> referred to a <i><a href="/wiki/April_Fools#April_fish" class="mw-redirect" title="April Fools">poisson d'avril</a></i> (April fool, literally "April's fish"), possibly the first reference to the celebration in France.<sup id="cite_ref-Amerval_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amerval-8">[8]</a></sup> Some writers suggest that April Fools' originated because in the Middle Ages, New Year's Day was celebrated on March 25 in most European towns,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> with a holiday that in some areas of France, specifically, ended on April 1,<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Santino_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santino-11">[11]</a></sup> and those who celebrated New Year's Eve on January 1 made fun of those who celebrated on other dates by the invention of April Fools' Day.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-10">[10]</a></sup> The use of January 1 as New Year's Day became common in France only in the mid-16th century,<sup id="cite_ref-Hoax_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoax-7">[7]</a></sup> and that date was not adopted officially until 1564, by the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Roussillon" title="Edict of Roussillon">Edict of Roussillon</a>, when France switched from the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>, as called for during the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a> in 1563.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1561, Flemish poet Eduard de Dene wrote of a nobleman who sent his servants on foolish errands on April 1.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoax_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoax-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, the origin of April Fools' Day is often attributed to the Dutch victory in 1572 at <a href="/wiki/Brielle" title="Brielle">Brielle</a>, where the Spanish Duke Álvarez de Toledo was defeated. <i>Op 1 april verloor Alva zijn bril</i> is a Dutch proverb, which can be translated as: "On the first of April, Alva lost his glasses." In this case, "bril" ("glasses" in Dutch) serves as a homonym for Brielle. This theory, however, provides no explanation for the international celebration of April Fools' Day. </p>
      <p>In 1686, <a href="/wiki/John_Aubrey" title="John Aubrey">John Aubrey</a> referred to the celebration as "Fooles holy day", the first British reference.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoax_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoax-7">[7]</a></sup> On April 1, 1698, several people were tricked into going to the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a> to "see the Lions washed".<sup id="cite_ref-Hoax_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoax-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Although no Biblical scholar or historian is known to have mentioned a relationship, some have expressed the belief that the origins of April Fools' Day may go back to the <a href="/wiki/Genesis_flood_narrative" title="Genesis flood narrative">Genesis flood narrative</a>. In a 1908 edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Weekly" title="Harper's Weekly">Harper's Weekly</a></i> cartoonist Bertha R. McDonald wrote: </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>Authorities gravely back with it to the time of <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah's Ark">ark</a>. The London <i><a href="/wiki/Public_Advertiser" title="Public Advertiser">Public Advertiser</a></i> of March 13, 1769, printed: "The mistake of Noah sending the dove out of the ark before the water had abated, on the first day of April, and to perpetuate the memory of this deliverance it was thought proper, whoever forgot so remarkable a circumstance, to punish them by sending them upon some sleeveless errand similar to that ineffectual message upon which the bird was sent by the patriarch".<sup id="cite_ref-harp_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harp-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-standing_customs">Long-standing customs</span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span></h3> 
      <p>In the UK, an April Fool prank is sometimes later revealed by shouting "April fool!" at the recipient, who becomes the "April fool". A study in the 1950s, by folklorists <a href="/wiki/Iona_and_Peter_Opie" title="Iona and Peter Opie">Iona and Peter Opie</a>, found that in the UK, and in countries whose traditions derived from the UK, the joking ceased at midday.<sup id="cite_ref-Opie_1967_245–246_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Opie_1967_245–246-13">[13]</a></sup> This continues to be the practice, with the custom ceasing at noon, after which time it is no longer acceptable to play pranks.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Thus a person playing a prank after midday is considered the "April fool" themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-Independent_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Scotland, April Fools' Day was originally called 'Huntigowk Day',<sup id="cite_ref-Opie_1967_245–246_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Opie_1967_245–246-13">[13]</a></sup> although this name seems to have fallen into disuse over the years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="There is nothing about it falling into disuse in the Opie's book cited for this section (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The name is a corruption of 'Hunt the Gowk', "gowk" being <a href="/wiki/Scots_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Scots (language)">Scots</a> for a cuckoo or a foolish person; alternative terms in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Gaelic</a> would be <i>Là na Gocaireachd</i>, 'gowking day', or <i>Là Ruith na Cuthaige</i>, 'the day of running the cuckoo'. The traditional prank is to ask someone to deliver a sealed message that supposedly requests help of some sort. In fact, the message reads "Dinna laugh, dinna smile. Hunt the gowk another mile." The recipient, upon reading it, will explain they can only help if they first contact another person, and they send the victim to this next person with an identical message, with the same result.<sup id="cite_ref-Opie_1967_245–246_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Opie_1967_245–246-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In England a "fool" is known by a few different names around the country, including "noodle", "gob", "gobby" or "noddy". </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span></h3> 
      <p>In Ireland, it was traditional to entrust the victim with an "important letter" to be given to a named person. That person would read the letter, then ask the victim to take it to someone else, and so on. The letter when opened contained the words "send the fool further".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prima_aprilis_in_Poland">Prima aprilis in Poland</span></h3> 
      <p>In Poland, <i>prima aprilis</i> ("1 April" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>) as a day of pranks is a centuries-long tradition. It is a day when many pranks are played: hoaxes – sometimes very sophisticated – are prepared by people, media (which often cooperate to make the "information" more credible) and even public institutions. Serious activities are usually avoided, and generally every word said on April 1 could be untrue. The conviction for this is so strong that the Polish anti-Turkish alliance with <a href="/wiki/Leopold_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold I, Holy Roman Emperor">Leopold I</a> signed on April 1, 1683, was backdated to March 31.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> However, for some in Poland <i>prima aprilis</i> ends at noon of April 1, and <i>prima aprilis</i> jokes after that hour are considered inappropriate and not classy. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nordic_countries">Nordic countries</span></h3> 
      <p>Danes, Finns, Icelanders, Norwegians and Swedes celebrate April Fools' Day (<i>aprilsnar</i> in Danish; <i>aprillipäivä</i> in Finnish; <i>aprilskämt</i> in Swedish). Most news media outlets will publish exactly one false story on April 1; for newspapers this will typically be a first-page article but not the top headline.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="April_fish">April fish</span></h3> 
      <p>In Italy, France, Belgium and French-speaking areas of Switzerland and Canada, the April 1 tradition is often known as "April fish" (<i>poisson d'avril</i> in French, <i>april vis</i> in Dutch or <i>pesce d'aprile</i> in Italian). Possible pranks include attempting to attach a paper fish to the victim's back without being noticed. This fish feature is prominently present on many late 19th- to early 20th-century French April Fools' Day <a href="/wiki/Postcard" title="Postcard">postcards</a>. Many newspapers also spread a false story on April Fish Day, and a subtle reference to a fish is sometimes given as a clue to the fact that it is an April Fools' prank.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span></h3> 
      <p>April Fools' Day is widely celebrated in Odessa and has the special local name <a href="/wiki/Humorina" title="Humorina">Humorina</a> - in Ukrainian Гуморина (<i>Gumorina</i>). This holiday arose in 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> April Fool prank is revealed by saying "<i>Первое Апреля, никому не верю</i>" (<i>Pervoye Aprelya, nikomu ne veryu</i>) - which means "April the First, I trust nobody" - to the recipient. The festival includes a large parade in the city center, free concerts, street fairs and performances. Festival participants dress up in a variety of costumes and walk around the city fooling around and pranking passersby. One of the traditions on April Fool's day is to dress up the main city monument in funny clothes. Humorina even has its own logo — a cheerful sailor in <a href="/wiki/Lifebuoy" title="Lifebuoy">lifebelt</a> — whose author was the artist, Arkady Tsykun.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> During the festival, special souvenirs bearing the logo are printed and sold everywhere. Since 2010, April Fools' Day celebrations include an International Clown Festival and both celebrated as one. In 2019, the festival was dedicated to the 100th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Odessa_Film_Studio" title="Odessa Film Studio">Odessa Film Studio</a> and all events were held with an emphasis on cinema.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lebanon">Lebanon</span></h3> 
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, an April Fool prank is revealed by saying <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic-language text">كذبة أول نيسان</span>‎ (which means "April First Lie") at the recipient. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish-speaking_countries">Spanish-speaking countries</span></h3> 
      <p>In many Spanish-speaking countries (and the Philippines), "Día de los Santos Inocentes" (<a href="/wiki/Massacre_of_the_Innocents#Feast_day" title="Massacre of the Innocents">Holy Innocents Day</a>) is a festivity which is very similar to April Fools' Day, but it is celebrated in late December (27, 28 or 29 depending on the location).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span></h3> 
      <p>Israel has adopted the custom of pranking on April Fools' Day.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>As well as people playing pranks on one another on April Fools' Day, elaborate pranks have appeared on radio and TV stations, newspapers, and websites, and have been performed by large corporations. In one famous prank in 1957, the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> broadcast a film in their <i><a href="/wiki/Panorama_(TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Panorama (TV series)">Panorama</a></i> current affairs series purporting to show Swiss farmers picking freshly-grown spaghetti, in what they called the <a href="/wiki/Spaghetti-tree_hoax" title="Spaghetti-tree hoax">Swiss Spaghetti Harvest</a>. The BBC was soon flooded with requests to purchase a spaghetti plant, forcing them to declare the film a hoax on the news the next day.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>With the advent of the Internet and readily available global news services, April Fools' pranks can catch and embarrass a wider audience than ever before.<sup id="cite_ref-Moran_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moran-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="December_28">December 28</span></h3> 
      <p>December 28, the equivalent day in Spain, <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">Hispanic America</a> and the Philippines, is also the Christian day of celebration of the "<a href="/wiki/D%C3%ADa_de_los_Inocentes" class="mw-redirect" title="Día de los Inocentes">Day of the Holy Innocents</a>.” The Christian celebration is a religious holiday in its own right, but the tradition of pranks is not, though the latter is observed yearly. In some regions of Hispanic America after a prank is played, the cry is made, "<i>Inocente palomita que te dejaste engañar</i>" ("You innocent little dove that let yourself be fooled!"; not to be confused with another meaning of <i>palomita</i>, which means “popcorn” in some dialects). </p>
      <p>In Argentina, the prankster says, “<i>¡Que la inocencia te valga!”</i> which roughly translates as advice to not be as gullible as the victim of the prank. In Spain, it is common to say just “<i>¡Inocente!”</i> (which in Spanish can mean "innocent” or "gullible").<sup id="cite_ref-Vilaweb_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vilaweb-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Colombia, the term is used as "Pásala por Inocentes", which roughly means: "Let it go; today it's Innocent's Day." </p>
      <p>In Belgium, this day is also known as the "Day of the innocent children" or "Day of the stupid children". It used to be a day where parents, grandparents, and teachers would fool the children in some way. But the celebration of this day has died out in favor of April Fools' Day. </p>
      <p>Nevertheless, on the Spanish island of <a href="/wiki/Menorca" title="Menorca">Menorca</a>, <i>Dia d'enganyar</i> ("Fooling day") is celebrated on April 1 because Menorca was a British possession during part of the 18th century. In Brazil, the "Dia da mentira" ("Day of the lie") is also celebrated on April 1<sup id="cite_ref-Vilaweb_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vilaweb-25">[25]</a></sup> due to the Portuguese influence. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_day_of_a_new_month">First day of a new month</span></h3> 
      <p>In many English-speaking countries, mainly Britain, Ireland, Australia, New Zealand and South Africa, it is a custom to say "pinch and a punch for the first of the month" or an alternative, typically by children. The victim might respond with "a flick and a kick for being so quick", and the attacker might reply with "a punch in the eye for being so sly".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Another custom in Britain and North America is to say "<a href="/wiki/Rabbit_rabbit_rabbit" title="Rabbit rabbit rabbit">rabbit rabbit</a>" upon waking on the first day of a month, for good luck.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span></h2> 
      <p>The practice of April Fool pranks and hoaxes is controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-Independent_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-yahoo1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo1-28">[28]</a></sup> The mixed opinions of critics are epitomized in the reception to the 1957 BBC "<a href="/wiki/Spaghetti-tree_hoax" title="Spaghetti-tree hoax">Spaghetti-tree hoax</a>", in reference to which, newspapers were split over whether it was "a great joke or a terrible hoax on the public".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The positive view is that April Fools' can be good for one's health because it encourages "jokes, hoaxes...pranks, [and] belly laughs", and brings all the benefits of laughter including stress relief and reducing strain on the heart.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> There are many "best of" April Fools' Day lists that are compiled in order to showcase the best examples of how the day is celebrated.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-31">[31]</a></sup> Various April Fools' campaigns have been praised for their innovation, creativity, writing, and general effort.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The negative view describes April Fools' hoaxes as "creepy and manipulative", "rude" and "a little bit nasty", as well as based on <i><a href="/wiki/Schadenfreude" title="Schadenfreude">schadenfreude</a></i> and deceit.<sup id="cite_ref-yahoo1_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo1-28">[28]</a></sup> When genuine news or a genuine important order or warning is issued on April Fools' Day, there is risk that it will be misinterpreted as a joke and ignored – for example, when <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>, known to play elaborate April Fools' Day hoaxes, announced the launch of <a href="/wiki/Gmail" title="Gmail">Gmail</a> with 1-<a href="/wiki/Gigabyte" title="Gigabyte">gigabyte</a> inboxes in 2004, an era when competing <a href="/wiki/Webmail" title="Webmail">webmail</a> services offered 4-<a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">megabytes</a> or less, many dismissed it as a joke outright.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On the other hand, sometimes stories intended as jokes are taken seriously. Either way, there can be adverse effects, such as confusion,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> misinformation, waste of resources (especially when the hoax concerns people in danger) and even legal or commercial consequences.<sup id="cite_ref-Hasham_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hasham-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bieber_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bieber-37">[37]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, the police warned ahead of the April Fools' in 2021 that posting or sharing fake news online could lead to maximum of five years imprisonment.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Other examples of genuine news on April 1 mistaken as a hoax include: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>1 April 1946: Warnings about the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Island_earthquake" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleutian Island earthquake">Aleutian Island earthquake</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> that killed 165 people in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.</li> 
       <li>1 April 2004: <a href="/wiki/Gmail" title="Gmail">Gmail</a> is announced to the public by <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>1 April 2005: News that the comedian <a href="/wiki/Mitch_Hedberg" title="Mitch Hedberg">Mitch Hedberg</a> had died on 29 March 2005.</li> 
       <li>1 April 2005: Announcement about <i><a href="/wiki/Powerpuff_Girls_Z" title="Powerpuff Girls Z">Powerpuff Girls Z</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Aniplex" title="Aniplex">Aniplex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toei_Animation" title="Toei Animation">Toei Animation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>1 April 2009: Announcement that the long running soap opera <i><a href="/wiki/Guiding_Light" title="Guiding Light">Guiding Light</a></i> was being cancelled.</li> 
       <li>1 April 2011: <a href="/wiki/Isaiah_Thomas_(basketball)" title="Isaiah Thomas (basketball)">Isaiah Thomas</a> declared for the <a href="/wiki/NBA_draft" title="NBA draft">NBA draft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span></h2> 
      <p>Books, films, telemovies and television episodes have used April Fools' Day as their title or inspiration. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Bryce_Courtenay" title="Bryce Courtenay">Bryce Courtenay</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/April_Fool%27s_Day_(novel)" title="April Fool's Day (novel)">April Fool's Day</a></i> (1993), whose title refers to the day Courtenay's son died. The 1990s <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcom</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Roseanne" title="Roseanne">Roseanne</a></i> featured an episode titled "April Fools' Day". This turned out to be intentionally misleading, as the episode was about <a href="/wiki/Tax_Day" title="Tax Day">Tax Day</a> in the United States on April 15&nbsp;– the last day to submit the previous year's tax information. Although Tax Day is usually April 15 as depicted in the episode, it can be moved back a few days if that day is on a weekend or a holiday in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> or some states, or due to natural disasters when it can occur as late as July 15. </p>
