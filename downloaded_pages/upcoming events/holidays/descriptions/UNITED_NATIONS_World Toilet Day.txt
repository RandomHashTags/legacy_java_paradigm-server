      <p><b>World Toilet Day</b> (<b>WTD</b>) is an official <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_secular_observances#November" title="List of minor secular observances">international observance day</a> on 19 November to inspire action to tackle the global <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:5_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-2">[2]</a></sup> Worldwide, 4.2&nbsp;billion people live without "<a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">safely managed sanitation</a>" and around 673&nbsp;million people practice <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JMP2017_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>74</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal 6</a> aims to "Ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> In particular, target 6.2 is to "End open defecation and provide access to sanitation and hygiene". When the Sustainable Development Goals Report 2020 was published, <a href="/wiki/Secretary-General_of_the_United_Nations" title="Secretary-General of the United Nations">United Nations Secretary-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Guterres" title="António Guterres">António Guterres</a> said, "Today, Sustainable Development Goal 6 is badly off track" and it "is hindering progress on the 2030 Agenda, the realization of human rights and the achievement of peace and security around the world".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>World Toilet Day exists to inform, engage and inspire people to take action toward achieving this goal. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">UN General Assembly</a> declared World Toilet Day an official UN day in 2013, after Singapore had tabled the resolution (its first resolution before the UN's General Assembly of 193 member states).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:12_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-7">[7]</a></sup> Prior to that, World Toilet Day had been established unofficially by the <a href="/wiki/World_Toilet_Organization" title="World Toilet Organization">World Toilet Organization</a> (a Singapore-based <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGO</a>) in 2001. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/UN-Water" title="UN-Water">UN-Water</a> is the official convener of World Toilet Day. UN-Water maintains the official World Toilet Day website and chooses a special theme for each year. In 2020 the theme was "<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_sanitation" title="Sustainable sanitation">Sustainable sanitation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-:13_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2019 the theme was 'Leaving no one behind', which is the central theme of the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a>. Themes in previous years include <a href="/wiki/Nature-based_solutions" title="Nature-based solutions">nature-based solutions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a>, toilets and jobs, and toilets and <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:9_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-11">[11]</a></sup> World Toilet Day is marked by communications campaigns and other activities. Events are planned by <a href="/wiki/UN_entity" class="mw-redirect" title="UN entity">UN entities</a>, international organizations, local <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">civil society organizations</a> and volunteers to <a href="/wiki/Raising_awareness" class="mw-redirect" title="Raising awareness">raise awareness</a> and inspire action. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">Toilets</a> are important because access to a safe functioning toilet has a positive impact on <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dignity" title="Dignity">human dignity</a>, and personal safety, especially for women.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-12">[12]</a></sup> Sanitation systems that do not safely treat excreta allow the spread of disease.<sup id="cite_ref-JMP2017_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-3">[3]</a></sup> Serious <a href="/wiki/Soil-transmitted_helminthiasis" title="Soil-transmitted helminthiasis">soil-transmitted diseases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">waterborne diseases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Typhoid_fever" title="Typhoid fever">typhoid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schistosomiasis" title="Schistosomiasis">schistosomiasis</a> can result. </p>

      <p>In 2013, <a href="/wiki/UN-Water" title="UN-Water">UN-Water</a> and the "Thematic Priority Area (TPA) on Drinking Water and Basic Sanitation" received the mandate to oversee World Toilet Day each year. This mandate is described in the United Nations Resolution A/67/L.75.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In consultation with the UN-Water World Toilet Day Task Force, made up of UN-Water member organizations, UN-Water selects the theme based on that year's <a href="/wiki/UN_World_Water_Development_Report" title="UN World Water Development Report">World Water Development Report</a> and develops content for World Toilet Day communications campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>UN-Water manages the World Toilet Day website which promotes key issues and stories, provides communications and campaigns resources, and announces events and opportunities to participate.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The overall World Toilet Day campaign mobilizes civil society, think tanks, <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-governmental organizations">non-governmental organizations</a>, academics, corporations and the general public to participate in the associated social media and communications campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-16">[16]</a></sup> Ultimately, the aim is to encourage organizations and governments to plan activities and action on <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> issues to make progress on <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal 6</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Starting in 2012, World Toilet Day themes were selected for each year and form the basis of the related communications campaigns.       <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Toilet_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_media_impacts">Social media impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Toilet_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Social media impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The World Toilet Day campaign and related publications reach millions of people through <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a>, dedicated websites and other channels.<sup id="cite_ref-UNWater2016_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNWater2016-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>21</span></sup> Over 100 events in 40 countries were registered on the World Toilet Day website in both 2016 and in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-UNWater2016_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNWater2016-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UNWater2017_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNWater2017-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>17</span></sup> In 2017, the hashtag #WorldToiletDay had a maximum potential reach of over 750&nbsp;million people on social media.<sup id="cite_ref-UNWater2017_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNWater2017-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>17</span></sup> In 2018, the maximum potential reach increased by 15%,compared to 2017; the online activity and authors also increased by 12% and 22% compared to 2017, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>32</span></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Toilet_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>On 19 November 2001, the <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Toilet_Organization" title="World Toilet Organization">World Toilet Organization</a> (WTO) was founded by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Sim" title="Jack Sim">Jack Sim</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Philanthropy" title="Philanthropy">philanthropist</a> from <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>. He subsequently declared 19 November as World Toilet Day.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The name "World Toilet Day" and not "World Sanitation Day" was chosen for ease of public messaging, even though toilets are only the first stage of sanitation systems.<sup id="cite_ref-tilley_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tilley-44">[44]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>World Toilet Day events and public awareness campaigns increase public awareness of the broader sanitation systems that include <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater treatment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fecal_sludge_management" title="Fecal sludge management">fecal sludge management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">municipal solid waste</a> management, <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> management, <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Handwashing" class="mw-redirect" title="Handwashing">handwashing</a>. Also, the UN <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a> call for more than just toilets. <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Goal 6</a> calls for adequate sanitation, which includes the whole system for assuring that waste is safely processed.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The WTO began pushing for global recognition for World Toilet Day and, in 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Sanitation_Alliance" title="Sustainable Sanitation Alliance">Sustainable Sanitation Alliance</a> (SuSanA) began to actively support World Toilet Day, too.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Their efforts to raise attention for the sanitation crisis were bolstered in 2010 when the <a href="/wiki/Human_right_to_water_and_sanitation" title="Human right to water and sanitation">human right to water and sanitation</a> was officially declared a <a href="/wiki/Human_right" class="mw-redirect" title="Human right">human right</a> by the UN.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2013, a joint initiative between the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Singapore" title="Government of Singapore">Government of Singapore</a> and the World Toilet Organization led to Singapore's first ever UN resolution, named "Sanitation for All".<sup id="cite_ref-:12_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-7">[7]</a></sup> The resolution calls for collective action to end the world's sanitation crisis. World Toilet Day was declared an official UN day in 2013. That resolution was adopted by 122 countries at the 67th session of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">UN General Assembly</a> in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a> (SDGs) replaced the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> (MDGs) in 2016. On World Toilet Day on 19 November 2015, United Nations Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a> urged broad action to renew efforts to provide access to adequate sanitation for all. He reminded everyone of the "Call to Action on Sanitation" which was launched in 2013, and the aim to end <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a> by 2025.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-47">[47]</a></sup> He also said: "By many accounts, sanitation is the most-missed target of the Millennium Development Goals."<sup id="cite_ref-:3_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-47">[47]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The UN Deputy Secretary-General, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Eliasson" title="Jan Eliasson">Jan Eliasson</a>, was honored on World Toilet Day in 2016 in New York for his deep commitment to breaking the sanitation <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UNWater2016_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNWater2016-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup> For example, he had delivered a video message to attendees of a <a href="/wiki/WaterAid" title="WaterAid">WaterAid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a> joint event in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> on World Toilet Day 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In 2016, UN-Water supported "A Toast for Toilets" in New York with the United Nations Mission of Singapore.<sup id="cite_ref-UNWater2016_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNWater2016-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Toilet_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">Sanitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">Open defecation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">Toilet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/WASH" title="WASH">WASH</a>

      <p>Worldwide, 4.2&nbsp;billion people live without "<a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">safely managed sanitation</a>" and around 673&nbsp;million people worldwide practise <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JMP2017_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>74</span></sup> Having to defecate in the open is especially difficult for women and girls. Women tend to resort to the cover of darkness to give them more privacy, but then risk being attacked when alone at night.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-49">[49]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>It has been estimated that 58% of all cases of <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a> worldwide in 2015 were caused by unsafe water, poor sanitation and poor hygiene practices, such as inadequate <a href="/wiki/Handwashing" class="mw-redirect" title="Handwashing">handwashing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> This resulted in half a million children under the age of five dying from diarrhea per year.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-51">[51]</a></sup> Providing sanitation has been estimated to lower the odds of children suffering diarrhea by 7–17%, and under-five mortality by 5–20%.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Human_right_to_water_and_sanitation" title="Human right to water and sanitation">Human Right to Water and Sanitation</a> was recognized as a <a href="/wiki/Human_right" class="mw-redirect" title="Human right">human right</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations (UN) General Assembly</a> on 28 July 2010. Lack of access to <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> (toilets) has an impact on <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dignity" title="Dignity">dignity</a>, and safety.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-12">[12]</a></sup> The spread of many diseases (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Soil-transmitted_helminthiasis" title="Soil-transmitted helminthiasis">soil-transmitted helminthiasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schistosomiasis" title="Schistosomiasis">schistosomiasis</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Stunted_growth" title="Stunted growth">stunted growth</a> in children is directly related to people being exposed to <a href="/wiki/Human_feces" title="Human feces">human feces</a> because toilets are either not available or not used. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goal_6" title="Sustainable Development Goal 6">Sustainable Development Goal 6</a> aims to provide sanitation for all.<sup id="cite_ref-JMP2017_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
