      <p><b>Loving Day</b> is an annual celebration held on June 12, the <a href="/wiki/Anniversary" title="Anniversary">anniversary</a> of the 1967 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> decision <i><a href="/wiki/Loving_v._Virginia" title="Loving v. Virginia">Loving v. Virginia</a></i> which struck down all <a href="/wiki/Anti-miscegenation_laws" title="Anti-miscegenation laws">anti-miscegenation laws</a> <a href="/wiki/Interracial_marriage_in_the_United_States" title="Interracial marriage in the United States">remaining in sixteen</a> U.S. states.<sup id="cite_ref-LDWP_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LDWP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LDVV_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LDVV-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LDHP_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LDHP-3">[3]</a></sup> In the United States, anti-miscegenation laws were <a href="/wiki/State_law" title="State law">U.S. state laws</a> banning <a href="/wiki/Interracial_marriage" title="Interracial marriage">interracial marriage</a>, mainly forbidding marriage between two different races, until the <a href="/wiki/Warren_Court" title="Warren Court">Warren Court</a> ruled unanimously in 1967 that these state laws were unconstitutional.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Chief Justice <a href="/wiki/Earl_Warren" title="Earl Warren">Earl Warren</a> wrote in the court <a href="/wiki/Majority_opinion" title="Majority opinion">majority opinion</a> that "the freedom to marry, or not marry, a person of another race resides with the individual, and cannot be infringed by the State."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Loving Day is not an officially recognized holiday by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._government" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. government">U.S. government</a>, despite attempts to make it so.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Loving Day is the biggest multiracial celebration in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-time_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loving_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Loving Day originated with the landmark U.S. Supreme Court case <i><a href="/wiki/Loving_v._Virginia" title="Loving v. Virginia">Loving v. Virginia</a></i>, which invalidated <a href="/wiki/Anti-miscegenation_laws_in_the_United_States" title="Anti-miscegenation laws in the United States">laws prohibiting interracial marriage</a>. The case was brought by <a href="/wiki/Mildred_and_Richard_Loving" title="Mildred and Richard Loving">Mildred Loving</a> (<span title="Name at birth"><a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9e" class="mw-redirect" title="Née">née</a></span><span class="nowrap">&nbsp;</span>Jeter), a woman classified as "colored" under Virginia's <a href="/wiki/Racial_Integrity_Act_of_1924" title="Racial Integrity Act of 1924">Racial Integrity Act of 1924</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mildred_and_Richard_Loving" title="Mildred and Richard Loving">Richard Loving</a>, a white man, who first met when she was 11 and he was 17. He was a family friend and over the years they courted. After she became pregnant, they married in Washington, D.C., in 1958, when she was 18.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Reportedly, Mildred did not realize that interracial marriage was illegal, and they were arrested a few weeks after they returned to their hometown north of <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond, Virginia</a>. They pleaded guilty to charges of "cohabiting as man and wife, against the peace and dignity of the Commonwealth", and avoided jail time by leaving Virginia and agreeing not to return to the state for 25 years.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Lovings moved to Washington, D.C., and began legal action by writing to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Attorney_General" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Attorney General">U.S. Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Kennedy referred the case to the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Warren_Court" title="Warren Court">Warren Court</a> unanimously <a href="/wiki/Loving_v._Virginia" title="Loving v. Virginia">ruled</a> in favor of the young couple, they returned to Virginia, where they lived with their three children. In 1975, Richard Loving died in a car accident. Mildred Loving died May 5, 2008, at the age of 68.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-12">[12]</a></sup> Each June 12, the anniversary of the ruling, Loving Day events around the country mark the advances of mixed-race couples and Mixed Race people.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loving_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Many organizations sponsor annual parties across the country, with Lovingday.org providing an online legal map, courtroom history of anti-miscegenation laws, as well as offering testimonials by and resources for interracial couples. Inspired by <a href="/wiki/Juneteenth" title="Juneteenth">Juneteenth</a> (which commemorates the end of slavery in the state of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>), Loving Day seeks both to commemorate and celebrate the Supreme Court's 1967 ruling, keeping its importance fresh in the minds of a generation which has grown up with interracial relationships being legal, as well as explore issues facing couples currently in interracial relationships. The Loving Day website features information, including court transcripts of the <i>Loving v. Virginia</i> case and of other court cases in which the legality of anti-miscegenation laws was challenged. To celebrate the holiday, people are encouraged to hold parties in which the case and its modern-day legacy are discussed, in smaller settings such as living rooms, backyards, etc., as well as in larger gatherings. Ken Tanabe is credited with forming the idea for Loving Day. He created the idea in 2004 for his senior thesis at <a href="/wiki/Parsons_the_New_School_of_Design" class="mw-redirect" title="Parsons the New School of Design">Parsons the New School of Design</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-time_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time-8">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loving_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <ul>
       <li><i><a href="/wiki/Loving_(2016_film)" title="Loving (2016 film)">Loving</a></i>, a 2016 film starring <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Negga" title="Ruth Negga">Ruth Negga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joel_Edgerton" title="Joel Edgerton">Joel Edgerton</a> as Mildred and Richard Loving, directed by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Nichols" title="Jeff Nichols">Jeff Nichols</a>. The film was selected to compete for the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d'Or">Palme d'Or</a> at the <a href="/wiki/2016_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2016 Cannes Film Festival">2016 Cannes Film Festival</a>, and was nominated for numerous awards, including a <a href="/wiki/74th_Golden_Globe_Awards" title="74th Golden Globe Awards">Golden Globe</a> nomination for <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Actor_%E2%80%93_Motion_Picture_Drama" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Actor – Motion Picture Drama">Best Actor</a> for Edgerton and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Academy Award</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Actress_%E2%80%93_Motion_Picture_Drama" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Actress – Motion Picture Drama">Golden Globe</a> nominations for <a href="/wiki/Negga" class="mw-redirect" title="Negga">Ruth Negga</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>A documentary, <i>The Loving Story</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which features rare contemporaneous photographs of the couple and details the history of the case and references Loving Day, premiered on <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> on Valentine's Day 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><i>New York Times</i> best-selling author <a href="/wiki/Heidi_W._Durrow" title="Heidi W. Durrow">Heidi W. Durrow</a><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> co-organized the second-largest celebration of Loving Day in the country with Fanshen Cox DiGiovanni, during the annual Mixed Roots Film and Literary Festival.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>The annual flagship Loving Day Celebration in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> was featured in the <a href="/wiki/British_Broadcasting_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="British Broadcasting Corporation">BBC</a> documentary series <i>Our World</i> in 2007, on the 40th anniversary of the <i>Loving</i> decision.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Coverage of the annual celebration has also been featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Magazine">Time Magazine</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Voice_of_America" title="Voice of America">Voice of America</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" class="mw-redirect" title="National Public Radio">National Public Radio</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and on <i><a href="/wiki/PBS_NewsHour" title="PBS NewsHour">PBS NewsHour</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Several cities and municipalities have issued proclamations officially recognizing Loving Day as a holiday, including <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caroline_County,_Virginia" title="Caroline County, Virginia">Caroline County, Virginia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> where the Lovings hailed from.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
      </ul>
