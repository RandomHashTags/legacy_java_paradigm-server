      <p><b>Holy Tuesday</b>, <b>Fig Tuesday</b><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, or <b>Great and Holy Tuesday</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">Μεγάλη Τρίτη</span>, <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language romanization">Megali Triti</i>) (lit. 'Great Third (Day)', i.e., <b>Great Tuesday</b>), is a day of <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">Holy Week</a>, which precedes <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Christianity">Western Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holy_Tuesday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Western Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Lent_calendar.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lent_calendar.svg/400px-Lent_calendar.svg.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lent_calendar.svg/600px-Lent_calendar.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Lent_calendar.svg/800px-Lent_calendar.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="256"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Lent_calendar.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div><i>Holy Tuesday</i> and other named days and day ranges around Lent and Easter in Western Christianity, with the fasting days of Lent numbered
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, the readings for the Novus Ordo are <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt1049.htm#1">Isaiah 49:1–6</a>; <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt2671.htm#1">Psalm 71:1–6</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt2671.htm#15">71:15</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt2671.htm#17">71:17</a>; <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/1_Corinthians#1:18" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/1 Corinthians">1 Corinthians 1:18–31</a>; and <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/John#13:21" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/John">John 13:21–33</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/John#13:36" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/John">13:36–38</a>. In the older form of the Mass known as the <a href="/wiki/Tridentine_Mass" title="Tridentine Mass">Tridentine Mass</a> the readings are taken from <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Jeremiah#11:18" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Jeremiah">Jeremiah 11:18–20</a> and the Gospel according to St. <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Mark#14:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Mark">Mark 14:1–72</a>; <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Mark#15:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Mark">15:1–46</a>. In the 1955 Holy Week Reform, the first 31 verses of the 14th chapter of St. Mark were removed. Those 31 verses are retained in the Roman Catholic Churches which celebrate the pre-1955 Holy Week.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Revised_Common_Lectionary" title="Revised Common Lectionary">Revised Common Lectionary</a>, which is used by the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist Church">Methodist Churches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran Church">Lutheran Churches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Catholic_Church" title="Old Catholic Church">Old Catholic Churches</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Reformed_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformed Church">Reformed Churches</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Holmes2012_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes2012-2">[2]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Scripture_lesson" class="mw-redirect" title="Scripture lesson">Scripture lessons</a> are <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Isaiah%2049:1–7&amp;version=nrsv">Isaiah 49:1–7</a> (First Reading), <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Psalms%2071:1–14&amp;version=nrsv">Psalm 71:1–14</a> (Psalm), <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=1%20Corinthians%201:18–31&amp;version=nrsv">1 Corinthians 1:18–31</a> (Second Reading), and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://bible.oremus.org/?passage=John%2012:20–36&amp;version=nrsv">John 12:20–36</a> (Gospel Reading).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In traditional Methodist usage, <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Worship_for_Church_and_Home_(1965)" title="Book of Worship for Church and Home (1965)">The Book of Worship for Church and Home</a></i> (1965) provides the following Collect for Holy Tuesday:<sup id="cite_ref-BOW1964_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOW1964-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>Almighty, everlasting God, grant us so perfectly to follow the passion of our Lord, that we may obtain the help and pardon of his all-sufficient grace; through him who lives and reigns with you, in the unity of the Holy Spirit, world without end. Amen.<sup id="cite_ref-BOW1964_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOW1964-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Christianity">Eastern Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holy_Tuesday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Eastern Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:RossGospWiseFoolVirginsF4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/RossGospWiseFoolVirginsF4.jpg/250px-RossGospWiseFoolVirginsF4.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b1/RossGospWiseFoolVirginsF4.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="335" data-file-height="213"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:RossGospWiseFoolVirginsF4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>The Wise and Foolish Virgins (from the <a href="/wiki/Rossano_Gospels" title="Rossano Gospels">Rossano Gospels</a>).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian Apostolic church">Armenian Apostolic church</a> and those <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a> that follow the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a>, this day is referred to as <i>Great and Holy Tuesday</i>, or <i>Great Tuesday</i>. On this day the Church commemorates the <a href="/wiki/Parable_of_the_Ten_Virgins" title="Parable of the Ten Virgins">Parable of the Ten Virgins</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Matthew#25:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Matthew">Matthew 25:1–13</a>), which forms one of the themes of the first three days of Holy Week, with its teaching about vigilance, and Christ as the Bridegroom. The bridal chamber is used as a symbol not only of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Sepulchre" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Sepulchre">Tomb of Christ</a>, but also of the blessed state of the saved on the <a href="/wiki/Day_of_Judgement" class="mw-redirect" title="Day of Judgement">Day of Judgement</a>. The theme of the <a href="/wiki/Parable_of_the_Talents" class="mw-redirect" title="Parable of the Talents">Parable of the Talents</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Matthew#25:14" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Matthew">Matthew 25:14–30</a>) is also developed in the hymns of this day.<sup id="cite_ref-Ware_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ware-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The day begins liturgically with <a href="/wiki/Vespers" title="Vespers">Vespers</a> on the afternoon of <a href="/wiki/Great_Monday" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Monday">Great Monday</a>, repeating some of the same <a href="/wiki/Sticheron" title="Sticheron">stichera</a> (hymns) from the night before. At <a href="/wiki/Great_Compline" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Compline">Great Compline</a> a <i><a href="/wiki/Triodion" title="Triodion">triode</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Canon_(hymnography)" title="Canon (hymnography)">Canon</a> composed of three <a href="/wiki/Canticle" title="Canticle">Odes</a>), written by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_of_Crete" title="Andrew of Crete">St. Andrew of Crete</a> is chanted. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Matins" title="Matins">Matins</a> service for Monday through Wednesday of Holy Week is known as the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.serfes.org/spiritual/april2009.htm">Bridegroom Service</a> or Bridegroom Prayer, because of their theme of Christ as the Bridegroom of the Church, a theme movingly expressed in the <a href="/wiki/Troparion" title="Troparion">troparion</a> that is solemnly chanted during them. On these days, an <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">icon</a> of "Christ the Bridegroom" is placed on an <a href="/wiki/Analogion" title="Analogion">analogion</a> in the center of the <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">temple</a>, portraying Jesus wearing the purple <a href="/wiki/Robe" title="Robe">robe</a> of mockery and crowned with a <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_thorns" title="Crown of thorns">crown of thorns</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Instruments_of_the_Passion" class="mw-redirect" title="Instruments of the Passion">Instruments of the Passion</a>). These Matins services are often chanted the evening before so more of the faithful may attend. The <a href="/wiki/Matins_Gospel" title="Matins Gospel">Matins Gospel</a> read on this day is from the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Matthew#22:15" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Matthew">22:15–23:39</a>. </p>
      <p>The four Gospels are divided up and read in their entirety at the <a href="/wiki/Little_Hours" title="Little Hours">Little Hours</a> (<a href="/wiki/Third_Hour" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Hour">Third Hour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sext" title="Sext">Sixth Hour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ninth_Hour" class="mw-redirect" title="Ninth Hour">Ninth Hour</a>) during the course of the first three days of Holy Week, halting at <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/John#13:31" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/John">John 13:31</a>. There are various methods of dividing the Gospels, but the following is the most common practice:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Holy and Great Tuesday
       </dt>
      </dl> 
      <ul>
       <li>Third Hour—The second half of Mark</li> 
       <li>Sixth Hour—The first third of Luke</li> 
       <li>Ninth Hour—The second third of Luke</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>At the Sixth Hour there is a reading from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezekiel" title="Book of Ezekiel">Book of Ezekiel</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt1201.htm#21">1:21–2:1</a> </p>
      <p>At the <a href="/wiki/Liturgy_of_the_Presanctified_Gifts" title="Liturgy of the Presanctified Gifts">Liturgy of the Presanctified Gifts</a>, some of the <a href="/wiki/Stichera" class="mw-redirect" title="Stichera">stichera</a> from the previous night's Matins (<a href="/wiki/Lauds" title="Lauds">Lauds</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aposticha" title="Aposticha">Aposticha</a>) are repeated at <i>Lord, I have cried</i> (see <a href="/wiki/Vespers#Eastern.2FOriental_Catholic_.26_Orthodox" title="Vespers">Vespers</a>). There are two Old Testament readings: <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Exodus#2:5" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Exodus">Exodus 2:5–10</a> and <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Job#1:13" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Job">Job 1:13–22</a>. There is no <a href="/wiki/Epistle" title="Epistle">Epistle</a> reading, but there is a <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospel</a> reading from <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Matthew#24:36" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Matthew">Matthew 24:36–26:2</a>. </p>
