      <p><b>Pi Day</b> is an <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_secular_observances#March" title="List of minor secular observances">annual celebration</a> of the mathematical constant <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span> (pi)</a>. Pi Day is observed on March 14 (3/14 in the <i>month/day</i> <a href="/wiki/Date_format" class="mw-redirect" title="Date format">format</a>) since 3, 1, and 4 are the first three <a href="/wiki/Significant_figures" title="Significant figures">significant digits</a> of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was founded in 1988 by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Shaw_(Pi)" title="Larry Shaw (Pi)">Larry Shaw</a>, an employee of the <a href="/wiki/Exploratorium" title="Exploratorium">Exploratorium</a>. Celebrations often involve eating <a href="/wiki/Pie" title="Pie">pie</a> or holding <a href="/wiki/Piphilology" title="Piphilology">pi recitation</a> competitions. In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> supported the designation of Pi Day.<sup id="cite_ref-HORRes_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HORRes-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>'s 40th General Conference designated Pi Day as the <a href="/wiki/International_Day_of_Mathematics" title="International Day of Mathematics">International Day of Mathematics</a> in November 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Alternative dates for the holiday include July 22 (22/7, an <a href="/wiki/Approximations_of_%CF%80" title="Approximations of π">approximation of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span></a>) and June 28 (6.28, an approximation of 2<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span> or <a href="/wiki/Tau_(mathematical_constant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tau (mathematical constant)">tau</a>). </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pi_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In 1988, the earliest known official or large-scale celebration of Pi Day was organized by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Shaw_(Pi)" title="Larry Shaw (Pi)">Larry Shaw</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Exploratorium" title="Exploratorium">San Francisco Exploratorium</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> where Shaw worked as a <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ABC_–_Science_–_10_Mar_2011_–_infinite_appeal_of_pi_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC_–_Science_–_10_Mar_2011_–_infinite_appeal_of_pi-8">[8]</a></sup> with staff and public marching around one of its circular spaces, then consuming fruit pies.<sup id="cite_ref-fresnobee_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fresnobee-9">[9]</a></sup> The Exploratorium continues to hold Pi Day celebrations.<sup id="cite_ref-exploratorium_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-exploratorium-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On March 12, 2009, the U.S. House of Representatives passed a <a href="/wiki/Non-binding_resolution" title="Non-binding resolution">non-binding resolution</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/H._RES._224_Supporting_the_designation_of_Pi_Day,_and_for_other_purposes" class="extiw" title="wikisource:H. RES. 224 Supporting the designation of Pi Day, and for other purposes">111 H. Res. 224</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-HORRes_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HORRes-4">[4]</a></sup> recognizing March 14, 2009, as National Pi Day.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> For Pi Day 2010, Google presented a <a href="/wiki/Google_Doodle" title="Google Doodle">Google Doodle</a> celebrating the holiday, with the word Google laid over images of circles and pi symbols;<sup id="cite_ref-GoogleDoodle_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoogleDoodle-12">[12]</a></sup> and for the 30th anniversary in 2018, it was a <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Ansel" title="Dominique Ansel">Dominique Ansel</a> pie with the <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">circumference</a> divided by its <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The entire month of March 2014 (3/14) was observed by some as "Pi Month".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In the year 2015, March 14 was celebrated as "Super Pi Day".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It had special significance, as the date is written as 3/14/15 in month/day/year format. At 9:26:53, the date and time together represented the first 10 digits of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and later that second Pi Instant represented all of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span>'s digits.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observance">Observance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pi_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Pi Day has been observed in many ways, including eating <a href="/wiki/Pie" title="Pie">pie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pieing" title="Pieing">throwing pies</a> and discussing the significance of the number <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span>, due to a <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">pun</a> based on the words "pi" and "pie" being <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophones</a> in English (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><small> </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>), and the coincidental circular shape of many pies.<sup id="cite_ref-landau_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-landau-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Also, some schools hold competitions as to which student can <a href="/wiki/Piphilology" title="Piphilology">recall pi to the highest number of decimal places</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2020, some events were canceled or modified due to <a href="/wiki/COVID-19" title="COVID-19">COVID-19</a> concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> has often mailed its <a href="/wiki/College_admissions_in_the_United_States#Application_process" title="College admissions in the United States">application decision letters</a> to prospective students for delivery on Pi Day.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Starting in 2012, MIT has announced it will post those decisions (privately) online on Pi Day at exactly 6:28&nbsp;pm, which they have called "Tau Time", to honor the rival numbers pi and <a href="/wiki/Tau_(2%CF%80)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tau (2π)">tau</a> equally.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2015, the regular decisions were put online at 9:26&nbsp;am, following that year's "pi minute",<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and in 2020, regular decisions are set to be released at 1:59 pm, making the first six digits of pi.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>June 28 is "Two Pi Day", also known as "Tau Day". 2<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span>, also known by the Greek letter <a href="/wiki/Tau" title="Tau">tau</a> (τ) is a common multiple in mathematical formulae. Some have argued that τ is the more fundamental constant, and that Tau Day should be celebrated instead.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Celebrations of this date jokingly suggest eating "twice the pie".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton, New Jersey</a>, hosts numerous events in a combined celebration of Pi Day and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>'s birthday, which is also March 14.<sup id="cite_ref-princeton_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-princeton-34">[34]</a></sup> Einstein lived in Princeton for more than twenty years while working at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Advanced_Study" title="Institute for Advanced Study">Institute for Advanced Study</a>. In addition to pie eating and recitation contests, there is an annual Einstein look-alike contest.<sup id="cite_ref-princeton_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-princeton-34">[34]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_dates">Alternative dates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pi_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alternative dates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Pi Day is most frequently observed on March 14, but related celebrations have been held on alternative dates. </p>
      <p><b>Pi Approximation Day</b> is observed on July 22 (22/7 in the <i>day/month</i> format), since the <a href="/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraction (mathematics)">fraction</a> <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1020198016">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style><span class="frac" role="math"><span class="num">22</span>⁄<span class="den">7</span></span> is a common <a href="/wiki/Approximations_of_%CF%80" title="Approximations of π">approximation of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">π</span></a>, which is accurate to two decimal places and dates from <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VerizonTodayInHistory_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VerizonTodayInHistory-35">[35]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><b>Two Pi Day</b>, also known as <b>Tau Day</b> for the mathematical constant <a href="/wiki/Tau_(mathematical_constant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tau (mathematical constant)">Tau</a>, which is approximated as 6.28, is observed on June 28 (6/28 in the <i>month/day</i> format).<sup id="cite_ref-lightyears_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lightyears-36">[36]</a></sup> </p>
