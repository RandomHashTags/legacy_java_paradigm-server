      <p><b>Loyalty Day</b> is observed on May 1 in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It is a day set aside "for the reaffirmation of loyalty to the United States and for the recognition of the heritage of American freedom". The date, May 1, was set in order to counter <a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">International Worker's Day</a>, and was conceived in the height of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Scare" title="Red Scare">Second Red Scare</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loyalty_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The holiday was first observed in 1921, during the <a href="/wiki/First_Red_Scare" title="First Red Scare">First Red Scare</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was originally called "Americanization Day,"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and it was intended to replace the May&nbsp;1 ("<a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a>") celebration of the <a href="/wiki/International_Workers%27_Day" title="International Workers' Day">International Workers' Day</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which commemorates the 1886 <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_affair" title="Haymarket affair">Haymarket affair</a> in Chicago.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>During the <a href="/wiki/Red_Scare" title="Red Scare">Second Red Scare</a>, it was recognized by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> on April 27, 1955,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and made an official reoccurring holiday on July 18, 1958 (Public Law 85-529).<sup id="cite_ref-US_Code_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Code-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Eisenhower_July_18,_1955_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eisenhower_July_18,_1955-7">[7]</a></sup> President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> proclaimed May 1, 1955, the first observance of Loyalty Day.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1958, Eisenhower urged Congress to move <a href="/wiki/Child_Health_Day" title="Child Health Day">Child Health Day</a> to the First Monday in October, to avoid conflicting with Loyalty Day.<sup id="cite_ref-Eisenhower_July_18,_1955_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eisenhower_July_18,_1955-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Loyalty Day has been recognized with an official proclamation every year by every president since its inception as a legal holiday in 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Loyalty Day events, mostly parades, are still held in: </p>

       <li><a href="/wiki/Standish,_Michigan" title="Standish, Michigan">Standish, Michigan</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statutory_definition">Statutory definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loyalty_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Statutory definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Loyalty Day is defined as follows in <a href="/wiki/Title_36_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 36 of the United States Code">36&nbsp;U.S.C.</a>&nbsp;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/36/115">§&nbsp;115</a>: </p> 
      <dl>
       <dd>
        (a) Designation.— May 1 is Loyalty Day.
       </dd> 
       <dd>
        (b) Purpose.— Loyalty Day is a special day for the reaffirmation of loyalty to the United States and for the recognition of the heritage of American freedom.
       </dd> 
       <dd>
        (c) Proclamation.— The President is requested to issue a proclamation— 
        <dl>
         <dd>
          (1) calling on United States Government officials to display the flag of the United States on all Government buildings on Loyalty Day; and
         </dd> 
         <dd>
          (2) inviting the people of the United States to observe Loyalty Day with appropriate ceremonies in schools and other suitable places.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
