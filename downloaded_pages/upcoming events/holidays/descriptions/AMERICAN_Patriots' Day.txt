      <p><b>Patriots' Day</b> (so punctuated in several U.S. states, but <b>Patriot's Day</b> in Maine)<sup id="cite_ref-KenneJourn_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KenneJourn-2">[2]</a></sup> is an annual event, formalized as several <a href="/wiki/State_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="State holiday">state holidays</a>, commemorating the <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">Battles of Lexington and Concord</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Menotomy" title="Battle of Menotomy">Battle of Menotomy</a>, the first battles of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patriots%27_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In 1894, the Lexington Historical Society petitioned the Massachusetts State Legislature to proclaim April 19 as "Lexington Day". Concord countered with "Concord Day". Governor <a href="/wiki/Frederic_T._Greenhalge" title="Frederic T. Greenhalge">Frederic T. Greenhalge</a> opted for a compromise: Patriots' Day. However the biggest battle fought on this day was in the town of Menotomy, now <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Massachusetts" title="Arlington, Massachusetts">Arlington, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Menotomy was on the Concord Road between Lexington and Concord and Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Battle_of_Menotomy_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Battle_of_Menotomy-4">[4]</a></sup> While the fighting was going on in Lexington and Concord, 5,100 militia men arrived in Menotomy from <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_County,_Massachusetts" title="Middlesex County, Massachusetts">Middlesex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Essex_County,_Massachusetts" title="Essex County, Massachusetts">Essex</a> Counties.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Battle_of_Menotomy_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Battle_of_Menotomy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These men took up positions along the road the British troops would take on their retreat to Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Battle_of_Menotomy_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Battle_of_Menotomy-4">[4]</a></sup> They placed themselves in and around houses, stone walls, fields and barns. The bloodiest fighting of the first day of the American Revolution took place inside a single house, <a href="/wiki/Jason_Russell_House" title="Jason Russell House">the Jason Russell House</a>, in Menotomy.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Battle_of_Menotomy_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Battle_of_Menotomy-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Eleven militia men died in this house fighting British troops trained in bayonet fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Patriots' Day was first proclaimed in Massachusetts in 1894 by Gov. Greenhalge, replacing <a href="/wiki/Fast_Day" title="Fast Day">Fast Day</a> as a public holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-KenneJourn_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KenneJourn-2">[2]</a></sup> The idea was introduced to the Governor by the statesman from Lowell, Isaac Henry Paige. It was established on April 19, commemorating the date of the <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">Battles of Lexington and Concord</a> and the larger <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Menotomy" title="Battle of Menotomy">Battle of Menotomy</a> in 1775, and consolidating the longstanding municipal observances of Lexington Day and Concord Day. It also marked the first bloodshed of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_riot_of_1861" title="Baltimore riot of 1861">Baltimore riot of 1861</a>, during which four members of the Massachusetts militia were slain and 36 injured. In Menotomy, now Arlington,<sup id="cite_ref-The_Battle_of_Menotomy_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Battle_of_Menotomy-4">[4]</a></sup> 25 militia men died and 40 British soldiers were killed.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The dual commemoration, Greenhalge explained, celebrated "the anniversary of the birth of liberty and union". It is likely that the battles that took place in Menotomy are not as well known as the smaller battles in Lexington and Concord because the town has had several names since that day in 1775. In 1938, with the generation that had fought in the Civil War largely off the voter rolls, the Massachusetts legislature passed a bill establishing the holiday "in commemoration of the opening events of the War of the Revolution".<sup id="cite_ref-Atlantic_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Maine followed Massachusetts in 1907 and replaced its Fast Day with Patriot's Day.<sup id="cite_ref-KenneJourn_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KenneJourn-2">[2]</a></sup> On June 10, 2017, Governor <a href="/wiki/Dannel_Malloy" title="Dannel Malloy">Dannel Malloy</a> signed a bill establishing Patriots' Day as a statewide unpaid holiday in Connecticut.<sup id="cite_ref-Sampson_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sampson-1">[1]</a></sup> On April 16, 2018 Connecticut became the 4th state to recognize the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-Rep-Am_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rep-Am-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patriots%27_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The holiday was originally celebrated on April 19, the actual anniversary of the battles (fought in 1775). Since 1969, it has been observed on the third Monday in April in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> (which until the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Compromise" title="Missouri Compromise">Missouri Compromise</a> of 1820 was part of Massachusetts). The Monday holiday creates a three-day <a href="/wiki/Long_weekend" title="Long weekend">long weekend</a>. It is also the first day of a vacation week for public schools in both states and a school holiday for many local colleges and universities, both public and private.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>The day is a <a href="/wiki/Public_school_(government_funded)" class="mw-redirect" title="Public school (government funded)">public school</a> observance day in <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Florida law also encourages people to celebrate it, though it is not treated as a public holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Connecticut began observance in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-Sampson_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sampson-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Observances and re-enactments of the battles occur annually at <a href="/wiki/Lexington_Green" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexington Green">Lexington Green</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Massachusetts" title="Lexington, Massachusetts">Lexington, Massachusetts</a> (around 6:00&nbsp;am) and the <a href="/wiki/Old_North_Bridge" title="Old North Bridge">Old North Bridge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Concord,_Massachusetts" title="Concord, Massachusetts">Concord, Massachusetts</a> (around 9:00&nbsp;am) and in Arlington, Massachusetts on the Sunday before Patriot's Day. Tours are available of the <a href="/wiki/Jason_Russell_House" title="Jason Russell House">Jason Russell House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Massachusetts" title="Arlington, Massachusetts">Arlington, Massachusetts</a> on Sunday and Monday.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On Monday morning, mounted re-enactors with state police escorts retrace the <a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere#midnight_ride" title="Paul Revere">Midnight Rides</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere" title="Paul Revere">Paul Revere</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Dawes" title="William Dawes">William Dawes</a>, calling out warnings the whole way.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>The most significant celebration of Patriots' Day is the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon" title="Boston Marathon">Boston Marathon</a>, which has been run every Patriots' Day since April 19, 1897 (except in 2020 and 2021) to mark the then-recently established holiday, with the race linking the <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athenian</a> and American struggles for liberty.<sup id="cite_ref-Atlantic_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-9">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sporting_events">Sporting events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patriots%27_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sporting events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon" title="Boston Marathon">Boston Marathon</a> has been run on Patriots' Day every year since its inception in 1897, even during the <a href="/wiki/World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="World War">World War</a> years, except in 2020 and 2021. <sup id="cite_ref-Atlantic_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-9">[9]</a></sup> Therefore, sometimes the holiday is referred to as "Marathon Monday".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, due to the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="COVID-19 in the United States">COVID-19 pandemic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/2020_Boston_Marathon" class="mw-redirect" title="2020 Boston Marathon">2020 Boston Marathon</a> was rescheduled to September in March<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and ultimately canceled in May.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Because of the lingering effects of the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="COVID-19 in the United States">COVID-19 pandemic</a> in 2021, the 2021 running of the Boston Marathon was moved to October 11. <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> have been scheduled to play at home in <a href="/wiki/Fenway_Park" title="Fenway Park">Fenway Park</a> on Patriots' Day every year since 1959. The game was postponed due to weather in 1959, 1961, 1965, 1967, 1984, and 2018. It was canceled in 1995 due to <a href="/wiki/1994%E2%80%9395_Major_League_Baseball_strike" title="1994–95 Major League Baseball strike">the baseball strike</a>, and again in 2020 due to <a href="/wiki/COVID-19" title="COVID-19">COVID-19</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The game was played in <a href="/wiki/2013_Boston_Red_Sox_season" title="2013 Boston Red Sox season">2013 </a> despite the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon_bombing" title="Boston Marathon bombing">Boston Marathon bombing</a> because it had finished before the bombs went off.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> From 1968 to 2006 the games started early, in the morning, around 11:00&nbsp;am. The early start to these games usually resulted in the games' ending just as the runners headed through <a href="/wiki/Kenmore_Square" title="Kenmore Square">Kenmore Square</a>. However, since 2007 the marathon has started between 9:30&nbsp;am and 10:00&nbsp;am, causing the racers to pass through Kenmore closer to the middle of the Red Sox game.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>
