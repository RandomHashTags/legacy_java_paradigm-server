      <p><b>World Malaria Day</b> (<b>WMD</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/International_observance" class="mw-redirect" title="International observance">international observance</a> commemorated every year on 25 April and recognizes global efforts to control <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>. Globally, 3.3 billion people in 106 countries are at risk of malaria.<sup id="cite_ref-WHO2010_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2010-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2012, malaria caused an estimated 627,000 deaths, mostly among African children.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Asia, Latin America, and to a lesser extent the Middle East and parts of Europe are also affected. </p>
      <p>World Malaria Day sprung out of the efforts taking place across the African continent to commemorate <b>Africa Malaria Day</b>. WMD is one of 11 official <a href="/wiki/Global_health" title="Global health">global public health</a> campaigns currently marked by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO), along with <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Day" title="World Health Day">World Health Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Blood_Donor_Day" title="World Blood Donor Day">World Blood Donor Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Immunization_Week" title="World Immunization Week">World Immunization Week</a>, World Antimicrobial Awareness Week, <a href="/wiki/World_Patient_Safety_Day" title="World Patient Safety Day">World Patient Safety Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Tuberculosis_Day" title="World Tuberculosis Day">World Tuberculosis Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Chagas_Disease_Day" title="World Chagas Disease Day">World Chagas Disease Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_No_Tobacco_Day" title="World No Tobacco Day">World No Tobacco Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Hepatitis_Day" title="World Hepatitis Day">World Hepatitis Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_AIDS_Day" title="World AIDS Day">World AIDS Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>According to the most recent World Malaria Report, the global tally of malaria reached 429,000 malaria deaths and 212 million new cases in 2015. The rate of new malaria cases fell by 21 per cent globally between 2010 and 2015, and malaria death rates fell by 29 per cent in the same period. In sub-Saharan Africa, case incidence and death rates fell by 21 per cent and 31 per cent, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Malaria_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p><b>World Malaria Day</b> was established in May 2007 by the 60th session of the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Assembly" title="World Health Assembly">World Health Assembly</a>, WHO's decision-making body. The day was established to provide "education and understanding of malaria" and spread information on "year-long intensified implementation of national malaria-control strategies, including community-based activities for malaria prevention and treatment in endemic areas."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Prior to the establishment of WMD, <b>Africa Malaria Day</b><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> was held on April 25. Africa Malaria Day began in 2001, one year after the historic <i>Abuja Declaration</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> was signed by 44 malaria-endemic countries at the African Summit on Malaria.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>World Malaria Day allows for corporations (such as <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>), multinational organizations (such as <a href="/wiki/Malaria_No_More" title="Malaria No More">Malaria No More</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>) and grassroots organizations (such as <a href="/wiki/Mosquitoes_Suck_Tour" title="Mosquitoes Suck Tour">Mosquitoes Suck Tour</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>) globally to work together to bring awareness to malaria and advocate for <a href="/wiki/Health_policy" title="Health policy">policy</a> changes.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Each World Malaria Day focuses on a specific theme. Current and past themes include the following: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>World Malaria Day 2019: "Zero malaria starts with me"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>World Malaria Day 2018: "Ready to beat malaria"</li> 
       <li>World Malaria Day 2017: "LETS Close The Gap"</li> 
       <li>World Malaria Day 2016: "End Malaria For Good"<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>World Malaria Day 2013-2014-2015: "Invest in the future: defeat malaria"<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Events">Global Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Malaria_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Global Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Malaria_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Leading to World Malaria Day 2014, the European Vaccine Initiative announced sixteen new projects for the acceleration of malaria vaccine development.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The projects were to be undertaken by an international consortium involving partners from the public and private sectors from Europe, USA and Africa. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Malaria_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>For World Malaria Day 2017, efforts across India included a series of measures to control the spread of this vector-borne disease. The coastal city of Mangaluru identified open water resources to target for eliminating mosquitos that spread malaria.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Events marking World Malaria Day 2014 in Nigeria included a demonstration of <a href="/wiki/Anti-malarial_bed_net" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-malarial bed net">anti-malarial bed nets</a>, testing and distribution of anti-malarial drugs, seminars on progress in combating and controlling malaria, and the inclusion of African footballers in the campaign to combat malaria.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2016, the Federal Ministry of Health has pledged to end Malaria.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The U.S. Ambassador to Nigeria, James F. Entwistle, has pledged continued support and noted the possibility of ending Malaria in Nigeria.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>April 25 was designated as <i>Malaria Awareness Day</i> in 2007 by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, who called on Americans to join in on the goal to eradicate malaria on the African continent.<sup id="cite_ref-GB_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB-27">[27]</a></sup> President Bush described it as a day when "we focus our attention on all who suffer from this terrible disease -- especially the millions on the continent of Africa. We remember the millions more who died from this entirely preventable and treatable disease." President Bush shared the White House's strategic plan against malaria, which included endeavors to distribute bed nets with the New York-based nonprofit group <a href="/wiki/Malaria_No_More" title="Malaria No More">Malaria No More</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WH_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WH-28">[28]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Other former U.S. Presidents are involved in anti-malaria efforts. Former President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Foundation" title="Clinton Foundation">Clinton Foundation</a> includes an anti-malaria component that, according to director <a href="/wiki/Inder_Singh_(philanthropist)" title="Inder Singh (philanthropist)">Inder Singh</a> has distributed anti-malaria drugs to millions in Africa and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-MIT_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Many prominent companies, organizations, and celebrities have declared initiatives to join the fight against malaria to mark this day. These include a $3 million "challenge grant" announced by <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> to match donations dollar by dollar to <a href="/wiki/Malaria_No_More" title="Malaria No More">Malaria No More</a>, as part of the "<a href="/wiki/Idol_Gives_Back" title="Idol Gives Back">Idol Gives Back</a>" episodes of <a href="/wiki/American_Idol" title="American Idol"><i>American Idol</i></a>, which was aired on Malaria Awareness Day. <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a> Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Don_Garber" title="Don Garber">Don Garber</a> also announced that the league would promote malaria awareness and bed net fundraising promotions in the month of April leading up to Malaria Awareness Day. Actress <a href="/wiki/Ashley_Judd" title="Ashley Judd">Ashley Judd</a> announced the April launch of a new initiative called "5 &amp; Alive", which will focus on the devastating effects of malaria on children under 5. The <a href="/wiki/Boys_%26_Girls_Clubs_of_America" title="Boys &amp; Girls Clubs of America">Boys &amp; Girls Clubs of America</a> announced their own national campaign called "Malaria Prevention: Deadly Disease. Simple Solution", which will partner with Malaria No More to approach all 4.7 Million Club members worldwide to ask for $10 bed net donations.<sup id="cite_ref-MNM_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MNM-30">[30]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The movement is not without its critics, such as the African economist <a href="/wiki/Dambisa_Moyo" title="Dambisa Moyo">Dambisa Moyo</a>, who warns that the short term benefits of aid such as mosquito nets can have long term detrimental effects to the sustainability of African economies. When the markets are flooded with foreign nets then this puts African entrepreneurs out of business.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
