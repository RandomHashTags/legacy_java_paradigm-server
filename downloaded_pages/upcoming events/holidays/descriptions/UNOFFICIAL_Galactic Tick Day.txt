      <p><b>Galactic Tick Day</b> is an awareness and education day that celebrates the movement of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> galaxy.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The day occurs at a regular interval of 1.7361 years (or 633.7 days),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which is called a galactic tick. The interval is derived from one <a href="/wiki/Centi" class="mw-redirect" title="Centi">centi</a>-<a href="/wiki/Arcsecond" class="mw-redirect" title="Arcsecond">arcsecond</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Galactic_year" title="Galactic year">galactic year</a>, which is the Solar System's roughly 225-million-year trip around the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">Galactic Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> One galactic tick is only about 0.00000077 percent (1/[360&nbsp;×&nbsp;60&nbsp;×&nbsp;60&nbsp;×&nbsp;100]) of a full galactic year.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The first Galactic Tick Day took place one galactic tick after <a href="/wiki/Hans_Lippershey" class="mw-redirect" title="Hans Lippershey">Hans Lippershey</a> filed the <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> for the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_telescope" title="History of the telescope">telescope</a> on October 2, 1608.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The first observance of the holiday was on September 29, 2016, the 235th Galactic Tick Day.<sup id="cite_ref-firstobservance_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-firstobservance-9">[9]</a></sup> Below is a list of further observances: </p>
