      <p><b>Washington's Birthday</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Federal_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Federal holidays in the United States">federal holiday in the United States</a> celebrated on the third Monday of February in honor of <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">president of the United States</a>, who was born on February 22, 1732. The <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Monday_Holiday_Act" title="Uniform Monday Holiday Act">Uniform Monday Holiday Act</a> of 1971 moved this holiday to the third Monday, which can occur from February 15 to 21, inclusive.<sup id="cite_ref-2manyCh1efs_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2manyCh1efs-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Colloquially, the day is also now widely known as <b>Presidents' Day</b> (though the placement of the apostrophe, if any, varies) and is often an occasion to remember all the presidents.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPostFeb14_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPostFeb14-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The day is a <a href="/wiki/State_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="State holiday">state holiday</a> in most states, with official names including Washington's Birthday, Presidents' Day, President's Day, and Washington's and Lincoln's Birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-WaPostFeb14_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPostFeb14-2">[2]</a></sup> The various states use 14 different names. Depending upon the specific law, the state holiday may officially celebrate Washington alone, Washington and <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Lincoln</a>, or some other combination of U.S. presidents (such as Washington and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>, who was born in April).<sup id="cite_ref-WaPostFeb14_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaPostFeb14-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Although <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Birthday" title="Lincoln's Birthday">Lincoln's Birthday</a>, February 12, was never a federal holiday, nearly half of the state governments have officially renamed their Washington's Birthday observances as "Presidents' Day", "Washington and Lincoln Day", or other such designations. (In <a href="/wiki/Historical_rankings_of_presidents_of_the_United_States" title="Historical rankings of presidents of the United States">historical rankings of presidents of the United States</a> Lincoln and Washington are frequently the top two presidents.) </p>
      <p>In the following states and <a href="/wiki/Possessions_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Possessions of the United States">possessions of the United States</a>, Washington's Birthday is an official state holiday and known as:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><i>Using "president" in the official title</i> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>"Presidents' Day" in Hawaii,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> New Mexico, North Dakota,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Oklahoma, Pennsylvania,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Puerto Rico, South Dakota, Texas, Vermont, and Washington<sup id="cite_ref-nlrodgers_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nlrodgers-7">[7]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>"President's Day" in Alaska, Idaho, Maryland, Nebraska, New Hampshire, Tennessee, West Virginia, and Wyoming</li> 
       <li>"Presidents Day" in Nevada, New Jersey, and Oregon</li> 
       <li>"Washington's Birthday/President's Day" in Maine</li> 
       <li>"Lincoln/Washington/Presidents' Day" in Arizona</li>
      </ul> 
      <p><i>Using "Washington" alone</i> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>George Washington Day in Virginia</li> 
       <li>Washington's Birthday in Illinois, Iowa,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Massachusetts,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Michigan,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Louisiana and New York<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p><i>Using both "Washington" and "Lincoln"</i> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Lincoln's and Washington's Birthday in Montana</li> 
       <li>Washington–Lincoln Day in Colorado,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Ohio<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Washington and Lincoln Day in Utah<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Washington's and Lincoln's Birthday in Minnesota<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p><i>Using "Washington" and another person</i> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>George Washington/Thomas Jefferson Birthday in Alabama<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>George Washington's Birthday and <a href="/wiki/Daisy_Bates_(civil_rights_activist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Daisy Bates (civil rights activist)">Daisy Bates</a> Day in Arkansas</li>
      </ul> 
      <p><i>Unnamed</i> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>"The third Monday in February" in California; Lincoln's birthday, February 12th, explicitly "known as" <i>Lincoln Day</i>, is a separate holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Several states honor presidents with official state holidays that do not fall on the third Monday of February. In <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, the state officially celebrates "Washington's Birthday" on the same day as the federal holiday. State law also directs the governor to issue an annual "Presidents Day" proclamation on May 29 (<a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>'s birthday), honoring the presidents with Massachusetts roots: Kennedy, <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Quincy_Adams" title="John Quincy Adams">John Quincy Adams</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">Calvin Coolidge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, while Washington's Birthday is a federal holiday, <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Birthday" title="Lincoln's Birthday">Abraham Lincoln's birthday</a> is still a state holiday, falling on February 12 regardless of the day of the week. </p>
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, Presidents' Day, at least as a state-government paid holiday, is observed on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(shopping)" title="Black Friday (shopping)">Friday following</a> <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">Thanksgiving</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> although the legal public holiday remains the third Monday in February.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, Washington's Birthday is not a state-government paid holiday, although until 2018 it was officially observed on <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Similarly, in <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, Washington's Birthday is observed on Christmas Eve, or the day preceding the weekend if Christmas falls on Saturday or Sunday, while Lincoln's Birthday is the day after Thanksgiving.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Washington%27s_Birthday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Centennial_celebration_of_Washington%27s_birth_day,_Philadelphia,_February_1832.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Centennial_celebration_of_Washington%27s_birth_day%2C_Philadelphia%2C_February_1832.png/220px-Centennial_celebration_of_Washington%27s_birth_day%2C_Philadelphia%2C_February_1832.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="498" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Centennial_celebration_of_Washington%27s_birth_day%2C_Philadelphia%2C_February_1832.png/330px-Centennial_celebration_of_Washington%27s_birth_day%2C_Philadelphia%2C_February_1832.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Centennial_celebration_of_Washington%27s_birth_day%2C_Philadelphia%2C_February_1832.png/440px-Centennial_celebration_of_Washington%27s_birth_day%2C_Philadelphia%2C_February_1832.png 2x" data-file-width="4376" data-file-height="9904"></a>

      <p><a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> was born on February 11, 1731 (<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">Old Style</a>), at his parents' Pope's Creek Estate near Colonial Beach in Westmoreland County, Virginia, now the <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Birthplace_National_Monument" title="George Washington Birthplace National Monument">George Washington Birthplace National Monument</a>. At the time, the entire British Empire, including its North American possessions, was on the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>; the Empire, not being bound to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, had not yet adopted the modern <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> that Catholic countries had adopted in 1582. Consequently, by the 1730s, the Julian calendar used by Britain and the Colonies was eleven days behind the Gregorian, because of <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">leap year</a> differences. Furthermore, the British <a href="/wiki/Civil_year" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil year">civil year</a> began on March 25 rather than January 1, so that dates in February (such as this one) 'belonged' to the preceding year. (See <a href="/wiki/Dual_dating" title="Dual dating">Dual dating</a>). In 1752, <a href="/wiki/Calendar_(New_Style)_Act_1750" title="Calendar (New Style) Act 1750">The British Empire switched to the Gregorian calendar</a>; since then, Americans born prior to 1752, including Washington, have typically had their birthdays recognized according to the Gregorian calendar (<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">"New Style" dates</a>). Since February 11 on the Julian calendar is February 22 on the Gregorian, Washington's birthday is today known as February 22, 1732.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The federal holiday honoring Washington was originally implemented by an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Congress" title="Act of Congress">Act of Congress</a> in 1879 for government offices in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a> (20&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large" title="United States Statutes at Large">Stat.</a>&nbsp;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/stat-20-277">277</a>) and expanded in 1885 to include all federal offices (23&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large" title="United States Statutes at Large">Stat.</a>&nbsp;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/stat-23-516">516</a>). As the first federal holiday to honor an American president, the holiday was celebrated on Washington's birthday under the Gregorian calendar, February 22.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On January 1, 1971, the federal holiday was shifted to the third Monday in February by the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Monday_Holiday_Act" title="Uniform Monday Holiday Act">Uniform Monday Holiday Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-unifmonday_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unifmonday-28">[28]</a></sup> This places it between February 15 and 21, which makes "Washington's Birthday" something of a misnomer, since it never occurs on Washington's actual birthday, February 22. (A rough analogue of this phenomenon can be seen in <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realms" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth realms">Commonwealth realms</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Official_Birthday" title="Queen's Official Birthday">reigning monarch's official birthday</a> is celebrated without regard to their actual date of birth.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>The first attempt to create a Presidents Day occurred in 1951 when the "President's Day National Committee" was formed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Harold_Stonebridge_Fischer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Harold Stonebridge Fischer (page does not exist)">Harold Stonebridge Fischer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton, California</a>, who became its National Executive Director for the next two decades. The purpose was not to honor any particular president but to honor the office of the presidency. It was first thought that March 4, the original inauguration day, should be deemed Presidents Day, but the bill recognizing March 4 stalled in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_Judiciary_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate Judiciary Committee">Senate Judiciary Committee</a> (which had authority over federal holidays). The committee felt that, given its proximity to Lincoln's and Washington's Birthdays, three holidays so close together would be unduly burdensome. But meanwhile the governors of a majority of the states issued proclamations declaring March 4 Presidents' Day in their respective jurisdictions.<sup id="cite_ref-Spectator_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spectator-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>An early draft of the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Monday_Holiday_Act" title="Uniform Monday Holiday Act">Uniform Monday Holiday Act</a> would have renamed the holiday "Presidents' Day" to honor the birthdays of both Washington and Lincoln, which would explain why the chosen date falls between the two, but this proposal failed in committee, and the bill was voted on and signed into law on June 28, 1968, keeping the name "Washington's Birthday".<sup id="cite_ref-Spectator_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spectator-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>By the mid-1980s, with a push from advertisers, the term "Presidents' Day" began its public appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-Arbelbide_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arbelbide-30">[30]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Washington's adopted hometown of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria,_Virginia" title="Alexandria, Virginia">Alexandria, Virginia</a>, celebrations are held throughout February.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>A food traditionally associated with the holiday is <a href="/wiki/Cherry_pie" title="Cherry pie">cherry pie</a>, based on the legendary chopping down of a cherry tree in Washington's youth.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Until the late 1980s, corporate businesses generally closed on this day, similar to present corporate practices on <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a> or Christmas Day.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> However, after having been moved to the third Monday, most businesses remain open with many offering sales and other promotions. Federal and state government services close (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">U.S. Postal Service</a>, state Departments of Motor Vehicles, federal and state courts).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Class schedules at universities and colleges vary depending on the school. Public elementary and secondary schools are generally closed, but some school districts, such as New York City, may close for an entire week as a "mid-winter recess".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The holiday is also a tribute to the general who created the first military badge of merit for the common soldier. Revived on Washington's 200th birthday in 1932, the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Heart" title="Purple Heart">Purple Heart</a> medal (which bears Washington's image) is awarded to soldiers who are injured in battle.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Community celebrations often display a lengthy heritage. Washington's hometown of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria,_Virginia" title="Alexandria, Virginia">Alexandria, Virginia</a>, hosts a month-long tribute, including what is claimed to be the nation's longest-running and largest George Washington Birthday parade,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Eustis,_Florida" title="Eustis, Florida">Eustis, Florida</a>, continues its annual "<a href="/wiki/GeorgeFest" title="GeorgeFest">GeorgeFest</a>" celebration begun in 1902.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver, Colorado</a>, there is a society dedicated to observing the day.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Birthplace_National_Monument" title="George Washington Birthplace National Monument">George Washington Birthplace National Monument</a> in <a href="/wiki/Westmoreland_County,_Virginia" title="Westmoreland County, Virginia">Westmoreland County, Virginia</a>, visitors are treated to birthday celebrations on the holiday,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> while at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vernon" title="Mount Vernon">Mount Vernon</a>, they last throughout the holiday weekend and through February 22.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Since 1862 there has been a tradition in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> that <a href="/wiki/George_Washington%27s_Farewell_Address" title="George Washington's Farewell Address">George Washington's Farewell Address</a> be read on his birthday. Citizens asked that this be done in light of the ongoing Civil War.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Punctuation">Punctuation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Washington%27s_Birthday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Punctuation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Because "Presidents' Day" is not the official name of the federal holiday there is variation in how it is rendered, both colloquially and in the name of official state holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-2manyCh1efs_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2manyCh1efs-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>When used with the intention of celebrating more than one individual, the form "Presidents' Day" was usual in the past. In recent years, as the use of <a href="/wiki/Noun_adjunct" title="Noun adjunct">attributive nouns</a> (nouns acting as modifiers) has become more widespread, the form "Presidents Day" has become more common;<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> the <i><a href="/wiki/Associated_Press_Stylebook" class="mw-redirect" title="Associated Press Stylebook">Associated Press Stylebook</a></i>, most newspapers and some magazines use this form.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>"President's Day" as an alternate rendering of "Washington's Birthday", or for the purpose of commemorating the presidency as an institution, is a proper use of a <a href="/wiki/English_possessive" title="English possessive">possessive</a> and is the legal rendering in eight states.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> </p>
