      <p><b>Memorial Day</b> (previously, but now seldom, called <b>Decoration Day</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Federal_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Federal holidays in the United States">federal holiday in the United States</a> for honoring and mourning the military personnel who have died in the performance of their military duties while serving in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vamd_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vamd-2">[2]</a></sup> The holiday is <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Monday_Holiday_Act" title="Uniform Monday Holiday Act">observed</a> on the last Monday of May. The holiday was formerly observed on May 30 from 1868 to 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Many people visit cemeteries and memorials on Memorial Day to honor and mourn those who died while serving in the U.S. Military. Many volunteers place an American flag on graves of military personnel in <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_cemetery" class="mw-redirect" title="United States national cemetery">national cemeteries</a>. </p>
      <p>Memorial Day is also considered the unofficial start of <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-Yan_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yan-4">[4]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">Labor Day</a>, the first Monday of September, marks the unofficial start of <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a>. </p>
      <p>Two other days celebrate those who have served or are serving in the U.S. military: <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Day#United_States" title="Armed Forces Day">Armed Forces Day</a> (which is earlier in May), an unofficial U.S. holiday for honoring those currently serving in the armed forces, and <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Day" title="Veterans Day">Veterans Day</a> (in November 11), which honors those who have served in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Claimed_origins">Claimed origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Claimed origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The history of Memorial Day in the United States is complex. The U.S. Department of Veterans' Affairs recognizes that approximately 25 places claim to have originated the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Columbus_State_University" title="Columbus State University">Columbus [Georgia] State University</a> there is a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://te.columbusstate.edu/faculty_pages/memorialday/index.php">Center for Memorial Day Research</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mississippi" title="University of Mississippi">University of Mississippi</a> incorporates a Center for Civil War Research that has also led research into Memorial Day's origins.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The practice of decorating soldiers' graves with flowers is an ancient custom.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Soldiers' graves were decorated in the U.S. before<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>. Many of the origination claims are myths, unsupported by evidence, while others are one-time cemetery dedications or funeral tributes. In 2014, one scholarly effort attempted to separate the myths and one-time events from the activities that actually led to the establishment of the federal holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-amazon.com_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon.com-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precedents_in_the_South">Precedents in the South</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precedents in the South">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Library_of_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Library of Congress">United States Library of Congress</a> website, "Southern women decorated the graves of soldiers even before the Civil War’s end. Records show that by 1865, Mississippi, Virginia, and South Carolina all had precedents for Memorial Day."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The earliest Southern Memorial Day celebrations were simple, somber occasions for veterans and their families to honor the dead and tend to local cemeteries.<sup id="cite_ref-Center(Firm)2000_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Center(Firm)2000-12">[12]</a></sup> In following years, the <a href="/wiki/Ladies%27_Memorial_Association" title="Ladies' Memorial Association">Ladies' Memorial Association</a> and other groups increasingly focused rituals on preserving Confederate Culture and the <a href="/wiki/Lost_Cause_of_the_Confederacy" title="Lost Cause of the Confederacy">Lost Cause of the Confederacy</a> narrative.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-13">[13]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Warrenton.2C_Virginia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Warrenton,_Virginia">Warrenton, Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Warrenton, Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On June 3, 1861, <a href="/wiki/Warrenton,_Virginia" title="Warrenton, Virginia">Warrenton, Virginia</a>, was the location of the first Civil War soldier's grave ever to be decorated, according to a Richmond <i>Times-Dispatch</i> newspaper article in 1906,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This decoration was for the funeral of the first soldier killed in action during the Civil War, <a href="/wiki/John_Quincy_Marr" title="John Quincy Marr">John Quincy Marr</a>, who died on June 1, 1861 during a skirmish at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fairfax_Court_House_(June_1861)" title="Battle of Fairfax Court House (June 1861)">Battle of Fairfax Courthouse</a> in Virginia. </p> 
      <h3><span id="Savannah.2C_Georgia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Savannah,_Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Savannah, Georgia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In July 1862, women in <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a> decorated the graves at Laurel Grove Cemetery of Colonel, <a href="/wiki/Francis_S._Bartow" title="Francis S. Bartow">Francis S. Bartow</a> and his comrades who died at Battle of Manassas (<a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_Bull_Run" title="First Battle of Bull Run">First Battle of Bull Run</a>) the year before.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Jackson.2C_Mississippi"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Jackson,_Mississippi">Jackson, Mississippi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jackson, Mississippi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On April 26, 1865, in <a href="/wiki/Jackson,_Mississippi" title="Jackson, Mississippi">Jackson, Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sue_Landon_Vaughan" title="Sue Landon Vaughan">Sue Landon Vaughan</a> supposedly decorated the graves of Confederate and Union soldiers. However, the earliest recorded reference to this event did not appear until many years after.<sup id="cite_ref-amazon.com_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon.com-10">[10]</a></sup> Regardless, mention of the observance is inscribed on southeast panel of the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_Monument_(Jackson,_Mississippi)" title="Confederate Monument (Jackson, Mississippi)">Confederate Monument</a> in Jackson, erected in 1891.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Charleston.2C_South_Carolina"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Charleston,_South_Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Charleston, South Carolina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On May 1, 1865, in <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>, recently freed African-Americans held a parade of 10,000 people to honor 257 dead Union soldiers, whose remains they had reburied from a mass grave in a Confederate prison camp.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Historian <a href="/wiki/David_W._Blight" title="David W. Blight">David W. Blight</a> cites contemporary news reports of this incident in the <i>Charleston Daily Courier</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/New-York_Tribune" title="New-York Tribune">New-York Tribune</a>.</i> Although Blight claimed that "African Americans invented Memorial Day in Charleston, South Carolina",<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> in 2012, the New York Times suggested that he "has no evidence" that the event in Charleston effectively led to General Logan’s call for the national holiday. Blight said, "I’m much more interested in the meaning that’s being conveyed in that incredible ritual than who’s first.”<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Accordingly, investigators for <a href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Magazine">Time Magazine</a>, <a href="/wiki/LiveScience" class="mw-redirect" title="LiveScience">LiveScience</a>, <a href="/wiki/RealClearPolitics" title="RealClearPolitics">RealClearLife</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snopes" title="Snopes">Snopes</a> have called this conclusion into question.<sup id="cite_ref-time.com_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time.com-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span id="Columbus.2C_Georgia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Columbus,_Georgia">Columbus, Georgia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Columbus, Georgia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">United States National Park Service</a><sup id="cite_ref-nps.gov_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps.gov-24">[24]</a></sup> and numerous scholars attribute the beginning of a Memorial Day practice in the South to a group of women of Columbus, Georgia.<sup id="cite_ref-amazon.com_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon.com-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The women were the Ladies Memorial Association of Columbus. They were represented by <a href="/wiki/Mary_Ann_Williams" title="Mary Ann Williams">Mary Ann Williams</a> (Mrs. Charles J. Williams) who, as Secretary, wrote a letter to press in March 1866 asking their assistance in establishing annual holiday to decorate the graves of soldiers throughout the south.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">[31]</a></sup> The letter was reprinted in several southern states and the plans were noted in newspapers in the north. The date of April 26 was chosen. The holiday was observed in Atlanta, Augusta, Macon, Columbus and elsewhere in Georgia as well as Montgomery, AL; Memphis, TN; Louisville, KY; New Orleans, LA; Jackson, MS and across the south.<sup id="cite_ref-amazon.com_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon.com-10">[10]</a></sup> In some cities, mostly in Virginia, other dates in May and June were observed. General John A. Logan commented on the observances in a speech to veterans on July 4, 1866 in Salem, IL.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> After General Logan's General Order No. 11 to the Grand Army of the Republic to observe May 30, 1868, the earlier version of the holiday began to be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Confederate_Memorial_Day" title="Confederate Memorial Day">Confederate Memorial Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-amazon.com_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon.com-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Columbus.2C_Mississippi"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Columbus,_Mississippi">Columbus, Mississippi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Columbus, Mississippi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>A year after the war's end, in April 1866, four women of Columbus gathered together to decorate the graves of the Confederate soldiers. They also felt moved to honor the Union soldiers buried there, and to note the grief of their families, by decorating their graves as well. The story of their gesture of humanity and reconciliation is held by some writers as the inspiration of the original Memorial Day despite its occurring last among the claimed inspirations.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precedents_in_the_North">Precedents in the North</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Precedents in the North">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span id="Gettysburg.2C_Pennsylvania"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gettysburg,_Pennsylvania">Gettysburg, Pennsylvania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gettysburg, Pennsylvania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The 1863 cemetery dedication at <a href="/wiki/Gettysburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Gettysburg, Pennsylvania">Gettysburg, Pennsylvania</a> included a ceremony of commemoration at the graves of dead soldiers. Some have therefore claimed that President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> was the founder of Memorial Day.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, Chicago journalist Lloyd Lewis tried to make the case that it was Lincoln's funeral that spurred the soldiers' grave decorating that followed.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Boalsburg.2C_Pennsylvania"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Boalsburg,_Pennsylvania">Boalsburg, Pennsylvania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Boalsburg, Pennsylvania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On July 4, 1864, ladies decorated soldiers' graves according to local historians in <a href="/wiki/Boalsburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Boalsburg, Pennsylvania">Boalsburg, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Boalsburg promotes itself as the birthplace of Memorial Day.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> However, no reference to this event existed until the printing of the History of the 148th Pennsylvania Volunteers in 1904.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In a footnote to a story about her brother, Mrs. Sophie (Keller) Hall described how she and Emma Hunter decorated the grave of Emma's father, Reuben Hunter. The original story did not account for Reuben Hunter's death occurring two months later on September 19, 1864. It also did not mention Mrs. Elizabeth Myers as one of the original participants. However, a bronze statue of all three women gazing upon Reuben Hunter's grave now stands near the entrance to the Boalsburg Cemetery. Although July 4, 1864 was a Monday, the town now claims that the original decoration was on one of the Sundays in October 1864.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Decoration_Day">National Decoration Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: National Decoration Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:John_A._Logan_(general).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/John_A._Logan_%28general%29.jpg/220px-John_A._Logan_%28general%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="332" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/John_A._Logan_%28general%29.jpg/330px-John_A._Logan_%28general%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/John_A._Logan_%28general%29.jpg/440px-John_A._Logan_%28general%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="603" data-file-height="910"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:John_A._Logan_(general).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>General <a href="/wiki/John_A._Logan" title="John A. Logan">John A. Logan</a>, who in 1868 issued a proclamation calling for "Decoration Day"
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>On May 5, 1868, General <a href="/wiki/John_A._Logan" title="John A. Logan">John A. Logan</a> issued a proclamation calling for "Decoration Day" to be observed annually and nationwide; he was commander-in-chief of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Army_of_the_Republic" title="Grand Army of the Republic">Grand Army of the Republic</a> (GAR), an organization of and for Union Civil War veterans founded in <a href="/wiki/Decatur,_Illinois" title="Decatur, Illinois">Decatur, Illinois</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JabbourJabbour2010_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JabbourJabbour2010-43">[43]</a></sup> With his proclamation, Logan adopted the Memorial Day practice that had begun in the Southern states three years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-amazon.com_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amazon.com-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The northern states quickly adopted the holiday. In 1868, memorial events were held in 183 cemeteries in 27 states, and 336 in 1869.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> One author claims that the date was chosen because it was not the anniversary of any particular battle.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> According to a White House address in 2010, the date was chosen as the optimal date for flowers to be in bloom in the North.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Michigan_state_holiday">Michigan state holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Michigan state holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:The_March_of_Time,_by_Henry_Sandham.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/The_March_of_Time%2C_by_Henry_Sandham.jpg/170px-The_March_of_Time%2C_by_Henry_Sandham.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/The_March_of_Time%2C_by_Henry_Sandham.jpg/255px-The_March_of_Time%2C_by_Henry_Sandham.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/The_March_of_Time%2C_by_Henry_Sandham.jpg/340px-The_March_of_Time%2C_by_Henry_Sandham.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2527" data-file-height="2332"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:The_March_of_Time,_by_Henry_Sandham.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

      <p>In 1871, Michigan made "Decoration Day" an official state holiday and by 1890, every northern state had followed suit. There was no standard program for the ceremonies, but they were typically sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Relief_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="Women's Relief Corps">Women's Relief Corps</a>, the women's auxiliary of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Army_of_the_Republic" title="Grand Army of the Republic">Grand Army of the Republic (GAR)</a>, which had 100,000 members. By 1870, the remains of nearly 300,000 Union dead had been reinterred in 73 national cemeteries, located near major battlefields and thus mainly in the South. The most famous are <a href="/wiki/Gettysburg_National_Cemetery" title="Gettysburg National Cemetery">Gettysburg National Cemetery</a> in Pennsylvania and <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a>, near Washington, D.C<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Waterloo.2C_New_York_proclamation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterloo,_New_York_proclamation">Waterloo, New York proclamation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Waterloo, New York proclamation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On May 26, 1966, President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> designated an "official" birthplace of the holiday by signing the presidential proclamation naming <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_(village),_New_York" title="Waterloo (village), New York">Waterloo, New York</a>, as the holder of the title. This action followed House Concurrent Resolution 587, in which the 89th Congress had officially recognized that the patriotic tradition of observing Memorial Day had begun one hundred years prior in Waterloo, New York.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The village credits druggist <a href="/wiki/Henry_C._Welles" title="Henry C. Welles">Henry C. Welles</a> and county clerk <a href="/wiki/John_B._Murray_(general)" title="John B. Murray (general)">John B. Murray</a> as the founders of the holiday. Scholars have determined that the Waterloo account is a myth.<sup id="cite_ref-time.com_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time.com-20">[20]</a></sup> Snopes and <a href="/wiki/Live_Science" title="Live Science">Live Science</a> also discredit the Waterloo account.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_national_history">Early national history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Early national history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In April 1865, following <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln#Assassination" title="Abraham Lincoln">Lincoln's assassination</a>, commemorations were widespread. The more than 600,000 soldiers of both sides who died in the Civil War meant that burial and memorialization took on new cultural significance. Under the leadership of women during the war, an increasingly formal practice of decorating graves had taken shape. In 1865, the federal government also began creating the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Cemetery_System" title="United States National Cemetery System">United States National Cemetery System</a> for the Union war dead.<sup id="cite_ref-WaughGallagher2009_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WaughGallagher2009-56">[56]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>By the 1880s, ceremonies were becoming more consistent across geography as the GAR provided handbooks that presented specific procedures, poems, and Bible verses for local post commanders to utilize in planning the local event. Historian Stuart McConnell reports: </p> 
      <blockquote>
       <p>on the day itself, the post assembled and marched to the local cemetery to decorate the graves of the fallen, an enterprise meticulously organized months in advance to assure that none were missed. Finally came a simple and subdued graveyard service involving prayers, short patriotic speeches, and music ... and at the end perhaps a rifle salute.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote>

         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Confederate_Memorial_at_Alabama_State_Capitol_Apr2009.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>In 1868, some Southern public figures began adding the label "Confederate" to their commemorations and claimed that Northerners had appropriated the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nps.gov_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nps.gov-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-google1_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google1-59">[59]</a></sup> The first official celebration of Confederate Memorial Day as a public holiday occurred in 1874, following a proclamation by the Georgia legislature.<sup id="cite_ref-GeorgiaInfo_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GeorgiaInfo-60">[60]</a></sup> By 1916, ten states celebrated it, on June 3, the birthday of CSA President <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Davis" title="Jefferson Davis">Jefferson Davis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GeorgiaInfo_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GeorgiaInfo-60">[60]</a></sup> Other states chose late April dates, or May 10, commemorating Davis' capture.<sup id="cite_ref-GeorgiaInfo_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GeorgiaInfo-60">[60]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Ladies%27_Memorial_Association" title="Ladies' Memorial Association">Ladies' Memorial Association</a> played a key role in using Memorial Day rituals to preserve Confederate culture.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-13">[13]</a></sup> Various dates ranging from April 25 to mid-June were adopted in different Southern states. Across the South, associations were founded, many by women, to establish and care for permanent cemeteries for the Confederate dead, organize commemorative ceremonies, and sponsor appropriate monuments as a permanent way of remembering the Confederate dead. The most important of these was the <a href="/wiki/United_Daughters_of_the_Confederacy" title="United Daughters of the Confederacy">United Daughters of the Confederacy</a>, which grew from 17,000 members in 1900 to nearly 100,000 women by <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. They were "strikingly successful at raising money to build Confederate monuments, lobbying legislatures and Congress for the reburial of Confederate dead, and working to shape the content of history textbooks."<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>By 1890, there was a shift from the emphasis on honoring specific soldiers to a public commemoration of the Confederate South.<sup id="cite_ref-Center(Firm)2000_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Center(Firm)2000-12">[12]</a></sup> Changes in the ceremony's hymns and speeches reflect an evolution of the ritual into a symbol of cultural renewal and conservatism in the South. By 1913, David Blight argues, the theme of American nationalism shared equal time with the Confederate.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decoration_Day_to_Memorial_Day">Decoration Day to Memorial Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Decoration Day to Memorial Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>By the 20th century, various Union memorial traditions, celebrated on different days, merged, and Memorial Day eventually extended to honor all Americans who died while in the U.S. military service.<sup id="cite_ref-vamd_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vamd-2">[2]</a></sup> Indiana from the 1860s to the 1920s saw numerous debates on how to expand the celebration. It was a favorite lobbying activity of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Army_of_the_Republic" title="Grand Army of the Republic">Grand Army of the Republic</a> (GAR). An 1884 GAR handbook explained that Memorial Day was "the day of all days in the G.A.R. Calendar" in terms of mobilizing public support for pensions. It advised family members to "exercise great care" in keeping the veterans sober.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> </p> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:DecorationDayMcCutcheon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/DecorationDayMcCutcheon.jpg/220px-DecorationDayMcCutcheon.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/DecorationDayMcCutcheon.jpg/330px-DecorationDayMcCutcheon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/DecorationDayMcCutcheon.jpg/440px-DecorationDayMcCutcheon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2126" data-file-height="2260"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:DecorationDayMcCutcheon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>"On Decoration Day" Political cartoon c. 1900 by <a href="/wiki/John_T._McCutcheon" title="John T. McCutcheon">John T. McCutcheon</a>. Caption: "You bet I'm goin' to be a soldier, too, like my Uncle David, when I grow up."
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p> Memorial Day speeches became an occasion for veterans, politicians, and ministers to commemorate the Civil War and, at first, to rehash the "atrocities" of the enemy. They mixed religion and celebratory nationalism for the people to make sense of their history in terms of sacrifice for a better nation. People of all religious beliefs joined together and the point was often made that German and Irish soldiers&nbsp;– ethnic minorities which faced discrimination in the United States&nbsp;– had become true Americans in the "baptism of blood" on the battlefield.<sup id="cite_ref-Samito2009_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samito2009-64">[64]</a></sup> </p>
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       See also: <a href="/wiki/Anti-Irish_sentiment#19th_century" title="Anti-Irish sentiment">Anti-Irish sentiment §&nbsp;19th century</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anti-German_sentiment#United_States" title="Anti-German sentiment">Anti-German sentiment §&nbsp;United States</a>
      </div> 
      <p>In the national capital in 1913 the four-day "Blue-Gray Reunion" featured parades, re-enactments, and speeches from a host of dignitaries, including President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>, the first Southerner elected to the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> since the War. <a href="/wiki/James_Thomas_Heflin" title="James Thomas Heflin">James Heflin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> gave the main address. Heflin was a noted orator; his choice as Memorial Day speaker was criticized, as he was opposed for his support of segregation; however, his speech was moderate in tone and stressed national unity and goodwill, gaining him praise from newspapers.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The name "Memorial Day", which was first attested in 1882, gradually became more common than "Decoration Day" after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a><sup id="cite_ref-GoddardZon2008_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoddardZon2008-66">[66]</a></sup> but was not declared the official name by federal law until 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-Axelrod2007_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Axelrod2007-67">[67]</a></sup> On June 28, 1968, Congress passed the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Monday_Holiday_Act" title="Uniform Monday Holiday Act">Uniform Monday Holiday Act</a>, which moved four holidays, including Memorial Day, from their traditional dates to a specified Monday in order to create a convenient three-day weekend.<sup id="cite_ref-Public_Law_90-363_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Public_Law_90-363-68">[68]</a></sup> The change moved Memorial Day from its traditional May 30 date to the last Monday in May. The law took effect at the federal level in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-Public_Law_90-363_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Public_Law_90-363-68">[68]</a></sup> After some initial confusion and unwillingness to comply, all 50 states adopted Congress's change of date within a few years. </p>
      <p>By the early 20th century, the GAR complained more and more about the younger generation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1913, one Indiana veteran complained that younger people born since the war had a "tendency ... to forget the purpose of Memorial Day and make it a day for games, races and revelry, instead of a day of memory and tears".<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Indeed, in 1911 the scheduling of the Indianapolis Motor Speedway car race (later named the <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_500" title="Indianapolis 500">Indianapolis 500</a>) was vehemently opposed by the increasingly elderly GAR. The state legislature in 1923 rejected holding the race on the holiday. But the new <a href="/wiki/American_Legion" title="American Legion">American Legion</a> and local officials wanted the big race to continue, so Governor Warren McCray vetoed the bill and the race went on.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_religious_holiday">Civil religious holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Civil religious holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Remembering_the_fallen_at_ANC_on_Memorial_Day_150525-A-FT656-762.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Remembering_the_fallen_at_ANC_on_Memorial_Day_150525-A-FT656-762.jpg/220px-Remembering_the_fallen_at_ANC_on_Memorial_Day_150525-A-FT656-762.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Remembering_the_fallen_at_ANC_on_Memorial_Day_150525-A-FT656-762.jpg/330px-Remembering_the_fallen_at_ANC_on_Memorial_Day_150525-A-FT656-762.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Remembering_the_fallen_at_ANC_on_Memorial_Day_150525-A-FT656-762.jpg/440px-Remembering_the_fallen_at_ANC_on_Memorial_Day_150525-A-FT656-762.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1594"></a>

      </div> 
      <p>Memorial Day endures as a holiday which most businesses observe because it marks the unofficial beginning of summer. The <a href="/wiki/Veterans_of_Foreign_Wars" title="Veterans of Foreign Wars">Veterans of Foreign Wars</a> (VFW) and <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Union_Veterans_of_the_Civil_War" title="Sons of Union Veterans of the Civil War">Sons of Union Veterans of the Civil War</a> (SUVCW) advocated returning to the original date. The VFW stated in 2002: </p> 
      <blockquote>
       <p>Changing the date merely to create three-day weekends has undermined the very meaning of the day. No doubt, this has contributed a lot to the general public's nonchalant observance of Memorial Day.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>In 2000, Congress passed the <a href="/wiki/National_Moment_of_Remembrance" title="National Moment of Remembrance">National Moment of Remembrance</a> Act, asking people to stop and remember at 3:00 pm.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On Memorial Day, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_United_States" title="Flag of the United States">flag of the United States</a> is raised briskly to the top of the staff and then solemnly lowered to the <a href="/wiki/Half-mast" title="Half-mast">half-staff</a> position, where it remains only until noon.<sup id="cite_ref-PostPost2011_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PostPost2011-73">[73]</a></sup> It is then raised to full-staff for the remainder of the day.<sup id="cite_ref-Congress2009_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Congress2009-74">[74]</a></sup> </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Memorial_Day_Observance_in_small_New_England_town.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Memorial_Day_Observance_in_small_New_England_town.jpg/220px-Memorial_Day_Observance_in_small_New_England_town.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Memorial_Day_Observance_in_small_New_England_town.jpg/330px-Memorial_Day_Observance_in_small_New_England_town.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Memorial_Day_Observance_in_small_New_England_town.jpg/440px-Memorial_Day_Observance_in_small_New_England_town.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1743" data-file-height="1390"></a>

         </div>Memorial Day observances in small <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> towns are often marked by dedications and remarks by veterans and politicians.

      <p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Memorial_Day_Concert" title="National Memorial Day Concert">National Memorial Day Concert</a> takes place on the west lawn of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The concert is broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Broadcasting Service">PBS</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" class="mw-redirect" title="National Public Radio">NPR</a>. Music is performed, and respect is paid to the people who gave their lives for their country. </p>
      <p>Across the United States, the central event is attending one of the thousands of parades held on Memorial Day in large and small cities. Most of these feature marching bands and an overall military theme with the Active Duty, Reserve, National Guard and Veteran service members participating along with military vehicles from various wars.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Scholars,<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> following the lead of sociologist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bellah" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Bellah">Robert Bellah</a>, often make the argument that the United States has a secular "<a href="/wiki/American_civil_religion" title="American civil religion">civil religion</a>" – one with no association with any religious denomination or viewpoint – that has incorporated Memorial Day as a sacred event. With the Civil War, a new theme of death, sacrifice and rebirth enters the civil religion. Memorial Day gave ritual expression to these themes, integrating the local community into a sense of nationalism. The American civil religion, in contrast to that of France, was never anticlerical or militantly secular; in contrast to Britain, it was not tied to a specific denomination, such as the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>. The Americans borrowed from different religious traditions so that the average American saw no conflict between the two, and deep levels of personal motivation were aligned with attaining national goals.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Longest_observance">Longest observance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Longest observance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Since 1868 <a href="/wiki/Doylestown,_Pennsylvania" title="Doylestown, Pennsylvania">Doylestown, Pennsylvania</a>, has held annual Memorial Day parades which it claims to be the nation's oldest continuously running. <a href="/wiki/Grafton,_West_Virginia" title="Grafton, West Virginia">Grafton, West Virginia</a> has also had an ongoing parade since 1868. However, the Memorial Day parade in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Wisconsin" title="Rochester, Wisconsin">Rochester, Wisconsin</a>, predates Doylestown's by one year.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorial_Day_poppies">Memorial Day poppies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Memorial Day poppies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Remembrance_poppy" title="Remembrance poppy">Remembrance poppy</a>
      </div> 
      <p>In 1915, following the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Ypres" title="Second Battle of Ypres">Second Battle of Ypres</a>, Lieutenant Colonel <a href="/wiki/John_McCrae" title="John McCrae">John McCrae</a>, a physician with the Canadian Expeditionary Force, wrote the poem, "<a href="/wiki/In_Flanders_Fields" title="In Flanders Fields">In Flanders Fields</a>". Its opening lines refer to the fields of poppies that grew among the soldiers' graves in Flanders.<sup id="cite_ref-Tucker2014_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker2014-83">[83]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1918, inspired by the poem, <a href="/wiki/YWCA_USA" title="YWCA USA">YWCA</a> worker <a href="/wiki/Moina_Michael" title="Moina Michael">Moina Michael</a> attended a YWCA Overseas War Secretaries' conference wearing a silk poppy pinned to her coat and distributed over two dozen more to others present. In 1920, the National American Legion adopted it as their official symbol of remembrance.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc10nov06_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc10nov06-84">[84]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_traditions">Related traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Related traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span id="Decoration_Day_.28Appalachia_and_Liberia.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Decoration_Day_(Appalachia_and_Liberia)">Decoration Day (Appalachia and Liberia)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Decoration Day (Appalachia and Liberia)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Decoration_Day_(Appalachia_and_Liberia)" title="Decoration Day (Appalachia and Liberia)">Decoration Day (Appalachia and Liberia)</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Decoration Days in Southern <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">Appalachia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a> are an unbroken tradition which arose by the 19th century. Decoration practices are localized and unique to individual families, cemeteries, and communities, but common elements that unify the various Decoration Day practices are thought to represent <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretism</a> of predominantly Christian cultures in 19th century Southern Appalachia with pre-Christian influences from Scotland, Ireland, and African cultures. Appalachian and Liberian cemetery decoration traditions are thought to have more in common with one another than with United States Memorial Day traditions which are focused on honoring the military dead.<sup id="cite_ref-jabbourblog_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jabbourblog-85">[85]</a></sup> Appalachian and Liberian cemetery decoration traditions pre-date the United States Memorial Day holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-alabama-encyclopedia_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alabama-encyclopedia-86">[86]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the United States, cemetery decoration practices have been recorded in the Appalachian regions of West Virginia, Virginia, Kentucky, Tennessee, northern South Carolina, northern Georgia, northern and central Alabama, and northern Mississippi. Appalachian cemetery decoration has also been observed in areas outside Appalachia along routes of westward migration from that region: northern Louisiana, northeastern Texas, Arkansas, eastern Oklahoma, and southern Missouri. </p>
      <p>According to scholars Alan and Karen Jabbour, "the geographic spread ... from the Smokies to northeastern Texas and Liberia, offer strong evidence that the southern Decoration Day originated well back in the nineteenth century. The presence of the same cultural tradition throughout the Upland South argues for the age of the tradition, which was carried westward (and eastward to Africa) by nineteenth-century migration and has survived in essentially the same form till the present."<sup id="cite_ref-JabbourJabbour2010_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JabbourJabbour2010-43">[43]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>While these customs may have inspired in part rituals to honor military dead like Memorial Day, numerous differences exist between Decoration Day customs and Memorial Day, including that the date is set differently by each family or church for each cemetery to coordinate the maintenance, social, and spiritual aspects of decoration.<sup id="cite_ref-jabbourblog_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jabbourblog-85">[85]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hooker_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hooker-87">[87]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Ives" title="Charles Ives">Charles Ives</a>'s symphonic poem <i>Decoration Day</i> depicted the holiday as he experienced it in his childhood, with his father's band leading the way to the town cemetery, the playing of "<a href="/wiki/Taps" title="Taps">Taps</a>" on a trumpet, and a livelier march tune on the way back to the town. It is frequently played with three other Ives works based on holidays, as the second movement of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Symphony:_New_England_Holidays" title="A Symphony: New England Holidays">A Symphony: New England Holidays</a></i>.</li> 
       <li>American rock band <a href="/wiki/Drive-By_Truckers" title="Drive-By Truckers">Drive-By Truckers</a> released a <a href="/wiki/Jason_Isbell" title="Jason Isbell">Jason Isbell</a>–penned song titled "Decoration Day" on their 2003 <a href="/wiki/Decoration_Day_(album)" title="Decoration Day (album)">album of the same title</a>.</li>
      </ul>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/ANZAC_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="ANZAC Day">ANZAC Day</a>, April 25, an analogous observance in Australia and New Zealand</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Armistice_Day" title="Armistice Day">Armistice Day</a>, November 11, the original name of Veterans Day in the United States and Remembrance Day in Canada, the United Kingdom, and other Commonwealth nations</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Heroes%27_Day" title="Heroes' Day">Heroes' Day</a>, various dates in various countries recognizing national heroes</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/International_Day_of_United_Nations_Peacekeepers" title="International Day of United Nations Peacekeepers">International Day of United Nations Peacekeepers</a>, May 29, international observance recognizing United Nations peacekeepers</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Remembrance_Day" title="Remembrance Day">Remembrance Day</a>, November 11, a similar observance in Canada, the United Kingdom, and many other Commonwealth nations originally marking the end of World War I</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Remembrance_of_the_Dead" title="Remembrance of the Dead">Remembrance of the Dead</a> ("Dodenherdenking"), May 4, a similar observance in the Netherlands</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Volkstrauertag" title="Volkstrauertag">Volkstrauertag</a> ("People's Mourning Day"), a similar observance in Germany usually in November</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Yom_HaZikaron" title="Yom HaZikaron">Yom Hazikaron</a> (Israeli memorial day), day before <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(Israel)" title="Independence Day (Israel)">Independence Day (Israel)</a>, around <a href="/wiki/Iyar" title="Iyar">Iyar</a> 4</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Decoration_Day_(Canada)" title="Decoration Day (Canada)">Decoration Day (Canada)</a>, a Canadian holiday that recognizes veterans of Canada's military which has largely been eclipsed by the similar Remembrance Day</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day_(South_Korea)" title="Memorial Day (South Korea)">Memorial Day (South Korea)</a>, June 6, the day to commemorate the men and women who died while in military service during the Korean War and other significant wars or battles</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Victoria_Day" title="Victoria Day">Victoria Day</a>, a Canadian holiday on the last Monday before May 25 each year, lacks the military memorial aspects of Memorial Day but serves a similar function as marking the start of cultural summer</li>
      </ul>
