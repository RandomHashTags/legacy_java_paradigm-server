      <p><b>International Literacy Day</b> is an international observance, celebrated each year on 8 September, that was declared by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> on 26 October 1966 at the 14th session of UNESCO's General Conference. It was celebrated for the first time in 1967. Its aim is to highlight the importance of <a href="/wiki/Literacy" title="Literacy">literacy</a> to individuals, communities and societies. Celebrations take place in several countries.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rationale">Rationale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Literacy_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rationale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Some 775 million lack minimum literacy skills; one in five adults are still not literate and two-thirds of them are women;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> 60.7 million children are out-of-school and many more attend irregularly or <a href="/wiki/Dropping_out" title="Dropping out">drop out</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>According to UNESCO’s "Global Monitoring Report on Education for All (2006)",<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> has the lowest regional adult <a href="/wiki/Literacy_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Literacy rate">literacy rate</a> (58.6%), followed by <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a> (59.7%).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Countries with the lowest literacy rates in the world are <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a> (12.8%), <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a> (14.4%) and <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a> (19%). The report shows a clear connection between illiteracy and countries in <a href="/wiki/Extreme_poverty" title="Extreme poverty">severe poverty</a>, and between illiteracy and <a href="/wiki/Gender_roles" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender roles">prejudice against women</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebrations">Celebrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Literacy_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Celebrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>Celebrations of International Literacy Day have included specific themes, in line with <a href="/wiki/Education_For_All" title="Education For All">Education For All</a> goals and other United Nations programs such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Literacy_Decade" title="United Nations Literacy Decade">United Nations Literacy Decade</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The celebration's theme for 2007 and 2008 was “Literacy and Health”, with prizes awarded to organizations at the forefront of health education.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This was also the thematic emphasis of the 2007–2008 biennium of the United Nations Literacy Decade.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In particular, International Literacy Day 2008 had a strong emphasis on Literacy and Epidemics with a focus on communicable diseases such as HIV, tuberculosis and malaria, some of the world's forefront public health concerns. For 2009–2010 the emphasis was placed on “Literacy and Empowerment”,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> with special consideration to <a href="/wiki/Gender_Equality" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender Equality">gender equality</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Empowerment" title="Empowerment">empowerment</a> of women. The theme of the 2011–2012 celebrations is “Literacy and Peace”.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The following writers are supporting UNESCO through the Writers for Literacy Initiative:<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Atwood" title="Margaret Atwood">Margaret Atwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Auster" title="Paul Auster">Paul Auster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Claudel" title="Philippe Claudel">Philippe Claudel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paulo_Coelho" title="Paulo Coelho">Paulo Coelho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Delerm" title="Philippe Delerm">Philippe Delerm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatou_Diome" title="Fatou Diome">Fatou Diome</a>, Chahdortt Djavann, Nadine Gordimer, <a href="/wiki/Amitav_Ghosh" title="Amitav Ghosh">Amitav Ghosh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marc_Levy" title="Marc Levy">Marc Levy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Manguel" title="Alberto Manguel">Alberto Manguel</a>, Anna Moi, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Momaday" class="mw-redirect" title="Scott Momaday">Scott Momaday</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toni_Morrison" title="Toni Morrison">Toni Morrison</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89rik_Orsenna" title="Érik Orsenna">Érik Orsenna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gis%C3%A8le_Pineau" title="Gisèle Pineau">Gisèle Pineau</a>, El Tayeb Salih, Francisco Jose Sionil, <a href="/wiki/Wole_Soyinka" title="Wole Soyinka">Wole Soyinka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amy_Tan" title="Amy Tan">Amy Tan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mikl%C3%B3s_V%C3%A1mos" title="Miklós Vámos">Miklós Vámos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abdourahman_Waberi" title="Abdourahman Waberi">Abdourahman Waberi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wei_Wei_(female_writer)" title="Wei Wei (female writer)">Wei Wei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banana_Yoshimoto" title="Banana Yoshimoto">Banana Yoshimoto</a>. Not only the writers contribute to raising awareness to the problem of illiteracy: along with the writers’ engagement, there are various companies and charity organizations that support the fight against illiteracy. Some supporters of International Literacy Day include the Global Development Research Center, <a href="/wiki/Montblanc_(pens)" class="mw-redirect" title="Montblanc (pens)">Montblanc</a>, the National Institute for Literacy, and <a href="/wiki/Rotary_International" title="Rotary International">Rotary International</a>. </p>
