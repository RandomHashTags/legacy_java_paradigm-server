      <p><b>Juneteenth</b> (a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <i>June</i> and <i>nineteenth</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup> – also known as <b>Freedom Day</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>Jubilee Day</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <b>Liberation Day</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> and <b>Emancipation Day</b><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> – is a holiday celebrating the emancipation of those who had been <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">enslaved in the United States</a>. Originating in <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a>, it is now celebrated annually on the <a href="/wiki/June_19" title="June 19">19th of June</a> throughout the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, with varying official recognition. It is commemorated on the anniversary date of the June 19, 1865 announcement by <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> general <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Granger" title="Gordon Granger">Gordon Granger</a>, proclaiming freedom from slavery in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gates_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gates-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation</a> had officially outlawed slavery in Texas and the other states in rebellion against the <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> almost two and a half years earlier. Enforcement of the Proclamation generally relied on the advance of Union troops. Texas being the most remote of the <a href="/wiki/Slave_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave states">slave states</a> had a low presence of Union troops as the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> ended; thus enforcement there had been slow and inconsistent before Granger's announcement.<sup id="cite_ref-Gates_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gates-7">[7]</a></sup> Although Juneteenth generally celebrates the end of slavery in the United States, it was still legal and practiced in two <a href="/wiki/Border_states_(American_Civil_War)" title="Border states (American Civil War)">Union border states</a> (Delaware and Kentucky) until later that year when ratification of the <a href="/wiki/Thirteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Thirteenth Amendment to the Constitution</a> abolished <a href="/wiki/Chattel_slavery" class="mw-redirect" title="Chattel slavery">chattel slavery</a> nationwide in December.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-war_powers_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-war_powers-11">[a]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Celebrations date to 1866, at first involving church-centered community gatherings in Texas. It spread across <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">the South</a> and became more commercialized in the 1920s and 1930s, often centering on a <a href="/wiki/Food_festival" title="Food festival">food festival</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a> of the 1960s, it was eclipsed by the struggle for postwar civil rights, but grew in popularity again in the 1970s with a focus on <a href="/wiki/African_American" class="mw-redirect" title="African American">African American</a> freedom and arts.<sup id="cite_ref-cruz_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cruz-12">[11]</a></sup> By the 21st century, Juneteenth was celebrated in most major cities across the United States. Activists are campaigning for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> to recognize Juneteenth as a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Public holidays in the United States">national holiday</a>. Hawaii, North Dakota and South Dakota are the only states that do not recognize Juneteenth, according to the&nbsp;Congressional Research Service.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Modern observance is primarily in local celebrations. Traditions include public readings of the Emancipation Proclamation, singing traditional songs such as "<a href="/wiki/Swing_Low,_Sweet_Chariot" title="Swing Low, Sweet Chariot">Swing Low, Sweet Chariot</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Lift_Every_Voice_and_Sing" title="Lift Every Voice and Sing">Lift Every Voice and Sing</a>", and reading of works by noted <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African-American</a> writers such as <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Ellison" title="Ralph Ellison">Ralph Ellison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maya_Angelou" title="Maya Angelou">Maya Angelou</a>. Celebrations include <a href="/wiki/Rodeos" class="mw-redirect" title="Rodeos">rodeos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Street_fair" title="Street fair">street fairs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbecue" title="Barbecue">cookouts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Family_reunion" title="Family reunion">family reunions</a>, park parties, <a href="/wiki/Historical_reenactment" title="Historical reenactment">historical reenactments</a>, and Miss Juneteenth contests. The <a href="/wiki/Mascogos" title="Mascogos">Mascogos</a>, descendants of <a href="/wiki/Black_Seminoles" title="Black Seminoles">Black Seminoles</a>, who escaped from U.S. slavery in 1852 and settled in <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, also celebrate Juneteenth. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_slavery_in_Texas">End of slavery in Texas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juneteenth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: End of slavery in Texas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> issued the <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation</a> on September 22, 1862.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-14">[13]</a></sup> It became effective on January 1, 1863, declaring that all enslaved persons in the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate States of America</a> in rebellion and not in Union hands were freed.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-14">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-war_powers_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-war_powers-11">[a]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>More isolated geographically, planters and other slaveholders had migrated into Texas from eastern states to escape the fighting, and many brought enslaved people with them, increasing by the thousands the enslaved population in the state at the end of the Civil War.<sup id="cite_ref-gates_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gates-15">[14]</a></sup> Although most lived in rural areas, more than 1,000 resided in both <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a> by 1860, with several hundred in other large towns.<sup id="cite_ref-barr24_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barr24-16">[15]</a></sup> By 1865, there were an estimated 250,000 enslaved people in Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-gates_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gates-15">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-17">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p> Despite the surrender of General <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Lee" title="Robert E. Lee">Robert E. Lee</a> at <a href="/wiki/Appomattox_Court_House_National_Historical_Park" title="Appomattox Court House National Historical Park">Appomattox Court House</a> on April 9, 1865, the western <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Trans-Mississippi" title="Army of the Trans-Mississippi">Army of the Trans-Mississippi</a> did not surrender until June 2.<sup id="cite_ref-gates2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gates2-18">[17]</a></sup> On the morning of Monday, June 19, 1865, <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union</a> Major General <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Granger" title="Gordon Granger">Gordon Granger</a> arrived on the island of <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a><sup id="cite_ref-auto1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-19">[18]</a></sup> to take command of the more than 2,000 federal troops<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> recently landed in the department of Texas to enforce the <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">emancipation</a> of its slaves and oversee a <a href="/wiki/Peaceful_transition_of_power" title="Peaceful transition of power">peaceful transition of power</a>, additionally nullifying all laws passed within Texas during the war by Confederate lawmakers.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-19">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Texas_Historical_Commission" title="Texas Historical Commission">Texas Historical Commission</a> and Galveston Historical Foundation report that Granger’s men marched throughout Galveston reading <a href="/wiki/General_Order_No._3" title="General Order No. 3">General Order No. 3</a> first at Union Army Headquarters at the Osterman Building (formerly at the intersection of Strand Street and 22nd Street, since demolished), in the <a href="/wiki/Strand_Historic_District" title="Strand Historic District">Strand Historic District</a>. Next they marched to the 1861 Customs House and Courthouse before finally marching to the Negro Church on Broadway, since renamed <a href="/wiki/Reedy_Chapel-AME_Church" title="Reedy Chapel-AME Church">Reedy Chapel-AME Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> The order informed all Texans that, in accordance with a Proclamation from the Executive of the United States, all slaves were free:<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>The people of Texas are informed that, in accordance with a proclamation from the Executive of the United States, all slaves are free. This involves an absolute equality of personal rights and rights of property between former masters and slaves, and the connection heretofore existing between them becomes that between employer and hired labor. The freedmen are advised to remain quietly at their present homes and work for wages. They are informed that they will not be allowed to collect at military posts and that they will not be supported in idleness either there or elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-TXJ19_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TXJ19-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>Longstanding urban legend places the historic reading of General Order No. 3 at <a href="/wiki/Ashton_Villa" title="Ashton Villa">Ashton Villa</a>, however no extant historical evidence supports such claims.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> On June 21, 2014, the Galveston Historical Foundation and <a href="/wiki/Texas_Historical_Commission" title="Texas Historical Commission">Texas Historical Commission</a> erected a Juneteenth plaque where the Osterman Building once stood signifying the location of Major General Granger's Union Headquarters and subsequent issuance of his general orders.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Although this event is popularly thought of as "the end of slavery", emancipation for those enslaved in two Union <a href="/wiki/Border_states_(American_Civil_War)" title="Border states (American Civil War)">border states</a> (Delaware and Kentucky), would not come until several months later, on December 18, 1865, when ratification of the <a href="/wiki/Thirteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Thirteenth Amendment</a> was announced.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWynn2009_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWynn2009-25">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-war_powers_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-war_powers-11">[a]</a></sup> The freedom of formerly enslaved people in Texas was given final legal status in a series of <a href="/wiki/Texas_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas Supreme Court">Texas Supreme Court</a> decisions between 1868 and 1874.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_celebrations">Early celebrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juneteenth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early celebrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>Formerly enslaved people in Galveston celebrated after the announcement.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> The following year, <a href="/wiki/Freedman" title="Freedman">freedmen</a> in Texas organized the first of what became the annual celebration of "Jubilee Day" on June 19.<sup id="cite_ref-TXJ19_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TXJ19-22">[21]</a></sup> Early celebrations were used as political rallies to give voting instructions to newly freed slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-27">[26]</a></sup> Early independence celebrations often occurred on January 1 or 4.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilson2006239_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilson2006239-28">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p> In some cities black people were barred from using public parks because of state-sponsored <a href="/wiki/Racial_segregation_in_the_United_States" title="Racial segregation in the United States">segregation</a> of facilities. Across parts of Texas, freed people pooled their funds to purchase land to hold their celebrations.<sup id="cite_ref-gates_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gates-15">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TXJ19_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TXJ19-22">[21]</a></sup> The day was first celebrated in Austin in 1867 under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Freedmen%27s_Bureau" title="Freedmen's Bureau">Freedmen's Bureau</a>, and it had been listed on a "calendar of public events" by 1872.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWynn2009_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWynn2009-25">[24]</a></sup> That year black leaders in Texas raised $1,000 for the purchase of 10 acres (4&nbsp;ha) of land to celebrate Juneteenth, today known as <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Park_(Houston)" title="Emancipation Park (Houston)">Emancipation Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMustakeem2007_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMustakeem2007-29">[28]</a></sup> The observation was soon drawing thousands of attendees across Texas; an estimated 30,000 black people celebrated at Booker T. Washington Park in <a href="/wiki/Limestone_County,_Texas" title="Limestone County, Texas">Limestone County, Texas</a>, established in 1898 for Juneteenth celebrations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWynn2009_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWynn2009-25">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight2011_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight2011-30">[29]</a></sup> By the 1890s Jubilee Day had become known as Juneteenth.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-17">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Emancipation_Day_in_Richmond,_Virginia,_1905.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Emancipation_Day_in_Richmond%2C_Virginia%2C_1905.jpg/220px-Emancipation_Day_in_Richmond%2C_Virginia%2C_1905.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Emancipation_Day_in_Richmond%2C_Virginia%2C_1905.jpg/330px-Emancipation_Day_in_Richmond%2C_Virginia%2C_1905.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Emancipation_Day_in_Richmond%2C_Virginia%2C_1905.jpg/440px-Emancipation_Day_in_Richmond%2C_Virginia%2C_1905.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="636"></a>

        </div>

      <p> In the early 20th century, economic and political forces led to a decline in Juneteenth celebrations. From 1890 to 1908, Texas and all former Confederate states passed new constitutions or amendments that effectively <a href="/wiki/Disfranchisement_after_Reconstruction_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Disfranchisement after Reconstruction era">disenfranchised black people</a>, excluding them from the political process. White-dominated state legislatures passed <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crow" class="mw-redirect" title="Jim Crow">Jim Crow laws</a> imposing second-class status.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-31">[30]</a></sup> Gladys L. Knight writes the decline in celebration was in part because "upwardly mobile blacks [...] were ashamed of their slave past and aspired to assimilate into mainstream culture. Younger generations of blacks, becoming further removed from slavery were occupied with school [...] and other pursuits." Others who migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_United_States" title="Northern United States">Northern United States</a> couldn't take time off or simply dropped the celebration.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight2011_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight2011-30">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> forced many black people off farms and into the cities to find work. In these urban environments, African Americans had difficulty taking the day off to celebrate. From 1936 to 1951 the <a href="/wiki/Texas_State_Fair" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas State Fair">Texas State Fair</a> served as a destination for celebrating the holiday, contributing to its revival. In 1936 an estimated 150,000 to 200,000 people joined the holiday's celebration in Dallas. In 1938, Texas governor <a href="/wiki/James_V._Allred" title="James V. Allred">James V. Allred</a> issued a proclamation stating in part:<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">[31]</a></sup> </p> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942">
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>Whereas, the Negroes in the State of Texas observe June 19 as the official day for the celebration of Emancipation from slavery; and </p>
       <p>Whereas, June 19, 1865, was the date when General Robert [sic] S. Granger, who had command of the Military District of Texas, issued a proclamation notifying the Negroes of Texas that they were free; and </p>
       <p>Whereas, since that time, Texas Negroes have observed this day with suitable holiday ceremony, except during such years when the day comes on a Sunday; when the Governor of the State is asked to proclaim the following day as the holiday for State observance by Negroes; and </p>
       <p>Whereas, June 19, 1938, this year falls on Sunday; NOW, THEREFORE, I, JAMES V. ALLRED, Governor of the State of Texas, do set aside and proclaim the day of June 20, 1938, as the date for observance of EMANCIPATION DAY </p>
       <p> in Texas, and do urge all members of the Negro race in Texas to observe the day in a manner appropriate to its importance to them.</p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>Seventy thousand people attended a "Juneteenth Jamboree" in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">[31]</a></sup> From 1940 through 1970, in the second wave of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a>, more than five million black people left Texas, Louisiana and other parts of the South for the North and the West Coast. As historian <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Wilkerson" title="Isabel Wilkerson">Isabel Wilkerson</a> writes, "The people from Texas took Juneteenth Day to Los Angeles, <a href="/wiki/Oakland" class="mw-redirect" title="Oakland">Oakland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, and other places they went."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> In 1945, Juneteenth was introduced in San Francisco by an immigrant from Texas, Wesley Johnson.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-34">[33]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>During the 1950s and 1960s, the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a> focused the attention of African Americans on expanding freedom and integrating. As a result, observations of the holiday declined again (though it was still celebrated regionally in Texas).<sup id="cite_ref-:5_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-27">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilson2006239_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilson2006239-28">[27]</a></sup> It soon saw a revival as black people began tying their struggle to that of ending slavery. In <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, some campaigners for equality wore Juneteenth buttons. During the 1968 <a href="/wiki/Poor_People%27s_Campaign" title="Poor People's Campaign">Poor People's Campaign</a> to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>, called by Rev. <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Abernathy" title="Ralph Abernathy">Ralph Abernathy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Christian_Leadership_Conference" title="Southern Christian Leadership Conference">Southern Christian Leadership Conference</a> made June 19 the "Solidarity Day of the Poor People’s Campaign".<sup id="cite_ref-:7_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-34">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWynn2009_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWynn2009-25">[24]</a></sup> In the subsequent revival, large celebrations in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> emerged<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005-35">[34]</a></sup> as well as across the Eastern United States.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-36">[35]</a></sup> In 1974 <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a> began holding large-scale celebrations again,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-17">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">Fort Worth, Texas</a>, followed the next year. Around 30,000 people attended festivities at Sycamore Park in Fort Worth the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-27">[26]</a></sup> The 1978 Milwaukee celebration was described as drawing over 100,000 attendees.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-36">[35]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prayer_Breakfast_and_Commemorative_Celebration">Prayer Breakfast and Commemorative Celebration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juneteenth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prayer Breakfast and Commemorative Celebration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In 1979, Democratic State Representative <a href="/wiki/Al_Edwards_(politician)" title="Al Edwards (politician)">Al Edwards</a> of <a href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a> successfully sponsored legislation to make Juneteenth a paid Texas state holiday. The same year he hosted the inaugural Al Edwards’ Prayer Breakfast and Commemorative Celebration on the grounds of the 1859 home, Ashton Villa. As one of the few existing buildings from the Civil War era and popular in local myth and legend as the location of Major General Granger’s announcement, Edwards’ annual celebration includes a local historian dressed as the Union general<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> reading General Order No. 3 from the second story balcony of the home. The Emancipation Proclamation is also read and speeches are made.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Representative Al Edwards died of natural causes April 29, 2020 at the age of 83,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> but the annual prayer breakfast and commemorative celebration continued at Ashton Villa with the late legislator's son, Jason Edwards, speaking in his father’s place.,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_recognition">Official recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juneteenth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Official recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>In the late 1970s when the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Legislature" title="Texas Legislature">Texas Legislature</a> declared Juneteenth a "holiday of significance [...] particularly to the blacks of Texas,"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilson2006239_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilson2006239-28">[27]</a></sup> it became the first state to establish Juneteenth as a state holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> The bill passed through the Texas Legislature in 1979 and was officially made a state holiday on January 1, 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWynn2009_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWynn2009-25">[24]</a></sup> Juneteenth is a "partial staffing" holiday in Texas; government offices do not close but agencies may operate with reduced staff, and employees may either celebrate this holiday or substitute it with one of four "optional holidays" recognized by Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="now contains info about FY 2020–21; says nothing about Juneteenth (Emancpation Day) being 'partial' or 'optional' (December 2020)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup> In the late 1980s, there were major celebrations of Juneteenth in California, Wisconsin, Illinois, Georgia, and Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-17">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1996, the first legislation to recognize "Juneteenth Independence Day" was introduced in the U.S. House of Representatives, H.J. Res. 195, sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Barbara-Rose_Collins" title="Barbara-Rose Collins">Barbara-Rose Collins</a> (D-MI). In 1997 Congress recognized the day through Senate Joint Resolution 11 and House Joint Resolution 56. In 2013, the U.S. Senate passed Senate Resolution 175, acknowledging Lula Briggs Galloway (late president of the National Association of Juneteenth Lineage) who "successfully worked to bring national recognition to Juneteenth Independence Day", and the continued leadership of the National Juneteenth Observance Foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-sr175_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sr175-45">[44]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Activists are pushing Congress to recognize Juneteenth as a national holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> Organizations such as the National Juneteenth Observance Foundation are seeking a Congressional designation of Juneteenth as a national day of observance.<sup id="cite_ref-gates_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gates-15">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2020, state governors of Virginia, New York, and New Jersey signed an executive order recognizing Juneteenth as a paid day of leave for state employees.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_growth">Subsequent growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Juneteenth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subsequent growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <p>Since the 1980s and 1990s, the holiday has been more widely celebrated among African-American communities and has seen increasing mainstream attention in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight2011_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight2011-30">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-50">[49]</a></sup> In 1991, there was an exhibition by the <a href="/wiki/Anacostia_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Anacostia Museum">Anacostia Museum</a> (part of the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>) called “Juneteenth ’91, Freedom Revisited”.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005-35">[34]</a></sup> In 1994, a group of community leaders gathered at Christian Unity Baptist Church in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> to work for greater national celebration of Juneteenth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight2011_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight2011-30">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-50">[49]</a></sup> Expatriates have celebrated it in cities abroad, such as Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-moskin2004_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moskin2004-51">[50]</a></sup> Some US military bases in other countries sponsor celebrations, in addition to those of private groups.<sup id="cite_ref-moskin2004_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moskin2004-51">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jtcom02_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtcom02-52">[51]</a></sup> In 1999, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Ellison" title="Ralph Ellison">Ralph Ellison</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Juneteenth_(novel)" title="Juneteenth (novel)">Juneteenth</a></i> was published, increasing recognition of the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuzzio1999_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuzzio1999-53">[52]</a></sup> By 2006, at least 200 cities celebrated the day.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005-35">[34]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The holiday has gained mainstream awareness outside African-American communities through depictions in entertainment media, such as episodes of TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Atlanta_(TV_series)#ep9" title="Atlanta (TV series)">Atlanta</a></i> (2016)<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Black-ish_(season_4)#Episodes" title="Black-ish (season 4)">Black-ish</a></i> (2017),<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> the latter of which featured musical numbers about the holiday by <a href="/wiki/Aloe_Blacc" title="Aloe Blacc">Aloe Blacc</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Fonzworth_Bentley" title="Fonzworth Bentley">Fonzworth Bentley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> In 2018 <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> added Juneteenth to its calendars in <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> under official US holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup> In 2020, several American corporations and educational institutions including <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a>, announced that they would treat Juneteenth as a company holiday, providing a paid day off to their workers,<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Calendar" title="Google Calendar">Google Calendar</a> added Juneteenth to its US Holidays calendar.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> Also in 2020, a number of major universities formally recognized Juneteenth,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LyCNN_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LyCNN-63">[62]</a></sup> either as a "day of reflection" or as a university holiday with paid time off for faculty and staff.<sup id="cite_ref-LyCNN_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LyCNN-63">[62]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>In 2020, in the midst of the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a> and the worldwide protests sparked by the police killing of <a href="/wiki/George_Floyd" title="George Floyd">George Floyd</a>, controversy ensued when <a href="/wiki/President_Donald_Trump" class="mw-redirect" title="President Donald Trump">President Donald Trump</a> scheduled his first political rally since the pandemic's outbreak for Juneteenth in <a href="/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Tulsa, Oklahoma">Tulsa, Oklahoma</a>, site of the 1921 <a href="/wiki/Tulsa_race_massacre" title="Tulsa race massacre">race massacre</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Greenwood_District,_Tulsa" title="Greenwood District, Tulsa">Greenwood district</a>. In response, he rescheduled the rally for the following day.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup> In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i>, Trump said, "I did something good: I made Juneteenth very famous. It’s actually an important event, an important time. But nobody had ever heard of it."<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Recognition of Juneteenth varies across the United States. It is not <a href="/wiki/Federal_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal holiday">officially recognized by the federal government</a>, although the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> unanimously passed a <a href="/wiki/Simple_resolution" title="Simple resolution">simple resolution</a> in 2018 in honor of the day,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup> and legislation has been introduced in Congress several times to make it either a "national day of observance" (akin to <a href="/wiki/Flag_Day_(United_States)" title="Flag Day (United States)">Flag Day</a> or <a href="/wiki/Patriot_Day" title="Patriot Day">Patriot Day</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[69]</a></sup> or a full-scale federal holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-Shabad_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shabad-71">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Most states recognize it in some way, either as a ceremonial observance or a state holiday. Texas was the first state to recognize the date, in 1980. By 2002, eight states officially recognized Juneteenth<sup id="cite_ref-:6_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-73">[72]</a></sup> and four years later 15 states recognized the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilson2006239_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilson2006239-28">[27]</a></sup> By 2008, nearly half of states observed the holiday as a ceremonial observance.<sup id="cite_ref-cruz_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cruz-12">[11]</a></sup> By 2019, 47 states and the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a> recognized Juneteenth,<sup id="cite_ref-Shabad_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shabad-71">[70]</a></sup> although only one state (Texas) has adopted the holiday as a paid holiday for state employees.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[73]</a></sup> In 2020, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> Governor <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Baker" title="Charlie Baker">Charles Baker</a> issued a proclamation that the day would be marked as "Juneteenth Independence Day". This followed the filing of bills by both the House and Senate to make Juneteenth a state holiday. Baker did not comment on these bills specifically, but promised to grant the observance of Juneteenth greater importance.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[74]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The only three states yet to legally recognize Juneteenth as either a state or ceremonial holiday are Hawaii, North Dakota, and South Dakota.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-76">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[76]</a></sup> Governor Kristi Noem of South Dakota proclaimed June 19, 2020, as Juneteenth Day, spurring calls for it to be recognized annually, rather than just for 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[77]</a></sup> Similarly, North Dakota Governor <a href="/wiki/Doug_Burgum" title="Doug Burgum">Doug Burgum</a> announced that the state would formally recognize Friday, June 19, 2020, as Juneteenth Day in North Dakota for the year 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[78]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Some cities and counties have recognized Juneteenth through proclamation. In 2020, Juneteenth was formally recognized by <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> (as an annual official city holiday and public school holiday, starting in 2021);<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[79]</a></sup> by the <a href="/wiki/City_and_County_of_Honolulu" class="mw-redirect" title="City and County of Honolulu">City and County of Honolulu</a> (as an "annual day of honor and reflection"),<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[80]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a> (as a day of remembrance and action and a paid holiday for city employees).<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[81]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Some private companies have adopted Juneteenth as a paid day off for employees, while others have officially marked the day in other ways, such as <a href="/wiki/Moments_of_silence" class="mw-redirect" title="Moments of silence">moments of silence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[82]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[83]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The holiday is considered the "longest-running African-American holiday"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight2011_30-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight2011-30">[29]</a></sup> and has been called "America's second Independence Day". Juneteenth is usually celebrated on the third Saturday in June], NYT (June 18, 2004).<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[84]</a></sup> Historian Mitch Kachun considers that celebrations of the end of slavery have three goals: "to celebrate, to educate, and to agitate".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHumeArceneaux2008156_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHumeArceneaux2008156-86">[85]</a></sup> Early celebrations consisted of <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>, fishing, and rodeos. African Americans were often prohibited from using public facilities for their celebrations, so they were often held at churches or near water. Celebrations were also characterized by elaborate large meals and people wearing their best clothing.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight2011_30-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight2011-30">[29]</a></sup> It was common for former slaves and their descendants to make a <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a> to Galveston.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005_35-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005-35">[34]</a></sup> As early festivals received news coverage, Janice Hume and Noah Arceneaux consider that they "served to assimilate African-American memories within the dominant 'American story'.<span class="nowrap">&nbsp;</span>"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHumeArceneaux2008159_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHumeArceneaux2008159-87">[86]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Observance today is primarily in local celebrations.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-88">[87]</a></sup> In many places Juneteenth has become a <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">multicultural</a> holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHumeArceneaux2008158_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHumeArceneaux2008158-89">[88]</a></sup> Traditions include public readings of the <a href="/wiki/Emancipation_Proclamation" title="Emancipation Proclamation">Emancipation Proclamation</a>, singing traditional songs such as "<a href="/wiki/Swing_Low,_Sweet_Chariot" title="Swing Low, Sweet Chariot">Swing Low, Sweet Chariot</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Lift_Every_Voice_and_Sing" title="Lift Every Voice and Sing">Lift Every Voice and Sing</a>", and reading of works by noted <a href="/wiki/Black_studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Black studies">African-American writers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Ellison" title="Ralph Ellison">Ralph Ellison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maya_Angelou" title="Maya Angelou">Maya Angelou</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-88">[87]</a></sup> Celebrations include picnics, <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">rodeos</a>, street fairs, cookouts, <a href="/wiki/Family_reunion" title="Family reunion">family reunions</a>, park parties, <a href="/wiki/Historical_reenactment" title="Historical reenactment">historical reenactments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> festivals and Miss Juneteenth contests.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKnight2011_30-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnight2011-30">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005_35-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005-35">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-moskin2004_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moskin2004-51">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[89]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[90]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_soft_drink_flavors" title="List of soft drink flavors">Strawberry soda</a> is a traditional drink associated with the celebration.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005_35-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaynes2005-35">[34]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mascogos" title="Mascogos">Mascogos</a>, the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Black_Seminoles" title="Black Seminoles">Black Seminoles</a>, who have resided in <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> since 1852, also celebrate Juneteenth.<sup id="cite_ref-El_Universal_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-El_Universal-92">[91]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Juneteenth celebrations often include lectures and exhibitions on African-American culture.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHumeArceneaux2008156_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHumeArceneaux2008156-86">[85]</a></sup> The modern holiday places much emphasis upon teaching about African-American heritage. Karen M. Thomas wrote in <i>Emerge</i> that "community leaders have latched on to [Juneteenth] to help instill a sense of heritage and pride in black youth." Celebrations are commonly accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Voter_registration_in_the_United_States" title="Voter registration in the United States">voter registration efforts</a>, the performing of plays, and retelling stories.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[92]</a></sup> The holiday is also a celebration of <a href="/wiki/Soul_food" title="Soul food">soul food</a> and other food with African-American influences. In <i>Tourism Review International</i>, Anne Donovan and Karen DeBres write that "Barbecue is the centerpiece of most Juneteenth celebrations".<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[93]</a></sup> </p>
