      <p>The <b>Cry of Dolores</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[n 1]</a></sup> (Spanish: <i lang="es-MX">Grito de Dolores</i>) occurred in <a href="/wiki/Dolores_Hidalgo" title="Dolores Hidalgo">Dolores</a>, Mexico, on 16 September 1810, when Roman Catholic priest <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Hidalgo_y_Costilla" title="Miguel Hidalgo y Costilla">Miguel Hidalgo y Costilla</a> rang his church bell and gave the <a href="/wiki/Pronunciamiento" title="Pronunciamiento">call to arms</a> that triggered the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_War_of_Independence" title="Mexican War of Independence">Mexican War of Independence</a>. </p>
      <p>Every year on the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eve" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:eve">eve</a> of Independence Day, the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Mexico" title="President of Mexico">President of Mexico</a> re-enacts the cry from the balcony of the <a href="/wiki/National_Palace_(Mexico)" title="National Palace (Mexico)">National Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, while ringing the same bell Hidalgo used in 1810. </p>

      <p>In the 1810s, what would become Mexico was still <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a>, part of the Spanish crown. The independence movement began to take shape when <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Bernardo_Guti%C3%A9rrez_de_Lara" class="mw-redirect" title="José Bernardo Gutiérrez de Lara">José Bernardo Gutiérrez de Lara</a> went to the small town of Dolores (now known as Dolores Hidalgo) and asked the local Roman Catholic priest, Miguel Hidalgo, to help initiate an effort to free New Spain from Spanish control.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2020)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>Gutiérrez de Lara went to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> for military support (being the first Mexican to do so).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Hidalgo remained in Dolores, waiting for Gutiérrez de Lara to return with military support. However, fearing arrest,<sup id="cite_ref-Kirkwood_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirkwood-2">[1]</a></sup> Hidalgo told his brother Mauricio to make the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriff</a> free the pro-independence inmates there. Mauricio and armed men set 80 inmates free in the early morning of 16 September 1810.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> Around 2:30&nbsp;a.m., Hidalgo ordered the church bells to be rung and gathered his congregation. Flanked by <a href="/wiki/Ignacio_Allende" title="Ignacio Allende">Ignacio Allende</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Aldama" title="Juan Aldama">Juan Aldama</a>, he addressed the people in front of his church, urging them to revolt. His speech became known as the "Cry of Dolores". </p>
      <p>The liberated country adopted Mexico as its official name. Mexico's independence from Spain took a decade of <a href="/wiki/Mexican_War_of_Independence" title="Mexican War of Independence">war</a>. Gutiérrez de Lara commanded and led Mexico to victory. Independence was achieved by the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Independence_of_the_Mexican_Empire" title="Declaration of Independence of the Mexican Empire">Declaration of Independence of the Mexican Empire</a> on 28 September 1821. However, Hidalgo is credited as being the "father of his country".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exact_words_and_meaning">Exact words and meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cry_of_Dolores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exact words and meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
      <p> Scholars have not been able to reach a consensus on the exact words Miguel Hidalgo said at the time. Michael Meyer has noted: </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>"The exact words of this most famous of all Mexican speeches are not known, or, rather, they are reproduced in almost as many variations as there are historians to reproduce them."<sup id="cite_ref-Meyer_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meyer-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>Meyer also argues that: </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>...the essential spirit of the message is... 'My children: a new dispensation comes to us today. Will you receive it? Will you free yourselves? Will you recover the lands stolen three hundred years ago from your forefathers by the hated Spaniards? We must act at once... Will you defend your religion and your rights as true patriots? Long live Our Lady of Guadalupe! Death to bad government! Death to the Gachupines!'<sup id="cite_ref-Meyer_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meyer-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>In contrast, William F. Cloud divides the sentiments above between both Hidalgo and the crowd: </p> 
      <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942">
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>[Hidalgo] told them that the time for action on their part had now come. When he asked, 'Will you be slaves of Napoleon or will you as patriots defend your religion, your hearths, and your rights?' there was a unanimous cry, 'We will defend to the utmost! Long live religion, long live our most holy mother of Guadalupe! Long live America! Death to bad government, and death to the Gachupines!'<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>Many believe that Hidalgo's Grito condemned the notion of monarchy and criticized the current social order in detail. In fact, his opposition was targeted to Spain and its viceroy in Mexico: that is, not against the monarchy in general but against "bad government". The Grito also emphasized loyalty to the Catholic religion, a sentiment with which both Mexican-born <a href="/wiki/Criollo_people" title="Criollo people"><i>Criollos</i></a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Peninsulars" class="mw-redirect" title="Peninsulars">Peninsulares</a></i> (native Spaniards) could sympathize. However, the strong anti-Spanish cry of "Death to Gachupines" (Gachupines being a slur given to Peninsulares) would have shocked Mexico's elites.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirkwood_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirkwood-2">[1]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_festivities">National festivities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cry_of_Dolores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: National festivities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The day of 16 September was first celebrated in 1812 in <a href="/wiki/Huichapan" title="Huichapan">Huichapan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hidalgo_(state)" title="Hidalgo (state)">Hidalgo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> It was given the status of a national holiday in the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Apatzing%C3%A1n" title="Constitution of Apatzingán">Constitution of Apatzingán</a>, ratified by the conventions of 1822 and 1824, and first celebrated nationally in 1825.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Cry of Dolores has assumed an almost mythical status.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> Since the late 20th century, the event has come to symbolize Mexican independence and to initiate Independence Day ceremonies the following day (16 September). Independence Day in Mexico is a <a href="/wiki/Fiestas_Patrias_(Mexico)" title="Fiestas Patrias (Mexico)">patriotic holiday</a>, marked by parades, concerts, patriotic programs, drum and bugle and marching band competitions, and special programs on the national and local media outlets.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Every 15 September at around 11&nbsp;p.m., the President of Mexico stands on the balcony of the <a href="/wiki/National_Palace_(Mexico)" title="National Palace (Mexico)">National Palace</a> in Mexico City and rings the same bell that Hidalgo rang in 1810, which was moved to the National Palace. The President then recites a shout of patriotism (a <a href="/wiki/Grito_Mexicano" class="mw-redirect" title="Grito Mexicano">Grito Mexicano</a>) based upon the "Grito de Dolores", with the names of the important heroes of the Mexican War of Independence who were there on that historic day. The Grito ends with the threefold shout of <i>¡Viva México!</i> </p>
      <p>The Grito often differs slightly from year to year to reflect recent sentiments, or a preference by the President for a shorter or longer shout. This is the version often recited by the President of Mexico: </p>

      <p>Beneath the balcony of the National Palace, there is a large crowd in the <a href="/wiki/Plaza_de_la_Constituci%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Plaza de la Constitución">Plaza de la Constitución</a> (also called the <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%B3calo" title="Zócalo">Zócalo</a>), to hear the recitation. The event draws up to half a million spectators from all over Mexico and tourists worldwide. After the President recites each line beginning with "¡Viva(n)!", the crowd responds by repeating, "¡Viva(n)!" </p>
      <p>After the recitation, the President rings the bell one last time and waves the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Mexico" title="Flag of Mexico">Flag of Mexico</a> to the applause of the crowd. </p>
      <p>This is followed by the playing of the <a href="/wiki/Himno_Nacional_Mexicano" title="Himno Nacional Mexicano">Mexican national anthem</a> by a military band from the Mexican Armed Forces. The crowd sings along. The ceremonies conclude with a spectacular fireworks display at the Zócalo grounds. </p>
      <p>On the morning of 16 September, or Independence Day, the <a href="/wiki/September_16_military_parade" title="September 16 military parade">national military parade</a> in honor of the holiday starts in the Zócalo and its outskirts, passes the Hidalgo Memorial and ends on the <a href="/wiki/Paseo_de_la_Reforma" title="Paseo de la Reforma">Paseo de la Reforma</a>, Mexico City’s main boulevard, passing "<a href="/wiki/El_%C3%81ngel" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ángel">El Ángel de la Independencia</a>" memorial column and other places along the way. </p>

      <p>The Grito is not always re-enacted at the National Palace; some years it is performed in Dolores Hidalgo, Guanajuato, where it originally happened. This is especially common in the final year of a President's term. </p>
      <p>President <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Calder%C3%B3n" title="Felipe Calderón">Felipe Calderón</a> made an exception by re-enacting the Grito in Dolores Hidalgo as part of the <a href="/wiki/Celebration_of_Mexican_political_anniversaries_in_2010" title="Celebration of Mexican political anniversaries in 2010">bicentennial celebrations</a> on 16 September 2010, even though he had already done so the night before from the <a href="/wiki/National_Palace_(Mexico)" title="National Palace (Mexico)">National Palace</a> balcony to launch the celebrations.<sup id="cite_ref-calderon_life_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-calderon_life-12">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-afp_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-afp-13">[12]</a></sup> As a result, in 2012, Calderón's final year as President, he did not go to Dolores Hidalgo but gave the Grito from the National Palace balcony instead. President <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Pe%C3%B1a_Nieto" title="Enrique Peña Nieto">Enrique Peña Nieto</a> did not give the Grito in Dolores Hidalgo in any of his six years as President, becoming the fourth president to break the tradition. </p>
      <p>Many presidents add their "personal touch" to the Grito and this can be controversial. President <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Fox" title="Vicente Fox">Vicente Fox</a> frequently took liberties with it, adding and removing items, addressing Mexicans in both genders, and in 2001 wishing long life to "our agreements".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> Peña Nieto gave "vivas" to victims of recent earthquakes in 2017. </p>
      <p>During Peña Nieto's presidency, the Grito became an occasion for political protest against him and his <a href="/wiki/Institutional_Revolutionary_Party" title="Institutional Revolutionary Party">Institutional Revolutionary Party</a> (PRI). On 15 September 2016, a month after the president appeared to be humiliated by U.S. Presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>, thousands of citizens marched, yelled, and carried signs. They tried to enter the <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%B3calo" title="Zócalo">Zócalo</a> during the Grito, but were blocked by a wall of soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> News outlets within Mexico failed to acknowledge the protest. The event was well-attended but opponents charge that the PRI brought <i>acarreados</i> (poor people or hand-picked party members) as a fake show of support.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Grito was also disrupted in 2006 by a demonstration called the Plantón. Crowds loyal to losing candidate <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Manuel_L%C3%B3pez_Obrador" title="Andrés Manuel López Obrador">Andrés Manuel López Obrador</a> protested alleged irregularities in the <a href="/wiki/2006_Mexican_general_election" title="2006 Mexican general election">general election</a> just concluded, and the Grito could not be delivered at the Zócalo but was spoken at the National Palace.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> López Obrador won the presidency in 2018. </p>
      <p>In 2020, the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a> caused the Grito to be done remotely.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Similar celebrations to the Presidential one occur in cities and towns throughout Mexico, and in Mexican <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_mission" title="Diplomatic mission">embassies</a> and consulates worldwide on 15 or 16 September. The chief executive, ambassador, or consul rings a bell and recites the traditional words, including the names of independence heroes and local <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/patriot" class="extiw" title="wikt:patriot">patriots</a>, and ending with the threefold shout of <i>Viva Mexico!</i> The bell rings a second time, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Mexico" title="Flag of Mexico">Mexican flag</a> is waved, and everyone sings the National Anthem, followed by fireworks. There are also celebrations in schools throughout <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and in these cases whenever the bell ringing is reenacted the school or university head utters the traditional words. </p>
