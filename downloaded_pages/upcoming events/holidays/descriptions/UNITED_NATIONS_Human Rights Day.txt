      <p><b>Human Rights Day</b> is <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_secular_observances#December" title="List of minor secular observances">celebrated</a> annually across the world on <a href="/wiki/10_December" class="mw-redirect" title="10 December">10 December</a> every year. </p>
      <p>The date was chosen to honor the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a>'s adoption and proclamation, on 10 December 1948, of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> (UDHR), the first global enunciation of <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> and one of the first major achievements of the new <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>. The formal establishment of Human Rights Day occurred at the 317th Plenary Meeting of the General Assembly on 4 December 1950, when the General Assembly declared resolution 423(V), inviting all member states and any other interested organizations to celebrate the day as they saw fit.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The day is normally marked both by high-level political conferences and meetings and by cultural events and exhibitions dealing with human rights issues. Besides, it is traditionally on 10 December that the five-yearly <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Prize_in_the_Field_of_Human_Rights" title="United Nations Prize in the Field of Human Rights">United Nations Prize in the Field of Human Rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> are awarded. Many <a href="/wiki/International_organization" title="International organization">governmental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-governmental organizations">non-governmental organizations</a> active in the human rights field also schedule special events to commemorate the day, as do many civil and social-cause organisations. </p>

      <p><a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">Human Rights</a> Day is the day in 1948 the United Nations General Assembly adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The formal inception of Human Rights Day dates from 1950, after the Assembly passed resolution 423(V) inviting all States and interested organisations to adopt 10 December of each year as Human Rights Day.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The popularity of the day can be shown by the fact that the commemorative Human Rights Day stamp issued by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Postal_Administration" title="United Nations Postal Administration">United Nations Postal Administration</a> in 1952, received approximately 200,000 advance orders.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>When the General Assembly adopted the Declaration, with 48 states in favor and eight abstentions, it was proclaimed as a "common standard of achievement for all peoples and all nations", towards which individuals and societies should "strive by progressive measures, national and international, to secure their universal and effective recognition and observance". The measure was received by both advocates and critics alike as "being more declarative than legislative, more suggestive than binding."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Although the Declaration with its broad range of <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">political, civil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic,_social_and_cultural_rights" title="Economic, social and cultural rights">economic, social and cultural rights</a> is not a binding document, it inspired more than 60 human rights instruments which together constitute an international standard of human rights. Today the general consent of all United Nations Member States on the basic Human Rights laid down in the Declaration makes it even stronger and emphasizes the relevance of Human Rights in our<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2017)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> daily lives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>The High Commissioner for Human Rights, as the main United Nations rights official and his Office plays a major role in coordinating efforts for the yearly observation of Human Rights Day: </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>Today, poverty prevails as the gravest human rights challenge in the world. Combating poverty, deprivation and exclusion is not a matter of charity, and it does not depend on how rich a country is. By tackling poverty as a matter of human rights obligation, the world will have a better chance of abolishing this scourge in our lifetime... <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">Poverty eradication</a> is an achievable goal.</p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        — <cite><a href="/wiki/UN_High_Commissioner_for_Human_Rights" class="mw-redirect" title="UN High Commissioner for Human Rights">UN High Commissioner for Human Rights</a> <a href="/wiki/Louise_Arbour" title="Louise Arbour">Louise Arbour</a>, 10 December 2006</cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>The 60th anniversary of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights occurred on 10 December 2008, and the UN Secretary-General launched a year-long campaign leading up to this anniversary.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Because the UDHR holds the world record as the most translated document (except for the Bible), organizations around the globe used the year to focus on helping people everywhere learn about their rights. </p>
      <p>On 9 December 2001, <a href="/wiki/United_States_President" class="mw-redirect" title="United States President">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> made a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_proclamation" class="mw-redirect" title="Presidential proclamation">Presidential proclamation</a> that Human Rights Week began on 9 December.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He also made the same proclamation on 10 December 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Date_variance">Date variance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Date variance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, Human Rights Day is celebrated on 21 March, in remembrance of the <a href="/wiki/Sharpeville_massacre" title="Sharpeville massacre">Sharpeville massacre</a> which took place on 21 March 1960. This massacre occurred as a result of protests against the <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">Apartheid</a> regime in South Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.gov.za/human-rights-day">South African Human Rights Day</a> was declared a national holiday when the <a href="/wiki/African_National_Congress" title="African National Congress">ANC</a> was elected as the government with <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a> as the first democratically elected leader.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Parliament's role on this day is to empower the people so that the democratic processes becomes known to all South Africans.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>It is celebrated on 11 December in <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
