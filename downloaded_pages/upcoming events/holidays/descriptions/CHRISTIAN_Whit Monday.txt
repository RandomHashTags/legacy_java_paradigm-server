      <p><b>Whit Monday</b> or <b>Pentecost Monday</b> (also known as <b>Monday of the Holy Spirit</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Holiday" title="Holiday">holiday</a> celebrated the day after <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Moveable_feast" title="Moveable feast">moveable feast</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Christian_calendar" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian calendar">Christian calendar</a>. It is moveable because it is determined by the date of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, it is the <b>Memorial of the Blessed Virgin Mary, Mother of the Church</b> Marking the resumption of Ordinary Time. </p>
      <p>Whit Monday gets its English name from "<a href="/wiki/Whitsunday" class="mw-redirect" title="Whitsunday">Whitsunday</a>", an English name for Pentecost, one of the three <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptismal</a> seasons. The origin of the name "Whit Sunday" is generally attributed to the white garments formerly worn by those newly baptized on this feast.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observance">Observance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whit_Monday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Monday after Pentecost is a <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a> in <a href="/wiki/Antigua_and_Barbuda" title="Antigua and Barbuda">Antigua and Barbuda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">The Bahamas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">The British Virgin Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">The Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis" title="Saint Kitts and Nevis">Saint Kitts and Nevis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">Saint Vincent and the Grenadines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. In many of these countries, Whit Monday is known as "the second day of Pentecost" or "the second Whitsun". </p>
      <p>In France, it became a work day for many workers from 2005 to 2007. This was to raise extra funds following the government's lack of preparation for a summertime heat wave, which led to a shortage of proper health care for the elderly.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It continues to be a "worked public holiday" in France.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a>, Whit Monday is considered to be a "favorite holiday", much like Christmas in many other countries. In Germany, Whit Monday (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Pfingstmontag</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Holy_Day_of_Obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Day of Obligation">Holy Day of Obligation</a> for <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrol" title="South Tyrol">South Tyrol</a>, it replaces the holiday of the local patron saint celebrated elsewhere in Italy. </p>
      <p>Until 1973, Whit Monday was a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Public holidays in the Republic of Ireland">public holiday in Ireland</a> (also called a bank holiday). It was a <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> until 1967. It was formally replaced by the fixed <a href="/wiki/Spring_Bank_Holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Spring Bank Holiday">Spring Bank Holiday</a> on the last Monday in May in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It was also a public holiday in various former British colonies, especially in the Pacific. It remains a public holiday in some of the countries of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Caribbean" title="Commonwealth Caribbean">Commonwealth Caribbean</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, Whit Monday was a public holiday until 2004; it was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/National_Day_of_Sweden" title="National Day of Sweden">National Day of Sweden</a> in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Although Whit Monday is a civil holiday in many countries, it was not a Catholic religious holiday any more than the other weekdays that follow <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a> Sunday. Until the <a href="/wiki/Mysterii_Paschalis" title="Mysterii Paschalis">1969 revision</a> of the <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">General Roman Calendar</a>, they were part of the <a href="/wiki/Octave_(liturgical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Octave (liturgical)">octave</a> of Pentecost, which was added in the 7th century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Monday after Pentecost is now the first day of the resumption of <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_Time" title="Ordinary Time">Ordinary Time</a>. While the details differ from diocese to diocese, the most widespread practice in Germany (where the holiday remains an obligation) was to have a compulsory <a href="/wiki/Votive_Mass" title="Votive Mass">votive Mass</a> of the Holy Spirit outranking even <a href="/wiki/Solemnities" class="mw-redirect" title="Solemnities">solemnities</a> (these would be local solemnities in that case). </p>
      <p>However, in February 2018, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a> declared that henceforth, Whit Monday will be the fixed date for the celebration of a new feast, officially known as the “Memorial of <a href="/wiki/Mary,_the_mother_of_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary, the mother of Jesus">Mary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mother_of_the_Church" title="Mother of the Church">Mother of the Church</a>” to be celebrated throughout the universal Church.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observance_in_Eastern_Orthodoxy">Observance in Eastern Orthodoxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whit_Monday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observance in Eastern Orthodoxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> Whit Monday is known as "Monday of the Holy Spirit" or "Day of the Holy Spirit" and is the first day of the <a href="/wiki/Afterfeast" title="Afterfeast">afterfeast</a> of Pentecost, being dedicated specifically to the honor of <a href="/wiki/God_in_Christianity" title="God in Christianity">God</a> the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a> and particularly in commemoration of his descent upon the apostles at Pentecost. The day following is known as Third Day of the Trinity.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the services on the Monday of the Holy Spirit many of the same hymns are sung as on the day of Pentecost itself. During the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a> the Deacon intones the same <a href="/wiki/Introit" title="Introit">introit</a> as on the day of Pentecost, and the <a href="/wiki/Dismissal_(liturgy)" title="Dismissal (liturgy)">dismissal</a> is the same as on the day of Pentecost. Special <a href="/wiki/Canon_(hymnography)" title="Canon (hymnography)">canons</a> to the Holy Spirit are chanted at <a href="/wiki/Compline" title="Compline">Compline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matins" title="Matins">Matins</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dates">Dates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whit_Monday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The table on the right provides columns giving the dates on which Whit Monday is observed in both <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Christianity" title="Eastern Christianity">Eastern Christianity</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodox">Oriental Orthodox</a> churches calculate <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Pascha</a> (Easter) differently from the West (see <a href="/wiki/Computus" class="mw-redirect" title="Computus">Computus</a>), and so the date of Whit Monday will be different most years. </p>
