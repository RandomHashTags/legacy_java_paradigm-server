      <p><b>Martin Luther King Jr. Day</b> (officially <b>Birthday of Martin Luther King, Jr.</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and sometimes referred to as <b>MLK Day</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Federal_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Federal holidays in the United States">federal holiday</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> marking the birthday of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr." title="Martin Luther King Jr.">Martin Luther King Jr.</a> It is observed on the third Monday of January each year. Born in 1929, Rev. Dr. King's actual birthday is January 15. The holiday is similar to holidays set under the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Monday_Holiday_Act" title="Uniform Monday Holiday Act">Uniform Monday Holiday Act</a>. The earliest Monday for this holiday is January 15 and the latest is January 21. </p>
      <p>King was the chief spokesperson for <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolent</a> activism in the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>, which protested racial discrimination in federal and state law. The campaign for a federal holiday in King's honor began soon after <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Martin_Luther_King_Jr." title="Assassination of Martin Luther King Jr.">his assassination</a> in 1968. President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> signed the holiday into law in 1983, and it was first observed three years later. At first, some states resisted observing the holiday as such, giving it alternative names or combining it with other holidays. It was officially observed in all 50 states for the first time in 2000. </p>

      <p>The idea of Martin Luther King Jr. Day as a holiday was promoted by <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">labor unions</a> in contract negotiations.<sup id="cite_ref-nation_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nation-2">[2]</a></sup> After King's death, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Representative" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Representative">U.S. Representative</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Conyers" title="John Conyers">John Conyers</a><sup id="cite_ref-20180110History.comBlakemore_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20180110History.comBlakemore-3">[3]</a></sup> (a Democrat from <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>) and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senator" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senator">U.S. Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Brooke" title="Edward Brooke">Edward Brooke</a> (a Republican from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>) introduced a bill in Congress to make King's birthday a national holiday. The bill first came to a vote in the U.S. House of Representatives in 1979. However, it fell five votes short of the number needed for passage.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolfen_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfen-4">[4]</a></sup> Two of the main arguments mentioned by opponents were that a paid holiday for federal employees would be too expensive and that a holiday to honor a private citizen would be contrary to longstanding tradition (King had never held public office).<sup id="cite_ref-Wolfen_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfen-4">[4]</a></sup> Only two other figures have national holidays in the U.S. honoring them: <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>. </p>

      <p>Soon after, the <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr._National_Historic_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King Jr. National Historic Site">King Center</a> turned to support from the corporate community and the general public. The success of this strategy was cemented when musician <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> released the single "<a href="/wiki/Happy_Birthday_(Stevie_Wonder_song)" title="Happy Birthday (Stevie Wonder song)">Happy Birthday</a>" to popularize the campaign in 1980 and hosted the Rally for Peace Press Conference in 1981. Six million signatures were collected for a petition to Congress to pass the law, termed by a 2006 article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i> as "the largest petition in favor of an issue in U.S. history".<sup id="cite_ref-nation_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nation-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Senators <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Helms" title="Jesse Helms">Jesse Helms</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Porter_East" title="John Porter East">John Porter East</a> (both <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republicans</a>) led the opposition to the holiday and questioned whether King was important enough to receive such an honor. Helms criticized King's opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> and accused him of espousing "action-oriented <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-dewar_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dewar-5">[5]</a></sup> Helms led a <a href="/wiki/Filibuster" title="Filibuster">filibuster</a> against the bill and on October 3, 1983, submitted a 300-page document to the Senate alleging that King had associations with <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communists</a>. Democratic New York Senator <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Patrick_Moynihan" title="Daniel Patrick Moynihan">Daniel Patrick Moynihan</a> declared the document a "packet of filth", threw it on the Senate floor and stomped on it.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_passage">Federal passage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Federal passage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">President Ronald Reagan</a> originally opposed the holiday, citing cost concerns. When asked to comment on Helms' accusations that King was a communist, the president said "We'll know in thirty-five years, won't we?", referring to the eventual release of <a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a> surveillance tapes that had previously been <a href="/wiki/Record_sealing" title="Record sealing">sealed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> But on November 2, 1983, Reagan signed a bill into law, proposed by <a href="/wiki/Katie_Hall_(politician)" title="Katie Hall (politician)">Representative Katie Hall</a> of Indiana, to create a federal holiday honoring King.<sup id="cite_ref-reagan_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reagan-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-statute_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-statute-10">[10]</a></sup> The final vote in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> on August 2, 1983 was 338–90 (242–4 in the <a href="/wiki/House_Democratic_Caucus" title="House Democratic Caucus">House Democratic Caucus</a> and 89–77 in the <a href="/wiki/House_Republican_Conference" title="House Republican Conference">House Republican Conference</a>) with 5 members voting present or abstaining,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dewar_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dewar-5">[5]</a></sup> while the final vote in the Senate on October 19, 1983 was 78–22 (41–4 in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_Democratic_Caucus" title="Senate Democratic Caucus">Senate Democratic Caucus</a> and 37–18 in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_Republican_Conference" title="Senate Republican Conference">Senate Republican Conference</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> both <a href="/wiki/Veto" title="Veto">veto</a>-proof margins. The holiday was observed for the first time on January 20, 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-statute_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-statute-10">[10]</a></sup> It is observed on the third Monday of January.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The bill also established the Martin Luther King Jr. Federal Holiday Commission to oversee observance of the holiday, and <a href="/wiki/Coretta_Scott_King" title="Coretta Scott King">Coretta Scott King</a>, King's wife, was made a member of this commission for life by <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">President George H. W. Bush</a> in May 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-bush_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bush-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State-level_passage">State-level passage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: State-level passage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Although the federal holiday honoring King was signed into law in 1983 and took effect three years later, not every U.S. state chose to observe the January holiday at the state level<sup id="cite_ref-20180110History.comBlakemore_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20180110History.comBlakemore-3">[3]</a></sup> until 1991, when the <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> legislature created "Civil Rights Day" and abolished its April "<a href="/wiki/Fast_Day" title="Fast Day">Fast Day</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-nhgov_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhgov-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1999, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> became the last state to name a holiday after King, which they first celebrated in January 2000&nbsp;– the first nation-wide celebration of the day with this name.<sup id="cite_ref-N.H.'s_Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day_Didn't_Happen_Without_A_Fight_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N.H.'s_Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day_Didn't_Happen_Without_A_Fight-18">[18]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1986, Arizona <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Arizona" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Governors of Arizona">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Babbitt" title="Bruce Babbitt">Bruce Babbitt</a>, a Democrat, created a paid state MLK holiday in Arizona by executive order just before he left office, but in 1987, his Republican successor <a href="/wiki/Evan_Mecham" title="Evan Mecham">Evan Mecham</a>, citing an attorney general's opinion that Babbitt's order was illegal, reversed Babbitt's decision days after taking office.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Later that year, Mecham proclaimed the third Sunday in January to be "Martin Luther King Jr./Civil Rights Day" in Arizona, albeit as an unpaid holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1990, Arizona voters were given the opportunity to vote on giving state employees a paid MLK holiday. That same year, the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> threatened to move <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXVII" title="Super Bowl XXVII">Super Bowl XXVII</a>, which was planned for Arizona in 1993, if the MLK holiday was voted down.<sup id="cite_ref-TucsonSentinel_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TucsonSentinel-21">[21]</a></sup> In the November election, the voters were offered two King Day options: Proposition 301, which replaced <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day" title="Columbus Day">Columbus Day</a> on the list of paid state holidays, and Proposition 302, which merged <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Birthday" title="Lincoln's Birthday">Lincoln's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington%27s_Birthday" title="Washington's Birthday">Washington's</a> birthdays into one paid holiday to make room for MLK Day. Both measures failed to pass, with only 49% of voters approving Prop 302, the more popular of the two options; although some who voted "no" on 302 voted "yes" on Prop 301.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Consequently, the state lost the chance to host Super Bowl XXVII, which was subsequently held at the <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bowl_(stadium)" title="Rose Bowl (stadium)">Rose Bowl</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pasadena,_California" title="Pasadena, California">Pasadena, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TucsonSentinel_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TucsonSentinel-21">[21]</a></sup> In a 1992 referendum, the voters, this time given only one option for a paid King Day, approved state-level recognition of the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-Reingold2000_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reingold2000-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On May 2, 2000, South Carolina governor <a href="/wiki/Jim_Hodges" title="Jim Hodges">Jim Hodges</a> signed a bill to make King's birthday an official state holiday. South Carolina was the last state to recognize the day as a paid holiday for all state employees. Before the bill, employees could choose between celebrating Martin Luther King Jr. Day or one of three <a href="/wiki/Confederate_holidays" class="mw-redirect" title="Confederate holidays">Confederate holidays</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_names">Alternative names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alternative names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>While all states now observe the holiday, some did not name the day after King. For example, in New Hampshire, the holiday was known as "Civil Rights Day" until 1999, when the State Legislature voted to change the name of the holiday to Martin Luther King Day.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt99_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt99-25">[25]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Several additional states have chosen to combine commemorations of King's birthday with other observances: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Lee_Day" title="Robert E. Lee Day">Robert E. Lee</a>/Martin Luther King Birthday".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>: "Martin Luther King Jr./Civil Rights Day".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>: it was known as "Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr.'s Birthday and <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Lee_Day" title="Robert E. Lee Day">Robert E. Lee's Birthday</a>" from 1985 to 2017. Legislation in March 2017 changed the name of the state holiday to "Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr.'s Birthday" and moved the commemoration of Lee to October.</li> 
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>: "Martin Luther King Jr.–<a href="/wiki/Idaho_Human_Rights_Day" title="Idaho Human Rights Day">Idaho Human Rights Day</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>: "Martin Luther King's and Robert E. Lee's Birthdays".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>: "Martin Luther King Jr. Civil Rights Day".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>: it was known as <a href="/wiki/Lee%E2%80%93Jackson%E2%80%93King_Day" title="Lee–Jackson–King Day">Lee–Jackson–King Day</a>, combining King's birthday with the established <a href="/wiki/Lee%E2%80%93Jackson_Day" title="Lee–Jackson Day">Lee–Jackson Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newcrisis_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newcrisis-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2000, Lee–Jackson Day was moved to the Friday before Martin Luther King Jr. Day, establishing Martin Luther King Jr. Day as a holiday in its own right.<sup id="cite_ref-lege_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lege-32">[32]</a></sup> Lee-Jackson day was eliminated in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>In <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>: it is known as "Martin Luther King Jr./Wyoming Equality Day". <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Elizabeth_Byrd" title="Harriet Elizabeth Byrd">Liz Byrd</a>, the first black woman in Wyoming legislature, introduced a bill in 1991 for Wyoming to recognize MLK day as a paid state holiday; however, she compromised on the name because her peers would not pass it otherwise.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observance">Observance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Observance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Workplace_leave">Workplace leave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Workplace leave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">

      <p>Overall, in 2007, 33% of employers gave employees the day off, a 2% increase over the previous year. There was little difference in observance by large and small employers: 33% for firms with over 1,000 employees; and, 32% for firms with under 1,000 employees. The observance is most popular among nonprofit organizations and least popular among factories and manufacturers.<sup id="cite_ref-bna_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bna-35">[35]</a></sup> The reasons for this have varied, ranging from the recent addition of the holiday to its occurrence just two weeks after the week between <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">New Year's Day</a>, when many businesses are closed for part or all of it. Additionally, many schools and places of higher education are closed for classes; others remain open but may hold seminars or celebrations of King's message. The observance of MLK Day has led to some colleges and universities extending their Christmas break to include the day as part of the break. Some employers use MLK Day as a <a href="/wiki/Long_weekend" title="Long weekend">floating or movable holiday</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="King_Day_of_Service">King Day of Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: King Day of Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:MLK_service_obama.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/MLK_service_obama.JPG/220px-MLK_service_obama.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/MLK_service_obama.JPG/330px-MLK_service_obama.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/MLK_service_obama.JPG/440px-MLK_service_obama.JPG 2x" data-file-width="560" data-file-height="373"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:MLK_service_obama.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> serving lunch at a Washington soup kitchen on MLK Jr. Day, 2010
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>The national Martin Luther King Day of Service<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> was started by former Pennsylvania U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Harris_Wofford" title="Harris Wofford">Harris Wofford</a> and Atlanta Congressman <a href="/wiki/John_Lewis" title="John Lewis">John Lewis</a>, who co-authored the King Holiday and Service Act. The federal legislation challenges Americans to transform the King Holiday into a day of citizen action <a href="/wiki/Volunteering" title="Volunteering">volunteer</a> service in honor of King. The federal legislation was signed into law by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">President Bill Clinton</a> on August 23, 1994. Since 1996, Wofford's former state office director, <a href="/wiki/Todd_Bernstein" title="Todd Bernstein">Todd Bernstein</a>, has been directing the annual Greater Philadelphia King Day of Service,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> the largest event in the nation honoring King.<sup id="cite_ref-slt_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slt-39">[39]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Since 1994, the day of service has been coordinated nationally by the <a href="/wiki/Corporation_for_National_and_Community_Service" title="Corporation for National and Community Service">Corporation for National and Community Service</a>, a federal agency, which provides grants to organizations that coordinate service activities on MLK Day.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The only other official national day of service in the U.S., as designated by the government, is <a href="/wiki/September_11_National_Day_of_Service" title="September 11 National Day of Service">September 11 National Day of Service</a> (9/11 Day).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_the_United_States">Outside the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Outside the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Municipal_government_of_Toronto" title="Municipal government of Toronto">City of Toronto government</a> in Ontario officially recognizes Martin Luther King Jr. Day, although not as a paid holiday: all government services and businesses remain open.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/City_of_Ottawa" title="City of Ottawa">Ottawa municipal government</a> in Ontario officially began observing this national holiday on January 26, 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In 1984, during a visit by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Sixth_Fleet" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Sixth Fleet">U.S. Sixth Fleet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Navy_Chaplain_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="Navy Chaplain Corps">Navy chaplain</a> Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Resnicoff" title="Arnold Resnicoff">Arnold Resnicoff</a> conducted the first Israeli presidential ceremony in commemoration of Martin Luther King Jr. Day, held in the <a href="/wiki/Beit_HaNassi" title="Beit HaNassi">President's Residence</a>, Jerusalem. <a href="/wiki/Aura_Herzog" title="Aura Herzog">Aura Herzog</a>, wife of Israel's then-President <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Herzog" title="Chaim Herzog">Chaim Herzog</a>, noted that she was especially proud to host this special event, because Israel had a national forest in honor of King, and that Israel and King shared the idea of "dreams".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Resnicoff continued this theme in his remarks during the ceremony, quoting the verse from Genesis, spoken by the brothers of Joseph when they saw their brother approach, "Behold the dreamer comes; let us slay him and throw him into the pit, and see what becomes of his dreams." Resnicoff noted that, from time immemorial, there have been those who thought they could kill the dream by slaying the dreamer, but – as the example of King's life shows – such people are always wrong.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Martin Luther King Jr. Day is observed in the Japanese city of <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a>. Mayor <a href="/wiki/Tadatoshi_Akiba" title="Tadatoshi Akiba">Tadatoshi Akiba</a> held a special banquet at the mayor's office as an act of unifying his city's call for peace with King's message of human rights.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Every year since 1987, the Dr. Martin Luther King Tribute and Dinner has been held in <a href="/wiki/Wassenaar" title="Wassenaar">Wassenaar</a>, The Netherlands.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The Tribute includes young people and veterans of the Civil Rights Movement as well as music. It always ends with everyone holding hands in a circle and singing "We Shall Overcome". The Tribute is held on the last Sunday in January.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> </p>
