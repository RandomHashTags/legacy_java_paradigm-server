   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading">Mole Day</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">

       Unofficial holiday
      </div> 
      <p> <b>Mole Day</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Unofficial_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Unofficial holiday">unofficial holiday</a> celebrated among chemists, chemistry students and chemistry enthusiasts on October 23, between 6:02&nbsp;a.m. and 6:02&nbsp;p.m.,<sup id="cite_ref-ncw_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncw-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> making the date 6:02 10/23 in the <a href="/wiki/Date_and_time_notation_in_the_United_States" title="Date and time notation in the United States">American style of writing dates</a>. The time and date are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Avogadro_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Avogadro number">Avogadro number</a>, which is approximately <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7023602000000000000♠"></span>6.02<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup></span>, defining the number of particles (atoms or molecules) in one <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> (mol) of <a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">substance</a>, one of the seven base <a href="/wiki/SI" class="mw-redirect" title="SI">SI</a> units. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Mole Day originated in an article in <i>The Science Teacher</i> in the early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-mole-history_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mole-history-3">[3]</a></sup> Inspired by this article, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.garrityfuneralhome.com/obituary/Maurice-Oehler">Maurice Oehler</a>, a <a href="/wiki/High_school_(North_America)" title="High school (North America)">high school</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> teacher from <a href="/wiki/Prairie_du_Chien,_Wisconsin" title="Prairie du Chien, Wisconsin">Prairie du Chien, Wisconsin</a>, founded the National Mole Day Foundation (NMDF) on May 15, 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-mole-history_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mole-history-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Many high schools around the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> celebrate Mole Day as a way to get their students interested in chemistry, with various activities often related to chemistry or <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mole-history_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mole-history-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a> sponsors <a href="/wiki/National_Chemistry_Week" title="National Chemistry Week">National Chemistry Week</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ncw_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncw-1">[1]</a></sup> which occurs from the Sunday through Saturday during which October 23 falls. This makes Mole Day an integral part of National Chemistry Week. </p>
