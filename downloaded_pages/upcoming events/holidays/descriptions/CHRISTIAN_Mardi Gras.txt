      <p><b>Mardi Gras</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɑːr/: 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span><span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span></span>/</a></span></span>), or <b>Fat Tuesday</b>, refers to events of the <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a> celebration, beginning on or after the Christian feasts of the <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">Epiphany</a> (Three Kings Day) and culminating on the day before <a href="/wiki/Ash_Wednesday" title="Ash Wednesday">Ash Wednesday</a>, which is known as <a href="/wiki/Shrove_Tuesday" title="Shrove Tuesday">Shrove Tuesday</a>. <i lang="fr" title="French-language text">Mardi Gras</i> is <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> for "Fat Tuesday", reflecting the practice of the last night of eating rich, fatty foods before the ritual <a href="/wiki/Lenten_sacrifice" title="Lenten sacrifice">Lenten sacrifices</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lenten</a> season. </p>
      <p>Related popular practices are associated with <a href="/wiki/Shrovetide" title="Shrovetide">Shrovetide</a> celebrations before the fasting and religious obligations associated with the <a href="/wiki/Penitential" title="Penitential">penitential</a> season of Lent. In countries such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, Mardi Gras is also known as Shrove Tuesday, which is derived from the word <i>shrive</i>, meaning "to administer the sacrament of <a href="/wiki/Confession_(religion)" title="Confession (religion)">confession</a> to; to absolve".<sup id="cite_ref-Melitta_Weiss_Adamson,_Francine_Segan_2008_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Melitta_Weiss_Adamson,_Francine_Segan_2008-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2> 
      <p>Some think Mardi Gras may be linked<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> with the ancient Roman <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> celebrations of spring and fertility such as <a href="/wiki/Saturnalia" title="Saturnalia">Saturnalia</a>, which dates back to 133–31 <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">BC</a>. This celebration honored the god of agriculture, Saturn. It was observed in mid-December, before the sowing of winter crops. It was a week-long festival when work and business came to a halt. Schools and courts of law closed, and the normal social patterns were suspended. On the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>, which the Romans adopted in 45 BC,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the winter solstice fell on December 25. Hence, the celebration gradually became associated with Christmas.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">Traditions</span></h2> 
      <p>The festival season varies from city to city, as some traditions, such as the one in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, consider Mardi Gras to stretch the entire period from <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Night_(holiday)" title="Twelfth Night (holiday)">Twelfth Night</a> (the last night of Christmas which begins <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(holiday)" title="Epiphany (holiday)">Epiphany</a>) to Ash Wednesday.<sup id="cite_ref-carnivalterminology_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carnivalterminology-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wild/Dufour/Cowan_(seas/New_Orl.)_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wild/Dufour/Cowan_(seas/New_Orl.)-6">[6]</a></sup> Others treat the final three-day period before Ash Wednesday as the Mardi Gras.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Mardi_Gras_in_Mobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Mardi Gras in Mobile">Mobile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, Mardi Gras–associated social events begin in November, followed by <a href="/wiki/Mystic_society" title="Mystic society">mystic society</a> balls on <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">Thanksgiving</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-carnivalterminology_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carnivalterminology-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MCA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCA-8">[8]</a></sup> then New Year's Eve, followed by parades and balls in January and February, celebrating up to midnight before <a href="/wiki/Ash_Wednesday" title="Ash Wednesday">Ash Wednesday</a>. In earlier times, parades were held on New Year's Day.<sup id="cite_ref-carnivalterminology_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carnivalterminology-5">[5]</a></sup> Carnival is an important celebration in <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> European nations.<sup id="cite_ref-Melitta_Weiss_Adamson,_Francine_Segan_2008_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Melitta_Weiss_Adamson,_Francine_Segan_2008-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Czech_Republic">Czech Republic</span></h3> 
      <p>In the Czech Republic it is a folk tradition to celebrate Mardi Gras, which is called Masopust (meat-fast i.e. beginning of fast there). There are celebration in many places including Prague<sup id="cite_ref-Masopust_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Masopust-9">[9]</a></sup> but the tradition also prevails in the villages such as <a href="/wiki/Star%C3%A9_Hamry" title="Staré Hamry">Staré Hamry</a>, whose the door-to-door processions there made it to the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Intangible_Cultural_Heritage_List" class="mw-redirect" title="UNESCO World Intangible Cultural Heritage List">UNESCO World Intangible Cultural Heritage List</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>

       Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Carnival_in_Germany,_Switzerland_and_Austria" title="Carnival in Germany, Switzerland and Austria">Karneval, Fasching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swabian%E2%80%93Alemannic_Fastnacht" class="mw-redirect" title="Swabian–Alemannic Fastnacht">Fastnacht</a>
      </div> 
      <p>The celebration on the same day in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> knows many different terms, such as <i>Schmutziger Donnerstag</i> or <i>Fetter Donnerstag</i> (Fat Thursday), <i>Unsinniger Donnerstag</i>, <i>Weiberfastnacht</i>, <i>Greesentag</i> and others, and are often only one part of the whole carnival events during one or even two weeks before Ash Wednesday be called <a href="/wiki/Carnival_in_Germany,_Switzerland_and_Austria" title="Carnival in Germany, Switzerland and Austria">Karneval, Fasching</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Swabian%E2%80%93Alemannic_Fastnacht" class="mw-redirect" title="Swabian–Alemannic Fastnacht">Fastnacht</a> among others, depending on the region. In <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">standard German</a>, <i>schmutzig</i> means "dirty", but in the Alemannic dialects <i>schmotzig</i> means "lard" (<a href="/wiki/Schmalz" class="mw-redirect" title="Schmalz">Schmalz</a>), or "fat";<sup id="cite_ref-Schmotzig_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schmotzig-11">[11]</a></sup> "Greasy Thursday", as remaining winter stores of lard and butter used to be consumed at that time, before the fasting began. <i>Fastnacht</i> means "Eve of the Fast", but all three terms cover the whole carnival season. The traditional start of the carnival season is on 11 November at 11:11 am (11/11 11:11). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span></h3> 
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> Mardi Gras is called Martedì Grasso (Fat Tuesday). It's the main day of <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a> along with the Thursday before, called Giovedí Grasso (Fat Thursday), which ratifies the start of the celebrations. The most famous <a href="/wiki/Carnivals" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnivals">Carnivals</a> in northern Italy are in Venice, Viareggio and Ivrea, while in the southern part of Italy the Sardinian <a href="/wiki/Sartiglia" class="mw-redirect" title="Sartiglia">Sartiglia</a> and the intriguing apotropaic masks, especially the mamuthones, issohadores, s'urtzu (and so on), are more popular, belonging to a very ancient tradition. Ivrea has the characteristic "<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Oranges" title="Battle of the Oranges">Battle of Oranges</a>" that finds its roots in medieval times. The Italian version of the festival is spelled Carnevale.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span></h3> 
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> the celebration is called Fettisdagen, when fastlagsbulle is eaten, more commonly called <a href="/wiki/Semla" title="Semla">Semla</a>. The name comes from the words "fett" (fat) and "tisdag" (Tuesday). Originally, this was the only day one should eat <a href="/wiki/Fastlagsbulle" class="mw-redirect" title="Fastlagsbulle">fastlagsbullar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-semla_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-semla-13">[13]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       See also: <a href="/wiki/Mardi_Gras_in_the_United_States" title="Mardi Gras in the United States">Mardi Gras in the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mardi_Gras_in_Mobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Mardi Gras in Mobile">Mardi Gras in Mobile</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mardi_Gras_in_New_Orleans" title="Mardi Gras in New Orleans">Mardi Gras in New Orleans</a>
      </div> 
      <p>While not observed nationally throughout the United States, a number of traditionally ethnic French cities and regions in the country have notable celebrations. Mardi Gras arrived in North America as a <a href="/wiki/French_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="French Catholic">French Catholic</a> tradition with the Le Moyne brothers,<sup id="cite_ref-MGtime_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MGtime-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Le_Moyne_d%27Iberville" title="Pierre Le Moyne d'Iberville">Pierre Le Moyne d'Iberville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Le_Moyne_de_Bienville" title="Jean-Baptiste Le Moyne de Bienville">Jean-Baptiste Le Moyne de Bienville</a>, in the late 17th century, when <a href="/wiki/King_Louis_XIV" class="mw-redirect" title="King Louis XIV">King Louis XIV</a> sent the pair to defend France's claim on the territory of <i><a href="/wiki/Louisiana_(New_France)" title="Louisiana (New France)">Louisiane</a></i>, which included what are now the U.S. states of <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and part of eastern <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MGtime_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MGtime-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The expedition, led by Iberville, entered the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> on the evening of 2 March 1699 (new style), <a href="/wiki/Lundi_Gras" title="Lundi Gras">Lundi Gras</a>. They did not yet know it was the river explored and claimed for France by <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier,_Sieur_de_La_Salle" title="René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle">René-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle</a> in 1683. The party proceeded upstream to a place on the east bank about 60 miles (100&nbsp;km) downriver from where <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> is today, and made camp. This was on 3 March 1699, Mardi Gras, so in honour of this holiday, Iberville named the spot <i>Point du Mardi Gras</i> (French: "Mardi Gras Point") and called the nearby tributary Bayou Mardi Gras.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Le_Moyne_de_Bienville" title="Jean-Baptiste Le Moyne de Bienville">Bienville</a> went on to found the settlement of <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile, Alabama</a> in 1702 as the first capital of French Louisiana.<sup id="cite_ref-TLhist_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TLhist-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1703 French settlers in Mobile established the <a href="/wiki/Mardi_Gras_in_Mobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Mardi Gras in Mobile">first organised Mardi Gras</a> celebration tradition in what was to become the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-MGtime_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MGtime-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MoMtime_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoMtime-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LOCgras_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOCgras-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MBAYgras_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MBAYgras-19">[19]</a></sup> The first informal <a href="/wiki/Mystic_society" title="Mystic society">mystic society</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Krewe" title="Krewe">krewe</a>, was formed in Mobile in 1711, the <i>Boeuf Gras Society</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-MoMtime_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoMtime-17">[17]</a></sup> By 1720, <a href="/wiki/Biloxi,_Mississippi" title="Biloxi, Mississippi">Biloxi</a> had been made capital of Louisiana. The French Mardi Gras customs had accompanied the colonists who settled there.<sup id="cite_ref-MGtime_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MGtime-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>In 1723, the capital of Louisiana was moved to <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, founded in 1718.<sup id="cite_ref-TLhist_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TLhist-16">[16]</a></sup> The first Mardi Gras parade held in New Orleans is recorded to have taken place in 1837. The tradition in New Orleans expanded to the point that it became synonymous with the city in popular perception, and embraced by residents of New Orleans beyond those of French or Catholic heritage. Mardi Gras celebrations are part of the basis of the slogan <i><a href="/wiki/Laissez_les_bons_temps_rouler" title="Laissez les bons temps rouler">Laissez les bons temps rouler</a></i> ("Let the good times roll").<sup id="cite_ref-MGtime_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MGtime-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (February 2014)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup> On Mardi Gras Day, the Tuesday before Ash Wednesday, the last parades of the season wrap up and the celebrations come to a close with the Meeting of the Courts (known locally as the Rex Ball). Other cities along the Gulf Coast with early French colonial heritage, from <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola, Florida</a>; <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a>; to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Charles,_Louisiana" title="Lake Charles, Louisiana">Lake Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lafayette,_Louisiana" title="Lafayette, Louisiana">Lafayette, Louisiana</a>; and north to <a href="/wiki/Natchez,_Mississippi" title="Natchez, Mississippi">Natchez, Mississippi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandria,_Louisiana" title="Alexandria, Louisiana">Alexandria, Louisiana</a>, have active Mardi Gras celebrations. </p>
      <p>Galveston's first recorded Mardi Gras celebration, in 1867, included a masked ball at Turner Hall (Sealy at 21st St.) and a theatrical performance from Shakespeare's "King Henry IV" featuring Alvan Reed (a justice of the peace weighing in at 350 pounds!) as Falstaff. The first year that Mardi Gras was celebrated on a grand scale in Galveston was 1871 with the emergence of two rival Mardi Gras societies, or "Krewes" called the Knights of Momus (known only by the initials "K.O.M.") and the Knights of Myth, both of which devised night parades, masked balls, exquisite costumes and elaborate invitations. The Knights of Momus, led by some prominent Galvestonians, decorated horse-drawn wagons for a torch lit night parade. Boasting such themes as "The Crusades," "Peter the Great," and "Ancient France," the procession through downtown Galveston culminated at Turner Hall with a presentation of tableaux and a grand gala. </p>
      <p>In the rural <a href="/wiki/Acadiana" title="Acadiana">Acadiana</a> area, many <a href="/wiki/Cajuns" title="Cajuns">Cajuns</a> celebrate with the <i><a href="/wiki/Courir_de_Mardi_Gras" title="Courir de Mardi Gras">Courir de Mardi Gras</a></i>, a tradition that dates to medieval celebrations in France.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Greater_St._Louis" title="Greater St. Louis">St. Louis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, founded in 1764 by French fur traders, claims to host the second largest Mardi Gras celebration in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The celebration is held in the historic French neighborhood, <a href="/wiki/Soulard,_St._Louis" title="Soulard, St. Louis">Soulard</a>, and attracts hundreds of thousands of people from around the country.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Although founded in the 1760s, the St. Louis Mardi Gras festivities only date to the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The city's celebration begins with "12th night," held on Epiphany, and ends on Fat Tuesday. The season is peppered with various parades celebrating the city's rich French Catholic heritage.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Costumes">Costumes</span></h2>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        <div class="thumbcaption">

          <a href="/wiki/File:Mardi_Gras_Pirates_New_Orleans_Louisiana_WPA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

        </div>

      </div> 
      <p>Mardi Gras, as a celebration of life before the more-somber occasion of Ash Wednesday, nearly always involves the use of masks and costumes by its participants, and the most popular celebratory colors are purple, green, and gold. In New Orleans, for example, these often take the shape of fairies, animals, people from myths, or various Medieval costumes<sup id="cite_ref-Gabbert1999_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gabbert1999-25">[25]</a></sup> as well as clowns and Indians (<a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> However, many costumes today are simply elaborate creations of colored feathers and capes. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> costumery, Mardi Gras costumes are not usually associated with such things as zombies, mummies, bats, blood, and the like, though death may be a theme in some. The Venice tradition has brought golden masks into the usual round of costumes.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_by_women">Exposure by women</span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:MardiGras2009CoffeeCustomers.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/MardiGras2009CoffeeCustomers.JPG/170px-MardiGras2009CoffeeCustomers.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/MardiGras2009CoffeeCustomers.JPG/255px-MardiGras2009CoffeeCustomers.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/MardiGras2009CoffeeCustomers.JPG/340px-MardiGras2009CoffeeCustomers.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2112" data-file-height="2816"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:MardiGras2009CoffeeCustomers.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>A topless woman at a coffee house, Mardi Gras Day in New Orleans, 2009
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Women exposing their breasts during Mardi Gras in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, US, has been documented since 1889, when the <i>Times-Democrat</i> decried the "degree of immodesty exhibited by nearly all female masqueraders seen on the streets." The practice was mostly limited to tourists in the upper Bourbon Street area.<sup id="cite_ref-sparks_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sparks-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-shrum_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shrum-29">[29]</a></sup> In the crowded streets of the French Quarter, generally avoided by locals on Mardi Gras Day, <a href="/wiki/Exhibitionism" title="Exhibitionism">flashers</a> on balconies cause crowds to form on the streets. </p>
      <p>In the last decades of the 20th century, the rise in producing commercial <a href="/wiki/Videotape" title="Videotape">videotapes</a> catering to <a href="/wiki/Voyeurism" title="Voyeurism">voyeurs</a> helped encourage a tradition of women baring their breasts in exchange for beads and trinkets. Social scientists studying "ritual disrobement" found, at Mardi Gras 1991, 1,200 instances of body-baring in exchange for beads or other favors.<sup id="cite_ref-shrum_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shrum-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
