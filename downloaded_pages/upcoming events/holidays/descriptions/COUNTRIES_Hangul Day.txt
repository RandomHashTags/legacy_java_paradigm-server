      <p>The <b>Korean Alphabet Day</b>, known as <b>Hangeul Day</b> (한글날) in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, and <b>Chosŏn'gŭl Day</b> in <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, is a national Korean commemorative day marking the invention and the proclamation of <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a> (한글; 조선글), the <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean language</a>, by the 15th-century Korean monarch <a href="/wiki/Sejong_the_Great" title="Sejong the Great">Sejong the Great</a>. It is observed on October 9 in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and on January 15 in <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>. Excluding the years 1990 to 2012, when the government maximized business days to expedite industrial growth, Hangul day has been a national holiday in South Korea since 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hangul_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Every 9 October is selected as Hangeul Day to spread its originality and excellence in addition to commemorating Sejong the King's achievement. Even in 2020, citizens hoist the Korean national flag to honor the day. Hangeul is one of few writing systems where both the founder and founding date have been revealed. To compensate its significance, Korean government legislated the following amendment below. </p>
      <p><b>Language Amendment Section 20 (Hangeul Day)</b> </p>
      <p>1. Government shall dictate every October 9 as Hangeul Day with commemorative event in order to spread Hangeul's originality and scientific superiority, further enhancing pan-national awareness and affection towards the language. </p>
      <p>2. Matters regarding commemorative event above shall abide presidential decree. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hangul_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Korea">South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hangul_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In South Korea, the holiday is called <b>Hangeul Proclamation Day</b>, or <b>Hangeul Day</b> for short, and is celebrated on October 9 to commemorate the promulgation of the <a href="/wiki/Hunminjeongeum" title="Hunminjeongeum">Hunminjeongeum</a> on October 9, 1446.<sup id="cite_ref-강경주_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-강경주-2">[2]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Korea">North Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hangul_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: North Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In North Korea, the holiday is called <b>Chosŏn'gŭl Day</b> and is celebrated on January 15 to commemorate the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Hunminjeongeum" title="Hunminjeongeum">Hunminjeongeum</a> on January 15, 1444.<sup id="cite_ref-강경주_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-강경주-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Yanbian_Korean_Autonomous_Prefecture" title="Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture">Yanbian Korean Autonomous Prefecture</a> in China designates September 2 as the "Day of the Korean Language" since 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Before the creation of Hangul, people in Korea (known as <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> at the time) primarily wrote using <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">Classical Chinese</a> alongside native phonetic writing systems that predate Hangul by hundreds of years, including <a href="/wiki/Idu_script" title="Idu script">idu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyangchal" title="Hyangchal">hyangchal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gugyeol" title="Gugyeol">gugyeol</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gakpil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gakpil (page does not exist)">gakpil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-phonetic_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phonetic-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, due to the fundamental differences between the Korean and Chinese languages, and the large number of characters needed to be learned, there was much difficulty in learning how to write using Chinese characters for the lower classes, who often didn't have the privilege of education. To assuage this problem, King Sejong created the unique alphabet known as Hangul to promote literacy among the common people.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Hangul Proclamation Day has been celebrated annually in South Korea on October 9 since 1970. However, this does not include the period from 1990 to 2012 since people were required to work for their businesses on this day instead of taking time off. It is also celebrated in North Korea on January 15.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite all of Hangul’s benefits, it nearly went out of existence during the Choseon dynasty. The elite of the time wanting to preserve their status saw Chinese characters as the only true way to write Korean. In the early 16th century, Hangul was effectively banned by the king. However, Hangul had a resurgence in the 19th century, and gradually grew more and more common, especially due to its role in Korean nationalism during the era of Japanese occupation. By the 1970’s the use of Chinese characters declined, and these days, almost all Korean is written in Hangul.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Sejong" class="mw-redirect" title="Sejong">Sejong</a> <a href="/wiki/Annals_of_the_Joseon_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Annals of the Joseon Dynasty">Sillok</a></i> (세종실록; 世宗實綠), King Sejong proclaimed publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Hunmin_Jeongeum" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunmin Jeongeum">Hunmin Jeongeum</a></i> (훈민정음; 訓民正音), the document introducing the newly created alphabet which was also originally called by the same name, in the ninth month of the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">lunar calendar</a> in 1446. In 1926, the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Language_Society" title="Korean Language Society">Korean Language Society</a>, whose goal was to preserve the Korean language during a time of rapid forced <a href="/wiki/Japanization" title="Japanization">Japanization</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> celebrated the <a href="/wiki/Radix" title="Radix">octosexigesimal</a> (68th) anniversary of the declaration of hangeul on the last day of the ninth month of the lunar calendar, which is on November 4 of the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>. Members of the Society declared it the first observance of "Gagyanal" (가갸날). The name came from "Gagyageul" (가갸글), an early colloquial name for hangeul, based on a mnemonic recitation beginning "gagya geogyeo" (가갸거겨). The name of the commemorative day was changed to "Hangullal" in 1928, soon after the term "<a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">hangul</a>", coined originally in 1913 by <a href="/wiki/Ju_Si-gyeong" title="Ju Si-gyeong">Ju Si-gyeong</a>, became widely accepted as the new name for the alphabet. The day was then celebrated according to the lunar calendar. </p>
      <p>In 1931, the celebration of the day was switched to October 29 of the Gregorian Calendar, the calendar which is in contemporary use. Three years later, the date was moved to October 28, to coordinate the date with that of the Julian Calendar, which had been in use during the 15th century, when King Sejong had made his proclamation. </p>
      <p>The discovery in 1940 of an original copy of the <i><a href="/wiki/Hunmin_Jeongeum_Haerye" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunmin Jeongeum Haerye">Hunmin Jeongeum Haerye</a></i>, a volume of commentary to the <i>Hunmin Jeongeum</i> that appeared not long after the document it commented upon, revealed that the <i>Hunmin Jeongeum</i> was announced during the first ten days (<i>sangsun</i>; <a href="/wiki/Hangeul" class="mw-redirect" title="Hangeul">상순</a>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">上旬</a>) of the ninth month. The tenth day of the ninth month of the 1446 lunar calendar was equivalent to October 9 of that same year's <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>. The <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korean</a> government, established in 1945, declared October 9 to be Hangeul Day, a yearly legal holiday which excused government employees from work. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Chaebol" title="Chaebol">Major employers</a> pressured the South Korean government to increase the country's annual number of work days. In 1991, to balance out the adoption of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Day" title="United Nations Day">United Nations Day</a>, it vacated Hangeul Day's status as a holiday. By law, Hangeul Day remained a national commemoration day, and the Hangeul Society campaigned for the holiday's restoration. On November 1, 2012, the Society won that campaign, when the National Assembly voted 189 to 4 (with 4 abstaining) in favor of a resolution that called for the return of Hangeul Day as a national holiday. This put pressure on the Lee Myung Bak administration, which applied the change in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebrations">Celebrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hangul_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Celebrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In 2009, a heavy bronze statue of King Sejong was revealed to the public. It towers over citizens and tourists at approximately 20 feet.[11][12] Underneath the large golden statue of King Sejong the Great of Joseon, there is a museum, which many people visit on Hangul Day. Inside the museum there are many exhibits explaining the creation of the language and technological advancements made during King Sejong’s reign. There is great respect for King Sejong, since the years he ruled is known as the golden period in Korean history.
