      <p><b>Cinco de Mayo</b> (<small>pronounced&nbsp;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[ˈsiŋko̞ ðe̞ ˈma̠ʝo̞]</a></span> in Mexico, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> for "Fifth of May") is an annual celebration held on May 5. The date is observed to commemorate the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Army" title="Mexican Army">Mexican Army</a>'s victory over the <a href="/wiki/Second_French_Empire" title="Second French Empire">French Empire</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Puebla" title="Battle of Puebla">Battle of Puebla</a>, on May 5, 1862, under the leadership of General <a href="/wiki/Ignacio_Zaragoza" title="Ignacio Zaragoza">Ignacio Zaragoza</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NatGeo_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatGeo-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Congress.gov_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Congress.gov-2">[2]</a></sup> The victory of the smaller Mexican force against a larger French force was a boost to morale for the Mexicans. Zaragoza died months after the battle due to illness. A year after the battle, a larger French force defeated the Mexican army at the Second Battle of Puebla, and Mexico City soon fell to the invaders. </p>
      <p>More popularly celebrated in the United States than in Mexico,<sup id="cite_ref-clnet_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clnet-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MexCon_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MexCon-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-go_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-go-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mn_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mn-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the date has become associated with the celebration of <a href="/wiki/Mexican-American" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican-American">Mexican-American</a> culture. These celebrations began in California, where they have been observed annually since 1863. The day gained nationwide popularity in the 1980s thanks especially to advertising campaigns by beer and wine companies. Today, Cinco de Mayo generates beer sales on par with the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl" title="Super Bowl">Super Bowl</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In Mexico, the commemoration of the battle continues to be mostly ceremonial, such as through military parades or battle reenactments. The city of <a href="/wiki/Puebla_(city)" title="Puebla (city)">Puebla</a> marks the event with an arts festival, a festival of local cuisine, and re-enactments of the battle. </p>
      <p>Cinco de Mayo is sometimes mistaken for <a href="/wiki/Cry_of_Dolores" title="Cry of Dolores">Mexico's Independence Day</a>—the most important national holiday in Mexico—which is celebrated on September 16, commemorating the <a href="/wiki/Cry_of_Dolores" title="Cry of Dolores">Cry of Dolores</a>, which, in 1810, initiated the war of Mexican independence from Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-NatGeo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatGeo-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DN_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DN-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_leading_to_the_Battle_of_Puebla">Events leading to the Battle of Puebla</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Events leading to the Battle of Puebla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">

      <p>Cinco de Mayo has its roots in the <a href="/wiki/Second_French_intervention_in_Mexico" title="Second French intervention in Mexico">Second French intervention in Mexico</a>, which took place in the aftermath of the 1846–48 <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">Mexican–American War</a> and the 1858–61 <a href="/wiki/Reform_War" title="Reform War">Reform War</a>. The Reform War was a civil war that pitted <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(Mexico)" title="Liberal Party (Mexico)">Liberals</a> (who believed in <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state" title="Separation of church and state">separation of church and state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_religion" title="Freedom of religion">freedom of religion</a>) against <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(Mexico)" title="Conservative Party (Mexico)">Conservatives</a> (who favored a tight bond between the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> and the Mexican state).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These wars nearly bankrupted the Mexican Treasury. On July 17, 1861, Mexican President <a href="/wiki/Benito_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Benito Juárez">Benito Juárez</a> issued a moratorium in which all foreign debt payments would be suspended for two years.<sup id="cite_ref-clnet_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clnet-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-inside-mexico_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside-mexico-11">[11]</a></sup> In response, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> sent naval forces to <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> to demand reimbursement. Britain and Spain negotiated with Mexico and peacefully withdrew, but France, at the time ruled by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a>, decided to use the opportunity to establish an empire in Mexico that would favor French interests, the <a href="/wiki/Second_Mexican_Empire" title="Second Mexican Empire">Second Mexican Empire</a>. The empire was part of an envisioned "<a href="/wiki/Latin_America#origin" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>" (term used to imply cultural kinship of the region with France) that would rebuild French influence in the American continent and exclude <a href="/wiki/Anglophone" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglophone">Anglophone</a> American territories.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_invasion_and_Mexican_victory">French invasion and Mexican victory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: French invasion and Mexican victory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Puebla" title="Battle of Puebla">Battle of Puebla</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Late in 1861, a well-armed French fleet attacked Veracruz, landing a large French force and driving President Juárez and his government into retreat.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Moving on from Veracruz towards <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, the French army encountered heavy resistance from the Mexicans close to <a href="/wiki/Puebla" title="Puebla">Puebla</a>, at the Mexican forts of Loreto and Guadalupe.<sup id="cite_ref-mexonline.com_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mexonline.com-13">[13]</a></sup> The French army of 8,000<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[note 1]</a></sup> attacked the poorly equipped Mexican army of 4,000.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[note 2]</a></sup> On May 5, 1862,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> the Mexicans decisively defeated the French army.<sup id="cite_ref-mexica.net_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mexica.net-20">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> The victory represented a significant morale boost to the Mexican army and the Mexican people at large<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-time.com_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time.com-24">[22]</a></sup> and helped establish a sense of national unity and patriotism.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Events_after_the_battle">Events after the battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Events after the battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <p>The Mexican victory, however, was short-lived. A year later, with 30,000 troops, the French were able to defeat the Mexican army, capture Mexico City, and install <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I_of_Mexico" title="Maximilian I of Mexico">Emperor Maximilian I</a> as ruler of Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-mexonline_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mexonline-26">[24]</a></sup> The French victory was itself short-lived, lasting only three years, from 1864 to 1867.<sup id="cite_ref-mexonline_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mexonline-26">[24]</a></sup> By 1865, "with the American Civil War now over, the U.S. began to provide more political and military assistance to Mexico to expel the French".<sup id="cite_ref-mexonline_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mexonline-26">[24]</a></sup> Upon the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a>, facing a persistent Mexican guerilla resistance, the threat of war with Prussia, and "the prospect of a serious scrap with the United States", retreated from Mexico starting in 1866.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> The Mexicans recaptured Mexico City, and Maximilian I was apprehended and executed, along with his Mexican generals <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Miram%C3%B3n" title="Miguel Miramón">Miguel Miramón</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Mej%C3%ADa_Camacho" title="Tomás Mejía Camacho">Tomás Mejía Camacho</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cerro_de_las_Campanas" title="Cerro de las Campanas">Cerro de las Campanas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quer%C3%A9taro" title="Querétaro">Querétaro</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-inside-mexico_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside-mexico-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mexonline_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mexonline-26">[24]</a></sup> "On June 5, 1867, Benito Juárez finally entered Mexico City where he installed a new government to the likes of Washington and reorganized his administration."<sup id="cite_ref-inside-mexico_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inside-mexico-11">[11]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The Battle of Puebla was significant, both nationally and internationally, for several reasons. First, "This battle was significant in that the 4,000 Mexican soldiers were greatly outnumbered by the well-equipped French army of 8,000 that had not been defeated for almost 50 years."<sup id="cite_ref-thebulletin.us_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thebulletin.us-28">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[note 3]</a></sup> Second, since the overall failed French intervention, some have argued that no country in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> has subsequently been invaded by any other <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> military force.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[note 4]</a></sup> Historian <a href="/wiki/Justo_Sierra" title="Justo Sierra">Justo Sierra</a> has suggested in his <i>Political Evolution of the Mexican People</i> that, had Mexico not defeated the French in Puebla on May 5, 1862, France would have gone to the aid of the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate States of America</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> and the United States' destiny would have been different.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[30]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_holiday">History of the holiday</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History of the holiday">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <p>According to a paper published by the <a href="/wiki/UCLA" class="mw-redirect" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> Center for the Study of Latino Health and Culture about the origin of the observance of Cinco de Mayo in the United States, the modern American focus on that day first started in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in 1863 in response to the resistance to French rule in Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-UCLA_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCLA-35">[31]</a></sup> "Far up in the gold country town of <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_California" title="Columbia, California">Columbia</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Columbia_State_Historic_Park" title="Columbia State Historic Park">Columbia State Park</a>) Mexican <a href="/wiki/Miner" title="Miner">miners</a> were so overjoyed at the news that they spontaneously fired off <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifle</a> shots and <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a>, sang patriotic songs and made impromptu speeches."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[32]</a></sup> </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>A 2007 UCLA Newsroom article notes that "the holiday, which has been celebrated in California continuously since 1863, is virtually ignored in Mexico."<sup id="cite_ref-UCLA_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCLA-35">[31]</a></sup> <i>TIME</i> magazine reports that "Cinco de Mayo started to come into vogue in 1940s America during the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Chicano_Movement" title="Chicano Movement">Chicano Movement</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-time.com_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time.com-24">[22]</a></sup> The holiday crossed over from California into the rest of the United States in the 1950s and 1960s but did not gain popularity until the 1980s when marketers, especially <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> companies, capitalized on the celebratory nature of the day and began to promote it.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[34]</a></sup> It grew in popularity and evolved into a celebration of Mexican culture and heritage, first in areas with large Mexican-American populations, like <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City"> New York</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raleigh" class="mw-redirect" title="Raleigh">Raleigh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orlando" class="mw-redirect" title="Orlando">Orlando</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tucson" class="mw-redirect" title="Tucson">Tucson</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[35]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>In a 1998 study in the <i>Journal of American Culture</i> it was reported that there were more than 120 official US celebrations of Cinco de Mayo in 21 different states. An update in 2006 found that the number of official Cinco de Mayo events was 150 or more, according to José Alamillo, a professor of <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_studies" title="Ethnic studies">ethnic studies</a> at <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_University" title="Washington State University">Washington State University</a> in Pullman, who has studied the <a href="/wiki/Cultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural">cultural</a> impact of Cinco de Mayo north of the border.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[36]</a></sup> Los Angeles' <a href="/wiki/Fiesta_Broadway" title="Fiesta Broadway">Fiesta Broadway</a> has been billed as the largest Cinco de Mayo celebration in the world, which it most certainly was at its peak in the 1990s when it attracted crowds of 500,000 or more. In recent years attendance has seen a dramatic decrease.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[38]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On June 7, 2005, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_resolution" title="Concurrent resolution">concurrent resolution</a> calling on the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> to issue a <a href="/wiki/Proclamation" title="Proclamation">proclamation</a> calling upon the people of the United States to observe Cinco de Mayo with appropriate ceremonies and activities.<sup id="cite_ref-thomas.loc.gov_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomas.loc.gov-43">[39]</a></sup> To celebrate, many display Cinco de Mayo banners while <a href="/wiki/School_district" title="School district">school districts</a> hold special events to educate students about its historical significance. Special events and celebrations highlight Mexican culture, especially in its music and regional dancing. Examples include <i><a href="/wiki/Baile_Folklorico" title="Baile Folklorico">baile folklórico</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Mariachi" title="Mariachi">mariachi</a> demonstrations held annually at the Plaza del <a href="/wiki/Pueblo_de_Los_%C3%81ngeles" title="Pueblo de Los Ángeles">Pueblo de Los Ángeles</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Olvera_Street" title="Olvera Street">Olvera Street</a>. Commercial interests in the United States have capitalized on the celebration, advertising Mexican products and services, with an emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-times_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-44">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYSE_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYSE-45">[41]</a></sup> foods, and music.<sup id="cite_ref-Alb_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alb-46">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Corona_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corona-47">[43]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Holdings" title="Nielsen Holdings">Nielsen</a>, in 2013 more than $600&nbsp;million worth of beer was purchased in the United States for Cinco de Mayo, more than for the <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl" title="Super Bowl">Super Bowl</a> or <a href="/wiki/St._Patrick%27s_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Patrick's Day">St. Patrick's Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[44]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <p>On May 9, 1862, President Juárez declared that the anniversary of the Battle of Puebla would be a national holiday regarded as "Battle of Puebla Day" or "Battle of Cinco de Mayo".<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[49]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Today, the commemoration of the battle is not observed as a national holiday in Mexico (i.e. not a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Mexico#Statutory_holidays" title="Public holidays in Mexico">statutory holiday</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[50]</a></sup> However, all public schools are closed nationwide in Mexico on May 5.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[52]</a></sup> The day is an official holiday in the State of <a href="/wiki/Puebla" title="Puebla">Puebla</a>, where the Battle took place, and also a full holiday (no work) in the neighboring State of <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[54]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Puebla, <a href="/wiki/Historical_reenactment" title="Historical reenactment">historical reenactments</a>, parades, and meals take place to commemorate the battle. Parade participants dress as French and Mexican soldiers to reenact the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-BusinessInsider_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BusinessInsider-59">[55]</a></sup> Every year the city also hosts the <i>Festival Internacional de Puebla</i>, which gathers national and international artists, traditional musicians and dancers.<sup id="cite_ref-BusinessInsider_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BusinessInsider-59">[55]</a></sup> As well as the <i>Festival Internacional del Mole</i>, with an emphasis on the city's iconic <i>mole poblano</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BusinessInsider_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BusinessInsider-59">[55]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In Mexico City, military commemoration is occasionally held at the <a href="/wiki/Campo_Marte" title="Campo Marte">Campo Marte</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[56]</a></sup> A street, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avenida_Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Avenida Cinco de Mayo (page does not exist)">Avenida Cinco de Mayo</a><sup class="noprint" style="font-style: normal;">&nbsp;[<a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avenida_Cinco_de_Mayo_(Ciudad_de_M%C3%A9xico)" class="extiw" title="es:Avenida Cinco de Mayo (Ciudad de México)">es</a>]</sup></i>, in the <a href="/wiki/Historic_Center_of_Mexico_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Historic Center of Mexico City">Historic Center of Mexico City</a> was named after the battle in 1862 by Benito Juárez. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere">Elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cinco_de_Mayo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Events tied to Cinco de Mayo also occur outside Mexico and the United States. As in the United States, celebrations elsewhere also emphasize Mexican cuisine, culture and music. For example, some Canadian pubs play Mexican music and serve Mexican food and drink,<sup id="cite_ref-Canadapubs_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadapubs-61">[57]</a></sup> and a sky-diving club near <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> holds a Cinco de Mayo skydiving event.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanc_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanc-62">[58]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, there is an annual Cinco de Mayo <a href="/wiki/Air_guitar" title="Air guitar">air guitar</a> competition,<sup id="cite_ref-Cayman_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cayman-63">[59]</a></sup> and at <a href="/wiki/Montego_Bay" title="Montego Bay">Montego Bay</a>, Jamaica, there is a Cinco de Mayo celebration.<sup id="cite_ref-Montego_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montego-64">[60]</a></sup> The city of <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, Australia, holds an annual Mexican Festival<sup id="cite_ref-Bris_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bris-65">[61]</a></sup> to honor the day, and celebrations are held in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a><sup id="cite_ref-London_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-London-66">[62]</a></sup> and New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-nz_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nz-67">[63]</a></sup> Other celebrations of the day can also be found in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, South Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lagos" title="Lagos">Lagos</a>, Nigeria,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[65]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Paris_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paris-70">[66]</a></sup> Cinco de Mayo is celebrated in Japan in <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Yoyogi_Park" title="Yoyogi Park">Yoyogi Park</a> Event Space as a celebration of Latin American culture.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" Thus, since Cinco de Mayo no army from another continent has <i>invaded</i> the Americas. The War of the Falklands War, for example, was fought in the Americas but the Islands were invaded by a military from the Americas (the Argentine military). They were subsequently defended (not <i>invaded</i>) by the UK. Another example, Pearl Harbor, experienced an attack, not an <i>invasion</i> by the Japanese. The only possible exception to the Cinco de Mayo claim above might be the brief occupation/invasion of two of the Alaskan Aleutian Islands by the Japanese military during WWII. This event, however, was so insignificant as to be virtually negligible: the islands invaded had a total population of 12 Americans and some 45 natives, the invasion was short-lived, and the battle fought there had no notoriety other than the psychological effect on the Americans that the Japanese had invaded American territory again (Alaska was not yet a full-fledged state). In short, the military importance of these small pieces of land was nowhere comparable to the superior military significance of the Battle of Puebla.</span> </li>
