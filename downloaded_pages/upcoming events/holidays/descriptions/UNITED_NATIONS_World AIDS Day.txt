      <p><b>World AIDS Day</b>, designated on 1 December every year since 1988,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_secular_observances#December" title="List of minor secular observances">international day</a> dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Raising_awareness" class="mw-redirect" title="Raising awareness">raising awareness</a> of the <a href="/wiki/AIDS_pandemic" class="mw-redirect" title="AIDS pandemic">AIDS pandemic</a> caused by the spread of <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> infection and mourning those who have died of the disease. The acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) is a life-threatening condition caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). The HIV virus attacks the immune system of the patient and reduces its resistance to other diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Government and health officials, non-governmental organizations, and individuals around the world observe the day, often with education on <a href="/wiki/AIDS_prevention" class="mw-redirect" title="AIDS prevention">AIDS prevention</a> and control. </p>
      <p>World AIDS Day is one of the eleven official <a href="/wiki/Global_health" title="Global health">global public health</a> campaigns marked by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO), along with <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Day" title="World Health Day">World Health Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Blood_Donor_Day" title="World Blood Donor Day">World Blood Donor Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Immunization_Week" title="World Immunization Week">World Immunization Week</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Tuberculosis_Day" title="World Tuberculosis Day">World Tuberculosis Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_No_Tobacco_Day" title="World No Tobacco Day">World No Tobacco Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Malaria_Day" title="World Malaria Day">World Malaria Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Hepatitis_Day" title="World Hepatitis Day">World Hepatitis Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_resistance#Global_action_plans_and_awareness" title="Antimicrobial resistance">World Antimicrobial Awareness Week</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Patient_Safety_Day" title="World Patient Safety Day">World Patient Safety Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Chagas_Disease_Day" title="World Chagas Disease Day">World Chagas Disease Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=World_AIDS_Day&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, AIDS has killed between 28.9&nbsp;million and 41.5&nbsp;million people worldwide, and an estimated 36.7&nbsp;million people are living with HIV,<sup id="cite_ref-UNAIDS2017_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNAIDS2017-4">[4]</a></sup> making it one of the most important global <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> issues in <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a>. Thanks to recent improved access to <a href="/wiki/Antiretroviral_drug" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiretroviral drug">antiretroviral treatment</a> in many regions of the world, the death rate from AIDS epidemic has decreased since its peak in 2005 (1&nbsp;million in 2016, compared to 1.9&nbsp;million in 2005).<sup id="cite_ref-UNAIDS2017_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNAIDS2017-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_AIDS_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:210px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:AidsRusStamp1993.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/AidsRusStamp1993.jpg/208px-AidsRusStamp1993.jpg" decoding="async" width="208" height="285" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/AidsRusStamp1993.jpg/312px-AidsRusStamp1993.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/AidsRusStamp1993.jpg/416px-AidsRusStamp1993.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1093" data-file-height="1500"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:AidsRusStamp1993.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Russian stamp, 1993
        </div>
       </div>

      <p>World AIDS Day was first conceived in August 1987 by James W. Bunn and Thomas Netter, two public information officers for the Global Programme on AIDS at the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> in Geneva, Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-NPR_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Bunn and Netter took their idea to Dr. Jonathan Mann, Director of the Global Programme on AIDS (now known as <a href="/wiki/UNAIDS" class="mw-redirect" title="UNAIDS">UNAIDS</a>). Mann liked the concept, approved it, and agreed with the recommendation that the first observance of World AIDS Day should be on 1 December 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-newsworks_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsworks-7">[7]</a></sup> Bunn, a former television broadcast journalist from San Francisco, had recommended the date of 1 December that believing it would maximize coverage of World AIDS Day by western news media, sufficiently long following the US elections but before the Christmas holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-newsworks_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsworks-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In its first two years, the theme of World AIDS Day focused on children and young people. While the choice of this theme was criticized at the time by some for ignoring the fact that people of all ages may become infected with HIV, the theme helped alleviate some of the stigma surrounding the disease and boost recognition of the problem as a family disease.<sup id="cite_ref-Speicher_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speicher-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Joint_United_Nations_Programme_on_HIV/AIDS" title="Joint United Nations Programme on HIV/AIDS">Joint United Nations Programme on HIV/AIDS</a> (UNAIDS) became operational in 1996, and it took over the planning and promotion of World AIDS Day.<sup id="cite_ref-Speicher_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speicher-8">[8]</a></sup> Rather than focus on a single day, UNAIDS created the World AIDS Campaign in 1997 to focus on year-round communications, prevention and education.<sup id="cite_ref-Speicher_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speicher-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Soest_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soest-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2004, the World AIDS Campaign became an independent organization.<sup id="cite_ref-Speicher_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speicher-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Soest_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soest-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Each year, Popes <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_II" class="mw-redirect" title="John Paul II">John Paul II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benedict_XVI" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedict XVI">Benedict XVI</a> have released a greeting message for patients and doctors on World AIDS Day.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2016, a collection of HIV and AIDS-related <a href="/wiki/NGOs" class="mw-redirect" title="NGOs">NGOs</a> (including Panagea Global AIDS and The AIDS and Rights Alliance for Southern Africa) started a campaign to rename World AIDS Day to World HIV Day. They claim the change will emphasize social justice issues, and the advancement of treatments like <a href="/wiki/PrEP" class="mw-redirect" title="PrEP">PrEP</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the USA, the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> began marking World AIDS Day with the iconic display of a 28 foot (8.5&nbsp;m) AIDS Ribbon on the building's North Portico in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> White House aide Steven M. Levine, then serving in President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>'s administration, proposed the display to symbolize the United States' commitment to combat the world AIDS epidemic through its landmark <a href="/wiki/President%27s_Emergency_Plan_for_AIDS_Relief" title="President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief">PEPFAR</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> display, now an annual tradition across four presidential administrations, quickly garnered attention, as it was the banner, sign or symbol to prominently hang from the White House since the <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> administration.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Since 1993, the President of the United States has made an official <a href="/wiki/List_of_observances_in_the_United_States_by_presidential_proclamation" title="List of observances in the United States by presidential proclamation">proclamation</a> for World AIDS Day (see section <a href="#US_Presidential_Proclamations_for_World_AIDS_Day">#US Presidential Proclamations for World AIDS Day</a> for copies of those proclamations). On 30 November 2017, President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> proclaimed World AIDS Day for 1 December.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_AIDS_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>All the World AIDS Day campaigns focus on a specific theme, chosen following consultations with <a href="/wiki/UNAIDS" class="mw-redirect" title="UNAIDS">UNAIDS</a>, <a href="/wiki/WHO" class="mw-redirect" title="WHO">WHO</a>, and a large number of grassroots, national and international agencies involved in the prevention and treatment of HIV/AIDS. As of 2008, each year's theme is chosen by the Global Steering Committee of the World AIDS Campaign (WAC).<sup id="cite_ref-Speicher_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speicher-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>For each World AIDS Day from 2005 through 2010, the theme was "Stop AIDS. Keep the Promise", designed to encourage political leaders to keep their commitment to achieving universal access to HIV/AIDS prevention, treatment, care, and support by the year 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Speicher_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Speicher-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2012, the multi-year theme for World AIDS Day is "Getting to Zero: Zero new HIV infections. Zero deaths from AIDS-related illness. Zero discrimination."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The US Federal theme for the year 2014 was "Focus, Partner, Achieve: An AIDS-Free Generation".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The themes are not limited to a single day but are used year-round in international efforts to highlight HIV/AIDS awareness within the context of other major global events including the <a href="/wiki/Group_of_Eight" title="Group of Eight">G8 Summit</a>, as well as local campaigns like the Student Stop AIDS Campaign in the UK.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
