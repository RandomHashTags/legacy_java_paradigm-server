      <p><b>Malcolm X Day</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_United_States#African_American_holidays" title="Public holidays in the United States">American holiday</a> in honor of <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a> that is celebrated on either May 19 (his birthday) or the third Friday of May. The commemoration of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights leader</a> has been proposed as an official state holiday in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> in 2015 and Missouri as recent as 2019. As of present, only the city of <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a> observes the holiday with city offices and schools closed.<sup id="cite_ref-NamesakeSchool_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NamesakeSchool-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malcolm_X_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The Malcolm X Day holiday has been an official holiday in the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a> since 1978. Since them, there have been multiple proposals for the holiday to be official elsewhere. Most recently in 2014, a proposal put forth by the Council of Islamic Organizations of Chicago to make the holiday in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chicagomonitor.com_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicagomonitor.com-2">[2]</a></sup> The Illinois proposal differs from the Berkeley, California resolution in that the holiday would be observed May 19 instead of the third Friday in May.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Before that, unsuccessful attempts were made in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, with numerous calls for it to be celebrated alongside <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Day" title="Martin Luther King Jr. Day">Martin Luther King Jr. Day</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as a federal holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1993, this holiday was proposed at the federal level to Congress as H.J.R. #323 by Congressman <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rangel" title="Charles Rangel">Charles Rangel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> Senate unanimously passed the resolution for the official holiday designation where the law "... officially designated 'May 19, 2015, and every May 19 thereafter' as Malcolm X Day.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Though the resolution passed making the holiday official, the Illinois official list of holidays still has yet to reflect the holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malcolm_X_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a>
      </div> 
      <p><b>Malcolm<span class="nowrap">&nbsp;</span>X</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/əm/: 'm' in 'rhythm'">əm</span></span><span class="wrap"> </span><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) (born <b>Malcolm Little</b>; May<span class="nowrap">&nbsp;</span>19, 1925&nbsp;– February<span class="nowrap">&nbsp;</span>21, 1965), and also known as <b>el-Hajj Malik el-Shabazz</b><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[A]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">الحاجّ مالك الشباز</span>‎), was an American Muslim minister and a <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_activist" class="mw-redirect" title="Human rights activist">human rights activist</a>. He was a courageous advocate for the rights of black people, a man who indicted white America in the harshest terms for its crimes against black Americans; detractors accused him of preaching <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a> and violence. He has been called one of the greatest and most influential <a href="/wiki/African_American" class="mw-redirect" title="African American">African Americans</a> in history. </p>
