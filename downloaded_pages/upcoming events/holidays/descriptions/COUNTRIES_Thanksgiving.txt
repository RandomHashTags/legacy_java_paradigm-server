      <p><b>Thanksgiving</b> is a national <a href="/wiki/Holiday" title="Holiday">holiday</a> celebrated on various dates in the United States, Canada, Grenada, Saint Lucia, and Liberia. It began as a day of giving thanks and sacrifice for the blessing of the <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a> and of the preceding year. Similarly named festival holidays occur in Germany and Japan. Thanksgiving is celebrated on the <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(Canada)" title="Thanksgiving (Canada)">second Monday of October</a> in Canada and on the <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(United_States)" title="Thanksgiving (United States)">fourth Thursday of November</a> in the United States and around the same part of the year in other places. Although Thanksgiving has historical roots in religious and cultural traditions, it has long been celebrated as a <a href="/wiki/Secularity" title="Secularity">secular</a> holiday as well. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2> 
      <p>Prayers of thanks and special thanksgiving ceremonies are common among almost all religions after harvests and at other times.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodg_1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodg_1-1">[1]</a></sup> The Thanksgiving holiday's history in North America is rooted in English traditions dating from the Protestant <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a>. It also has aspects of a <a href="/wiki/Harvest_festival" title="Harvest festival">harvest festival</a>, even though the harvest in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> occurs well before the late-November date on which the modern Thanksgiving holiday is celebrated.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodg_1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodg_1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the English tradition, days of thanksgiving and special thanksgiving religious services became important during the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a> in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a> and in reaction to the large number of religious holidays on the Catholic calendar. Before 1536 there were 95 Church holidays, plus 52 Sundays, when people were required to attend church and forego work and sometimes pay for expensive celebrations. The 1536 reforms reduced the number of Church holidays to 27, but some <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritans</a> wished to eliminate all Church holidays, including <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>. The holidays were to be replaced by specially called <a href="/wiki/Days_of_humiliation_and_thanksgiving" title="Days of humiliation and thanksgiving">Days of Fasting or Days of Thanksgiving</a>, in response to events that the Puritans viewed as acts of <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">special providence</a>. Unexpected disasters or threats of judgement from on high called for Days of Fasting. Special blessings, viewed as coming from God, called for Days of Thanksgiving. For example, Days of Fasting were called on account of drought in 1611, floods in 1613, and plagues in 1604 and 1622. Days of thanksgiving were called following the victory over the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armada" title="Spanish Armada">Spanish Armada</a> in 1588 and following the deliverance of <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> in 1705.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-3">[3]</a></sup> An unusual annual Day of Thanksgiving began in 1606 following the failure of the <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder_Plot" title="Gunpowder Plot">Gunpowder Plot</a> in 1605 and developed into <a href="/wiki/Guy_Fawkes_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Guy Fawkes Day">Guy Fawkes Day</a> on November 5.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-3">[3]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Canada">In Canada</span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(Canada)" title="Thanksgiving (Canada)">Thanksgiving (Canada)</a>
      </div> 
      <p>According to some historians, the first celebration of Thanksgiving in North America occurred during the 1578 voyage of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Frobisher" title="Martin Frobisher">Martin Frobisher</a> from England in search of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Other researchers, however, state that "there is no compelling narrative of the origins of the Canadian Thanksgiving day."<sup id="cite_ref-Kaufman_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufman-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The origins of Canadian Thanksgiving are also sometimes traced to the French settlers who came to <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> in the 17th century, who celebrated their successful harvests. The French settlers in the area typically had feasts at the end of the harvest season. They continued throughout the winter season, even sharing food with the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a> of the area.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruth_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruth-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As settlers arrived in Nova Scotia from New England after 1700, late autumn Thanksgiving celebrations became commonplace. New immigrants into the country—such as the Irish, Scottish, and Germans—also added their own traditions to the harvest celebrations. Most of the U.S. aspects of Thanksgiving (such as the turkey) were incorporated when <a href="/wiki/United_Empire_Loyalist" title="United Empire Loyalist">United Empire Loyalists</a> began to flee from the United States during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> and settled in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruth_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruth-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>

      <p><a href="/wiki/Pilgrims_(Plymouth_Colony)" title="Pilgrims (Plymouth Colony)">Pilgrims</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puritans" title="Puritans">Puritans</a> who emigrated from England in the 1620s and 1630s carried the tradition of Days of Fasting and Days of Thanksgiving with them to <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. The modern Thanksgiving holiday tradition is traced to a well-recorded 1619 event in Virginia and a sparsely documented 1621 celebration at <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. The 1619 arrival of 38 English settlers at <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Hundred" title="Berkeley Hundred">Berkeley Hundred</a> in Charles City County, Virginia, concluded with a religious celebration as dictated by the group's charter from the <a href="/wiki/London_Company" title="London Company">London Company</a>, which required "that the day of our ships arrival at the place assigned ... in the land of Virginia shall be yearly and perpetually kept holy as a day of thanksgiving to <a href="/wiki/Almighty_God" class="mw-redirect" title="Almighty God">Almighty God</a>." The 1621 Plymouth feast and thanksgiving was prompted by a good harvest. The Pilgrims celebrated this with Native Americans, who had helped them get through the previous winter by giving them food in that time of scarcity.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Julian_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Julian-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Several days of Thanksgiving were held in early New England history that have been identified as the "First Thanksgiving", including Pilgrim holidays in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Massachusetts" title="Plymouth, Massachusetts">Plymouth</a> in 1621 and 1623, and a Puritan holiday in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> in 1631.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker,_Chapter_1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker,_Chapter_1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-History2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History2-11">[11]</a></sup> According to historian Jeremy Bangs, director of the <a href="/wiki/Leiden_American_Pilgrim_Museum" title="Leiden American Pilgrim Museum">Leiden American Pilgrim Museum</a>, the Pilgrims may have been influenced by watching the annual services of Thanksgiving for the relief of the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Leiden" title="Siege of Leiden">siege of Leiden</a> in 1574, while they were staying in Leiden.<sup id="cite_ref-Bangs_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bangs-12">[12]</a></sup> Now called <i><a href="/wiki/3_October_Festival" title="3 October Festival">Oktober Feest</a>,</i> Leiden's autumn thanksgiving celebration in 1617 was the occasion for sectarian disturbance that appears to have accelerated the pilgrims' plans to emigrate to America.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Later in Massachusetts, religious thanksgiving services were declared by civil leaders such as <a href="/wiki/William_Bradford_(Plymouth_governor)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Bradford (Plymouth governor)">Governor Bradford</a>, who planned the colony's thanksgiving celebration and feast in 1623.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In the late 1630s, the <a href="/wiki/Pequots" title="Pequots">Pequot</a> were blamed for the killing of a white man, leading to the colonizers burning down Pequot villages and killing those who did not perish in the fires.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> Hundreds of Pequots were killed, leading Governor Bradford to proclaim that Thanksgiving from then on would be celebrating "the bloody victory, thanking God that the battle had been won."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The practice of holding an annual harvest festival did not become a regular affair in New England until the late 1660s.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Thanksgiving proclamations were made mostly by church leaders in New England up until 1682, and then by both state and church leaders until after the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. During the revolutionary period, political influences affected the issuance of Thanksgiving proclamations. Various proclamations were made by <a href="/wiki/Royal_colony" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal colony">royal governors</a>, and conversely by <a href="/wiki/Patriot_(American_Revolution)" title="Patriot (American Revolution)">patriot</a> leaders, such as <a href="/wiki/John_Hancock" title="John Hancock">John Hancock</a>, General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> each giving thanks to God for events favorable to their causes.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> As President of the United States, George Washington proclaimed the first nationwide thanksgiving celebration in America marking November 26, 1789, "as a day of public thanksgiving and prayer, to be observed by acknowledging with grateful hearts the many and signal favours of Almighty God".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h4><span id="Debate_about_the_nation.27s_first_celebrations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Debate_about_the_nation's_first_celebrations">Debate about the nation's first celebrations</span></h4> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Berkeley_Plantation,_Shrine_marking_1st_Thanksgiving_in_America.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>

        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>The question of where the first Thanksgiving was held in the United States has been a subject of dispute, primarily between New England and Virginia. The question is complicated by the concept of Thanksgiving as either a holiday celebration or a religious service. James Baker maintains, "The American holiday's true origin was the New England <a href="/wiki/Calvinist" class="mw-redirect" title="Calvinist">Calvinist</a> Thanksgiving. Never coupled with a Sabbath meeting, the Puritan observances were special days set aside during the week for thanksgiving and praise in response to God's providence."<sup id="cite_ref-Baker,_Chapter_1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker,_Chapter_1-10">[10]</a></sup> Baker calls the debate a "tempest in a beanpot" and "marvelous nonsense" based on regional claims.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker,_Chapter_1_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker,_Chapter_1-10">[10]</a></sup> However, the day for Thanksgiving services specifically codified in the founding <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Hundred" title="Berkeley Hundred">Berkeley Hundred</a> in 1619 was instrumental in President John F. Kennedy's attempt to strike a compromise between the regional claims, by issuing Proclamation 3560 on November 5, 1963, stating, "Over three centuries ago, our forefathers in Virginia and in Massachusetts, far from home in a lonely wilderness, set aside a time of thanksgiving. On the appointed day, they gave reverent thanks for their safety, for the health of their children, for the fertility of their fields, for the love which bound them together, and for the faith which united them with their God."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Other claims include an earlier religious service by Spanish explorers in Texas at <a href="/wiki/San_Elizario" class="mw-redirect" title="San Elizario">San Elizario</a> in 1598.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Historians Robyn Gioia and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Gannon_(historian)" title="Michael Gannon (historian)">Michael Gannon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">University of Florida</a> argue that the earliest Thanksgiving service in what is now the United States was celebrated by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish people">Spanish community</a> on September 8, 1565, in current <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine,_Florida" title="St. Augustine, Florida">Saint Augustine</a>, Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixing_date">Fixing date</span></h3> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span></h4> 
      <p>The earlier Thanksgiving celebrations in Canada has been attributed to the earlier onset of winter in the North, thus ending the harvest season earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-Kauf_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kauf-27">[27]</a></sup> Thanksgiving in Canada did not have a fixed date until the late 19th century. Prior to <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Confederation" title="Canadian Confederation">Canadian Confederation</a>, many of the individual colonial governors of the Canadian provinces had declared their own days of Thanksgiving. The first official Canadian Thanksgiving occurred on April 15, 1872, when the nation was celebrating the <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII" title="Edward VII">Prince of Wales</a>' recovery from a serious illness.<sup id="cite_ref-Kauf_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kauf-27">[27]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>By the end of the 19th century, Thanksgiving Day was normally celebrated on November 6. In the late 19th century, the Militia staged "sham battles" for public entertainment on Thanksgiving Day. The Militia agitated for an earlier date for the holiday, so they could use the warmer weather to draw bigger crowds.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, when <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> ended, the <a href="/wiki/Remembrance_Day" title="Remembrance Day">Armistice Day</a> holiday was usually held during the same week. To prevent the two holidays from clashing with one another, in 1957 the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian Parliament">Canadian Parliament</a> proclaimed Thanksgiving to be observed on its present date on the second Monday of October.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruth_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruth-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span></h4> 
      <p>Thanksgiving in the United States has been observed on differing dates. From the time of the <a href="/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States" title="Founding Fathers of the United States">Founding Fathers</a> until the time of Lincoln, the date of observance varied from state to state. The final Thursday in November had become the customary date in most U.S. states by the beginning of the 19th century, coinciding with, and eventually superseding the holiday of <a href="/wiki/Evacuation_Day_(New_York)" title="Evacuation Day (New York)">Evacuation Day</a> (commemorating the day the British exited the United States after the Revolutionary War).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Modern Thanksgiving was proclaimed for all states in 1863 by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>. Influenced by <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Josepha_Hale" title="Sarah Josepha Hale">Sarah Josepha Hale</a>, who wrote letters to politicians for approximately 40 years advocating an official holiday, Lincoln set national Thanksgiving by proclamation for the final Thursday in November, explicitly in celebration of the bounties that had continued to fall on the Union and for the military successes in the war, and also explicitly in "humble penitence for our national perverseness and disobedience."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Because of the ongoing <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, a nationwide Thanksgiving celebration was not realized until <a href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Era_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Reconstruction Era of the United States">Reconstruction</a> was completed in the 1870s. </p>
      <p>On October 31, 1939, President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> signed a <a href="/wiki/Franksgiving" title="Franksgiving">presidential proclamation</a> changing the holiday to the next to last Thursday in November, for business reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On December 26, 1941, he signed a <a href="/wiki/Joint_resolution" title="Joint resolution">joint resolution</a> of Congress changing the national Thanksgiving Day to the fourth Thursday in November.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Since 1971, when the American <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Monday_Holiday_Act" title="Uniform Monday Holiday Act">Uniform Monday Holiday Act</a> took effect, the American observance of <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day" title="Columbus Day">Columbus Day</a> has coincided with the Canadian observance of Thanksgiving.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observance">Observance</span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span></h3> 
      <p>In the <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_Australia" title="States and territories of Australia">Australian external territory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Island" title="Norfolk Island">Norfolk Island</a>, Thanksgiving is celebrated on the last Wednesday of November, similar to the pre–<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> American observance on the last Thursday of the month. This means the Norfolk Island observance is the day before or six days after the United States' observance. The holiday was brought to the island by visiting American <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">whaling</a> ships.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span></h3> 
      <p>In Brazil, National Thanksgiving Day was instituted by <a href="/wiki/Eurico_Gaspar_Dutra" title="Eurico Gaspar Dutra">President Gaspar Dutra</a>, through Law 781 of August 17, 1949, at the suggestion of Ambassador <a href="/wiki/Joaquim_Nabuco" title="Joaquim Nabuco">Joaquim Nabuco</a>, who was enthusiastic about the commemorations he saw in 1909 in <a href="/wiki/St._Patrick%27s_Cathedral_(Manhattan)" title="St. Patrick's Cathedral (Manhattan)">St. Patrick's Cathedral</a> as an ambassador in Washington. In 1966, Law 5110 established that the Thanksgiving celebration would take place on the fourth Thursday of November.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This date is celebrated by many families of American origin, by some Protestant Christian denominations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_of_Brazil" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church of Brazil">Evangelical Lutheran Church of Brazil</a> (which is of American origin), the <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_of_Brazil" title="Presbyterian Church of Brazil">Presbyterian Church</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">Baptist Church</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Church_in_Brazil" title="Methodist Church in Brazil">Methodist Church</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Nazarene" title="Church of the Nazarene">Church of the Nazarene</a>, and Methodist denominational universities. The day is also celebrated by <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Churches" class="mw-redirect" title="Evangelical Churches">evangelical churches</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Foursquare_Gospel_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Foursquare Gospel Church">Foursquare Gospel Church</a> in Brazil. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada_2">Canada</span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">

      </div>

      <p>Thanksgiving (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">l'Action de grâce</i>), occurring on the second Monday in October, is an annual Canadian <a href="/wiki/Holiday" title="Holiday">holiday</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gratitude" title="Gratitude">give thanks</a> at the close of the harvest season. Although the original act of Parliament references <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> and the holiday is celebrated in churches, the holiday is mostly celebrated in a <a href="/wiki/Secularity" title="Secularity">secular</a> manner. Thanksgiving is a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Canada" title="Public holidays in Canada">statutory holiday</a> in all provinces in Canada, except for <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. While businesses may remain open in these provinces, the holiday is nonetheless recognized and celebrated regardless of its status.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-stat_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stat-41">[41]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grenada">Grenada</span></h3> 
      <p>In the West Indian island of Grenada, in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, there is a national holiday known as Thanksgiving Day which is celebrated on October 25. Even though it bears the same name, and is celebrated at roughly the same time as the American and Canadian versions of Thanksgiving, this holiday is unrelated to either of those celebrations. Instead, the holiday marks the anniversary of the U.S.-led <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Grenada" class="mw-redirect" title="Invasion of Grenada">invasion of the island</a> in 1983, in response to the <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Deposition (politics)">deposition</a> and execution of the socialist Grenadian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bishop" title="Maurice Bishop">Maurice Bishop</a><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> by a military government from within his own party. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liberia">Liberia</span></h3> 
      <p>In the West African country of Liberia, Thanksgiving is celebrated on the first Thursday of November.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The Thanksgiving tradition there is rooted in the nation's founding as a colony of the <a href="/wiki/American_Colonization_Society" title="American Colonization Society">American Colonization Society</a> in 1821 by <a href="/wiki/Free_people_of_color" title="Free people of color">free people of color</a> from the United States. Although recognized throughout the country, Thanksgiving is practiced chiefly by <a href="/wiki/Americo-Liberian" class="mw-redirect" title="Americo-Liberian">Americo-Liberians</a>, descendants of Liberia's original African-American settlers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:25446_Pieterskerk.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/25446_Pieterskerk.jpg/260px-25446_Pieterskerk.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/25446_Pieterskerk.jpg/390px-25446_Pieterskerk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/25446_Pieterskerk.jpg/520px-25446_Pieterskerk.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5082" data-file-height="3695"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:25446_Pieterskerk.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Pieterskerk
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Many of the Pilgrims who migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Plantation</a> resided in the city of <a href="/wiki/Leiden" title="Leiden">Leiden</a> from 1609–1620 and had recorded their births, marriages, and deaths at the <a href="/wiki/Pieterskerk,_Leiden" title="Pieterskerk, Leiden">Pieterskerk</a> (St. Peter's church). In commemoration, a non-denominational Thanksgiving Day service is held each year on the morning of the American Thanksgiving Day in the Pieterskerk, a <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> church in Leiden, noting the hospitality the Pilgrims received in Leiden on their way to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Thanksgiving is observed by orthodox Protestant churches in the Netherlands on the first Wednesday in November (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Dankdag&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dankdag (page does not exist)">Dankdag</a><sup class="noprint" style="font-style: normal;">&nbsp;[<a href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biddag_en_Dankdag_voor_Gewas_en_Arbeid" class="extiw" title="nl:Biddag en Dankdag voor Gewas en Arbeid">nl</a>]</sup>). It is not a public holiday. Those who observe the day either go to church in the evening or take the day off and go to church in the morning (and occasionally afternoon) too. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span></h3> 
      <p>The Philippines, while it was an American colony in the first half of the 20th century, celebrated Thanksgiving as a special public holiday on the same day as the Americans. During the Japanese occupation during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, both the Americans and Filipinos celebrated Thanksgiving in secret. After Japanese withdrawal in 1945, the tradition continued until 1969. It was revived by President <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Marcos" title="Ferdinand Marcos">Ferdinand Marcos</a>, but the date was changed to be on every September 21, when martial law was imposed in the country. After <a href="/wiki/People_Power_Revolution" title="People Power Revolution">Marcos' ouster</a> in 1986, the tradition was no longer continued, due to the controversial events that occurred during his long administration.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>As of 2020, Thanksgiving has been revived as a commercial and cultural holiday, albeit stripped of its official status. <a href="/wiki/SM_Supermalls" title="SM Supermalls">SM Supermalls</a> led the way in the slow revival of Thanksgiving Day on the same day as in the U.S., as in the old days. Many malls and hotels offer special sales on this day, which is part of the long celebration of <a href="/wiki/Christmas_in_the_Philippines" title="Christmas in the Philippines">Christmas in the Philippines</a>, which begins in September (unlike on Black Friday in the United States). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rwanda">Rwanda</span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Rwanda" title="Public holidays in Rwanda">Public holidays in Rwanda</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Called <i>Umuganura Day</i>, this is a Thanksgiving festival to mark the start of the harvest in <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>. It is celebrated on the first Friday of August.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Lucia">Saint Lucia</span></h3> 
      <p>The nation of Saint Lucia celebrates Thanksgiving on the first Monday in October.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Thanksgiving_grace_1942.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Thanksgiving_grace_1942.jpg/220px-Thanksgiving_grace_1942.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Thanksgiving_grace_1942.jpg/330px-Thanksgiving_grace_1942.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Thanksgiving_grace_1942.jpg/440px-Thanksgiving_grace_1942.jpg 2x" data-file-width="516" data-file-height="384"></a>

         </div>Family saying <a href="/wiki/Grace_(prayer)" title="Grace (prayer)">grace</a> before Thanksgiving dinner in <a href="/wiki/Neffsville,_Pennsylvania" title="Neffsville, Pennsylvania">Neffsville, Pennsylvania</a>, 1942
        </div>

      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(United_States)" title="Thanksgiving (United States)">Thanksgiving (United States)</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Thanksgiving, celebrated on the fourth Thursday in November since 1941 due to federal legislation, has been an annual tradition in the United States by presidential proclamation since 1863 and by state legislation since the <a href="/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States" title="Founding Fathers of the United States">Founding Fathers of the United States</a>. Traditionally, Thanksgiving has been a celebration of the blessings of the year, including the harvest.<sup id="cite_ref-EB-TD_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-TD-48">[48]</a></sup> On Thanksgiving Day, it is common for Americans to share a family meal, attend <a href="/wiki/Church_service" title="Church service">church services</a>, and view special sporting events.<sup id="cite_ref-Counihan2013_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Counihan2013-49">[49]</a></sup> In addition, Thanksgiving is celebrated in public places with parades such as <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_Thanksgiving_Parade" class="mw-redirect" title="Macy's Thanksgiving Parade">Macy's Thanksgiving Parade</a><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, ABC Dunkin' Donuts Thanksgiving Day Parade<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, America's Hometown Thanksgiving Parade in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Massachusetts" title="Plymouth, Massachusetts">Plymouth</a>, Massachusetts, McDonald's Thanksgiving Parade in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, and Bayou Classic Thanksgiving Parade<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>. What Americans call the "<a href="/wiki/Christmas_and_holiday_season" title="Christmas and holiday season">Holiday Season</a>" generally begins with Thanksgiving.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The first day after Thanksgiving Day—<a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(shopping)" title="Black Friday (shopping)">Black Friday</a>—marks the start of the Christmas shopping season.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>The Harvest Thanksgiving Festival, <a href="/wiki/Erntedankfest_D%C3%BCsseldorf-Urdenbach" title="Erntedankfest Düsseldorf-Urdenbach">Erntedankfest</a>, is a popular German <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> festival on the first Sunday of October. The festival has a significant religious component, and many churches are decorated with autumn crops. In some places, there are <a href="/wiki/Procession" title="Procession">religious processions</a> or parades. Many <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> beer festivals, like the <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> <a href="/wiki/Oktoberfest" title="Oktoberfest">Oktoberfest</a>, take place within the vicinity of <i>Erntedankfest.</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research.       <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Labor_Thanksgiving_Day" title="Labor Thanksgiving Day">Labor Thanksgiving Day</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Labor Thanksgiving Day<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">勤労感謝の日</span>, <i lang="ja-Latn" title="Hepburn transliteration">Kinrō Kansha no Hi</i>)</span> is a <a href="/wiki/Holidays_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Holidays of Japan">national holiday in Japan</a>. It takes place annually on November 23. The law establishing the holiday, which was adopted during the <a href="/wiki/American_occupation_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="American occupation of Japan">American occupation</a> after World War II, cites it as an occasion for commemorating <a href="/wiki/Manual_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Manual labor">labor</a> and production and giving each other thanks. It has roots in the ancient <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> harvest ceremony (<i><a href="/wiki/Niiname-sai" class="mw-redirect" title="Niiname-sai">Niiname-sai</a></i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese-language text">新嘗祭</span>)</span>). </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Harvest_Festival_Flowers_at_Shrewsbury_United_Reformed_Church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1478601.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Harvest_Festival_Flowers_at_Shrewsbury_United_Reformed_Church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1478601.jpg/220px-Harvest_Festival_Flowers_at_Shrewsbury_United_Reformed_Church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1478601.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Harvest_Festival_Flowers_at_Shrewsbury_United_Reformed_Church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1478601.jpg/330px-Harvest_Festival_Flowers_at_Shrewsbury_United_Reformed_Church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1478601.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Harvest_Festival_Flowers_at_Shrewsbury_United_Reformed_Church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1478601.jpg/440px-Harvest_Festival_Flowers_at_Shrewsbury_United_Reformed_Church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1478601.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Harvest_Festival_Flowers_at_Shrewsbury_United_Reformed_Church_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1478601.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div><a href="/wiki/Harvest_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvest Festival">Harvest Festival</a> flowers at a church in <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury" title="Shrewsbury">Shrewsbury</a>, England
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Harvest_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvest Festival">Harvest Festival</a> of Thanksgiving does not have an official date in the United Kingdom; however, it is traditionally held on or near the Sunday of the <a href="/wiki/Harvest_moon" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvest moon">harvest moon</a> that occurs closest to the autumnal equinox. Harvest Thanksgiving in Britain also has pre-Christian roots when the Saxons would offer the first sheaf of barley, oats, or wheat to fertility gods. When the harvest was finally collected, communities would come together for a harvest supper.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> When Christianity arrived in Britain many traditions remained, and today the Harvest Festival is marked by churches and schools in late September/early October (same as Canada) with singing, praying and decorating with baskets of food and fruit to celebrate a successful harvest and to give thanks.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Collections of food are usually held which are then given to local charities which help the homeless and those in need. </p>
