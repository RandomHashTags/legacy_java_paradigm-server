      <p><a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_France" title="Administrative divisions of France">France is divided</a> into 18 administrative <b>regions</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">régions</i>, singular <i lang="fr" title="French language text">région</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[ʁeʒjɔ̃]</a></span>), of which 13 are located in <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_France" title="Metropolitan France">metropolitan France</a> (i.e. on the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> continent), while the other five are <a href="/wiki/Overseas_department_and_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Overseas department and region">overseas regions</a> (not to be confused with the <i><a href="/wiki/Overseas_collectivities" class="mw-redirect" title="Overseas collectivities">overseas collectivities</a></i>, which have a semi-autonomous status).<sup id="cite_ref-INSEE_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INSEE-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>All 13 metropolitan administrative regions (including <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> as of 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_France&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) are further subdivided into 2 to 13 administrative <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">departments</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Prefect_(France)" title="Prefect (France)">prefect</a> of each region's administrative center's department also acting as the regional prefect. The overseas regions administratively consist of only one department each and hence also have the status of <i>overseas departments</i>. </p>
      <p>Most administrative regions also have the status of regional <i><a href="/wiki/Territorial_collectivity" title="Territorial collectivity">territorial collectivities</a></i>, which comes with a <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a>, with departmental and communal collectivities below the region level. The exceptions are Corsica, <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mayotte" title="Mayotte">Mayotte</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, where region and department functions are managed by single local governments having consolidated jurisdiction, and which are known as <i><a href="/wiki/Single_territorial_collectivity" title="Single territorial collectivity">single territorial collectivities</a></i>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Further information: <a href="/wiki/Territorial_evolution_of_France" title="Territorial evolution of France">Territorial evolution of France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decentralisation_in_France" title="Decentralisation in France">Decentralisation in France</a>

      <h3><span id="1982.E2.80.932015"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1982–2015">1982–2015</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1982–2015">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The term <i>région</i> was officially created by the <a href="/wiki/Decentralisation_in_France" title="Decentralisation in France">Law of Decentralisation</a> (2 March 1982), which also gave regions their legal status. The first direct elections for regional representatives took place on 16 March 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Between 1982 and 2015, there were 22 regions in Metropolitan France. Before 2011, there were four <a href="/wiki/Overseas_department_and_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Overseas department and region">overseas regions</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>); in 2011 <a href="/wiki/Mayotte" title="Mayotte">Mayotte</a> became the fifth. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reform_and_mergers_of_regions">Reform and mergers of regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reform and mergers of regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In 2014, the French parliament passed a law reducing the number of metropolitan regions from 22 to 13 effective 1 January 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The law gave interim names for most of the new regions by combining the names of the former regions, e.g. the region composed of <a href="/wiki/Aquitaine" title="Aquitaine">Aquitaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poitou-Charentes" title="Poitou-Charentes">Poitou-Charentes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limousin" title="Limousin">Limousin</a> was temporarily called <i>Aquitaine-Limousin-Poitou-Charentes</i>. However, the combined region of Upper and Lower Normandy is simply called "Normandy" (<i>Normandie</i>). Permanent names were proposed by the new regional councils by 1 July 2016 and new names confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Conseil_d%27%C3%89tat_(France)" title="Conseil d'État (France)">Conseil d'État</a> by 30 September 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The legislation defining the new regions also allowed the Centre region to officially change its name to "<a href="/wiki/Centre-Val_de_Loire" title="Centre-Val de Loire">Centre-Val de Loire</a>" with effect from January 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Two regions, <a href="/wiki/Auvergne-Rh%C3%B4ne-Alpes" title="Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes">Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bourgogne-Franche-Comt%C3%A9" title="Bourgogne-Franche-Comté">Bourgogne-Franche-Comté</a>, opted to retain their interim names.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Regions lack separate legislative authority and therefore cannot write their own statutory law. They levy their own taxes and, in return, receive a decreasing part of their budget from the central government, which gives them a portion of the taxes it levies. They also have considerable budgets managed by a <a href="/wiki/Regional_councils_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional councils of France">regional council</a> <i>(conseil régional)</i> made up of representatives voted into office in regional elections. </p>
      <p>A region's primary responsibility is to build and furnish high schools. In March 2004, the French central government unveiled a controversial plan to transfer regulation of certain categories of non-teaching school staff to the regional authorities. Critics of this plan contended that tax revenue was insufficient to pay for the resulting costs, and that such measures would increase regional inequalities. </p>
      <p>In addition, regions have considerable discretionary power over infrastructural spending, e.g., education, public transit, universities and research, and assistance to business owners. This has meant that the heads of wealthy regions such as <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France" title="Île-de-France">Île-de-France</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne-Alpes" title="Rhône-Alpes">Rhône-Alpes</a> can be high-profile positions. </p>
      <p>Proposals to give regions limited legislative autonomy have met with considerable resistance; others propose transferring certain powers from the departments to their respective regions, leaving the former with limited authority. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overseas_regions">Overseas regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Overseas regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><b>Overseas region</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Région d'outre-mer</i>) is a recent designation, given to the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_department" class="mw-redirect" title="Overseas department">overseas departments</a> that have similar powers to those of the regions of <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_France" title="Metropolitan France">metropolitan France</a>. As integral parts of the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French Republic</a>, they are represented in the <a href="/wiki/French_National_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="French National Assembly">National Assembly</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="French Senate">Senate</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Economic_and_Social_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="French Economic and Social Council">Economic and Social Council</a>, elect a Member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> (MEP) and use the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> as their currency. </p>
      <p>Although these territories have had these political powers since 1982, when France's <a href="/wiki/Decentralisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Decentralisation">decentralisation</a> policy dictated that they be given elected <a href="/wiki/Regional_councils_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional councils of France">regional councils</a> along with other regional powers, the designation <i>overseas regions</i> dates only to the 2003 constitutional change; indeed, the new wording of the constitution aims to give no precedence to either appellation <i>overseas department</i> or <i>overseas region</i>, although the second is still virtually unused by French media. </p>
      <p>The following have overseas region status: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> (<a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>): 
        <ul>
         <li><b><a href="/wiki/Mayotte" title="Mayotte">Mayotte</a></b></li> 
         <li><b><a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a></b></li>
        </ul></li>
      </ul> 
      <ul>
       <li>in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>: 
        <ul>
         <li><b><a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a></b> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a></li> 
         <li><b><a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a></b> in the <a href="/wiki/Antilles" title="Antilles">Antilles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>)</li> 
         <li><b><a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a></b> in the <a href="/wiki/Antilles" title="Antilles">Antilles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>)</li>
        </ul></li>
      </ul> 
      <dl>
       <dd>
        ^ <a href="/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="Saint Pierre and Miquelon">Saint Pierre and Miquelon</a> (off Canada, in North America), once an overseas department, was demoted to a <a href="/wiki/Territorial_collectivity" title="Territorial collectivity">territorial collectivity</a> in 1985.
