      <p>This is an overview of the <b>legality of <a href="/wiki/Ayahuasca" title="Ayahuasca">ayahuasca</a> by country</b>. <a href="/wiki/N,N-Dimethyltryptamine" title="N,N-Dimethyltryptamine">DMT</a>, one of the active ingredients in ayahuasca, is classified as a Schedule I drug under the United Nations 1971 <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Psychotropic_Substances" title="Convention on Psychotropic Substances">Convention on Psychotropic Substances</a>, meaning that international trade in DMT is supposed to be closely monitored; use of DMT is supposed to be restricted to scientific research and medical use. Natural materials containing DMT, including ayahuasca, are not regulated under the 1971 Psychotropic Convention.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The majority of the world's nations classify DMT as a scheduled drug; however, few countries seem to have laws specifically addressing the possession or use of ayahuasca. </p>

         <td>Ayahuasca typically contains DMT, a Schedule I substance, making ayahuasca illegal under federal law. People have been federally prosecuted for ayahuasca possession as recently as 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> State law also typically outlaws DMT. However, federal courts have granted specific religious groups the right to use ayahuasca for ritual purposes. <p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Gonzales_v._O_Centro_Espirita_Beneficente_Uniao_do_Vegetal" class="mw-redirect" title="Gonzales v. O Centro Espirita Beneficente Uniao do Vegetal">Gonzales v. O Centro Espirita Beneficente Uniao do Vegetal</a></i>, the Supreme Court heard arguments on November 1, 2005, and unanimously ruled in February 2006 that the U.S. federal government must allow the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Uni%C3%A3o_do_Vegetal" title="Uniรฃo do Vegetal">Uniรฃo do Vegetal</a> (UDV) church to import and consume ayahuasca for religious ceremonies under the 1993 <a href="/wiki/Religious_Freedom_Restoration_Act" title="Religious Freedom Restoration Act">Religious Freedom Restoration Act</a>. In September 2008, the three <a href="/wiki/Santo_Daime" title="Santo Daime">Santo Daime</a> churches filed suit in federal court to gain legal status to import DMT-containing <a href="/wiki/Ayahuasca" title="Ayahuasca">ayahuasca</a> tea. The case, <i>Church of the Holy Light of the Queen v. Mukasey</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> presided over by Judge <a href="/wiki/Owen_M._Panner" title="Owen M. Panner">Owen M. Panner</a>, was ruled in favor of the Santo Daime church. As of March 21, 2009, a federal judge says members of the church in <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Oregon" title="Ashland, Oregon">Ashland</a> can import, distribute and brew ayahuasca. U.S. District Judge <a href="/wiki/Owen_Panner" class="mw-redirect" title="Owen Panner">Owen Panner</a> issued a permanent injunction barring the government from prohibiting or penalizing the sacramental use of "Daime tea". Panner's order said activities of The Church of the Holy Light of the Queen are legal and protected under <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_religion" title="Freedom of religion">freedom of religion</a>. His order prohibits the federal government from interfering with and prosecuting church members who follow a list of regulations set out in his order.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> </p><p>Except for <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental</a> purposes, growing, selling, or possessing <b><i>Banisteriopsis</i> spp.</b> is prohibited by the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_Act_159" title="Louisiana State Act 159">Louisiana State Act 159</a>. </p><p>In June 2019, the city of <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>, California, in January 2020, the city of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz,_California" title="Santa Cruz, California">Santa Cruz</a>, California, and in September 2020, the city of <a href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor,_Michigan" title="Ann Arbor, Michigan">Ann Arbor</a>, Michigan, decriminalized all "entheogenic" plants, including all natural materials used to produce ayahuasca.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In November 2020, more than 76 percent of voters in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington D.C.</a> voted in favor of the initiative to decriminalize entheogenic plants and fungi.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> </p> </td>
