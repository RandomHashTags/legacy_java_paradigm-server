      <p><a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> is divided into several levels of <a href="/wiki/Territorial_entities" class="mw-redirect" title="Territorial entities">territorial entities</a>. On the first level there are 27 regions:<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Oblasts_of_Ukraine" title="Oblasts of Ukraine">24 oblasts</a></li> 
       <li>one <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Republic_of_Crimea" title="Autonomous Republic of Crimea">autonomous republic</a></li> 
       <li>two <a href="/wiki/City_with_special_status" title="City with special status">cities with special status</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Following the <a href="/wiki/2014_Crimean_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="2014 Crimean crisis">2014 Crimean crisis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Republic_of_Crimea" title="Autonomous Republic of Crimea">Crimea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sevastopol" title="Sevastopol">Sevastopol</a> came under the <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> administration of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Crimea_by_the_Russian_Federation" title="Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation">claims them</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Crimea" title="Republic of Crimea">Republic of Crimea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_cities_of_Russia" title="Federal cities of Russia">federal city</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sevastopol" title="Sevastopol">Sevastopol</a>. Internationally, most states have not recognised the Russian claims. </p>
      <p>Ukraine <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_the_Ukrainian_SSR" title="Administrative divisions of the Ukrainian SSR">directly inherited</a> its administrative divisions from the <a href="/wiki/Republics_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Republics of the Soviet Union">local republican</a> administration of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic">Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic</a>, and the overall structure has not changed significantly since the middle of the 20th century. It is somewhat complex, as beside having several levels of a territorial subdivision it also has a classification for various <a href="/wiki/Populated_places_in_Ukraine" title="Populated places in Ukraine">populated places</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Cities_of_Ukraine" class="mw-redirect" title="Cities of Ukraine">cities</a>. </p>

      <p>According to the Article 133 of <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ukraine" title="Constitution of Ukraine">Constitution of Ukraine</a>, "the system of the administrative and territorial structure of Ukraine is composed of the Autonomous Republic of Crimea, oblasts, districts, cities, districts in city, settlements, and villages." Note, that although certain types of subdivision are not mentioned in Constitution of Ukraine (i.e. rural settlements), they are mentioned for regional composition. Also, for disambiguation regular raions (districts) are sometimes denoted as rural to distinguish them from raions in city.<sup id="cite_ref-regions_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-regions-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Ukraine's administrative divisions are divided as follows: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>By geographical characteristics the units are divided on regions (such as autonomous republic, oblasts, districts, cities with special status) and places of settlement (cities, towns, villages).</li> 
       <li>By their status they can be administrative-territorial units (oblasts and districts), self-governed territorial units (cities, towns, villages). Also the autonomous republic has a unique status of territorial autonomy, while districts in cities combine both characteristics of administrative territorial as well as self-governed territorial units.</li> 
       <li>By position in the system of administrative division of Ukraine, the units divided into territorial units of <i>prime level</i> (cities w/o district division, districts in cities, towns, villages), of <i>middle level</i> (districts, cities with district division), and of <i>higher level</i> (autonomous republic, oblasts, cities with special status).</li> 
       <li>Administrative division has changed because some territories are not under the control of the government. For example, Sievierodonetsk has become the new central regional center.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Regions, cities, districts are governed by a state administration, a chief of which is appointed by the president after a nomination by the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Ministers_of_Ukraine" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet of Ministers of Ukraine">cabinet of ministers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Crimea has its own cabinet of ministers, however the state administration is represented by the office of the Presidential Representative of Ukraine. A basic and the lowest level of administrative division is a settlement that is governed by a local council (rada). Cities as a settlement always carry a special status within a region and have their own form of self-administration (municipality – <i>vykonkom</i>) and some may consist of their own city's districts (raions). City municipalities are governed by a mayor and a city council (miskrada). Some smaller cities, towns, and rural localities may be under control of city municipalities based on larger cities. Towns as well as villages are not controlled by state administration and are self-governed by either a town council (selyshchna rada) or a village council (silrada) within the limits of the Constitution and the laws of Ukraine (article 140 of the Constitution of Ukraine). Village councils may carry a combined jurisdiction which may include several villages and hamlets (selyshche). Unlike villages, each town council always has a separate jurisdiction which may be part of bigger city's council. Hamlet (selyshche) is a non-governed rural locality and is governed by a village council of nearby village. </p>

Note, settlements such as cities do not necessary constitute the basic level of the Ukrainian administrative territorial system. For that purpose cities are categorized into own three categories that correspond to each level of subdivisions. Cities with special status and regional significance beside being divided into special districts in city may also include smaller cities (district significance), settlements, and/or villages. Please, note that the settlement's population size is not the only factor for its status. The final decision on status change is carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukrainian parliament">Ukrainian parliament</a>.       <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Administrative_divisions_of_Ukraine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_development_in_Ukraine" class="mw-redirect" title="Administrative divisions development in Ukraine">Administrative divisions development in Ukraine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_historical_regions" title="Ukrainian historical regions">Ukrainian historical regions</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Before the introduction of oblasts in 1932, Ukraine comprised 40 <a href="/wiki/Okruhas_of_Ukriane" class="mw-redirect" title="Okruhas of Ukriane">okruhas</a>, which had replaced the former <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Imperial</a> <a href="/wiki/Guberniya" class="mw-redirect" title="Guberniya">guberniya</a> (governorate) subdivisions. </p>
      <p>In 1932 the territory of the Ukrainian SSR was re-established based on oblasts. Excluded in the administrative changes was <a href="/wiki/Western_Ukraine" title="Western Ukraine">Western Ukraine</a>, which at that time formed part of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">Second Polish Republic</a> and shared in the Polish form of administrative division based on <a href="/wiki/Voivodeships_of_Poland" title="Voivodeships of Poland">voivodeships</a>. </p>
      <p>In the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> period, the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic">Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic</a> consisted of <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_the_Ukrainian_SSR" title="Administrative divisions of the Ukrainian SSR">25 oblasts and two cities with special status</a>. </p>
      <p>After the fall of the Soviet Union in 1991, Crimea obtained the status of an autonomous republic with its own government instead of a regional state administration. </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>The top level: duchy (land) → palatinate (voivodeship) → regiments (polk) / palanka → governorate → okruha → oblast → TBD</li> 
       <li>The intermediate level: apanage duchy → county (povit) / eldership (starostvo) → <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(county_division)" title="Hundred (county division)">hundered</a> (sotnia) → raion → county (povit)</li> 
       <li>The local level: volost → kurin / community (hromada) → volost → council (rada) → community (hromada)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autonomous_republic">Autonomous republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Administrative_divisions_of_Ukraine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Autonomous republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Republic_of_Crimea" title="Autonomous Republic of Crimea">Autonomous Republic of Crimea</a>
      </div> 
      <p>The Autonomous Republic of Crimea, formerly the Crimean Oblast of the Ukrainian SSR, geographically encompasses the major portion of the Crimean peninsula in southern <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. Its capital is <a href="/wiki/Simferopol" title="Simferopol">Simferopol</a>. The Autonomous Republic of Crimea is the only region within Ukraine that possesses its own constitution. </p>
      <p>On March 16, 2014, after the occupation of Crimea by the Russian military, a referendum on joining the Russian Federation was conducted. A majority of votes supported the measure. On March 21, 2014, the Russian Duma voted to annex Crimea as a <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Crimea" title="Republic of Crimea">subject</a> into the Russian Federation. The Ukrainian government does not recognize the referendum or annexation of Crimea as legitimate. On March 27, the UN General Assembly passed <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Resolution_68/262" title="United Nations General Assembly Resolution 68/262">Resolution 68/262</a> by 100 to 11 votes, recognizing the referendum as invalid and denying any legal change in the status of Crimea and Sevastopol. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oblasts">Oblasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Administrative_divisions_of_Ukraine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Oblasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oblasts_of_Ukraine" title="Oblasts of Ukraine">Oblasts of Ukraine</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Oblasts are on the first (top) level of the administrative division of Ukraine. </p>
      <p>Almost every oblast is named after its administrative center, except for four oblasts. Volyn' and Zakarpattia, whose respective capitals are Lutsk and Uzhhorod, are named after historic regions <a href="/wiki/Volhynia" title="Volhynia">Volhynia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Ruthenia" title="Carpathian Ruthenia">Transcarpathia</a>. The administrative centers of the Dnipropetrovsk Oblast and the Kirovohrad Oblast were renamed to Dnipro and Kropyvnytskyi in 2016, however as of 2017 the oblasts still officially bear the old soviet names as their change must be reflected in an amendment to the Ukrainian Constitution. </p>

      <p>Ukraine has two types of settlements: rural and urban. Rural populated areas (сільський населений пункт) can be either a village (село, <a href="/wiki/Village#Slavic_countries" title="Village">selo</a>) or a rural settlement (селище). Urban populated areas (міський населений пункт) can be either a city (містo) or an <a href="/wiki/Urban-type_settlement" title="Urban-type settlement">urban-type settlement</a> (селище міського типу). For the sake of brevity, urbanized settlements are sometimes classified as towns in the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>. </p>
      <p>Changes to a settlement's status can be made only by the <a href="/wiki/Verkhovna_Rada" title="Verkhovna Rada">Verkhovna Rada</a>. Please note that the size of a settlement does not ultimately define its status, although is a major factor. For example, the city of <a href="/wiki/Prypiat_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Prypiat (city)">Prypiat</a> still retains its status, while having a population of zero, due to its infrastructure, including buildings, roads, and utility networks. </p>
      <p>The typical Ukrainian <i>misto</i> ought to be considered a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a>, not a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a> (compare to <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">City status in the United Kingdom</a>). However, the city's subordination to either an oblast or raion also should be taken into account, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Political" class="mw-redirect" title="Political">political</a> sense. Some of urbanized settlements may be cities of raion subordination, although it could seem confusing, a type of settlement should be considered first as its status is given for administrative purposes. </p>

      <p>According to Ukrainian law a city (місто) in Ukraine is a locality of at least 10,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-UCSMBS_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCSMBS-23">[11]</a></sup> Cities may carry various status. Some may be of national importance, others of <a href="/wiki/City_of_regional_significance_(Ukraine)" title="City of regional significance (Ukraine)">regional (oblast) importance</a>, and the rest of district (raion) importance. For example, the cities of <i>Kyiv</i> and of <i>Sevastopol</i> have special status of national significance and each is officially classified as a <i>city with a special status</i>, which administratively is equivalent to an oblast. Mayors of those cities, in general, as are governors of oblasts, are appointed by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ukraine" title="President of Ukraine">President of Ukraine</a>. However, the status of the mayor of Kyiv is somewhat more complex, and for further information see <a href="/wiki/Legal_status_and_local_government_of_Kyiv" title="Legal status and local government of Kyiv">Legal status and local government of Kyiv</a>. The status of Sevastopol is also unique. </p>
      <p>Almost every oblast has at least one city of regional subordination (importance), which is the administrative center (capital) of that oblast. However, some other big cities within the oblast may have such status as well. The cities of oblast subordination have the same importance of a raion, and often are the administrative centers of such. In addition to regular raions, several Ukrainian cities with national or oblast status are further divided into <i>city raions</i> which may include other cities, towns, and/or villages. In 2010, Ukraine had 23 such cities with their own city raions. </p>
      <p>Many <b>raions</b> also have city municipalities of its level of subordination (importance). Those are usually the administrative centers (capitals). Notice that not all raions have a city as their administrative center; however all the raion centers are at least urban-like (urbanized). All administrative centers have their own form of self-administration. The municipalities of a raion subordination may administer several adjacent local councils (municipalities), usually rural. If a raion has several cities of raion (district) level, they may share administrative power for the raion. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_municipalities">Other municipalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Administrative_divisions_of_Ukraine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other municipalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In addition to city municipalities, Ukraine has urban-like municipalities. The lowest form of self-administration are the rural municipalities and villages. A rural municipality may consist of a single village, usually big, or a combination of other rural villages or localities. Note that some villages also have some additional, very small settlements. Those settlements, together with the home village, combine a local (rural) municipality (silrada). For simplicity's sake, a silrada (rural municipality) is usually referred to as a village and is the lowest level of administrative division. The status of any settlement is granted by the <a href="/wiki/Verkhovna_Rada" title="Verkhovna Rada">Verkhovna Rada</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amalgamated_hromadas">Amalgamated hromadas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Administrative_divisions_of_Ukraine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Amalgamated hromadas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amalgamated_hromada" title="Amalgamated hromada">Amalgamated hromada</a>
      </div> 
      <p>The smalgamated hromadas (<a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk">Об'єднана територіальна громада</span>, <i>Obyednana terytorialʹna hromada</i>) are part of administrative reform that started in 2015. It is intended to replace all councils (local level territorial units). In his draft <a href="/wiki/Ukraine%27s_constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukraine's constitution">constitutional</a> amendments of June 2014 <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_President" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukrainian President">Ukrainian President</a> <a href="/wiki/Petro_Poroshenko" title="Petro Poroshenko">Petro Poroshenko</a> proposed changing the administrative divisions of Ukraine, which should include regions, districts and "hromadas".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>On February 5, 2015, the law "On voluntary association of territorial communities" was adopted creating <i>united territorial communities</i> meaning <a href="/wiki/Settlement_council_(Ukraine)" title="Settlement council (Ukraine)">settlement councils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rural_council_(Ukraine)" title="Rural council (Ukraine)">rural councils</a> and a <a href="/wiki/City_of_district_significance_(Ukraine)" title="City of district significance (Ukraine)">city of district significance</a> can create a new administrative unit.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[13]</a></sup> Any amalgamated hromada with a city as an administrative centre is an urban hromada, any amalgamated hromada with an urban-type settlement as an administrative centre is a settlement hromada, and any amalgamated hromada with a village as an administrative centre is a rural hromada.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[14]</a></sup> New local <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_Ukraine" title="Elections in Ukraine">elections</a> in these united territorial communities were then held.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[15]</a></sup> 226 will be holding elections in 2018 and 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[16]</a></sup> The first 252 were held in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[17]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Law "On Local Self-Government in Ukraine" stipulates that local budgets should have enough money to be administered by local self-government bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-Espresogr_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Espresogr-30">[18]</a></sup> Because many of the small rural councils and cities of district significance could never hope to do this the new administrative unit <i>amalgamated hromada</i> was created.<sup id="cite_ref-Espresogr_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Espresogr-30">[18]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_administrations">Other administrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Administrative_divisions_of_Ukraine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other administrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Ukraine also has several settlements known as <i>viiskove mistechko</i> which were former military installations. Since the fall of the Soviet Union, the secrecy of such settlements has been unveiled, however, the towns are subordinated directly to the Ministry of Defense and do not have their own civil administrations. Such military installations are like ghost towns that are not even identified on a map. One of them, on the border of the <a href="/wiki/Kyiv_Oblast" title="Kyiv Oblast">Kyiv</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhytomyr_Oblast" title="Zhytomyr Oblast">Zhytomyr Oblasts</a> is Makarov-1.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>A special territory known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Zone_of_alienation" class="mw-redirect" title="Zone of alienation">zone of alienation</a></i> falls under the jurisdiction of the Ministry of Emergencies and was the most severely affected territory by the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a>. Additionally, various restricted nature preserves known as <i><a href="/wiki/Zapovednik" title="Zapovednik">Zapovednik</a></i> fall under the jurisdiction of the Ministry of Ecology. </p>
      <p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Donbass" title="War in Donbass">War in Donbass</a>, the status of <a href="/wiki/Civil%E2%80%93military_administrations_(Ukraine)" title="Civil–military administrations (Ukraine)">civil–military administrations</a> was created in territories of <a href="/wiki/Donetsk_Oblast" title="Donetsk Oblast">Donetsk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luhansk_Oblast" title="Luhansk Oblast">Luhansk</a> oblasts where the respective local government units cannot exercise their constitutionally guaranteed powers.<sup id="cite_ref-pravda_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pravda-32">[20]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Currently, Ukraine has the following 3 enclaves: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>The city of <a href="/wiki/Slavutych" title="Slavutych">Slavutych</a> which is administratively subordinated directly to <a href="/wiki/Kyiv_Oblast" title="Kyiv Oblast">Kyiv Oblast</a> while being completely surrounded by neighboring <a href="/wiki/Chernihiv_Oblast" title="Chernihiv Oblast">Chernihiv Oblast</a>.</li> 
       <li>The settlement of Bile which is located on <a href="/wiki/Snake_Island_(Black_Sea)" title="Snake Island (Black Sea)">Snake Island</a> and is fully surrounded by the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> was established in 2007 and is part of <a href="/wiki/Vylkove" title="Vylkove">Vylkove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odessa_Oblast" title="Odessa Oblast">Odessa Oblast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[21]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>The town of <a href="/wiki/Kotsiubynske" title="Kotsiubynske">Kotsiubynske</a> which is part of the Kyiv Oblast and is completely surrounded by the city of Kyiv.</li>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metropolitan_areas">Metropolitan areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Administrative_divisions_of_Ukraine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Metropolitan areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Ukraine has five major agglomerated metropolitan areas (<a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">conurbations</a>). These conurbation areas are not officially recognized and remain to be administered according to official <a href="/wiki/Oblast" title="Oblast">oblast</a>-<a href="/wiki/Raion" title="Raion">raion</a> system of subdivision. </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Kyiv_metropolitan_area" title="Kyiv metropolitan area">Kyiv</a> includes towns such as <a href="/wiki/Irpin" title="Irpin">Irpin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boryspil" title="Boryspil">Boryspil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fastiv" title="Fastiv">Fastiv</a></li> 
       <li>Kharkiv includes towns such as <a href="/wiki/Chuhuiv" title="Chuhuiv">Chuhuiv</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merefa" title="Merefa">Merefa</a>, and numerous other settlements</li> 
       <li>Donetsk includes towns such as <a href="/wiki/Makiivka" title="Makiivka">Makiivka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khartsyzk" title="Khartsyzk">Khartsyzk</a>, and others</li> 
       <li>Dnipro with <a href="/wiki/Kamianske" title="Kamianske">Kamianske</a> and <a href="/wiki/Novomoskovsk,_Ukraine" title="Novomoskovsk, Ukraine">Novomoskovsk</a></li> 
       <li>Odessa with <a href="/wiki/Chornomorsk" title="Chornomorsk">Chornomorsk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ovidiopol" title="Ovidiopol">Ovidiopol</a></li>
      </ul>

      <p>Beside the administrative divisions, geographical divisions are at times used for reference or statistical purposes. The division splits Ukraine into 4 to 6 geographic areas: <a href="/wiki/Western_Ukraine" title="Western Ukraine">Western Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Ukraine" title="Eastern Ukraine">Eastern Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ukraine" title="Southern Ukraine">Southern Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Ukraine" title="Central Ukraine">Central Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ukraine" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Ukraine">Northern Ukraine</a> (occasionally used). </p>
      <p>The neighboring countries such as Russia, Poland, Hungary and Romania sometimes identify certain parts of Ukraine. For example, Russia has territorial claims to Ukraine stating that the Southeastern Ukraine is part of Russia,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the same goes for Hungary that calls <a href="/wiki/Zakarpattia_Oblast" title="Zakarpattia Oblast">Zakarpattia Oblast</a> as Kárpátalja.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
