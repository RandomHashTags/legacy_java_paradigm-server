      <p><b>Norway</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> in the north of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. It is the western part of the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsula</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mainland" title="Mainland">mainland</a> of Norway is surrounded by the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> on the west side, and <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">borders</a> <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> to the east. The southern coast touches the <a href="/wiki/Oslofjord" title="Oslofjord">Oslofjord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skagerrak" title="Skagerrak">Skagerrak</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>. </p>
      <p>The Monarchy of Norway has been <a href="/wiki/Independent" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent">independent</a> since 1814. Its <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> is a king - <a href="/wiki/Harald_V_of_Norway" title="Harald V of Norway">Harald the 5th</a> (as of 2017). The national day is May 17, which celebrates Norway's <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> of 1814. The parliament is called <i>Stortinget</i> and its members are <a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">elected</a> by the people every 4 years. </p>
      <p>About 5 million people live in Norway. The <a href="/wiki/Capital_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital (city)">capital</a> is the city of <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a> is the national language. There are two official written versions of Norwegian called <i><a href="/wiki/Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Bokmål">Bokmål</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">Nynorsk</a></i>. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Sami&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Northern Sami (not yet started)">Northern Sami</a> language is spoken by around 90% of those who speak one of Norway's 3 Sami languages.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Northern Sami is also an official language in a number of <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipalities</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The battle of Hafrsfjord (872 A.D.) resulted in small kingdoms becoming one larger kingdom, ruled by <a href="/wiki/Harald_Fairhair" title="Harald Fairhair">Harald Fairhair</a>. After the king's death, again there were smaller kingdoms, inside Norway.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stockfish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stockfish (not yet started)">Stockfish</a> (or fish that has been <a href="/wiki/Freeze" class="mw-redirect" title="Freeze">freeze</a>-<a href="/w/index.php?title=Drying&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Drying (not yet started)">dried</a>, outside in cold weather) has been traded and <a href="/wiki/Export" title="Export">exported</a>; this happened as early as either the <a href="/wiki/9th_century" title="9th century">9th century</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-StockfishSensationQuoteYear875_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StockfishSensationQuoteYear875-13">[13]</a></sup> 10th century, or 11th century until 1066.<sup id="cite_ref-StockfishSensation_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StockfishSensation-14">[14]</a></sup> Other sources say that the exporting was happening as early as the 12th century; stockfish is one of the country's oldest [type of] <a href="/wiki/Thing" class="mw-disambig" title="Thing">things</a> to be sold for export.<sup id="cite_ref-StockfishSensation_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StockfishSensation-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1349 half of the Norwegian people died, getting sick from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">bubonic plague (or Black Death)</a>. </p>
      <p>When <a href="/w/index.php?title=Olaf_II_of_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Olaf II of Denmark (not yet started)">a Norwegian king</a> died in 1387, there was no Norwegian king until the <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a>. </p>
      <p>In 1397, Denmark, Norway and Sweden began the <a href="/wiki/Kalmar_Union" title="Kalmar Union">Kalmar Union</a>. </p>
      <p>The first [known] <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">map</a>, where Norway is drawn, was made in 1482.<sup id="cite_ref-FirstMapDagbladet_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FirstMapDagbladet-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FirstMap_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FirstMap-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Sweden left the <a href="/wiki/Kalmar_Union" title="Kalmar Union">Kalmar Union</a> in 1523. From 1536/1537, Denmark and Norway formed a <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a> that by 1660 became the state called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Denmark%E2%80%93Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Denmark–Norway (not yet started)">Denmark–Norway</a>; Norway was the weaker part of the union with <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. That union lasted until 1814,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> when the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Treaty_of_Kiel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Treaty of Kiel (not yet started)">Treaty of Kiel</a> said that Norway be <a href="/wiki/Ceded" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceded">ceded</a> (or given) to Sweden; Denmark did not cede the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, Iceland, and Greenland </p>
      <p>The Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> was written in 1814 and signed on 17th May that year. However, Denmark, on the losing side of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> wars, lost Norway to <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, on the winning side. </p>
      <p>A <a href="/w/index.php?title=Swedish%E2%80%93Norwegian_War_(1814)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Swedish–Norwegian War (1814) (not yet started)">Swedish–Norwegian War</a> started on 26 July 1814. It ended on 14 August, because of an <a href="/wiki/Agreement" title="Agreement">agreement</a>, called the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_of_Moss&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Convention of Moss (not yet started)">Convention of Moss</a>. </p>
      <p><b><a href="/wiki/Union_of_Sweden_and_Norway" title="Union of Sweden and Norway">The union with Sweden</a></b>: It started on 14 August 1814, when the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Convention_of_Moss&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Convention of Moss (not yet started)">Convention of Moss</a> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_of_Moss" class="extiw" title="en:Convention of Moss">(en)</a> was signed;<sup id="cite_ref-ConventionOfMoss_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConventionOfMoss-19">[19]</a></sup> the union went on for 90 years. </p>
      <p>The results of the election in 1882, led to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parlamentarism&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Parlamentarism (not yet started)">parlamentarism</a> becoming part of Norway's <a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Political system (not yet started)">political system</a>; the votes of the "swamp men" decided the outcome of the election; they included teachers, artists and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Craftsmen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Craftsmen (not yet started)">craftsmen</a> who were not poor but also did not own land; ownership of land or a contract to use land was necessary to get <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voting_right&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Voting right (not yet started)">voting rights</a>; the "swamp men" had bough cheap land which was nearly useless, except for getting a <a href="/wiki/Right_to_vote" class="mw-redirect" title="Right to vote">right to vote</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SwampMenDagsavisen_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SwampMenDagsavisen-20">[20]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The right to vote in [ <a href="/wiki/National" class="mw-redirect" title="National">national</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Local&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Local (not yet started)">local</a> ] <a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">elections</a>, was given to the public (or the <a href="/w/index.php?title=General_public&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="General public (not yet started)">general public</a>) in 1898.<sup id="cite_ref-SwampMen_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SwampMen-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The end of the <b><a href="/wiki/Union_of_Sweden_and_Norway" title="Union of Sweden and Norway">Union of Sweden and Norway</a></b>, was on 7 June 1905, when Norway got its <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a>. </p>
      <p>In 1905 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince_Carl_of_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Prince Carl of Denmark (not yet started)">Prince Carl of Denmark</a> was elected <a href="/w/index.php?title=King_of_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="King of Norway (not yet started)">King of Norway</a>. His name as a king, was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Haakon_VII_of_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Haakon VII of Norway (not yet started)">King Haakon VII</a>. </p>
      <p>In <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Norway was neutral and served as a trading nation. </p>
      <p>Norway tried to stay neutral in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, but was occupied by German forces from 9 April 1940 to 8 May 1945. </p>
      <p>In 1952 Norway became a member of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>. </p>
      <p>Oil was found in Norway's part of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, during the 1960s; the oil was found under the <a href="/wiki/Sea_floor" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea floor">sea floor</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_family_since_1905">Royal family since 1905</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Royal family since 1905">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Royal family since 1905">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/w/index.php?title=King_Haakon_VII&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="King Haakon VII (not yet started)">King Haakon VII</a> was already married, before he came to Norway (in 1905). His wife, Princess Maud, became <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maud_of_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Maud of Norway (not yet started)">Queen Maud</a>. Their son, Prince Alexander of Denmark, became Crown Prince Olav and followed after his father as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Olav_V_of_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Olav V of Norway (not yet started)">King Olav V</a> in 1957. Olav and his wife, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Princess_M%C3%A4rtha_of_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Crown Princess Märtha of Norway (not yet started)">Crown Princess Märtha</a>, had three children; Princess Ragnhild, Princess Astrid and Prince Harald (later Crown Prince Harald and in 1991 he followed his father as <a href="/wiki/Harald_V_of_Norway" title="Harald V of Norway">King Harald V</a>). </p>
      <p>King Harald is the first king born in Norway in over 600 years. He has two children; Princess Märtha Louise and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Prince_Haakon_of_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Crown Prince Haakon of Norway (not yet started)">Crown Prince Haakon Magnus</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Government">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Change section: Government">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ministry_of_Defence">Ministry of Defence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Ministry of Defence">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Change section: Ministry of Defence">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The Government has Norwegian soldiers working in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> (as of 2017)<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, together with soldiers from other countries that belong to <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Economy">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Change section: Economy">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Exports" class="mw-redirect" title="Exports">Exports</a> include&nbsp;: <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric">hydroelectric</a> power, and <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>. Other <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> are <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>. </p>
      <p>The government collects much money from various sources, and has policies intended to spread this wealth among Norwegians. This spread of wealth, is done both directly and indirectly. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnicity">Ethnicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Ethnicity">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Change section: Ethnicity">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Most people in Norway are ethnic Norwegians. They speak a language that is related to German and English. Swedish and Danish are so close to Norwegian that most Norwegians understand them. Across Norway, many different dialects are spoken. Norwegians disagree on how to make one correct written language. Therefore, there are two standard languages, Bokmål and Nynorsk. Nynorsk is used in writing in most of the western areas and in the central mountains. Bokmål is written by most people in the rest of the country. </p>
      <p>A native population of Norway, the <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sami people</a>, has its home in the northern parts of the country. Their language is not at all related to Norwegian. In some parishes in the far north, they make up the majority of people. Many Sami now live outside the Sami homeland, mostly in Oslo and other big cities. Earlier, Sami people were forced to speak Norwegian in school. Now Sami is taught as the first language in school for Sami children, and Norwegian is the first foreign language. </p>
      <p>Many immigrants have come to Norway in the last 30–40 years. They mostly live in and around Oslo, and in the other big cities. Many immigrants come from nearby countries, like Sweden, Denmark, Germany, Poland and Russia. There are also many from countries far away, such as <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. </p>
      <p>Traditionally, all Norwegians were <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutherans</a>, a variety of the <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> faith. Still, more than 80% of Norwegians are Lutherans. Other important faiths include <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, other Protestant groups and <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Tourism">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Change section: Tourism">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Among tourists to Norway, more come from <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> than from any other country. There are also many Swedes, Danes, British, Dutch and Italians visiting Norway. The Swedes and Danes often come in winter to go skiing. The others mainly come in summer. Many people visit Norway to see the Northern Lights, also known as the 'Aurora Borealis'. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Media">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Change section: Media">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The largest national newspapers in Norway are <a href="/w/index.php?title=Verdens_Gang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Verdens Gang (not yet started)">Verdens Gang</a> (VG), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aftenposten&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aftenposten (not yet started)">Aftenposten</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dagbladet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dagbladet (not yet started)">Dagbladet</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Culture">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Change section: Culture">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Norwegian culture can be compared to English culture in the way that it is considered a bad thing to show off, as opposed to the US, where this is more acceptable. This is a big aspect of Norwegian culture, and it is related to the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">egalitarianism</a>. Because of this, people will understate things, for example if a Norwegian says something is good or nice, it can mean that it's really great. </p>
      <p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Item&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Item (not yet started)">Items</a> from the Viking Age (in Norway), are shown in museums: One item is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gokstad_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gokstad ship (not yet started)">Gokstad ship</a>. </p>
      <p>Museums in Norway includes <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ibsen_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Ibsen Museum (not yet started)">The Ibsen Museum</a> - named after <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Ibsen" title="Henrik Ibsen">Henrik Ibsen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The farmers' culture (<i>bondekulturen</i>) was brutal.<sup id="cite_ref-ForestKillingsOfBabies_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ForestKillingsOfBabies-24">[24]</a></sup> Unwanted babies were "placed in the <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>" (<i>sette barn på skogen</i>) to die, until the <a href="/wiki/Nineteenth_century" class="mw-redirect" title="Nineteenth century">nineteenth century</a>; in the end, the [rural] police authority - consisting of individuals called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lensmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lensmann (not yet started)">lensmann</a> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lensmann" class="extiw" title="en:Lensmann">(en)</a> - were able to control [and stop] these <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crimes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ForestKillingsOfBabies_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ForestKillingsOfBabies-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Modern, cambered skis were invented in the Norwegian province of Telemark in the early 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Politics">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Change section: Politics">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In Norway power is shared among three branches: The justice sector, the government and the <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliament</a> (<a href="/wiki/Stortinget" class="mw-redirect" title="Stortinget">Stortinget</a>). Norway also has a king, <a href="/wiki/Harald_V_of_Norway" title="Harald V of Norway">Harald V</a>, but he does not have any real power and acts as a symbol and <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">ambassador</a>. This form of government is called a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a>. Elections are held every four years, and the winner of the election is the party or <a href="/wiki/Coalition" title="Coalition">coalition</a> of parties that gets the most votes and seats in the parliament. After the elections are done, the winners work together to find out who the <a href="/wiki/Prime_minister" title="Prime minister">prime minister</a> should be, as well as who the other <a href="/wiki/Minister_(government)" title="Minister (government)">ministers</a> should be. </p>
      <p>Here is a short summary of the biggest political parties in Norway, from left to right on the <a href="/wiki/Politics#Left-wing_and_right-wing_political_parties" title="Politics">political axis</a>: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><a href="/wiki/Red_(Norway)" class="mw-redirect" title="Red (Norway)">Red</a> (Rødt): A <a href="/w/index.php?title=Revolutionary_socialist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Revolutionary socialist (not yet started)">revolutionary socialist</a> party which works for equality of income, labour rights, a controlled economy and <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Socialist_Left_Party_(Norway)" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Left Party (Norway)">Socialist Left Party</a> (Sosialistisk venstreparti): The party is not very radical and is concerned with environmental issues as well as education. The party is traditionally regarded as the "teacher's party" because of their focus on learning and school. One might call SV more of a <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democratic</a> party than a socialist party, since their socialist views have faded over the years. They were more radical in the 1970s and 80s.</li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Labour_Party" title="Norwegian Labour Party">Norwegian Labour Party</a> (Arbeiderpartiet): The Labour Party is the biggest party in Norway. They work for a strong economy with many regulations on private businesses, and are traditionally the party for workers, securing labour rights and the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>.</li> 
       <li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_Party_(Norway)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Centre Party (Norway) (not yet started)">Centre Party</a> (Senterpartiet): The Centre Party used to be known as the farmer's party; they no longer use this name, but still they are mostly popular in the countryside and other rural regions, since they work for the environment and protection of Norwegian farmers. For example, raising <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a>, or putting <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a> on <a href="/wiki/Import" title="Import">imports</a>, to make foreign food cost more so that people will buy from Norwegian farmers. This is called <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a>.</li> 
       <li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Green party (not yet started)">Green Party</a> (Miljøpartiet de Grønne): The Green party works for the environment only, and has recently been gaining a lot of popularity for its radical politics. They are mostly a left-side party, but will switch sides if it helps the environment.</li> 
       <li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liberal_Party_(Norway)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Liberal Party (Norway) (not yet started)">Liberal Party</a> (Venstre): Even though it is called Left in English, it is actually a <a href="/wiki/Social_liberal" class="mw-redirect" title="Social liberal">social liberal</a> party that belongs to the centre-right side of Norwegian politics. They work for liberal rights like <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> and they are also concerned with environment. Because of this they heavily support <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a>.</li> 
       <li>Kristelig Folkeparti: The Christian People's Party is at present more active in local than national politics.</li> 
       <li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservative_Party_(Norway)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Conservative Party (Norway) (not yet started)">Conservative Party</a> (Høgre): The flagship of the right side of politics, it is a conservative party and is the second biggest party in Norway. It works for a <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a>, liberal rights and equality of opportunity. They are friendly towards private businesses and support economic growth by making taxes smaller, so that more people can start businesses.</li> 
       <li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Progress_Party_(Norway)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Progress Party (Norway) (not yet started)">Progress Party</a> (Fremskrittspartiet): The Progress Party is a right-side party. It works for stronger <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">immigration laws</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">free market</a> and tough penalties for <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crimes</a>. They also support private schools and <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">flat taxes</a>.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>The Conservative Party and the Progress Party are currently working together in a <a href="/wiki/Coalition" title="Coalition">coalition</a> government, after getting elected in the 2013 elections. It's also a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_government&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Minority government (not yet started)">minority government</a>, so it has to work together with the other parties in the parliament to make change. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Largest_cities">Largest cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=11" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Largest cities">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Change section: Largest cities">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The city with the most people living there (or <a href="/wiki/Inhabitant" class="mw-redirect" title="Inhabitant">inhabitants</a>) is <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>. The city of <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a> has 251,000 people living there; the city of <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a> has 172,000 people in its <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>. </p>
