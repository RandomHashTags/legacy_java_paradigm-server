   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate Change Performance Index</h1>

      <p>The <b>Climate Change Performance Index (CCPI)</b> is a scoring system designed by the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> environmental and development organisation <a href="/wiki/Germanwatch" title="Germanwatch">Germanwatch</a> e.V. to enhance transparency in international climate politics. On the basis of standardised criteria, the index evaluates and compares the climate protection performance of 57 countries and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union (EU)</a> (Status 2020), which are together responsible for more than 90% of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. The Climate Change Performance Index was first published in 2005 and an updated version is presented at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Climate_Change_conference" title="United Nations Climate Change conference">UN Climate Change Conference</a> annually. Germanwatch publishes the index in cooperation with the <a href="/w/index.php?title=NewClimate_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="NewClimate Institute (page does not exist)">NewClimate Institute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_Action_Network" title="Climate Action Network">Climate Action Network</a> International and with financial support from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barthel_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Barthel Foundation (page does not exist)">Barthel Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The most important results are available in German, English, French and Spanish. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_Change_Performance_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In 2017, the underlying <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodology</a> of the CCPI was revised and adapted to the new climate policy framework of the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a> from 2015. The CCPI was extended in order to include the measurement of a country’s progress towards the <a href="/wiki/Nationally_Determined_Contributions" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationally Determined Contributions">Nationally Determined Contributions</a> (NDCs) and the country’s 2030 targets. The national performances are assessed based on 14 indicators in the following four categories: </p>
      <p>1. <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">GHG emissions</a> (weighting 40%) </p>
      <p>2. <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a> (weighting 20%) </p>
      <p>3. <a href="/wiki/Energy_consumption" title="Energy consumption">Energy use</a> (weighting 20%) </p>
      <p>4. <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Politics of global warming">Climate policy</a> (weighting 20%) </p>
      <p>The three categories “GHG emissions”, “Renewable energy” and “Energy use” are each defined by four equally weighted indicators: 1) current level, 2) recent developments (5-year trend), 3) 2°C compatibility of the current performance and 4) 2°C compatibility of 2030 target. These twelve indicators are complemented by two indicators, measuring the country's performance regarding its national climate policy framework and implementation as well as regarding international climate <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomacy</a> in the category "Climate Policy". The data for the “Climate policy” category is assessed annually in a comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> study. Its basis is the performance rating by climate change experts from <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organisations</a>, <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tanks</a> within the countries that are evaluated. In a questionnaire, they give a rating on the most important measures of their governments. The results are rated as <i>very high</i>, <i>high</i>, <i>medium</i>, <i>low</i> or <i>very low</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_Change_Performance_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The most recent results illustrate the main regional differences in climate protection efforts and performance within the 57 evaluated countries and the EU. According to the CCPI, none of the countries has yet achieved a performance across all indicators that can be qualified as <i>very high</i>, because no country fulfills the requirements to limit global warming to well below 2°C, as agreed in the Paris Agreement. This is why the first three places in the final ranking remain unoccupied. In the index for 2020, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> led the ranking, followed by <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. The last three ranks were taken by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Taipei" title="Chinese Taipei">Chinese Taipei</a>. </p>
