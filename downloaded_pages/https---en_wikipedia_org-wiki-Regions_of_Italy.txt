      <p>The <b>regions of Italy</b> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">regioni d'Italia</i>) are the first-level <a href="/wiki/Constituent_entity" class="mw-redirect" title="Constituent entity">constituent entities</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Republic">Italian Republic</a>, constituting its second <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics" title="Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics">NUTS</a> administrative level.<sup id="cite_ref-eurostat_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurostat-1">[1]</a></sup> There are 20 regions, of which five have greater autonomy than the other fifteen. Under the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Constitution">Italian Constitution</a>, each region is an autonomous entity with defined powers. With the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Aosta_Valley" title="Aosta Valley">Aosta Valley</a>, each region is divided into a number of <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Italy" title="Provinces of Italy">provinces</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>During the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a>, regions were administrative districts of the central state. Under the Republic, they were granted a measure of political autonomy by the 1948 <a href="/wiki/Italian_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Constitution">Italian Constitution</a>. The original draft list comprised the <a href="/wiki/Salento" title="Salento">Salento</a> region (which was eventually included in the <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulia</a>). <i>Friuli</i> and <i>Venezia Giulia</i> were separate regions, and <a href="/wiki/Basilicata" title="Basilicata">Basilicata</a> was named <i>Lucania</i>. <a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">Abruzzo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molise" title="Molise">Molise</a> were identified as separate regions in the first draft. They were later merged into <i>Abruzzo e Molise</i> in the final constitution of 1948. They were separated in 1963. </p>
      <p>Implementation of regional autonomy was postponed until the first Regional elections of 1970. The ruling <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democracy_(Italy)" title="Christian Democracy (Italy)">Christian Democracy</a> party did not want the opposition <a href="/wiki/Italian_Communist_Party" title="Italian Communist Party">Italian Communist Party</a> to gain power in the regions where it was historically rooted (the <i>red belt</i> of <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">Emilia-Romagna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Umbria" title="Umbria">Umbria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">Marches</a>). </p>
      <p>Regions acquired a significant level of autonomy following a constitutional reform in 2001 (brought about by a centre-left government and confirmed by popular referendum), which granted them residual policy competence. A further <a href="/wiki/Federalist" title="Federalist">federalist</a> reform was proposed by the regionalist party <a href="/wiki/Lega_Nord" title="Lega Nord">Lega Nord</a> and in 2005, the centre-right government led by <a href="/wiki/Silvio_Berlusconi" title="Silvio Berlusconi">Silvio Berlusconi</a> proposed a new reform that would have greatly increased the power of regions.<sup id="cite_ref-fedref_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fedref-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The proposals, which had been particularly associated with <a href="/wiki/Lega_Nord" title="Lega Nord">Lega Nord</a>, and seen by some as leading the way to a federal state, were rejected in the <a href="/wiki/2006_Italian_constitutional_referendum" title="2006 Italian constitutional referendum">2006 Italian constitutional referendum</a> by 61.7% to 38.3%.<sup id="cite_ref-fedref_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fedref-2">[2]</a></sup> The results varied considerably among the regions, ranging from 55.3% in favor in <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a> to 82% against in <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fedref_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fedref-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Every region has a statute that serves as a regional constitution, determining the form of government and the fundamental principles of the organization and the functioning of the region, as prescribed by the <i>Constitution of Italy</i> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Italy#Art._123" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Constitution of Italy">Article 123</a>). Although all the regions except <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a> define themselves in various ways as an "autonomous Region" in the first article of their Statutes,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> fifteen regions have ordinary statutes and five have special statutes, granting them extended autonomy. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions_with_ordinary_statute">Regions with ordinary statute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Regions with ordinary statute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>These regions, whose statutes are approved by their regional councils, were created in 1970, even though the Italian Constitution dates back to 1948. Since the constitutional reform of 2001 they have had residual legislative powers. <i>The regions have exclusive legislative power with respect to any matters not expressly reserved to state law</i> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Italy#Art._117" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Constitution of Italy">Article 117</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> Yet their financial autonomy is quite modest: they just keep 20% of all levied taxes, mostly used to finance the <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Italy" title="Healthcare in Italy">region-based healthcare system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rai_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rai-9">[8]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autonomous_regions_with_special_statute">Autonomous regions with special statute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Autonomous regions with special statute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Autonomous_Regions_of_Italy.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Autonomous_Regions_of_Italy.svg/150px-Autonomous_Regions_of_Italy.svg.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Autonomous_Regions_of_Italy.svg/225px-Autonomous_Regions_of_Italy.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Autonomous_Regions_of_Italy.svg/300px-Autonomous_Regions_of_Italy.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="735" data-file-height="867"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Autonomous_Regions_of_Italy.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Autonomous regions
        </div>

      <p><a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Italy#Art._116" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Constitution of Italy">Article 116</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Italian_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Constitution">Italian Constitution</a></i> grants <a href="/wiki/Home_rule" title="Home rule">home rule</a> to five regions, namely <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trentino-Alto_Adige/S%C3%BCdtirol" title="Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol">Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aosta_Valley" title="Aosta Valley">Aosta Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friuli_Venezia_Giulia" title="Friuli Venezia Giulia">Friuli Venezia Giulia</a>, allowing them some legislative, administrative and financial power to a varying extent, depending on their specific statute. These regions became autonomous in order to take into account cultural differences and protect linguistic minorities. Moreover, the government wanted to prevent their secession from Italy after the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prof._Dr._Hiroko_Kudo_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prof._Dr._Hiroko_Kudo-10">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutions">Institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Each region has an elected parliament, called <i>Consiglio Regionale</i> (regional council), or <i>Assemblea Regionale</i> (regional assembly) in Sicily, and a government called <i>Giunta Regionale</i> (regional committee), headed by a governor called <i>Presidente della Giunta Regionale</i> (president of the regional committee) or <i>Presidente della Regione</i> (regional president). The latter is directly elected by the citizens of each region, with the exceptions of Aosta Valley and Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol regions where the president is chosen by the regional council. </p>
      <p>Under the 1995 electoral law, the winning coalition receives an absolute majority of seats on the council. The president chairs the <i>giunta</i>, and nominates or dismisses its members, called <i><a href="/wiki/Assessor_(Italy)" title="Assessor (Italy)">assessori</a></i>. If the directly elected president resigns, new elections are called immediately. </p>
      <p>In the Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol region, the regional council is made up of the joint session of the two provincial councils of Trentino and of South Tyrol. The regional president is one of the two provincial commissioners. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Representation_in_the_Senate">Representation in the Senate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_of_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Representation in the Senate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Italian_senators.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Italian_senators.png/220px-Italian_senators.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="285" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Italian_senators.png/330px-Italian_senators.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Italian_senators.png/440px-Italian_senators.png 2x" data-file-width="1632" data-file-height="2112"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Italian_senators.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Number of senators currently assigned to each Region.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Article 57 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Italy" title="Constitution of Italy">Constitution of Italy</a> establishes that the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_the_Italian_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate of the Italian Republic">Senate of the Italian Republic</a> is elected on a regional basis (excluding 6 senators elected by <a href="/wiki/Overseas_constituencies_of_Italian_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Overseas constituencies of Italian Parliament">Italians residing abroad</a> and a small number of <a href="/wiki/Senator_for_life" title="Senator for life">senators for life</a>) by Italian citizens aged 25 or older. </p>
      <p>The 309 senators are assigned to each region proportionally according to their population. However, Article 57 of the Constitution provides that no region can have fewer than seven senators representing it, except for the <a href="/wiki/Aosta_Valley" title="Aosta Valley">Aosta Valley</a> (which has one) and <a href="/wiki/Molise" title="Molise">Molise</a> (which has two). </p>
