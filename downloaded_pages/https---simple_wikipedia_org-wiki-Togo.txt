      <p><b>Togo</b> is a small <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>. It is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a> to the east and <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a> to the north. The capital city <a href="/wiki/Lom%C3%A9" title="Lomé">Lomé</a> is located along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a>. In 2016, about 7.5 million people lived there.<sup id="cite_ref-UN_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UN-3">[3]</a></sup> The official language is <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>. Togo was a hub of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a> for Europeans. Togo was apart of the <a href="/wiki/Slave_Coast" title="Slave Coast">Slave Coast</a>. Many Togolese descendants live in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>. The country is known for <a href="/wiki/Voodoo" title="Voodoo">Voodoo</a>. Togo is also known for it’s various flora and fauna. Monkeys, snakes, and lizards are numerous in many areas in Togo. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Togo&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Togo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>From the 11th century to the 16th century, various tribes entered the region from all directions: the&nbsp;Ewé&nbsp;from the east, and the&nbsp;Mina&nbsp;and&nbsp;Guin&nbsp;from the west; most of them settled in coastal areas. The Portuguese explored the area in the late 15th century. As Togoland, it became a protectorate of Germany in 1884. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Togo was ceded to the French. Togo gained independence from <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> rule in the 1960s. <a href="/wiki/General_officer" title="General officer">General</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnassingb%C3%A9_Eyad%C3%A9ma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gnassingbé Eyadéma (not yet started)">Gnassingbé Eyadéma</a> became the military leader in 1967. When Eyadéma Resigned in 2002 and later died in 2005, his son, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Faure_Gnassingbe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Faure Gnassingbe (not yet started)">Faure Gnassingbe</a> became president. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Togo&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Economy">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Togo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Economy">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> of this small African country is based on <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. Agriculture provides jobs for 65% of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Labor_force&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Labor force (not yet started)">labor force</a> according to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=CIA_Factbook&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="CIA Factbook (not yet started)">CIA Factbook</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Agricultural products include <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa" title="Cocoa">cocoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yam" title="Yam">yams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cassava" class="mw-redirect" title="Cassava">cassava</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tapioca" title="Tapioca">tapioca</a>), <a href="/wiki/Corn" class="mw-redirect" title="Corn">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>. <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">Industries</a> include <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, agricultural processing, <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Handicraft" title="Handicraft">handicrafts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beverage" class="mw-redirect" title="Beverage">beverages</a>. Togo's economy is primarily agricultural. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Togo&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Government">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Togo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Change section: Government">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The president of Togo is elected for 5 years. The president is also the commander of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Togolese_Armed_Forces&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Togolese Armed Forces (not yet started)">armed forces</a>. The president also has the right to begin legislation and dissolve <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliament</a>. </p>
      <p>After Togo's independence from France, <a href="/wiki/General_officer" title="General officer">General</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gnassingb%C3%A9_Eyad%C3%A9ma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gnassingbé Eyadéma (not yet started)">Gnassingbé Eyadéma</a> became the military leader. When Eyadéma died in 2005, his son <a href="/w/index.php?title=Faure_Gnassingbe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Faure Gnassingbe (not yet started)">Faure Gnassingbe</a> became president. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions">Regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Togo&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Regions">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Togo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Change section: Regions">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Togo is divided into 5 <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">regions</a>. The regions are divided into 30 <a href="/wiki/Prefecture" title="Prefecture">prefectures</a> and 1 <a href="/wiki/Lom%C3%A9" title="Lomé">commune</a>. From north to south the regions are <a href="/wiki/Savanes_Region,_Togo" title="Savanes Region, Togo">Savanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kara_Region" title="Kara Region">Kara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centrale_Region" title="Centrale Region">Centrale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plateaux_Region,_Togo" title="Plateaux Region, Togo">Plateaux</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Region" title="Maritime Region">Maritime</a>. </p>
