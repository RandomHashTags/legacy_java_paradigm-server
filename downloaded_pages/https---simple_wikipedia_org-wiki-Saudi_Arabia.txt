      <p><b>Saudi Arabia</b><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[b]</a></sup> officially known as the <b>Kingdom of Saudi Arabia</b> ,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[c]</a></sup> is a country located in <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a> constituting the bulk of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. With a land area of approximately 2,150,000&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup> (830,000&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi), Saudi Arabia is geographically the 5th-largest state in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and 2nd-largest state in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a> after <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>. Saudi Arabia is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a> to the northeast, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> to the southeast, and <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> to the south. It is separated from <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aqaba" title="Gulf of Aqaba">Gulf of Aqaba</a>. It is the only nation with both a <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> coast and a <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> coast, and most of its land is dry and barren. Saudi Arabia is the birthplace of Arabs and Islam <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[9]</a></sup> and sometimes called "the Land of the Two Holy Mosques" in reference to <a href="/wiki/Masjid_al-Haram" title="Masjid al-Haram">Al-Masjid al-Haram</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>), and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Masjid_an-Nabawi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Al-Masjid an-Nabawi (not yet started)">Al-Masjid an-Nabawi</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>), the two holiest places in Islam. Arabic is the exclusive official language in Saudi Arabia. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a> is the <a href="/wiki/State_religion" title="State religion">state religion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[10]</a></sup> The practice of non-Islamic religions is forbidden in public but not in private. <sup id="cite_ref-irf2010_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irf2010-14">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hanbali" title="Hanbali">Hanbali</a> school of faith has a big influence, and the ultra-conservative&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Wahhabism" class="mw-redirect" title="Wahhabism">Wahhabism</a>&nbsp;religious movement within&nbsp;Sunni Islam&nbsp;has been called "the predominant feature of Saudi culture". </p>
      <p>Saudi Arabia is the largest state in western <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> by land area (most of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>) and the second-largest in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab World">Arab World</a>. It has an estimated population of 27 million, of which 8.8 million are registered foreign expatriates and an estimated 1.5 million are <a href="/wiki/Illegal_immigrant" class="mw-redirect" title="Illegal immigrant">illegal immigrants</a>. Saudi nationals comprise an estimated 16 million people.<sup id="cite_ref-American_Bedu_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American_Bedu-15">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Saudi Arabia has the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> reserves and is the world's largest oil <a href="/wiki/Export" title="Export">exporter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[13]</a></sup> Oil accounts for more than 90% of exports and nearly 75% of government revenues, facilitating the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[14]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> groups such as <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a> have expressed concern about the state of human rights in Saudi Arabia. </p>
      <p>Saudi Arabia has cities that are important to the Muslim <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>. Many Muslims from around the world visit <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> in Saudi Arabia to make a <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a>. The pilgrimage is one of the "pillars of Islam". Other big cities are <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">Jeddah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dammam" title="Dammam">Dammam</a>. The pilgrimage is called <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">hajj</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic language</a>. Somebody who makes a pilgrimage to Mecca is called a <i>hajj</i> in the Arabic language. People who are not Muslim are not allowed to enter Mecca. </p>
      <p>Most people speak the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic language</a>. Many people from other countries work in Saudi Arabia. They are called <a href="/wiki/Guest_worker" title="Guest worker">guest workers</a> or expats. The money, or <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> is called the <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Riyal" class="mw-redirect" title="Saudi Riyal">Saudi Riyal</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saudi_Arabia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The area of modern-day Saudi Arabia formerly consisted of four distinct regions:&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Hejaz" title="Hejaz">Hejaz</a>,&nbsp;Najd, and parts of&nbsp;Eastern Arabia&nbsp;(Al-Ahsa) and Southern Arabia&nbsp;('Asir).<sup>[10]</sup>&nbsp;The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia was founded in 1932 by&nbsp;Ibn Saud. He united the four regions into a single state through a series of conquests beginning in 1902 with the capture of&nbsp;Riyadh, the ancestral home of his family, the&nbsp;House of Saud. The country has since been an&nbsp;absolute monarchy, governed along&nbsp;Islamic&nbsp;lines. </p>
      <p>The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia was founded by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Saud_of_Saudi_Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Saud of Saudi Arabia">Abdul-Aziz bin Saud</a> (known for most of his career as <i>Ibn Saud</i>) in 1932. The conquests which eventually led to the creation of the Kingdom began in 1902 when he captured <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>, the ancestral home of his family, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Saud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="House of Saud (not yet started)">House of Saud</a>. The Saudi Arabian government, which is an <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a>, refers to its system of government as being <a href="/wiki/Islamic" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic">Islamic</a>. It has a strong basis in <a href="/wiki/Wahhabi" title="Wahhabi">Wahhabism</a>, a minority school of thought in Islam. The kingdom is sometimes called "The Land of the Two Holy Mosques" in reference to <a href="/wiki/Masjid_al-Haram" title="Masjid al-Haram">Masjid al-Haram</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Masjid_al-Nabawi" class="mw-redirect" title="Masjid al-Nabawi">Masjid al-Nabawi</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>), the two holiest places in <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saudi_Arabia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Politics">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Politics">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Iqra.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iqra.jpg/230px-Iqra.jpg" decoding="async" width="230" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iqra.jpg/345px-Iqra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iqra.jpg/460px-Iqra.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="416"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Iqra.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Verses from the Quran. The <a href="/wiki/Quran" class="mw-redirect" title="Quran">Quran</a> is the official constitution of the country and a primary source of law. Arabia is unique in enshrining a religious text as a political document
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>Saudi Arabia is an <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-18">[15]</a></sup> However, according to the Basic Law of Saudi Arabia adopted by royal decree in 1992, the king must comply with <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Sharia</a> (Islamic law) and the <a href="/wiki/Quran" class="mw-redirect" title="Quran">Quran</a>, while the Quran and the <a href="/wiki/Sunnah" title="Sunnah">Sunnah</a> (the traditions of Muhammad) are declared to be the country's constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-Gerhard_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gerhard-19">[16]</a></sup> The primary source of law is the Islamic <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Sharia</a> derived from the teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sunnah" title="Sunnah">Sunnah</a> (the traditions of the Prophet).<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-20">[17]</a></sup> Saudi Arabia is unique among modern Muslim states in that Sharia is not <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">codified</a> and there is no system of judicial precedent, giving judges the power to use independent legal reasoning to make a decision. Saudi judges tend to follow the principles of the <a href="/wiki/Hanbali" title="Hanbali">Hanbali</a> school of jurisprudence (or <i><a href="/wiki/Fiqh" title="Fiqh">fiqh</a></i>) found in pre-modern texts<sup id="cite_ref-Haffner58_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haffner58-21">[18]</a></sup> and noted for its literalist interpretation of the Qur'an and <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Because the judge is empowered to disregard previous judgments (either his own or of other judges) and may apply his personal interpretation of Sharia to any particular case, divergent judgements arise even in apparently identical cases,<sup id="cite_ref-Sharia_Inc_Courts_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sharia_Inc_Courts-23">[20]</a></sup> making predictability of legal interpretation difficult.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[21]</a></sup> The Sharia court system constitutes the basic judiciary of Saudi Arabia and its judges (qadi) and lawyers form part of the ulema, the country's Islamic scholars. </p>
      <p>Royal decrees are the other main source of law; but are referred to as <i>regulations</i> rather than <i>laws</i> because they are subordinate to the Sharia.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-20">[17]</a></sup> Royal decrees supplement Sharia in areas such as labor, commercial and corporate law. Additionally, traditional tribal law and custom remain significant.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[22]</a></sup> Extra-Sharia government tribunals usually handle disputes relating to specific royal decrees.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup> Final appeal from both Sharia courts and government tribunals is to the King and all courts and tribunals follow Sharia rules of evidence and procedure.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell268_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell268-27">[24]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women">Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saudi_Arabia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Women">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Change section: Women">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Further information: <a href="/wiki/Women_and_Islam" title="Women and Islam">Women and Islam</a>

      <p>A <a href="/wiki/Hijab" title="Hijab">hijab</a> is a traditional Islamic norm whereby women are required "to draw their outer garments around them (when they go out or are among men)" and dress in a modest manner.<sup id="cite_ref-hijab_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hijab-28">[25]</a></sup> Saudi Arabia is different from many Islamic societies in the extent of the covering that it considers Islamically correct hijab (everything except the hands and eyes) and the fact that covering is enforced by <i>Mutaween</i> or religious police. </p>
      <p>Among unrelated men, women must cover the parts of the body that are awrah (private). In much of Islam, a women's face is not considered awrah. In Saudi Arabia and some other Arab states, all of the body is considered awrah except the hands and eyes. Accordingly, most women are expected to wear the <a href="/wiki/Hijab" title="Hijab">hijab</a> (head covering), a full black cloak called an abaya, and a face-veil called <a href="/wiki/Niqab" class="mw-redirect" title="Niqab">niqab</a>. Many historians and Islamic scholars argue that the <a href="/wiki/Quran" class="mw-redirect" title="Quran">Quran</a> was interpreted to require the veil as part of adapting it to tribal traditions.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[26]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[29]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saudi_Arabia&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Geography">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Change section: Geography">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

         </div>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nejd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nejd (not yet started)">Nejd</a> landscape: desert and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuwaiq&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tuwaiq (not yet started)">Tuwaiq</a> Escarpment near <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>
        </div>

      <p>Saudi Arabia is home to the largest mass of sand<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[30]</a></sup> on earth, known as the Rub-al Khali desert (Rub-al Khali means "empty quarter"). The <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> is very <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">hot</a>. There are almost no rivers or lakes in the country. There are many <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wadis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wadis (not yet started)">wadis</a>. </p>
      <p>The countries of <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> are <a href="/wiki/South" title="South">south</a> of Saudi Arabia. Saudi Arabia is <a href="/wiki/West" title="West">west</a> of <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>. All of these countries, except Jordan and Iraq, make the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>. </p>
      <p>Animal life includes <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyena" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyena">hyenas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongoose" title="Mongoose">mongooses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baboon" title="Baboon">baboons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hare" title="Hare">hares</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sand_rat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sand rat (not yet started)">sand rats</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jerboas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jerboas (not yet started)">jerboas</a>. There used to be many larger animals such as <a href="/wiki/Gazelle" title="Gazelle">gazelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oryx" title="Oryx">oryx</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopards</a>. By the 1950s hunting from motor vehicles made these animals almost <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a>. Birds include falcons (which are caught and trained for hunting), eagles, hawks, vultures, sand grouse and <a href="/wiki/Bulbul" title="Bulbul">bulbuls</a>. There are several species of snakes, many of which are venomous, and numerous types of lizards. There is a wide variety of marine life in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>. Domesticated animals include camels, sheep, goats, donkeys, and chickens. </p>
      <p>Because it is mostly a desert Saudi Arabia’s plant life is mostly small herbs and shrubs that need little water. There are a few small areas of grass and trees in southern Asir. The <a href="/wiki/Date_(fruit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Date (fruit)">date</a> palm (<i>Phoenix dactylifera</i>) is widespread. </p>
