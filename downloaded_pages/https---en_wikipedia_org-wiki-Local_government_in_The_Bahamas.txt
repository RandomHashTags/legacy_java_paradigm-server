      <p><a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">Local government</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a> exists in two forms, namely second-schedule and third-schedule district councils. There are a total of 32 local government districts: 13 second-schedule districts, which are further sub-divided into <a href="/wiki/Township" title="Township">town areas</a>, and 19 third-schedule districts, which are all <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Unitary authorities">unitary authorities</a>. The second and third schedules together make up the first schedule.<sup id="cite_ref-A_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A-1">[1]</a></sup> Local government policy is formulated and administered by the Department of Lands and Local Government through the Office of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_Bahamas" title="Prime Minister of the Bahamas">Prime Minister</a>. The day-to-day policy handling of the portfolio falls to the Minister of Local Government who also is empowered to create new <a href="/wiki/Local_government_areas" class="mw-redirect" title="Local government areas">local government areas</a> from time to time based on <a href="/wiki/Demographics" class="mw-redirect" title="Demographics">demographics</a>. The administrative and financial management is overseen by the ministry’s <a href="/wiki/Permanent_secretary" title="Permanent secretary">permanent secretary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-A1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A1-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_government_in_The_Bahamas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Local government previously existed in the Bahamas in the form of appointed "Board of Works". Here towns and villages held their influence over these Board of Works, but almost all final decisions were made by the <a href="/wiki/Central_government" title="Central government">central government</a> through that islands' <a href="/wiki/Commissioner" title="Commissioner">Commissioner</a>. The modern system of local government that is in use today was implemented on <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas_Local_Government_Act_1996" title="The Bahamas Local Government Act 1996">8 March 1996</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Out_Islands" title="Out Islands">Out Islands</a> of the country could now enjoy a somewhat greater degree of autonomy, but <a href="/wiki/New_Providence_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="New Providence Island">New Providence Island</a>, in which the capital city <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a> is located, was to be directly governed by the central government. The act that implemented local government had described all districts as either being Second-Schedule or Third-Schedule districts. <sup id="cite_ref-NEW_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEW-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Districts_of_The_Bahamas">Districts of The Bahamas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_government_in_The_Bahamas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Districts of The Bahamas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The <b>Districts of the Bahamas</b> provide a system of <a href="/wiki/Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Government">Local Government</a> everywhere in <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a> except <a href="/wiki/New_Providence" title="New Providence">New Providence</a> (where <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a> the capital is located, whose affairs are handled directly by the central government). The current system dates from 1996 when 23 <a href="/wiki/District" title="District">districts</a> were "created" by <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas_Local_Government_Act_of_1996" class="mw-redirect" title="The Bahamas Local Government Act of 1996">The Bahamas Local Government Act of 1996</a>– a further 9 have been added since 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Local Government in The Bahamas has seen great success since its introduction, but there has been concern over the case of New Providence and whether or not it should have local government. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_Councils">Types of Councils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_government_in_The_Bahamas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types of Councils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Every district in the Bahamas has a districts council.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A district council is a corporate body with <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_succession" title="Perpetual succession">perpetual succession</a>; capable of entering into contracts, of suing and being sued, of acquiring, holdings, leasing and disposing of property of any description, and of doing all such things and entering into such transactions that are within the scope of the <i>Local Government Act.</i><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> District Councillors are elected by the population of that district in accordance with <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas_Local_Government_Act_1996" title="The Bahamas Local Government Act 1996">Local Government Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As stated in <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas_Local_Government_Act_1996" title="The Bahamas Local Government Act 1996">The Bahamas Local Government Act 1996</a>, Districts councillors shall within two weeks of their election, elect from among themselves a Chief Councillor.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Chief Councillor shall be the representative of a Districts Council for all affairs. He or she is to preside over all meetings and also themselves co-ordinate these meetings.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>All districts councils are classed as first-schedule councils. The first-schedule is further sub-divided into two types of councils: two tier second-schedule district councils that have town committees within their jurisdiction, and <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary</a> third-tier district councils.<sup id="cite_ref-A2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A2-10">[10]</a></sup> Second-schedule districts have the following statutory boards and committees<b>:</b> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Road Traffic Licensing Authority</li> 
       <li>Port and Harbour Authority</li> 
       <li>Hotel Licensing Board</li> 
       <li>Liquor and Shop Licensing</li> 
       <li>Town Planning Committee</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Town committees are sub-structures of the second-schedule district councils, but are also corporate bodies themselves. They share responsibility with second-schedule district councils for a number of the schedule local government functions. They also have statutory responsibility for local regulation and licensing within their jurisdiction.<sup id="cite_ref-A3_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A3-11">[11]</a></sup> Third-schedule districts councils are unique within the Bahamas because they combine the responsibilities of the second-schedule districts and of the town committees. Both second- and third-schedule district councils carry out a building control function...<sup id="cite_ref-A4_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A4-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections">Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_government_in_The_Bahamas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Local government elections take place once every three years in the Bahamas with the most recent elections taking place in June 2011 in which 391 positions were contested.<sup id="cite_ref-A5_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A5-13">[13]</a></sup> The voting system used in local government elections is the <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post" class="mw-redirect" title="First-past-the-post">first-past-the-post</a> system. Both councillors of third-schedule district councils and members of town committees are directly elected, while members of second-schedule councils are indirectly elected from town committees. Third schedule district councils have between five and nine members, whereas the size of councils in both second-schedule councils and town committees varies according to population size. <a href="/wiki/By_election" class="mw-redirect" title="By election">By elections</a> are held whenever the need arises. A councillor is deemed to have resigned if they are absent for three consecutive meetings.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
      <p>For both types of district councils the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Councillor" title="Chief Councillor">Chief Councillors</a> and their deputies are indirectly elected from amongst the elected officials. They serve for the lifetime of the council and the Minister of Local Government determines their <a href="/wiki/Stipend" title="Stipend">stipend</a>. Second-schedule district councils' statutory boards also elect chairpersons and their deputies from amongst their members.<sup id="cite_ref-A6_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A6-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
