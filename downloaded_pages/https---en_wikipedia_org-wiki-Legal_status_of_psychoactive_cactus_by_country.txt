   <td style="background:#9F9;vertical-align:middle;text-align:center;" class="table-yes">Legal</td> 
   <td>Only peyote and mescaline itself are controlled substances in the United States. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.deadiversion.usdoj.gov/schedules/orangebook/c_cs_alpha.pdf">Substance: Peyote, DEA#: 7415, CSA SCH: I, NARC: N, Other names: Cactus which contains mescaline</a> <p>Following the passage of the <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_Religious_Freedom_Act" title="American Indian Religious Freedom Act">American Indian Religious Freedom Act</a> Amendments of 1994, United States <a href="/wiki/Federal_law" title="Federal law">federal law</a> (and many <a href="/wiki/State_law" title="State law">state laws</a>) protects the <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Possession_(law)" title="Possession (law)">possession</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingestion" title="Ingestion">consumption</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plant_cultivation" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant cultivation">cultivation</a> of peyote as part of "<a href="/wiki/Bona_fide" class="mw-redirect" title="Bona fide">bona fide</a> religious ceremonies" (the federal statute is the American Indian Religious Freedom Act, codified at <a href="/wiki/Title_42_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 42 of the United States Code">42&nbsp;U.S.C.</a>&nbsp;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/1996a">§&nbsp;1996a</a>, "Traditional Indian religious use of the peyote sacrament", exempting only use by Native American persons. US v. Boyll expanded permitted use to all persons engaged in traditional Indian use, regardless of race. All US states with the exception of Idaho and Texas allow usage by non-native, non-enrolled persons in the context of ceremonies of the <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Church" title="Native American Church">Native American Church</a>. Some states such as Arizona additionally exempt any general bona fide religious activity or spiritual intent. US jurisdictions enacted these specific statutory exemptions partially in reaction to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">US Supreme Court</a>'s decision in <i><a href="/wiki/Employment_Division_v._Smith" title="Employment Division v. Smith">Employment Division v. Smith</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Supreme_Court_cases,_volume_494" title="List of United States Supreme Court cases, volume 494">494</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Reports" title="United States Reports">U.S.</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/494/872/">872</a> (1990), which held that laws prohibiting the use of peyote that do not specifically exempt religious use nevertheless do not violate the <a href="/wiki/Free_Exercise_Clause" title="Free Exercise Clause">Free Exercise Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First Amendment</a>. </p> </td>
