      <p><b>North Korea</b> (officially called the <b>Democratic People's Republic of Korea</b> (<b>DPRK</b>)), is a <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> in the northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a> <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsula</a>. North Korea is next to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital city</a> of North Korea is <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyŏngyang</a>, which is also the largest city. </p>
      <p>The country was founded in 1948 after it had been freed from <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> occupation, and a <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialist</a> <a href="/wiki/State" title="State">state</a> backed by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> was established. <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">The Republic of Korea</a> is the southern half of the <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean Peninsula</a>, and was occupied by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and the U.S. set up a <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a> in the south. At first there was a war between the armies of the North and South in what is called the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, but while the fighting stopped in 1953, the war never officially ended. North Korea was friendly with China and Russia but never was formally allied with either and became more isolated over time. While South Korea had an unstable, <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a> government after independence, the North went through steady development, was richer, and had a better <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a> than South until the 1980s, when the South became more <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a>. Soon afterwards, the North's main trading partners, the Soviet Union, collapsed. This left North Korea stranded and isolated. Throughout the 1990s, North Korea suffered from <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a>. As South Korea got richer and life got better there, North Korea got poorer and life became worse there. Afterwards, things stabilized but continued to lag behind the South. </p>
      <p>North Korea is generally described as a <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinist</a> <a href="/wiki/Dictatorship" title="Dictatorship">dictatorship</a>. The country's leader <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung" class="mw-redirect" title="Kim Il-sung">Kim Il-sung</a> said that the government was following his own ideology of "<a href="/wiki/Juche" title="Juche">Juche</a>", which means "self-reliance". Later on, the country's leaders began to remove "communism" from North Korean laws and philosophy. After Kim Il-sung died during the disasters of the 1990s, his son <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">Kim Jong-il</a> took his place and was promoted by the government as the leader who led North Korea out of the disasters. North Korea was the only communist country in history where leadership was directly given to the former leader's son after his death. This is called <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_monarchy" title="Hereditary monarchy">hereditary rule</a>. Kim Jong-il enacted a new policy of "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Songun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Songun (not yet started)">Songun</a>", or "military-first", which turned the country into a military state. When he died in 2011, his youngest son <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-un" title="Kim Jong-un">Kim Jong-un</a> took his place and continues to lead the country today. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_History">Early History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Early History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Early History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Historians believe that the <a href="/wiki/Korean_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Korean people">Korean people</a> have lived in the area for thousands of years. Before 1910, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> was one country. It had a king and people were mostly farmers. The country was peaceful and was not communist. In 1910, that changed. <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> went to war. Since Japan and Russia were both very close to Korea, Japan took Korea for themselves as part of Japan. Japan then had control. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Divided_Korea">Divided Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Divided Korea">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Change section: Divided Korea">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Between 1910 and the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> in 1945, Korea was considered a part of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> due to Japan's unethical colonization of Korea. In 1945, the <a href="/wiki/USSR" class="mw-redirect" title="USSR">USSR</a> declared war on Japan and the United States bombed it (Nagasaki and Hiroshima); severely weakening its <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empire</a> and forcing it to surrender. Japan's weak status allowed the Soviets to enter Korea fairly freely and occupy the northern half whilst the United States took the southern half. Each then created governments supporting their own ideologies, Marxist–Leninist Single Party State (North) and Democratic Capitalist State (South).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">source?</span></a></i>]</sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Korean_War">The Korean War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: The Korean War">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Change section: The Korean War">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>
      </div> 
      <p>In 1950, North Korea sent soldiers to South Korea. North Korea wanted to bring together North and South Korea to form a single Korean country, and Korean families that had been split by the division of North and South to be together again. The North Korean leaders wanted South Korea to be <a href="/wiki/Communist" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist">communist</a>, like North Korea and the Soviet Union were. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> sent soldiers to Korea. These soldiers came from many countries. These countries did not like Communism (to learn more, see the article about the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>). If South Korea became Communist, then maybe other countries would too. General Douglas MacArthur led the soldiers. </p>
      <p>North Korea had taken over much of South Korea by force. With the help of the other countries, South Korea took back their land, and even much of North Korea, up to the <a href="/wiki/Yalu_River" title="Yalu River">Yalu River</a>, which forms the <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">border</a> between North Korea and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. China, which was also communist, helped the North Koreans to get the land back that the South Korean soldiers had taken. </p>
      <p>After three years, in 1953, North Korea and South Korea both decided that no one would win the war and both countries signed an <a href="/wiki/Armistice" title="Armistice">armistice</a>, which is an agreement that made both countries stop fighting. North Korea and South Korea were divided by a <a href="/wiki/Korean_Demilitarized_Zone" title="Korean Demilitarized Zone">demilitarized zone</a>, or DMZ, which is a special place that surrounds the border between North and South Korea where both countries can not place lots of soldiers, so that fighting does not start again. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Times">Modern Times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Modern Times">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Change section: Modern Times">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Even though the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Demilitarized_Zone" title="Korean Demilitarized Zone">Korean Demilitarized Zone</a> is meant to stop problems between the two countries, sometimes soldiers on both sides of the border fire their guns at each other. A special town in the zone, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Panmunjom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Panmunjom (not yet started)">Panmunjom</a>, is called the Joint Security Area, or JSA, and sometimes the leaders of both countries meet there to talk about possibly coming back together. </p>
      <p>North Korea is one of the few countries in the world that has made <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear missiles</a>, which can kill many people if they are exploded. North Korea will not say how many nukes it has, but other countries think that the North Korean government probably has built ten missiles so far out of a deadly <a href="/w/index.php?title=Periodic_Table_of_the_Elements&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Periodic Table of the Elements (not yet started)">element</a> called <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>. </p>
      <p>In October 2006, North Korea said that it tested one of its nuclear bombs. Although the North Korean government said that the test was not dangerous, many other countries and the United Nations were nonetheless enraged. </p>
      <p>Three years later in 2009, North Korea did another test, which broke a United Nations law called Resolution 1718, which said North Korea could not keep building and testing nuclear bombs. </p>
      <p>In 2010, a South Korean warship sank, killing over 40 soldiers. An international investigation concluded that North Korea had sunk a South Korean warship with a <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedo</a>. North Korea strongly said that it did not have anything to do with the sinking. When the United States and South Korea planned to set up defenses in case North Korea tried to attack again, North Korea's National Defense Commission threatened on to start a war with its nuclear weapons. </p>
      <p>In April 2012, North Korea launched a rocket called Bright Star 3. The reasons for the launch were science and the 100th anniversary of the birth of the country's founder, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-Sung" title="Kim Il-Sung">Kim Il-Sung</a>. </p>
      <p>The government said the rocket carried a weather <a href="/wiki/Satellite_(artificial)" title="Satellite (artificial)">satellite</a> so the government could find out what the weather would be. However, other countries said that the weather satellite was a story made up by the government so the real purpose of the rocket would not the known—which most countries thought was to test a nuclear missile that could be launched at the United States or South Korea. South Korean leaders said it would shoot the rocket down when it came over South Korea. </p>
      <p>Because of this rocket, other countries stopped helping North Korea, even though the government invited other countries to see the rocket launch to make it seem like the North Koreans had nothing to hide from other countries. The rocket was eventually launched, but it did not work and crashed just a minute and thirty seconds after it was launched. In December 2012, the government tried to launch the rocket again. It worked this time and went into orbit circling the Earth, though the United States said that it was very unstable and might fall back to Earth. Experts in Europe noticed the satellite's reflection was fluctuating (getting brighter then dimmer); indicating that the satellite is tumbling in its orbit. </p>
      <p>In February 2013, North Korea tested a nuclear bomb for the 3rd time, causing much outrage from other countries. The government also released many videos that depicted possible missile targets in the United States. North Korea however does not posses missiles that could reach US mainland but some say Hawaii is a possibility. It is highly unlikely that North Korea would ever fire against the US, Japan or South Korea. Many missiles depicted in DPRK parades are fakes used to exaggerate North Korea's military strength.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">source?</span></a></i>]</sup> Most of its inventory dates from the Soviet years. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_Government">Politics and Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Politics and Government">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Change section: Politics and Government">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>People often think that North Korea is a communist country. It is actually a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Socialist-military_dictatorship&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Socialist-military dictatorship (not yet started)">socialist-military dictatorship</a>. In its most recent constitutional change, the word 'communism' was removed. Large pictures of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> were removed from <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung_Square" title="Kim Il-sung Square">Kim Il-Sung square</a> in 2012. The government has a similar structure to the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (USSR), once a close ally, but it is very different from the USSR. Leaders of the USSR were elected by a group of government officials. In North Korea, the new leader is the current leader's male heir. For this reason, North Korea is often referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">hereditary</a> dictatorship. </p>
      <p>North Korea's official state ideology is <a href="/wiki/Juche" title="Juche">Juche</a>. That is a form of socialism developed by the country's founder, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-Sung" title="Kim Il-Sung">Kim Il-Sung</a>. Juche means self-reliance. It teaches that to achieve true <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> and become self-sufficient, the state must become fully isolated from the rest of society. </p>
      <p>The first head of state and Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Workers_Party_of_North_Korea" title="Workers Party of North Korea">Workers Party of North Korea</a> was <a href="/wiki/Kim_Tu-bong" title="Kim Tu-bong">Kim Tu-bong</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the late 1950s, the second head of state and party chairman was <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-Sung" title="Kim Il-Sung">Kim Il-Sung</a>. </p>
      <p>In July 1994, Kim died. His son, <a href="/wiki/Kim_J%C5%8Fng-Il" class="mw-redirect" title="Kim Jŏng-Il">Kim Jŏng-Il</a>, took over. He became the third head of state and party chairman. </p>
      <p>In December 2011, Kim died. His son, <a href="/wiki/Kim_J%C5%8Fng-Un" class="mw-redirect" title="Kim Jŏng-Un">Kim Jŏng-Un</a> became the head of the government. </p>
      <p><i>Songun</i> is a North Korean idea. It means "army first." The job of every North Korean person is to feed the Army. Kim Jŏng-Un is the "Chairman of the National Defense Commission of North Korea". That is one of many jobs he has. He is like a king, and can do what he wants. The average North Korean citizen makes around $900 a year. Kim Jŏng-Un makes around $800,000 a year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">source?</span></a></i>]</sup> He lives in a palace. He has lots of soldiers who go wherever he goes to protect him. </p>
      <p>North Korea is technically a multi-party state since other parties do exist besides the Korean Workers' Party (KWP). However, the KWP also controls the other parties so it can stay in power. The <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> claims that North Korea's citizens have freedom of speech, religion and press. In real life, these citizens do not have these rights. People can be jailed if they criticise the party, government, or leaders. North Koreans are encouraged to report family members to the police if they think they are doing something illegal. In return they get more privileges. If someone is caught doing a crime, their whole family will be sent along with them to a labour camp. Most die there, but a few escape. </p>
      <p>North Korean people have very little <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a>. They get their news from the Korean Central News Agency (KCNA). The KCNA makes sure all the country's <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> news makes the government look good. This is called <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a>. The government blocks access to the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. Only a few trusted military officials and party members can access the world Internet. It has been reported that almost all North Koreans do not know that men have landed on the moon. The government runs an <a href="/wiki/Intranet" title="Intranet">intranet</a> service that is available on all North Korean computers. It is nothing like the real Internet but is filled with propaganda that makes the government, party, and leaders look good. The government also tries to keep ideas from other countries out of North Korea. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital punishment">Capital punishment</a> is a common type of punishment in North Korea. <a href="/wiki/Stealing" class="mw-redirect" title="Stealing">Stealing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drug_smuggling" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug smuggling">drug smuggling</a>, attempted murder, <a href="/wiki/Spy" class="mw-redirect" title="Spy">spying</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse" title="Slaughterhouse">slaughtering</a> <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> for food, trying to challenge the laws of North Korea, traveling without permission, looking at <a href="/wiki/Media" class="mw-disambig" title="Media">media</a> <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">unapproved by North Korea</a> (which includes South Korean dramas, pornography, and Western movies), possessing a USB drive, and practicing religions that are against worshipping <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-un" title="Kim Jong-un">Kim Jong-un</a> and his family can all result in the death penalty. </p>
      <p>In parts of the country, there is not enough food. Currently, other countries give food to some people in North Korea. This is called <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_aid" title="Foreign aid">foreign aid</a></i>. The aid sometimes stops coming if North Korea is thought to be testing nuclear bombs. Very recently, North Korea's food aid stopped after the government launched a satellite in April 2012. Other countries said North Korea had broken their side of an agreement. The North Korean government said that it was the United States that had broken the agreement. </p>
      <p>It is hard for people from other countries to visit North Korea. Visitors must be guided by two army members called "minders". The minders try and make sure the people do not find out about anything that might make the government look bad. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Economy">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Change section: Economy">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>North Korea is one of the poorest countries in the world. This is due to the government not wanting to allow North Koreans to earn their own money outside of their government-given jobs, to allow people to work the jobs they want, and to trade with other countries. </p>
      <p>Although Pyongyang (which is the one of the few areas foreign travelers are allowed to visit) makes the country look like it is richer and more prosperous than it truly is, life looks extremely different throughout the rest of the country. Only the highest-ranking North Koreans are allowed to live in Pyongyang, and they must have permission from the government to do so. Unlike the rest of the country, people often own cars and bicycles, there is a subway system, and power exists throughout the city. Meanwhile, very few people in he <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">countryside</a> own a car, or even a bicycle, and animal-drawn carts are very common. Electricity in rural areas is rare, if it even exists at all. Proof of this is that when seen from space, there are almost no lights that can be seen outside of Pyongyang. Even in Pyongyang, <a href="/wiki/Power_cut" title="Power cut">power cuts</a> are common. </p>
      <p>As said earlier, <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a> is very common way to die throughout the country. The <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> in most of the country is not good enough to grow enough <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> to feed people without any kind of <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>. As a result, people often steal human <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">poop</a> to <a href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilize</a> the soil. People also go out into nature to look for anything to eat such as grass, bark, frogs, worms, etc. </p>
      <p>Since the government does not provide the average North Korean person enough resources to survive, most North Koreans get what they need from the black market. Even though buying and selling on the black market is illegal, people are able to avoid punishment by bribing officials. They might give things such as money, gifts, or sexual favors. Most people buying and selling at the black market are women. This is because all adult men are required by law to go to work during workhours, even when their workplace cannot or will not pay them for their work. </p>
      <p>Since electricity is rare throughout North Korea, tasks such as cooking and washing clothes take much longer and are more tiring. People often wash clothes in a river or creek and must start fires before they can cook. </p>
      <p>Although <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">healthcare</a> is free in North Korea, most <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospitals</a> and doctor's offices do not have the medicines or supplies needed to correctly treat patients. As a result, many patients die or become crippled for the rest of their lives in the hospital. On one occasion, North Korea hired an eye surgeon from Nepal to because there was not a single eye surgeon in the country. </p>
      <p>In order to raise money for the country, the government sometimes sends North Korean citizens to its allies, like China and Russia, to do work there. These jobs include food service in North Korean restaurants and <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>. Even outside of North Korea, these North Koreans are often not allowed to interact with the local people outside of work, are forced to live in North Korean communities, and must obey North Korean laws. </p>
      <p>Other ways North Korea makes money is through selling illegal drugs to other countries, making fake money of other countries, and stealing money online. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_Religion">Culture and Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Culture and Religion">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Change section: Culture and Religion">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Historically, both <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and North Korea have the same set of values. In 1945, the <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsula</a> was divided. Since then, the government of both North and South Korea were different. This has led to different developments in both North and South Korea. <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a> says that free religious activities do not exist in North Korea. The culture in Korea has been influenced by that of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Despite this, Korea has developed a cultural identity that is different from that of Mainland China.<sup id="cite_ref-Fairbank_et_al._12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fairbank_et_al.-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Literature and arts in North Korea are state-controlled. Specialized committees of the KWP are responsible for this.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Film is also a significant artistic medium in North Korea and Kim Jong Il's manifesto <i>The Cinema and Directing</i> (1987) is the basis for the nation's filmmakers.<sup id="cite_ref-Anna_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anna-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Korean culture came under attack during the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Japanese rule</a> from 1910 to 1945. Japan enforced a <a href="/wiki/Cultural_assimilation" title="Cultural assimilation">cultural assimilation</a> policy. During the Japanese rule, Koreans were forced to learn and speak Japanese, adopt the Japanese family name system and <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> religion, and were forbidden to write or speak the <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean language</a> in schools, businesses, or public places.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In addition, the Japanese altered or destroyed various Korean monuments including <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gyeongbokgung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gyeongbokgung (not yet started)">Gyeongbok Palace</a> and documents which portrayed the Japanese in a negative light were revised. </p>

      <p>Both Koreas share a <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> heritage and a recent history of Christian and <a href="/wiki/Cheondoism" title="Cheondoism">Cheondoism</a> ("religion of the Heavenly Way") movements. The North Korean constitution states that freedom of religion is permitted.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> According to the Western standards of religion, the majority of the North Korean population could be characterized as non-religious.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">source?</span></a></i>]</sup> However, the cultural influence of such traditional religions as Buddhism and Confucianism still have an effect on North Korean spiritual life.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>It seems that Buddhists are accepted more than other religious groups. Christians are said to be severely persecuted by the authorities, often by throwing them in prison or executing them publicly. Buddhists are given limited funding by the government to promote the religion, because Buddhism played an integral role in traditional Korean culture.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In May 2014, an American tourist was arrested at Pyongyang Sunan Airport after it was discovered he left a Bible in a nightclub on the DPRK's east coast. He was convicted of attempting to overthrow the government but was eventually released several months later. At the time of his imprisonment, there were two other American citizens held by North Korea awaiting transfer to political prison camps. Both have since been released. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Armed_Forces">Armed Forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Armed Forces">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Change section: Armed Forces">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Even though the country only has fewer than 25 million people, the country still has the world's fourth largest standing military at 1.28 million soliders.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This is because North Korea has a law that requires all men to join the military for ten years after they graduate high school. </p>
      <p>The government says it keeps a large military to protect the country from its enemies, particularly the United States and Japan. However, it is more likely that it exists to keep the government in power. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provinces">Provinces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Provinces">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Change section: Provinces">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The land of North Korea is divided into nine <a href="/wiki/Provinces" class="mw-redirect" title="Provinces">areas called provinces</a> and two cities. </p>
      <p>The nine provinces are: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Pyongannam</li> 
       <li>Pyonganbuk</li> 
       <li>Chagang</li> 
       <li>Hwanghaenam</li> 
       <li>Hwanghaebuk</li> 
       <li>Kangwon</li> 
       <li>Hamgyongnam</li> 
       <li>Hamgyongbuk</li> 
       <li>Ryanggang</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>The main cities are: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>Pyongyang, the capital city and also the largest city</li> 
       <li>Rason, a special city where other countries can make money, called a Special Economic Zone.</li>
      </ul>
