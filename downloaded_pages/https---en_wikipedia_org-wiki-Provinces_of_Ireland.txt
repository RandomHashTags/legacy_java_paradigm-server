      <p>Since the early 17th century, there have been four <b>Provinces of Ireland</b>: <a href="/wiki/Connacht" title="Connacht">Connacht</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leinster" title="Leinster">Leinster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Munster</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> word for this territorial division, <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cúige</i>, meaning "fifth part", indicates that there were once five; however, in the medieval period there were more. The number of provinces and their delimitation fluctuated until 1610, when they were permanently set by the English administration of <a href="/wiki/James_I" class="mw-redirect" title="James I">James I</a>. The provinces of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> no longer serve administrative or political purposes but function as historical and cultural entities. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In modern <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> the word for province is <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cúige</i> (pl. <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cúigí</i>). The modern Irish term derives from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cóiced</i> (pl. <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cóiceda</i>) which literally meant "a fifth".<sup id="cite_ref-Koch_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch-1">[1]</a></sup> This term appears in 8th-century law texts such as <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Miadslechta</i> and in the legendary tales of the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Cycle" title="Ulster Cycle">Ulster Cycle</a> where it refers to the five kingdoms of the "Pentarchy".<sup id="cite_ref-Koch_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch-1">[1]</a></sup> MacNeill enumerates the five earliest fifths mentioned, these comprising the kingdoms of Ulster, Connacht, Munster, Tara (North Leinster) and Dinn Riogh (South Leinster), located on the Barrow. The earliest hero tales name the Boyne as the dividing boundary between Ulster and Leinster, indicating that no province representative of Meath or Brega was yet in existence. The kings of Tara and Dinn Riogh were said to derive from the same lineage, which ruled all the Laigin.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the 12th century <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Lebor_na_Cert" title="Lebor na Cert">Lebor na Cert</a></i> (Book of Rights), the term means province, seemingly having lost its fractional meaning with seven <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cúigeadh</i> listed.<sup id="cite_ref-Koch_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch-1">[1]</a></sup> Similarly this seems to be the case in regards to titles with the <a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Ulster" title="Annals of Ulster">Annals of Ulster</a> using the term <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rex in Chóicid</i> (king of the fifth/province) for certain overkings.<sup id="cite_ref-Koch_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch-1">[1]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">

      </div> 
      <p>The origins of the provinces of Ireland can be traced to the medieval <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cóiceda</i> (literally "fifths") or "over-kingdoms" of Ireland. There were theoretically five such over-kingdoms, however in reality during the historical period there were always more.<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IrishKings_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IrishKings-4">[4]</a></sup> At the start of the 9th century the following are listed: <a href="/wiki/Airg%C3%ADalla" title="Airgíalla">Airgíalla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connachta" title="Connachta">Connachta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laigin" title="Laigin">Laigin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="Northern Uí Néill">Northern Uí Néill</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ailech" class="mw-redirect" title="Ailech">Ailech</a>), <a href="/wiki/Southern_U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="Southern Uí Néill">Southern Uí Néill</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Meath" title="Kingdom of Meath">Mide</a>), <a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Mumu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ulaid" title="Ulaid">Ulaid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IrishKings_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IrishKings-4">[4]</a></sup> These seven over-kingdoms are again listed in the 12th-century <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Lebor na Cert</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Koch_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch-1">[1]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Each over-kingdom was divided into smaller territorial units, the definition of which, whilst not consistent in Irish law tracts, followed a pattern of different grades.<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup> In theory in the early medieval period: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>A province was ruled by a "king of over-kings", known as a <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rí ruírech</i>. This was the highest rank allowed for in Irish law tracts despite claims by some dynasties to the symbolic title of <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rí Temro</i> (king of Tara), also known as the <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">ard rí</i> (<a href="/wiki/High_King_of_Ireland" title="High King of Ireland">High King of Ireland</a>);<sup id="cite_ref-Koch_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup> The term <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rí ruírech</i> was replaced at a later date by the term <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rí cóicid</i>, "king of a fifth".<sup id="cite_ref-Koch_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch-1">[1]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Each province was made up of several petty-kingdoms that corresponded roughly to the size of modern <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_Ireland" title="Counties of Ireland">Irish counties</a> or dioceses, and were ruled by an overking known as a <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">ruirí</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Each of these petty-kingdoms was further subdivided into smaller petty-kingdoms known as a <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/T%C3%BAath" title="Túath">túath</a></i> (a group of people), equating at their largest to the size of an <a href="/wiki/Baronies_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Baronies of Ireland">Irish barony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup> These túath were ruled by a king, or <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rí</i>, and were also known as a <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rí túaithe</i>, or "king of the people".<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup> By the 10th century the rulers of a <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">túath</i> were no longer assumed to be kings but became referred to as <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">tigern</i> (a lord) or <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">toísech</i> (a leader) instead.<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p>This pyramid structure however by the later medieval period had little validity.<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf8-10_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf8-10-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Paul MacCotter proposes the following structure of lordship in the 12th century: High-king of Ireland; semi-provincial king, such as Connacht, Ulaid, <a href="/wiki/Desmumu" class="mw-redirect" title="Desmumu">Desmumu</a>; regional king, such as <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Fiatach" title="Dál Fiatach">Dál Fiatach</a> and <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_Fhiachrach_Aidni" class="mw-redirect" title="Uí Fhiachrach Aidni">Uí Fhiachrach Aidni</a>; local king or king of a <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%ADcha_c%C3%A9t" title="Trícha cét">trícha cét</a></i>, such as <a href="/wiki/Leth_Cathail" class="mw-redirect" title="Leth Cathail">Leth Cathail</a> or Cenél Guaire; and <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">taísig túaithe</i> at the bottom.<sup id="cite_ref-MacCotter46_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacCotter46-5">[5]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_medieval_period">Early medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Ireland900.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ireland900.png/220px-Ireland900.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="281" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ireland900.png/330px-Ireland900.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ireland900.png/440px-Ireland900.png 2x" data-file-width="2060" data-file-height="2630"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Ireland900.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Map of Ireland's over-kingdoms circa 900 AD.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>The kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Osraige" title="Osraige">Osraige</a>, which had its genealogy traced back by early Irish genealogists to the Laigin, was part of Mumu from the 6th to 8th century and ruled by the <a href="/wiki/Corcu_Lo%C3%ADgde" title="Corcu Loígde">Corcu Loígde</a> dynasty.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval358_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval358-6">[6]</a></sup> By the 7th century Osraige had lost their dependence on the Corcu Loígde,<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval358_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval358-6">[6]</a></sup> with the restoration of the local <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Birn" title="Dál Birn">Dál Birn</a> dynasty. Osraige remained part of Mumu until 859 when <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1el_Sechnaill_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Máel Sechnaill I">Máel Sechnaill I</a>, king of the <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_N%C3%A9ill" title="Uí Néill">Uí Néill</a>, forced Mumu to surrender it to his overlordship.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval358_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval358-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf19_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf19-7">[7]</a></sup> After this situation ended it became an independent kingdom which gradually moved towards the Laigin sphere of influence as they sought to claim the Laigin kingship.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval358_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval358-6">[6]</a></sup> It was during the 9th century that Osraige, ruled by <a href="/wiki/Cerball_mac_D%C3%BAnlainge" title="Cerball mac Dúnlainge">Cerball mac Dúnlainge</a>, became a major political player.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval74_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval74-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Airgíalla had come under the dominance of the Ulaid,<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf21_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf21-9">[9]</a></sup> however <a href="/wiki/Niall_Caille" title="Niall Caille">Niall Caille</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ed_Oirdnide" title="Áed Oirdnide">Áed Oirdnide</a>, brought it under the hegemony of the Northern Uí Néill after defeating the combined forces of the Airgíalla and Ulaid at the battle of Leth Cam in 827.<sup id="cite_ref-Bardonpg14_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bardonpg14-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval490_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval490-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf24_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf24-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval12_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval12-13">[13]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_medieval_period">Later medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>After a period of dynastic infighting in the early 12th century, Osraige fragmented and after a heavy defeat at the hands of High-King <a href="/wiki/Muirchertach_Mac_Lochlainn" title="Muirchertach Mac Lochlainn">Muirchertach Mac Lochlainn</a> became part of Laigin. In 1169, the king of Osraige, <a href="/wiki/Mac_Giolla_Ph%C3%A1draig_dynasty" title="Mac Giolla Phádraig dynasty">Domnall Mac Gilla Pátraic</a>, hired the Norman knight <a href="/wiki/Maurice_de_Prendergast" title="Maurice de Prendergast">Maurice de Prendergast</a> to resist the Laigin king, <a href="/wiki/Diarmait_Mac_Murchada" class="mw-redirect" title="Diarmait Mac Murchada">Diarmait Mac Murchada</a>, who had also recruited Norman aid.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval32_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval32-14">[14]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1118, the king of Connacht, <a href="/wiki/Tairrdelbach_Ua_Conchobair" title="Tairrdelbach Ua Conchobair">Tairrdelbach Ua Conchobair</a>, aided the <a href="/wiki/Mac_C%C3%A1rthaigh" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac Cárthaigh">Mac Cárthaigh</a> of south Munster in a rebellion against the ruling <a href="/wiki/U%C3%AD_Briain" class="mw-redirect" title="Uí Briain">Uí Briain</a> dynasty.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> This resulted in the division of Mumu into two: <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Tuadmumu</i> (<a href="/wiki/Thomond" title="Thomond">Thomond</a>, meaning "north Munster") to the north under the Uí Briain; and <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Desmumu</i> (<a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Desmond" title="Kingdom of Desmond">Desmond</a>, meaning "south Munster") to the south under the Mac Cárthaigh.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> Ua Conchobair would then conquer the heartland of the Uí Briain situated around modern <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">County Clare</a> and make it part of Connacht.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> This was to force them to accept <a href="/wiki/Cormac_Mac_Carthaig" class="mw-redirect" title="Cormac Mac Carthaig">Cormac Mac Carthaig</a>, king of Desmumu, as the king of Mumu.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> Despite Ua Conchobair's aid, Mac Carthaig and the Uí Briain would form an alliance to campaign against Connacht's hegemony, and by 1138 ended the threat from that kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> The following decades would see Mumu united and repartitioned several times as the Uí Briain and Mac Cárthaigh vied for complete control.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1168, the king of Connacht, <a href="/wiki/Ruaidr%C3%AD_Ua_Conchobair" title="Ruaidrí Ua Conchobair">Ruaidrí Ua Conchobair</a>, ensured Mumu remained divided.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="King of England">king of England</a>, landed in Ireland in 1171, the Mac Cárthaigh submitted to him to prevent an Uí Briain invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> The Uí Briain eventually followed suit in submitting to Henry II.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> The eagerness of these submissions encouraged Henry II to revive the papal grant, <a href="/wiki/Laudabiliter" title="Laudabiliter">Laudabiliter</a>, for Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval458_15-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval458-15">[15]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_Ireland">Norman Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Norman Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Osraige would be amongst the first Irish kingdoms to fall following the <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman invasion of Ireland">Norman invasion of Ireland</a> in 1170, and was soon afterwards split from Leinster and made part of the royal demesne lands of Waterford.<sup id="cite_ref-DuffyMedieval358_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuffyMedieval358-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In the years following the invasion, the kingdoms of Connacht, Desmumu, Laigin, Mide, Tuadmumu, and Ulaid formed the basis for the Norman liberties of Connacht, Desmond, Leinster, Meath, Thomond and Ulster respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MacCotter186_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacCotter186-17">[17]</a></sup> These liberties were later subdivided into smaller ones that became the basis for the counties of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Northern Uí Néill remained outside of Norman control, eventually absorbing the greater part of Airgíalla, which had by the end of the 12th century lost its eastern territory (afterwards known as "English Oriel" and later as Louth) to the Normans.<sup id="cite_ref-Clontarf24_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clontarf24-12">[12]</a></sup> Airgíalla would eventually no longer be reckoned an over-kingdom however it survived in present-day County Monaghan for as long as the Gaelic order survived,<sup id="cite_ref-Connolly12_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Connolly12-18">[18]</a></sup> with the last king of Airgíalla being Hugh Roe McMahon, who reigned from 1589 until his execution in September/October 1590. </p>
      <p>With the collapse of English control in Ireland following the <a href="/wiki/Bruce_campaign_in_Ireland" title="Bruce campaign in Ireland">Bruce campaign in Ireland</a> in 1315, and the subsequent collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Earldom_of_Ulster" title="Earldom of Ulster">Earldom of Ulster</a>, the Gaelic order had a resurgence and the Clandeboye O'Neills of the Northern Uí Néill stepped into the power vacuum in Ulster bringing it under the sovereignty of the <a href="/wiki/O%27Neill_dynasty" title="O'Neill dynasty">O'Neills</a> of Tyrone. After this they claimed for the first time the title of <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rí Ulad</i>, "king of Ulster", amalgamating their territory into one united province. This reduced the number of provinces to five—Connact, Leinster, Meath, Munster, and Ulster. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tudor_period">Tudor period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tudor period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:1716_Homann_Map_of_Ireland_-_Geographicus_-_Ireland-homann-1716.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div><a href="/wiki/Johann_Homann" title="Johann Homann">Johann Homann</a>'s 1716 map of Ireland. Note that he incorrectly places <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">County Clare</a> in Connacht; it had actually been returned to Munster in the immediate years after 1660.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Mary I</a> (1553–1558), the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Deputy_of_Ireland" title="Lord Deputy of Ireland">Lord Deputy of Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Radclyffe,_3rd_Earl_of_Sussex" title="Thomas Radclyffe, 3rd Earl of Sussex">Thomas Radclyffe, 3rd Earl of Sussex</a>, sought to divide Ireland into six parts—Connaught, Leinster, Meath, Nether Munster, Ulster, and Upper Munster.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup> His administrative reign in Ireland however was cut short and even with his reappointment by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a> (1558–1603) this plan was never implemented.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Sir <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sidney" title="Henry Sidney">Henry Sidney</a> during his three tenures as Lord Deputy created two presidencies headed by a Lord President to administer <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_Connaught" title="Lord President of Connaught">Connaught</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_Munster" title="Lord President of Munster">Munster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup> In an attempt to reduce the importance of the province of Munster, Sydney, using the <a href="/wiki/River_Shannon" title="River Shannon">River Shannon</a> as a natural boundary took Thomond and made it into the <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">county of Clare</a> as part of the presidency of Connaught in 1569.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup> Around 1600 near the end of Elizabeth's reign, Clare was made an entirely distinct presidency of its own under the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Thomond" title="Earl of Thomond">Earls of Thomond</a> and would not return to being part of Munster until after the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="Restoration (England)">Restoration</a> in 1660.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The exact boundaries of the provinces of Ireland during the Tudor period changed several times, usually as a result of the creation of new counties: </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>County Clare upon its creation in 1569 was transferred from Munster to Connacht, and was only restored to Munster after 1660.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/County_Longford" title="County Longford">County Longford</a> upon its creation in 1583 was transferred from Leinster to Connacht.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crawford_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-19">[19]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/County_Cavan" title="County Cavan">County Cavan</a> was created in 1584 and transferred from Connacht to Ulster.<sup id="cite_ref-roche_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roche-20">[20]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/County_Louth" title="County Louth">County Louth</a>, which had long being part of <a href="/wiki/The_Pale" title="The Pale">The Pale</a>, was transferred from Ulster to Leinster.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <p>It would not be until the reign of Elizabeth's successor, <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James I</a>, that Meath by 1610 would cease to be considered a province and that the provincial borders would be permanently set.<sup id="cite_ref-Falkiner_16-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falkiner-16">[16]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The earliest recorded mention of the major division of Ireland is in the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Cycle" title="Ulster Cycle">Ulster Cycle</a> of legends, such as the <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1in_B%C3%B3_C%C3%BAailnge" title="Táin Bó Cúailnge">Táin Bó Cúailnge</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hogan1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan1-22">[22]</a></sup> The <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Táin</i> is set during the reign of <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Conchobar Mac Nessa</i>, king of <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>, and is believed to have happened in the 1st century.<sup id="cite_ref-Hurbert169-171_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurbert169-171-23">[23]</a></sup> In this period Ireland is said to have been divided into five independent over-kingdoms, or <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cuigeadh</i> whose <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">rí</i> (kings) were of equal rank, not subject to a central monarchy.<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Koch_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koch-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hurbert169-171_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurbert169-171-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ossianic75_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ossianic75-24">[24]</a></sup> Pseudo-historians called this era <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Aimser na Coicedach</i>, which has been translated as: "Time of the Pentarchs";<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup> "Time of the Five Fifths";<sup id="cite_ref-Hurbert169-171_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurbert169-171-23">[23]</a></sup> and "Time of the provincial kings".<sup id="cite_ref-Ossianic75_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ossianic75-24">[24]</a></sup> It was also described as "the Pentarchy".<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hogan1_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan1-22">[22]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The five provinces that made up the Pentarchy where:<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hogan1_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan1-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hurbert169-171_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurbert169-171-23">[23]</a></sup> </p> 
      <ul>
       <li><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Connacht</i>, with its royal seat at <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Rathcroghan" title="Rathcroghan">Cruachan</a></i>.</li> 
       <li><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Ulaid</i> (Ulster), with its royal seat at <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Navan_Fort" title="Navan Fort">Emain Macha</a></i>.</li> 
       <li><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Muman</i> (Munster), with its royal seat at <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Teamhair Erann</i>.</li> 
       <li><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Laigen Tuathgabair</i> (North Leinster), with its royal seat at <i><a href="/wiki/Hill_of_Tara" title="Hill of Tara">Tara</a></i> (before it became the seat of the High King).</li> 
       <li><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Laigen Desgabair</i> (South Leinster), with its royal seat at <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Dinn Riogh</i>.</li>
      </ul> 
      <p>Historians <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Keating" title="Geoffrey Keating">Geoffrey Keating</a> and <a href="/wiki/T._F._O%27Rahilly" title="T. F. O'Rahilly">T. F. O'Rahilly</a> differ suggesting that it is Munster, not Leinster, that formed two of the fifths.<sup id="cite_ref-Ossianic75_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ossianic75-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JohnMacNeill102_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JohnMacNeill102-25">[25]</a></sup> These two fifths were called by Keating: <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Cuigeadh Eochaidh</i> (eastern Munster) and <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Cuigeadh Con Raoi</i> (western Munster),<sup id="cite_ref-JohnMacNeill102_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JohnMacNeill102-25">[25]</a></sup> both named after their respective king. <a href="/wiki/Eoin_MacNeill" title="Eoin MacNeill">Eoin MacNeill</a> discounts this suggestion citing the <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Táin Bó Cúailnge</i>, which makes mention of <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Eochaidh</i> as king of all Munster, with <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Cu Roi</i> simply a "great Munster hero".<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup> He also cites that the <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Táin</i> makes mention of the four fifths of Ireland that waged war on Ulster, which made reference to only one Munster.<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup> Another reason given by MacNeill was a problem made by Keating himself. According to Keating, when the province of <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Míde</i> was being founded, it was created from portions of each province which all met at the hill of Uisnech. The boundaries given by Keating himself for the five provinces however meant that this would have been highly unlikely, with the boundary between his Munster fifths nowhere near this area.<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Pseudo-historians list 84 kings of Ireland prior to the formation of the Pentarchy. When this mythical kingship was interrupted is a matter of dispute. The Annals of Tigernach state that Ireland was divided into the five upon the slaying of <a href="/wiki/Conaire_M%C3%B3r" title="Conaire Mór">Conaire Mór</a>, however it is suggested alternatively that it happened upon the death of Conaire's father, <a href="/wiki/Etersc%C3%A9l_M%C3%B3r" title="Eterscél Mór">Eterscél Mór</a>, the 84th king of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Ossianic75_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ossianic75-24">[24]</a></sup> Keating however suggests it occurred in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Eochu_Feidlech" title="Eochu Feidlech">Eochu Feidlech</a> who was the 82nd king of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-Ossianic75_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ossianic75-24">[24]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>MacNeill claims that this division of Ireland into five is pre-historic and pre-Gaelic, describing the Pentarchy as "the oldest certain fact in the political history of Ireland".<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup> The notion of Ireland being divided into five permeated itself throughout Irish literature over the centuries despite what the <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">cuigeadh</i> representing no longer existing by the time of Saint Patrick in the 5th century.<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup> By then, Ireland had become divided into seven over-kingdoms.<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeill_21-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeill-21">[21]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="The_Three_Collas_and_the_founding_of_Airg.C3.ADalla"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Three_Collas_and_the_founding_of_Airgíalla">The Three Collas and the founding of Airgíalla</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Three Collas and the founding of Airgíalla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The main body of the events in the myth of <a href="/wiki/The_Three_Collas" title="The Three Collas">the Three Collas</a> may have occurred in the late 4th to early 5th century, however as the centuries passed the myth underwent updating and alteration.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> The most oft quoted version of their story was written by Geoffrey Keating in the 17th century in his work the <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Foras_Feasa_ar_%C3%89irinn" title="Foras Feasa ar Éirinn">Foras Feasa ar Éirinn</a></i>, also known as "The History of Ireland".<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In it the Three Collas—Colla Menn, Colla Da Crioch, and Colla Uais—were the sons of Eocaidh Doimlén.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> It is from them that the Airgíalla are said to descend, branching off from the rest of the Connachta.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> The Northern and Southern Uí Néill dynasties are claimed to descend from Eocaidh's brother, <a href="/wiki/Fiacha_Sraibhtine" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiacha Sraibhtine">Fiacha Sraibhtine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> According to the story the Collas were told by Fiacha's son, <a href="/wiki/Muiredach_Tirech" title="Muiredach Tirech">Muiredach Tirech</a>, the High King of Ireland, to conquer land of their own to pass on to their descendants, directing them to wage war on the Ulaid to avenge a slight against their great-grandfather <a href="/wiki/Cormac_mac_Airt" title="Cormac mac Airt">Cormac mac Airt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Collas with their army along with a host from Connacht marched to <a href="/wiki/Achaidh_Leithdeircc" title="Achaidh Leithdeircc">Achaidh Leithdeircc</a> in Fernmagh, southern Ulaid, and fought the Ulaid in seven battles over the course of seven days.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> The host from Connacht fought the first six battles, and the Collas fought the seventh.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> It is after this last battle that the king of Ulaid, <a href="/wiki/Fergus_Foga" class="mw-redirect" title="Fergus Foga">Fergus Foga</a>, was killed and his army routed.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> The Collas then pursued the Ulaid east of the "Glen Righe" (the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Newry_River" title="Newry River">Newry River</a> in eastern County Armagh), before returning to loot and burn the Ulaid capital, Emain Macha, after which it never again had a king.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> They then took possession of central Ulaid spanning the modern counties of Armagh, Fermanagh, Londonderry, Monaghan and Tyrone founding the over-kingdom of Airgíalla.<sup id="cite_ref-Schlegel160-4_26-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schlegel160-4-26">[26]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>In modern times the provinces have become associated with groups of counties, although they have no <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Law of the Republic of Ireland">legal status</a>. They are today seen mainly in a sporting context, as Ireland's four professional rugby teams in <a href="/wiki/Pro14" title="Pro14">Pro14</a> play under the names of the provinces, and the <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Athletic_Association" title="Gaelic Athletic Association">Gaelic Athletic Association</a> has separate <a href="/wiki/GAA_provincial_council" title="GAA provincial council">GAA provincial councils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Provincial_championships" title="Provincial championships">Provincial championships</a>. </p>
      <p>Six of the nine Ulster counties form modern-day <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, which is part of the United Kingdom. Northern Ireland is a province of the United Kingdom, and is sometimes referred to by this term. </p>
      <p>In modern times politically the four provinces were referred to in the proposal of <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/%C3%89ire_Nua" title="Éire Nua">Éire Nua</a></i> ("New Ireland"), which was supported by the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_IRA" class="mw-redirect" title="Provisional IRA">Provisional IRA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> during the 1970s and early 1980s for a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federal</a> <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">United Ireland</a>. The proposal was particularly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>-based leadership group centred on <a href="/wiki/Ruair%C3%AD_%C3%93_Br%C3%A1daigh" title="Ruairí Ó Brádaigh">Ruairí Ó Brádaigh</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1ith%C3%AD_%C3%93_Conaill" title="Dáithí Ó Conaill">Dáithí Ó Conaill</a>, who were the authors of the policy. <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Éire Nua</i> envisaged a united Ireland that would be created when the British withdrew from <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federal</a> state with assemblies for each of its four historic provinces. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetic_description">Poetic description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Poetic description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ard_Ruide">Ard Ruide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ard Ruide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Cuinbattle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Cuinbattle.jpg/170px-Cuinbattle.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Cuinbattle.jpg/255px-Cuinbattle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Cuinbattle.jpg/340px-Cuinbattle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="994" data-file-height="1432"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Cuinbattle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>"<a href="/wiki/C%C3%BA_Chulainn" title="Cú Chulainn">Cuchulain in Battle</a>", illustration by <a href="/wiki/J._C._Leyendecker" title="J. C. Leyendecker">J. C. Leyendecker</a> in T. W. Rolleston's <i>Myths &amp; Legends of the Celtic Race</i>, 1911
        </div>
       </div>
      </div> 
      <p>The <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Dindsenchas" title="Dindsenchas">dinnseanchas</a></i> poem named <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">Ard Ruide</i> (Ruide Headland) poetically describes the five kingdoms of Ireland. Below is a translation from <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a>: </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>Connacht in the west is the kingdom of learning, the seat of the greatest and wisest druids and magicians; the men of Connacht are famed for their eloquence, their handsomeness and their ability to pronounce true judgement. </p>
       <p>Ulster in the north is the seat of battle valour, of haughtiness, strife, boasting; the men of Ulster are the fiercest warriors of all Ireland, and the queens and goddesses of Ulster are associated with battle and death. </p>
       <p>Leinster, the eastern kingdom, is the seat of prosperity, hospitality, the importing of rich foreign wares like silk or wine; the men of Leinster are noble in speech and their women are exceptionally beautiful. </p>
       <p>Munster in the south is the kingdom of music and the arts, of harpers, of skilled <a href="/wiki/Fidchell" title="Fidchell">ficheall</a> players and of skilled horsemen. The fairs of Munster were the greatest in all Ireland. </p>
       <p> The last kingdom, Meath, is the kingdom of Kingship, of stewardship, of bounty in government; in Meath lies the <a href="/wiki/Hill_of_Tara" title="Hill of Tara">Hill of Tara</a>, the traditional seat of the <a href="/wiki/High_King_of_Ireland" title="High King of Ireland">High King of Ireland</a>. The ancient earthwork of Tara is called Rath na Ríthe ('Ringfort of the Kings').</p>
      </blockquote> 
      <h3><span id="The_T.C3.A1in"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Táin">The Táin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: The Táin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poem</a>, <i>An <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1in_B%C3%B3_C%C3%BAailnge" title="Táin Bó Cúailnge">Táin Bó Cúailnge</a></i> (the Cattle Raid of Cooley) describes a war between Connacht and Ulster, and is perhaps best known for <a href="/wiki/C%C3%BA_Chulainn" title="Cú Chulainn">Cú<span class="nowrap">&nbsp;</span>Chulainn</a>'s single handed defence of Ulster against the champions of Connacht in turn, while his comrades were disabled by a spell. </p>
