      <p>The <b>Gabonese Republic, </b> or <b>Gabon</b>, is a country in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. It is located on the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. It has borders with <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a>. Its capital is <a href="/wiki/Libreville" title="Libreville">Libreville</a>, which is also the largest city in the country. Gabon has an area of almost 270,000 square kilometres (100,000&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi). 1.5 million people live in Gabon. </p>
      <p>Gabon was a <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colony</a> of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. It became a new country on August 17, 1960. Since 1960, the country has had only 3 presidents. At first it had one-party rule. In the early 1990s, the country switched to a multi-party government. </p>
      <p>Gabon is one of the richest countries in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>. It has 4th highest HDI and third highest GDP per capita (PPP) in the region. Gabon has high levels of income inequality, and there are significant amounts of poverty in the country. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The first people who lived in the country were <a href="/wiki/Pygmies" class="mw-redirect" title="Pygmies">pygmies</a>. Later, <a href="/wiki/Bantu_people" title="Bantu people">Bantu people</a> took over the area. Bad weather did not let people form a local culture, as had happened in the south (Congo) or north (<a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a>). </p>
      <p>In the 15th century, the first Europeans came to the area. Starting from the 16th century, the coast was used for <a href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a>. </p>
      <p>In the 19th century, French explorer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre_Savorgnan_de_Brazza&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pierre Savorgnan de Brazza (not yet started)">Pierre Savorgnan de Brazza</a> organized the first journey to the Gabon-Congo area in 1875. After 10 years, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> took over the whole country. At that time a small amount of Bantu people lived in the country. </p>
      <p>In 1910 Gabon was one of the French colonies which formed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=French_Equatorial_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="French Equatorial Africa (not yet started)">French Equatorial Africa</a> (together with Congo, <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>). French Equatorial Africa lasted to 1959. On 17 August 1960, Gabon became a new country. </p>
      <p>Since 1960, Gabon has only had 3 presidents. In 1961 <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_M%27ba" title="Léon M'ba">Léon M'ba</a> became the first president. In 1967 after his death <a href="/wiki/Omar_Bongo" title="Omar Bongo">Omar Bongo</a> became the president and ruled the country to 2009. In 2009, Omar Bongo died and his son <a href="/wiki/Ali_Bongo_Ondimba" title="Ali Bongo Ondimba">Ali Bongo Ondimba</a> took over as president. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Geography">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Geography">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tleft">

      <p>Gabon is on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast of central Africa. It is on the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. Gabon generally has an <a href="/w/index.php?title=Equatorial_climate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Equatorial climate (not yet started)">equatorial climate</a>. Rainforests cover 85% of the country. There are three distinct regions: the coastal plains (ranging between 20 and 300&nbsp;km from the ocean's shore), the mountains (the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cristal_Mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cristal Mountains (not yet started)">Cristal Mountains</a> to the northeast of Libreville, the Chaillu Massif in the centre, culminating at 1575 m with Mont Iboundji), and the <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> in the east. The coastal plains form a large section of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a>'s <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_Equatorial_coastal_forests&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Atlantic Equatorial coastal forests (not yet started)">Atlantic Equatorial coastal forests</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a> and contain patches of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_African_mangroves&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Central African mangroves (not yet started)">Central African mangroves</a> especially on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Muni_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Muni River (not yet started)">Muni River</a> estuary on the border with <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>. </p>
      <p>Gabon's largest river is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ogoou%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ogooué (not yet started)">Ogooué</a> which is 1200&nbsp;km long. Gabon has three <a href="/w/index.php?title=Karst_topography&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Karst topography (not yet started)">karst</a> areas where there are hundreds of caves in the <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> rocks. Some of the caves include Grotte du Lastoursville, Grotte du Lebamba, Grotte du Bongolo, and Grotte du Kessipougou. Many caves have not been explored yet. A National Geographic Expedition visited the caves in the summer of 2008 to document them (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://blogs.nationalgeographic.com/blogs/gabon-caves">Expedition Website</a>). </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Politics">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Change section: Politics">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The first Gabonese president was Leon Mba. His successor was Omar Bongo, from 1967 until his death in 2009. Under his governance Gabon had just one political party between 1968 and 1990. It was called PDG. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecomonics">Ecomonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Ecomonics">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Change section: Ecomonics">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Gabon has nine states. The soil of Gabon is rich in the metals uranium, manganese, and petrolium. Therefore, these three elements, such as metal exploited in Port-Gentil, Iranium in Munana, and the manganese in Franceville. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Culture">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Change section: Culture">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Gabon has a wide culture. Before colonialism, Gabon's people believed their ancestral spirit as religion, like bwiti, mvett, djobi. </p>
      <p>After colonialism, others religions such as <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> came to be added to the first <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animist</a> believers. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Gabon_national_football_team" title="Gabon national football team">Gabon national football team</a> has represented the nation since 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Gabon were joint hosts, along with <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>, of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=2012_Africa_Cup_of_Nations&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="2012 Africa Cup of Nations (not yet started)">2012 Africa Cup of Nations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> They were the only hosts of the competition's <a href="/wiki/2017_Africa_Cup_of_Nations" title="2017 Africa Cup of Nations">2017 tournament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Gabon has excellent <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Recreational fishing">recreational fishing</a>. It is considered one of the best places in the world to catch <a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_tarpon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Atlantic tarpon (not yet started)">Atlantic tarpon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
