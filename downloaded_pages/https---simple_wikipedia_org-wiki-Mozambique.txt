      <p><b>Mozambique</b>, officially the <b>Republic of Mozambique</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Its capital is <a href="/wiki/Maputo" title="Maputo">Maputo</a>. There are about 31 million people. In 1975, Mozambique became independent from <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. (National Day on 25th June, 1975) The country was named Moçambique by the Portuguese after the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Island_of_Mozambique&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Island of Mozambique (not yet started)">Island of Mozambique</a> which was the capital of Portuguese Africa. After independence from Portugal it became the <a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Republic_of_Mozambique&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="People's Republic of Mozambique (not yet started)">People's Republic of Mozambique</a>. There was a big <a href="/wiki/Mozambican_Civil_War" title="Mozambican Civil War">civil war</a> from 1977 to 1992. </p>
      <p>Mozambique has many natural resources. <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> are among the country's most important partners. It is the second poorest country in the world. They have the world's lowest <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>. In 2012, large natural gas reserves were found in Mozambique. The revenues from the reserves might dramatically change the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The only official language of Mozambique is <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>. About half the people speak it as a second language and very few as a first language. Languages widely spoken natively include <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Makhuwa_language&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Makhuwa language (not yet started)">Makhuwa</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sena_language&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sena language (not yet started)">Sena</a>. The largest religion in Mozambique is <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. There are also <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> and African traditional religious minorities. </p>
      <p>Mozambique is a member of the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_of_Portuguese_Language_Countries&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Community of Portuguese Language Countries (not yet started)">Community of Portuguese Language Countries</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Latin_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Latin Union (not yet started)">Latin Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organisation_of_Islamic_Cooperation" title="Organisation of Islamic Cooperation">Organisation of Islamic Cooperation</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_African_Development_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Southern African Development Community (not yet started)">Southern African Development Community</a>. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Geography and climate">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: Geography and climate">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>At 309,475&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi (801,537&nbsp;km<sup>2</sup>), Mozambique is the world's 35th-largest country. It is bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a> to the northwest, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> to the west and <a href="/wiki/Swaziland" class="mw-redirect" title="Swaziland">Swaziland</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> to the southwest. </p>
      <p>The country is divided into two regions by the <a href="/wiki/Zambezi_River" title="Zambezi River">Zambezi River</a>. To the north of the Zambezi River, the narrow coastline moves inland to hills and low <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateaus</a>. Further west are rugged highlands, which include the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Niassa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Niassa (not yet started)">Niassa</a> highlands, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Namuli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Namuli (not yet started)">Namuli</a> or Shire highlands, Angonia highlands, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tete&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tete (not yet started)">Tete</a> highlands and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Makonde_(District)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Makonde (District) (not yet started)">Makonde</a> plateau, covered with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Miombo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Miombo (not yet started)">miombo</a> woodlands. To the south of the Zambezi River, the lowlands are broader with the Mashonaland plateau and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lebombo_mountains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lebombo mountains (not yet started)">Lebombo mountains</a> in the deep south. </p>

      <p>The country has five main rivers and several smaller ones. The largest and most important is the Zambezi. The country has four notable lakes: <a href="/wiki/Lake_Niassa" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Niassa">Lake Niassa</a> (or Malawi), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Chiuta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lake Chiuta (not yet started)">Lake Chiuta</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Cahora_Bassa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lake Cahora Bassa (not yet started)">Lake Cahora Bassa</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Shirwa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lake Shirwa (not yet started)">Lake Shirwa</a>, all in the north. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Climate">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Climate">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Mozambique has a tropical climate with two seasons. The wet season is from October to March and the dry season from April to September. Conditions vary depending on <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>. Rainfall is heavy along the coast and is less in the north and south. Yearly precipitation changes from 500 to 900&nbsp;mm (19.7 to 35.4&nbsp;in) depending on the region. <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">Cyclones</a> are common during the wet season. Average temperature ranges in Maputo are from 13 to 24&nbsp;°C (55.4 to 75.2&nbsp;°F) in July to 22 to 31&nbsp;°C (71.6 to 87.8&nbsp;°F) in February.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Mozambique is divided into ten <a href="/wiki/Province" title="Province">provinces</a> (<i>provincias</i>) and one capital city (<i>cidade capital</i>) with provincial status. The provinces are divided into 129 <a href="/wiki/District" title="District">districts</a> (<i>distritos</i>). The districts are further divided in 405 "Postos Administrativos" (Administrative Posts) and then into Localidades (Localities), the lowest geographical level of the central state administration. Since 1998, 43 "Municípios" (Municipalities) have been created in Mozambique. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Culture">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Change section: Culture">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts">Arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Arts">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Change section: Arts">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The music of Mozambique can be for many purposes. These can be religious or for traditional ceremonies. Musical instruments are usually handmade. Some of the instruments used include <a href="/wiki/Drums" class="mw-redirect" title="Drums">drums</a> made of wood and animal skin; the <i>lupembe</i>, a woodwind instrument made from animal horns or wood; and the <a href="/wiki/Marimba" class="mw-redirect" title="Marimba">marimba</a>, which is a kind of <a href="/wiki/Xylophone" title="Xylophone">xylophone</a> native to Mozambique. The marimba is a popular instrument with the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chopi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chopi (not yet started)">Chopi</a> of the south central coast. They are famous for their musical skill and dance. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Makonde_people&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Makonde people (not yet started)">Makonde</a> are renowned for their <a href="/wiki/Wood_carving" title="Wood carving">wood carving</a> and elaborate masks that are commonly used in traditional dances. There are two different kinds of wood carvings. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shetani (not yet started)">Shetani</a>, (evil spirits), which are mostly carved in heavy <a href="/wiki/Ebony" class="mw-disambig" title="Ebony">ebony</a>, tall, and elegantly curved with symbols and nonrepresentational faces. The <a href="/wiki/Ujamaa" title="Ujamaa">Ujamaa</a> are <a href="/wiki/Totemism" title="Totemism">totem-type</a> carvings which show lifelike faces of people and various figures. Theses sculptures are usually called “family trees” because they tell stories of many generations. </p>
      <p>During the last years of the colonial period, Mozambican art showed the <a href="/wiki/Oppression" title="Oppression">oppression</a> by the colonial power, and became a symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Resistance" class="mw-disambig" title="Resistance">resistance</a>. After independence in 1975, the modern art came into a new phase. The two best known and most influential contemporary Mozambican artists are the painter <a href="/w/index.php?title=Malangatana_Ngwenya&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Malangatana Ngwenya (not yet started)">Malangatana Ngwenya</a> and the sculptor <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alberto_Chissano&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alberto Chissano (not yet started)">Alberto Chissano</a>. Art during the 1980s and 1990s shows the political struggle, civil war, suffering, starvation and struggle. </p>
      <p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dances&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dances (not yet started)">Dances</a> are usually highly developed traditions throughout Mozambique. There are many different kinds of dances from tribe to tribe which are usually <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritualistic</a> in nature. The Chopi, for instance, act out battles dressed in animal skins. The men of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Makua_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Makua (people) (not yet started)">Makua</a> dress in colourful outfits and masks while dancing on stilts around the village for hours. Groups of women in the northern part of the country have a traditional dance called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tufo_(dance)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tufo (dance) (not yet started)"><i>tufo</i></a>, to celebrate <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> holidays.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Food">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozambique&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Change section: Food">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Because the Portuguese were there for nearly 500 years, they greatly impacted the cuisine of Mozambique. Crops such as <a href="/wiki/Cassava" class="mw-redirect" title="Cassava">cassava</a> (a starchy root) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cashew_nuts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cashew nuts (not yet started)">cashew nuts</a> (Mozambique was once the largest producer of these nuts), and <i>pãozinho</i> were brought in by the Portuguese. The use of <a href="/wiki/Spices" class="mw-redirect" title="Spices">spices</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seasonings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Seasonings (not yet started)">seasonings</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Onions" class="mw-redirect" title="Onions">onions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bay_leaves" class="mw-redirect" title="Bay leaves">bay leaves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>, fresh <a href="/wiki/Coriander" title="Coriander">coriander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paprika" title="Paprika">paprika</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chili_peppers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chili peppers (not yet started)">chili peppers</a>, red sweet peppers, and <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> were introduced by the Portuguese. So was <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a> (a type of grass), and <a href="/wiki/Potatoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Potatoes">potatoes</a>. <i>Prego</i> (steak roll), <i>rissois</i> (battered shrimp), <i>espetada</i> (<a href="/wiki/Kebab" title="Kebab">kebab</a>), <i>pudim</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pudding" title="Pudding">pudding</a>), and the popular <i>inteiro com piripiri</i> (whole chicken in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Piri-piri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Piri-piri (not yet started)">piri-piri</a> sauce) are all Portuguese dishes commonly eaten in present-day Mozambique. </p>
