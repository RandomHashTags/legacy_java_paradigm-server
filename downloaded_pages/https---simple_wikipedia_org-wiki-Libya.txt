      <p><b>Libya</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl"><span style="font-size: 120%;">‏ليبيا</span></span>‎ <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="DIN 31635 Arabic (Arabic language) transliteration">Lībyā</i></i>, <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a>: ⵍⵉⴱⵢⴰ <i>Libya</i>), officially the <b>State of Libya</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-Herald_StateofLibya_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herald_StateofLibya-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-state-libya_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-state-libya-6">[6]</a></sup> is a country in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. It is bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> to the southeast, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a> to the south, and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> to the west. It covers an area of almost 1.8&nbsp;million square kilometres (700,000&nbsp;sq&nbsp;mi). It is the 17th <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_outlying_territories_by_total_area" class="mw-redirect" title="List of countries and outlying territories by total area">largest country in the world</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Geography">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: Geography">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Libya's <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">borders</a> touch the countries of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>. To its north is the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Capital_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital (city)">capital</a> of Libya is <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a> on the sea. Tripoli has about one million people.<sup id="cite_ref-cia_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia-8">[8]</a></sup> Libya covers an area of about 1,760,000&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Square_kilometre" title="Square kilometre">km<sup>2</sup></a> (679,540&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Square_mile" title="Square mile">sq&nbsp;mi</a>). </p>
      <p><sup id="cite_ref-collins_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The highest point in Libya is Bikku Bitti 2,267 m above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> and the lowest point is Sabkhat Ghuzayyil -47 m at below sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-cia_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia-8">[8]</a></sup> Most of the country is flat, with large plains. Because it is so dry, only 1.03% of the land is suitable for farming.<sup id="cite_ref-cia_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The area around Tripoli is called <i>Tripolitania</i>, and it was the most developed during the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> occupation. </p>
      <p>Cyrenaica is an area of the north east coast.<sup id="cite_ref-mw_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mw-10">[10]</a></sup> It is divided from Tripolitania by the Gulf of Sirte.<sup id="cite_ref-charkow_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charkow-11">[11]</a></sup> It was named by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a> who built the city of Cirene in <a href="/w/index.php?title=630_BC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="630 BC (not yet started)">630 BC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mw_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mw-10">[10]</a></sup> It includes the cities of Tobruk and <a href="/wiki/Benghazi" title="Benghazi">Benghazi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mw_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mw-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Fezzan is an area of desert in south west Libya which the Italians made a part of Tripoli in 1912.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> After the war this area was governed by France, who wanted to annex to their Empire. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_people">The people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: The people">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: The people">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of Libya in 2011 was said to be about 6,597,960.<sup id="cite_ref-cia_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia-8">[8]</a></sup> This is not a large number for a country that has such a large <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">area</a>, so the <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> of Libya is low. This is because much of Libya is in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>. Most people in Libya live in cities on the <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coast</a>. People from Libya are called <b>Libyans</b>. </p>
      <p>Libyans are mostly <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arabs</a>, though many are <a href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berbers</a>, a group which includes the <a href="/wiki/Nomadic_people" title="Nomadic people">nomadic</a> <a href="/wiki/Tuareg_people" title="Tuareg people">Tuareg</a> of North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> About 95% of Libyans are of Arab-Berber origin.<sup id="cite_ref-ce_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ce-14">[14]</a></sup> Nearly all Libyans are <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Muslims</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Economy">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Change section: Economy">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">Oil</a> was discovered in Libya in 1958 and is about 95% of the country's export income.<sup id="cite_ref-ce_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ce-14">[14]</a></sup> Oil is about 25% of Libya's <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ce_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ce-14">[14]</a></sup> Other exports include <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>. Because so much of the country is <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a>, Libya has to import about 75% of its food.<sup id="cite_ref-ce_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ce-14">[14]</a></sup> It does grow <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olives" class="mw-redirect" title="Olives">olives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Date_palm" title="Date palm">dates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a> and many vegetables. In 1984, a 3,862&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a> (2,400&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>) pipeline was started to bring underground water from the Sahara to coastal areas for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>. The pipeline which will take 25 years to complete has been estimated to cost about $25 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-ce_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ce-14">[14]</a></sup> It is called the Great Man-made River, and is the largest water development scheme in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-cia_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The money of Libya is called the <b>Libyan dinar</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-collins_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collins-9">[9]</a></sup> It was made to take the place of the old money, the Libyan pound, in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There are 1000 dirhams in a dinar. Dinar is the name of the <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> countries.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The name comes from an old Roman coin, the <a href="/wiki/Denarius" title="Denarius">denarius</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_History">Politics and History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Politics and History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Change section: Politics and History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Temple_of_Zeus_Cyrene.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Temple_of_Zeus_Cyrene.jpg/220px-Temple_of_Zeus_Cyrene.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Temple_of_Zeus_Cyrene.jpg/330px-Temple_of_Zeus_Cyrene.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Temple_of_Zeus_Cyrene.jpg/440px-Temple_of_Zeus_Cyrene.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200"></a>

      <p>Libya is made up of three regions, Tripolitania, Cyrenaica, and the Fezzan. Tripolitania is the area on the north west coast, once called the Kingdom of Tripoli. It was ruled by Turks from the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. The USA went to war with the Kingdom of Tripoli in 1805 over the problems of piracy in the Mediterranean. The USA had refused to pay increased "protection" money to the Turkish rulers. </p>
      <p>Tripolitania and Cyrenaica were captured by the Italians during the Italian-Turkish War of 1911-1912.<sup id="cite_ref-mw_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mw-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hue_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hue-18">[18]</a></sup> The reason for the war was to set up a colony for Italy. They said that this area was once part of the Roman Empire and belonged to Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-hc_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hc-19">[19]</a></sup> The Italians were the first country to use <a href="/wiki/Aeroplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Aeroplane">aeroplanes</a> to drop bombs when they attacked Tripoli in 1911.<sup id="cite_ref-hc_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hc-19">[19]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Many thousands of Italian settlers moved to Libya to set up businesses and farms, which were going to supply food and produce for Italy and its Empire<sup id="cite_ref-hc_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hc-19">[19]</a></sup> Libyans had some of their land in Cyrenaica taken from them by force and about 70,000 people died during the battles, starved, or were decimated by the terrible epidemic (called "<a href="/wiki/Spanish_flu" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish flu">Spanish flu</a>") of 1918. Many thousands escaped to Egypt, but soon moved back when the new Italian governor Italo Balbo started a friendly attitude toward Arabs. </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Italian_Libya" title="Italian Libya">Italian Libya</a> enjoyed in the late 1930s a huge development, with the creation of new railways, ports, hospitals, airports, roads.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In those years the agricultural economy boomed, thanks to the creation of many dozens of new villages for Italian &amp; Arab farmers. There was even an international race-car competition outside Tripoli (<i>Grand Prix of Tripoli</i> <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>). </p>
      <p>Much of the North African Campaign of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> was fought in Libya, including the Battle of Tobruk. The British captured Tripolitania in 1942 and ruled it until 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-hue_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hue-18">[18]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_of_Libya">United Kingdom of Libya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: United Kingdom of Libya">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Change section: United Kingdom of Libya">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>After World War II, the regions of Libya were ruled by military governors from both Britain and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> made Libya an independent country, the United Kingdom of Libya, in 1951. This was to be a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a>, ruled by King Idris I and his successors. Idris (Muhammad Idris bin Muhammad al-Mahdi as-Senussi) (13 March 1890—25 May 1983)<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> had been the <a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">Emir</a> of Cyrenaica, but went into <a href="/wiki/Exile" title="Exile">exile</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in 1922. At the end of the war, he returned as emir, with support of Britain. He was also asked to be Emir of Tripolitania. He was able to unite the three regions and became the king of the United Kingdom of Libya on 24 December 1951. </p>
      <p>The problems facing Libya were huge. The country was poor, with little in the way of goods to export. Only 250,000 people could read.<sup id="cite_ref-feb_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feb-23">[23]</a></sup> There were only 16 Libyan college graduates, no Libyan doctors, <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharmacist" title="Pharmacist">pharmacists</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Surveyor" class="mw-redirect" title="Surveyor">surveyors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-feb_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feb-23">[23]</a></sup> The United Nations estimated that 10% of the people were blind from eye diseases, especially <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trachoma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Trachoma (not yet started)">trachoma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-feb_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feb-23">[23]</a></sup> Idris was a religious leader did not take much interest in the affairs of government.<sup id="cite_ref-charkow_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charkow-11">[11]</a></sup> His government was seen to be corrupt, and did nothing about an increasing rise in Arab nationalism which had brought <a href="/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamal Abdel Nasser">Nasser</a> to power in Egypt in 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-charkow_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charkow-11">[11]</a></sup> Once oil was discovered, Libya became one of the largest oil producing countries in the world. Many Libyans felt that Cyrenaica was getting more of the oil money than the rest of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-charkow_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charkow-11">[11]</a></sup> A lot of the money from oil was also going to overseas companies.<sup id="cite_ref-hc_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hc-19">[19]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libyan_Arab_Republic">Libyan Arab Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Libyan Arab Republic">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Change section: Libyan Arab Republic">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In the early morning of 1 September 1969, a group of military officers took over the government in a <a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d'état">coup d'état</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-otd_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-otd-24">[24]</a></sup> Colonel <a href="/wiki/Muammar_al-Gaddafi" title="Muammar al-Gaddafi">Muammar al-Gaddafi</a> was named as chief of staff of the military.<sup id="cite_ref-otd_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-otd-24">[24]</a></sup> From 1970-1972 he served as Prime Minister.<sup id="cite_ref-otd_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-otd-24">[24]</a></sup> He began a <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a> system named "The Third Universal Theory". This is a mix of <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribal</a> government. It was to be put in place by the Libyan people themselves in a unique form of "direct democracy." Al-Gaddafi called this "jamahiriya". </p>
      <p>Some of his first actions were to take back control of the oil and send the remaining Italian settlers back to Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-hc_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hc-19">[19]</a></sup> He also closed down the American <a href="/wiki/USAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAF">USAF</a> base.<sup id="cite_ref-hc_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hc-19">[19]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span id="Great_Socialist_People.27s_Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Socialist_People's_Libyan_Arab_Jamahiriya">Great Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Great Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Change section: Great Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In 1977 Libya became "Al-Jamahiriya al-`Arabiyah al-Libiyah ash-Sha`biyah al-Ishtirakiyah al-Uzma" . In <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, the name means the "Great <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">Socialist</a> People's Libyan <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> Jamahiriya". </p>
      <p>Libya was alleged to have sponsored anti-western groups. Gaddafi openly supported independence movements like <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Mandela" title="Nelson Mandela">Nelson Mandela</a>'s African National Congress, <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_Liberation_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Palestinian Liberation Organization">Palestinian Liberation Organization</a>, the Irish Republican Army, the <a href="/wiki/Polisario_Front" title="Polisario Front">Polisario Front</a> (Western Sahara) and more. Because of this Libya's foreign relations with several western nation were negatively effected, and it would become a reason for US to bomb Libya in 1986. Gaddafi survived the bombing, the action of US was condemned by many countries and UN general assembly. </p>
      <p>Libya had also supplied weapons and money to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a> during its fight with the British government in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-brewer_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brewer-25">[25]</a></sup> Gaddafi developed a good relationship with revolutionary Colombian Marxist–Leninist guerrilla group called <a href="/wiki/FARC" title="FARC">FARC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Activities also included such as Libyan contribution towards <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>, where Gaddafi had provided 20 launchers, 60 SA-7 missiles, machine guns, mortars and mines to the Argentinian government.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Libya was alleged to have role in bombing of <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103" title="Pan Am Flight 103">Pan Am Flight 103</a> over <a href="/wiki/Lockerbie" title="Lockerbie">Lockerbie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 1988 and French UTA Flight 772 in 1989, despite the investigation found no evidence which involves any Libyan role.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The United Nations put economic sanctions in place against Libya in 1992. These sanctions stopped other countries selling weapons, investing money, or even allowing their people to visit Libya.<sup id="cite_ref-sanction_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sanction-30">[30]</a></sup> After six days when US had captured Iraq's Saddam Hussein, Gaddafi renounced Libya's weapons of mass destruction programs and welcomed international inspections to verify that he would follow through on the commitment.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Gaddafi said he hopes that other nations would follow his example. UN removed the sanctions in the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-sanction_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sanction-30">[30]</a></sup> Gaddafi solved the Lockerbie plane crash issue by paying US$2.7 billion to the families of the victims. Prime Minister of Libya Shukri Ghanem, told in the interviews that Gaddafi was "paying the price for peace" with the West, and suggested that Libya had no role in the case.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> United Nations observers would acknowledge such statement and cast doubt towards the whole issue.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Gaddafi also set about having normal relations with other countries. Western European leaders and many working-level and commercial delegations were able to visit the country. He made his first trip to Western Europe in 15 years when he traveled to Brussels in April 2004. He reattached the ties with Russia once again, which had remained idle since the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>By 2010, Libya's <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> was highest in Africa, the country ranked at #53 overall as 'High standard' on living bases.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Libya remained a debt-free nation.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2011, Human rights in Libya were praised by the U.N. Human rights council.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> However, after <a href="/wiki/2011_Libyan_Civil_War" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 Libyan Civil War">civil war</a> the human rights seems to be getting worse.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> On March 2013, according to the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://barnabasfund.org/">Barnabas Fund</a>, at least 48 Christians were <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">tortured</a> inside Libya, one died in custody. </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suffrage">Suffrage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Suffrage">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Change section: Suffrage">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Every Libyan who is older than 18 can vote. This means that voting (also known as "suffrage") in Libya is <i>universal</i>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_War">Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=11" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Civil War">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Change section: Civil War">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>In February 2011, a <a href="/wiki/2011_Libyan_civil_war" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 Libyan civil war">civil war</a> broke out in Libya when rebels fought against Colonel Muammar al-Gaddafi and his government. <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> intervened in the war in favor of the rebels. However, some rebels had links with <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">al-Qaeda</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> NATO ended its mission in October after Colonel al-Gaddafi was reported to have been killed. In the aftermath of the civil war, a low-level rebellion by al-Gaddafi loyalists continued. Armed militias filled the gap left by the revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Two different national governments were organized in Tripoli and <a href="/wiki/Tobruk" title="Tobruk">Tobruk</a> and many fighters did not follow either of them. </p>
