      <p><b>Switzerland</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Schweiz</i>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Suisse</i>; <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Svizzera</i>; <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">Romansh</a>: <i lang="rm">Svizra</i>; <a href="/wiki/Swiss_German" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss German">Swiss German</a>: <i>Schwiz</i>) is a comparatively small country in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>. The official name of Switzerland is <i>Confoederatio Helvetica</i>. This is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and is not often used except for state documents. Switzerland is a <a href="/wiki/Confederation" title="Confederation">confederation</a> of even smaller states, which are the 26 cantons. </p>
      <p>Switzerland is known for its <a href="/wiki/Neutrality_(international_relations)" title="Neutrality (international relations)">neutrality</a>. A country is neutral when it does not take sides among the countries who are at war.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> Switzerland has been neutral since 1815. Many international organizations are in Switzerland. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> has a main office (but not its headquarters) in <a href="/wiki/Geneva_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Geneva City">Geneva</a>. Its predecessor organization, the League of Nations, was headquartered in Geneva. </p>
      <p>There are four official languages in Switzerland: <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Romansh" class="mw-redirect" title="Romansh">Romansh</a>. Some cantons and even towns have two languages spoken in them, and the largest canton by area, <a href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a>, has three. About 2/3 of the population speak German; French is spoken in the west of the country, while Italian is spoken in the canton of <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">Ticino</a> and Romansh in parts of Graubünden. Romansh language is spoken less than by 1% of the population. </p>
      <p>The capital of Switzerland is <a href="/wiki/Bern_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bern (city)">Bern</a>. The largest city of Switzerland is <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Zürich City">Zürich</a>. </p>
      <p>To the north of Switzerland is <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. East of Switzerland are <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a>. To the south of Switzerland is <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. To the west of Switzerland is <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. </p>

      <p>The area of Switzerland is 41,285&nbsp;km². The confederation is divided into 23 full states called <i><a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">cantons</a></i>. All 26 cantons are: <a href="/wiki/Aargau" title="Aargau">Aargau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Appenzell_Innerrhoden" title="Appenzell Innerrhoden">Appenzell Innerrhoden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Appenzell_Ausserrhoden" title="Appenzell Ausserrhoden">Appenzell Ausserrhoden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basel-Stadt" title="Basel-Stadt">Basel-Stadt</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Basel-Land&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Basel-Land (not yet started)">Basel-Land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bern_(canton)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bern (canton)">Berne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fribourg_(canton)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fribourg (canton)">Fribourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geneva_(canton)" title="Geneva (canton)">Geneva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glarus_(canton)" title="Glarus (canton)">Glarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jura_(canton)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jura (canton)">Jura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuch%C3%A2tel" title="Neuchâtel">Neuchâtel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nidwalden" title="Nidwalden">Nidwalden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obwalden" title="Obwalden">Obwalden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schaffhausen" title="Schaffhausen">Schaffhausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schwyz" class="mw-redirect" title="Schwyz">Schwyz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solothurn" class="mw-redirect" title="Solothurn">Solothurn</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Gallen" title="St. Gallen">St. Gallen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thurgau" title="Thurgau">Thurgau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">Ticino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Uri" class="mw-redirect" title="Canton of Uri">Uri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valais" title="Valais">Valais</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vaud" title="Vaud">Vaud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zug" title="Zug">Zug</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich_Canton" class="mw-redirect" title="Zürich Canton">Zürich</a>. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a> are very tall in the center and south of Switzerland. About 60% of Switzerland is in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> area. Only few people live here. The highest mountain is the <i>Dufour Peak</i> at 4,634 m. </p>
      <p>Many of the mountains have ice all year. This ice is called <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>. The rivers <a href="/wiki/Rhine" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Rhône">Rhône</a>, and many other <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> start in the mountains of Switzerland. </p>
      <p>There are many <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> in Switzerland. The biggest lakes are all in the north and west: <a href="/wiki/Lake_Geneva" title="Lake Geneva">Lake Geneva</a> (<i>Lac Léman</i>), <a href="/wiki/Lake_Z%C3%BCrich" title="Lake Zürich">Lake Zürich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Neuch%C3%A2tel" title="Lake Neuchâtel">Lake Neuchâtel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a> (<i>Bodensee</i>). </p>
      <p>Mountains in the north of Switzerland are fewer and smaller. That is why most Swiss people live in cities and towns in the north. The <a href="/wiki/Jura_mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Jura mountains">Jura mountains</a> are in the northwest of Switzerland. </p>

      <p>There are about 8.4 million people in Switzerland. About 64% of the people speak Swiss German (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> Alemannic) as their first language, in northern and central Switzerland. 19% of the people speak <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> as their first language, mainly in the west of Switzerland. 8% of the people speak <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, in the south of Switzerland. Only 1% of the people speak Romansh, in the southeastern part of Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-federalstatistics_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-federalstatistics-11">[9]</a></sup> Romansh is an old language that is similar to Latin. </p>
      <p>The German-speaking people of Switzerland do not actually speak "German" as their native language. Swiss people speak something called <a href="/wiki/Alemannic_German" title="Alemannic German">Alemannic</a> which has its own writing language and grammar but still is normally considered a German dialect. Alemannic may be difficult for Germans to understand. Swiss people do write like the people from Germany and also speak standard German very well, especially in the larger cities. </p>
      <p>About 23% of the people in Switzerland do not come from Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> They come from other places to usually work in Switzerland. </p>
      <p>The religion of most people in Switzerland is <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. 43% of the population follow Catholicism. 35% of the population follow Protestantism. 2% follow Eastern Orthodoxy. The religion of 4% of the population is <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. The rest follow other religions, or they have no religion. </p>
      <p>Switzerland is famous for its <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a>, banking system, <a href="/wiki/Watch" title="Watch">watches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a>. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Switzerland&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Switzerland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In 1291, people from Uri, Schwyz and Unterwalden wanted to be free. They signed a contract to work together called the <i>Eternal Alliance</i>. Together, they could be free from the people of <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a>, who were very strong. In 1315 the people from the Eternal Alliance fought the Habsburgs in battles at Morgarten, Sempach and Näfels. The people of the Eternal Alliance won all the battles. </p>
      <p>People from other areas signed the contract and joined the Eternal Alliance. More and more people worked together to be free. In 1648, other countries from Europe made an agreement that Switzerland was free. The name of this agreement was the <i><a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a></i>. More areas came to be part of Switzerland. </p>
      <p>Switzerland was important to the <a href="/wiki/Slave_trade" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a> that forced <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">black</a> <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africans</a> to be sold as slaves. Swiss companies made <a href="/w/index.php?title=Indiennes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Indiennes (not yet started)">indiennes</a>, which are a kind of cloth. These cloths were sent to Africa and exchanged for captured slaves. Some Swiss also joined French companies already making indiennes or owned <a href="/wiki/Plantations" class="mw-redirect" title="Plantations">plantations</a> with slaves in French <a href="/wiki/Colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonies">colonies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1798, France invaded Switzerland. The ruler of France was <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>. He changed many laws. In 1815 Switzerland again became <a href="/wiki/Independent" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent">independent</a> from France. Other countries agreed at the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> that Switzerland was free and neutral. </p>
      <p>The constitution of Switzerland that was made after a short <a href="/wiki/Civil_war" title="Civil war">civil war</a> in 1848 was replaced in 1999. Switzerland did not fight in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> or <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Since 2002, Switzerland is part of the United Nations. It did not join the United Nations for 57 years because of its neutrality. </p>

      <p>Switzerland is a <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a>. Switzerland does not have the same kind of president as the United States or France. Seven people (called ministers) do the job of president. They are called the Federal Council in English, <i>Bundesrat</i> in German, <i>Conseil Fédéral</i> in French, <i>Consiglio Federale</i> in Italian and <i>Cussegl Federal</i> in Romansh. Every year one of these people is made president. The president is not more important than the other six people. At present 3 of the 7 people are women. </p>

      <p>There are two parts of <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliament</a> in Switzerland. The <i>Council of States</i> and the <i>National Council</i>. Only both together can make laws. There are 46 people in the Council of States. Every full canton of Switzerland can send 2 people. There are 200 people in the National Council. The biggest canton sends most people to the National Council. The smallest 6 cantons can only send one person to the National Council. </p>
      <p>The people of Switzerland can collect signatures if they do not like a new law or a law change. This is called a <i>referendum</i>. If enough people sign a referendum, the people vote. The people can also collect signatures to change the <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a>. This is called an <i>initiative</i>. The constitution is the basic law of a country. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Switzerland&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Economy">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Switzerland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Change section: Economy">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Switzerland is not a member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, but it is member of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Free_Trade_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="European Free Trade Agreement (not yet started)">European Free Trade Agreement</a> (EFTA). The EFTA makes trade with other countries in Europe easier. In 1999 Switzerland and the European Union made a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a>. This contract makes <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> even easier. They recently made two other contracts. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">banks</a> of Switzerland and the insurance companies in Switzerland together produce eleven per cent of the <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a>. Tourism is important in Switzerland. There are many places for tourists. <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">Davos</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Moritz" title="St. Moritz">St. Moritz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pontresina" title="Pontresina">Pontresina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flims" title="Flims">Flims</a> are in Switzerland. These towns are important both in winter (for skiing) and in summer. Tourists also like the cities of <a href="/wiki/Lucerne" title="Lucerne">Lucerne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geneva_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Geneva City">Geneva</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Zürich City">Zürich</a>. </p>
      <p>In 2011 the <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmaceutical drug">pharmaceutical</a> industry in Switzerland directly and indirectly employed about 135,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-Vaterlaus_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vaterlaus-14">[12]</a></sup> The companies <a href="/wiki/Novartis" class="mw-redirect" title="Novartis">Novartis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hoffmann-La_Roche" title="Hoffmann-La Roche">Roche</a> are the second and third largest pharma companies in the world. They both have invented many life saving drugs because of well developed <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> facilities. </p>

Most Swiss literature was written in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> from 1291 until 1798. <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> became popular in Bern and elsewhere in the 18th century and many words also in the German speaking parts of Switzerland come from the French and are not known to Germans. <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian language</a> and Romansch-Latin literature are less common in Switzerland. </p>

Switzerland was the joint host, with <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Euro_2008" class="mw-redirect" title="Euro 2008">Euro 2008</a> tournament. Many Swiss also follow <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a>.       <p>The many lakes in the country make Switzerland a good place for sailing. They were the first European team to win the <a href="/w/index.php?title=America%27s_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="America's Cup (not yet started)">America's Cup</a> in 2003. They also won in 2007. Tennis has become more popular. Swiss players such as <a href="/wiki/Martina_Hingis" title="Martina Hingis">Martina Hingis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Federer" title="Roger Federer">Roger Federer</a> have won multiple <a href="/wiki/Grand_Slam_(tennis)" title="Grand Slam (tennis)">Grand Slams</a>. </p>

      <p><a href="/wiki/Motorsport" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorsport">Motorsport</a> racecourses and events were banned in Switzerland after the <a href="/w/index.php?title=1955_Le_Mans_disaster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="1955 Le Mans disaster (not yet started)">1955 Le Mans disaster</a>. There were exceptions for events such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillclimbing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hillclimbing (not yet started)">Hillclimbing</a>. This ban was overturned in June 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> During this period, the country still had successful racing drivers such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clay_Regazzoni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Clay Regazzoni (not yet started)">Clay Regazzoni</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sebastian_Buemi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sebastian Buemi (not yet started)">Sebastian Buemi</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jo_Siffert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jo Siffert (not yet started)">Jo Siffert</a> and successful <a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Touring_Car_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="World Touring Car Championship (not yet started)">World Touring Car Championship</a> driver <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alain_Menu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alain Menu (not yet started)">Alain Menu</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=A1_Team_Switzerland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A1 Team Switzerland (not yet started)">Switzerland</a> also won the <a href="/wiki/A1_Grand_Prix" title="A1 Grand Prix">A1GP World Cup of Motorsport</a> in <a href="/w/index.php?title=2007%E2%80%9308_A1_Grand_Prix_season&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="2007–08 A1 Grand Prix season (not yet started)">2007–08</a> with driver <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neel_Jani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Neel Jani (not yet started)">Neel Jani</a>. Swiss <a href="/w/index.php?title=Motorcycle_racer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Motorcycle racer (not yet started)">motorcycle racer</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_L%C3%BCthi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thomas Lüthi (not yet started)">Thomas Lüthi</a> won the 2005 <a href="/w/index.php?title=MotoGP&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="MotoGP (not yet started)">MotoGP</a> World Championship in the 125cc category. </p>
      <p>Traditional sports include Swiss wrestling or "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Schwingen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schwingen (not yet started)">Schwingen</a>". It is an old tradition from the rural central cantons and considered the national sport by some. <a href="/wiki/Hornussen" title="Hornussen">Hornussen</a> is another native Swiss sport. It is like a cross between baseball and golf.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Steinstossen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Steinstossen (not yet started)">Steinstossen</a> is the Swiss version of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stone_put&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stone put (not yet started)">stone put</a>, a competition in throwing a heavy stone. It is recorded to have taken place in <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a> in the 13th century. It is also central to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Unspunnenfest&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Unspunnenfest (not yet started)">Unspunnenfest</a>, first held in 1805.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup> </p>
