      <p><b>Finland</b> (<a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>: <i>Suomi</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> in Northern <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and is a member state of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. Finland is one of the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a> and is also part of <a href="/wiki/Fennoscandia" title="Fennoscandia">Fennoscandia</a>. Finland is located between the 60th and 70th latitudes North. Its neighbours are <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> to the west, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> to the east and <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> to the south, beyond the sea called <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">Gulf of Finland</a>. Most of western and southern coast is on the shore of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>. </p>
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Capital_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital (city)">capital</a> of Finland is <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>; the second largest city is <a href="/wiki/Tampere" title="Tampere">Tampere</a>. The official <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> of the country is the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> (EUR); before 2002 it was the <a href="/wiki/Markka" class="mw-redirect" title="Markka">markka</a>, the Finnish mark (FIM). The <a href="/wiki/President" title="President">president</a> of Finland is <a href="/wiki/Sauli_Niinist%C3%B6" title="Sauli Niinistö">Sauli Niinistö</a>. 5.5 million people live in Finland. <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> both are the official <a href="/wiki/Language" title="***REMOVED***">languages</a> of Finland; the most spoken languages is Finnish, mother tongue of about 90% of the population. Swedish is spoken by the Swedish speaking minority of Finland, called the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Finnish_Swede&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Finnish Swede (not yet started)">Finnish Swedes</a>, who make up 5% of the total population. Finland became <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independent</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> on 6 December 1917. </p>
      <p>The most important cities and towns in Finland are <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Espoo" title="Espoo">Espoo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tampere" title="Tampere">Tampere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vantaa" title="Vantaa">Vantaa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turku" title="Turku">Turku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oulu" title="Oulu">Oulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lahti" title="Lahti">Lahti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuopio" title="Kuopio">Kuopio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jyv%C3%A4skyl%C3%A4" title="Jyväskylä">Jyväskylä</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pori" title="Pori">Pori</a>. </p>
      <p>Finland is a highly industrialised <a href="/wiki/First_World" title="First World">First World</a> country. The most important Finnish industrial products are <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> products such as machines and electronics. <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> (the mobile company) is originally a company of Finland, named after a small town called <a href="/wiki/Nokia,_Finland" title="Nokia, Finland">Nokia</a>. </p>
      <p>Finland has been top of the list of least corrupt countries on the <a href="/wiki/Corruption_Perceptions_Index" title="Corruption Perceptions Index">Corruption Perceptions Index</a> more times than any other country. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People_and_culture">People and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: People and culture">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Change section: People and culture">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The people of Finland are called Finns. Most Finns speak <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> as their mother tongue. About six percent of Finns have the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish language</a> as their mother tongue. They live mostly in the western part of Finland and on <a href="/wiki/%C3%85land" class="mw-redirect" title="Åland">Åland</a> (Finnish Ahvenanmaa) </p>
      <p>Finns also study mandatory English and Swedish in school. Most Finns work either in services (that is: shops, banks, offices or businesses) or in factories. Finns often like <a href="/wiki/Sauna" title="Sauna">saunas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>. Many Finnish families have <a href="/w/index.php?title=Summer_cottage&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Summer cottage (not yet started)">summer cottages</a>, small houses where they go to relax on their summer holidays. The most important <a href="/wiki/Festival" title="Festival">festivals</a> that Finnish people celebrate are <a href="/w/index.php?title=Midsummer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Midsummer (not yet started)">Midsummer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>. </p>
      <p>The most popular sports in Finland are <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Track and field">track and field</a> and <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> (soccer). Finns have also won events in <a href="/wiki/Swimming" title="Swimming">swimming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motor_sports" title="Motor sports">motor sports</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnastics</a>. </p>
      <p>There is a group of a few thousand <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Samis</a> (also called Lapps) in the most northern part of Finland, called <a href="/wiki/Lapland" title="Lapland">Lapland</a>. Most of the Samis live in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>. Many Sami people farm <a href="/wiki/Reindeer" title="Reindeer">reindeers</a>. Originally, Samis were <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hunter-gatherers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hunter-gatherers (not yet started)">hunter-gatherers</a>. In the past the Sami were <a href="/wiki/Nomad" class="mw-redirect" title="Nomad">nomads</a>, but nowadays they live in regular houses. </p>
      <p>Very few people in Finland are from other countries. In 2016 about 4% of residents were born in another country.<sup id="cite_ref-gsi_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gsi-7">[7]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_and_weather">Nature and weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Nature and weather">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Change section: Nature and weather">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>Most of Finland is covered by pine <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swan</a>, which was considered holy long ago, is the national bird of Finland. Wood is the most important natural resource of Finland. It is estimated that up to one-third of all wood resources of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> are in Finland. </p>
      <p>The national animal of Finland is the <a href="/wiki/Brown_bear" title="Brown bear">brown bear</a>. The largest animal is the <a href="/wiki/Elk" class="mw-redirect" title="Elk">elk</a>, a type of moose, which is a member of the deer family. </p>
      <p>There are hundreds of rivers and thousands of fresh water lakes. <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">Fishing</a> is a popular <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sport</a>. It is estimated there are almost 180,000 lakes in Finland. </p>
      <p>Many islands in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> belong to Finland, too. Thousands of islands are part of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%85land" class="mw-redirect" title="Åland">Åland</a> <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>. Tourists from all over the world come to see the fells and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_lights" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern lights">northern lights</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lapland" title="Lapland">Lapland</a>. </p>
      <p>The highest mountain of Finland is Halti, which is 1328 meters high. The largest lake is Saimaa, 4,400 square kilometres. The longest river of Finland is Tornionjoki. The largest river (by watershed) is Kemijoki, 552 kilometres long. </p>
      <p>The weather in Finland varies widely by season. Summer usually lasts from May to early September, and temperatures can reach up to +35&nbsp;°C. Autumns are dark and rainy. Winter snow usually begins to fall in Helsinki in early December (in Lapland it can fall as early as October) and in the winter the temperature can drop to -30&nbsp;°C. Winter usually lasts to mid-March, when the snow melts in Helsinki (in Lapland the snow usually doesn't melt until early May), and Spring lasts till late May. Spring can be erratic, and the weather can change from frost to sunshine within a matter of days. The famed <a href="/wiki/Northern_Lights" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Lights">Northern Lights</a> are common in Lapland. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: History">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Change section: History">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>People first came to Finland 10,000 years ago. That was just after an <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>, after a glacier that covered the ground had receded. </p>
      <p>Some think the first people in Finland already spoke a language similar to the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish language</a> that is spoken today. It is known that an early form of the Finnish language was spoken in Finland in the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. (The Iron Age in Finland was 2,500–800 years ago). </p>
      <p>The first residents in Finland hunted animals, as "<a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a>". Some people started to <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Agriculture">farm</a> crops about 5,200 years ago. Farming slowly became more and more popular and became the major way of life until the modern age. </p>

      </div> 
      <p>The ancient Finns were <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagans</a>. The most important god of the Finnish pantheon was <a href="/wiki/Ukko" title="Ukko">Ukko</a>. He was a god of <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a>, much like <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>, another Scandinavian god-king. These powers were common among the pagan god kings in pantheons ranging from the Finnish Ukko, to the Scandinavian/Germanic/Saxon <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>, all the way east to <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> of the Greeks and Jupiter of the Romans. </p>
      <p>Around a thousand years ago, when most of Europe was adopting Christianity, Finland also began following Christianity. During the <a href="/wiki/Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformation">Reformation</a> of Christianity in the 16th century, most Finns became <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestants</a>. Some pagan practices still remain amongst the now Christian Finns, such as <a href="/wiki/Bear_worship" title="Bear worship">bear worship</a>. </p>
      <p>From the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> Finland was a part of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>. Then, in the year <a href="/wiki/1809" title="1809">1809</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> took Finland from Sweden. Finland was a part of Russia, but after a short period of time it became <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">autonomous</a>. The Finns essentially controlled Finland, though the Tsar was in control officially. Finns could create their own laws and had their own currency, (called the <i><a href="/wiki/Markka" class="mw-redirect" title="Markka">markka</a></i>), their own <a href="/wiki/Stamp" class="mw-redirect" title="Stamp">stamps</a> and own <a href="/wiki/Custom" title="Custom">customs</a>. However, Finland did not have its own <a href="/wiki/Army" title="Army">army</a>. </p>
      <p>During the <a href="/w/index.php?title=1905_Russian_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="1905 Russian Revolution (not yet started)">1905 Russian Revolution</a>, in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Duchy_of_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grand Duchy of Finland (not yet started)">Grand Duchy of Finland</a>: the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Social Democratic Party of Finland (not yet started)">Social Democrats</a> organised the <a href="/w/index.php?title=General_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="General strike (not yet started)">general strike</a> of 1905 (12–19 November&nbsp;[<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 30 October&nbsp;– 6 November]). The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_Guards_(Finland)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Red Guards (Finland) (not yet started)">Red Guards</a> were formed. On 12 August&nbsp;[<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 30 July]&nbsp;1906, Russian artillerymen and military engineers <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sveaborg_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sveaborg Rebellion (not yet started)">rose to rebellion</a> in the fortress of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sveaborg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sveaborg (not yet started)">Sveaborg</a> (later called <a href="/wiki/Suomenlinna" title="Suomenlinna">Suomenlinna</a>), Helsinki. The Finnish Red Guards supported the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sveaborg_Rebellion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sveaborg Rebellion (not yet started)">Sveaborg Rebellion</a> with a general strike, but the mutiny was quelled by loyal troops and ships of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baltic_Fleet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Baltic Fleet (not yet started)">Baltic Fleet</a> within 60 hours. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_independence">After independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: After independence">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Change section: After independence">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>On 6 December 1917, Finland became independent, which meant that it no longer was a part of Russia. There was a <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">communist revolution</a> in Russia and after 1922 Russia was a part of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. There were <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communists</a> in Finland too, who tried to create a revolution in Finland This attempt at revolution caused the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Finnish_civil_war&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Finnish civil war (not yet started)">Finnish civil war</a>. The communists lost the civil war, and Finland did not change its old capitalist system </p>
      <p><a href="/wiki/Stalin" class="mw-redirect" title="Stalin">Stalin</a>, who was the leader of the Soviet Union, did not like having a <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist</a> country as its neighbour. Stalin wanted Finland to become a <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> state and be a part of the Soviet Union. The leaders of Finland refused: they wanted to stay independent. The Soviet Union sent many troops across the eastern border of Finland to try to make Finland join them, which resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Winter_War" title="Winter War">Winter War</a>. The Soviet Union eventually won, and took most of <a href="/wiki/Karelia" title="Karelia">Karelia</a> and other parts of Finland. </p> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Finnish_soldiers_during_the_Winter_War.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Finnish_soldiers_during_the_Winter_War.jpg/250px-Finnish_soldiers_during_the_Winter_War.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Finnish_soldiers_during_the_Winter_War.jpg/375px-Finnish_soldiers_during_the_Winter_War.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Finnish_soldiers_during_the_Winter_War.jpg/500px-Finnish_soldiers_during_the_Winter_War.jpg 2x" data-file-width="860" data-file-height="575"></a> 
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a href="/wiki/File:Finnish_soldiers_during_the_Winter_War.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
         </div>Finnish soldiers at the time of war
        </div>

      <p><a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> was the dictator of Germany, and he wanted to invade the Soviet Union. Finland wanted to retrieve the areas that it had lost, so they joined the German invasion, which started with <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">Operation Barbarossa</a> in 1941. The Finnish part of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> is called the <a href="/wiki/Continuation_War" title="Continuation War">Continuation War</a> in Finland. However, Finland was not a <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascist</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemitic</a> country. Finns were interested in freedom rather than dictatorship. </p>
      <p>While Germany was losing the war, Finland had already progressed into the Soviet Union in order to regain the areas lost in the previous peace. Finland wanted to end the war with the Soviet Union, which resulted in peace. Once again Finland had to give up the areas they had conquered. This time, the peace with the Soviet Union made Finland and Germany enemies. Finns fought the Germans, and Germans retreated to <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, burning down all of Lapland behind them. This is called the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lapland_War&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lapland War (not yet started)">Lapland War</a>. Finland remained independent. </p>
      <p>After the war, many factories were built in Finland. Many people moved from farms to cities. At that time, big factories manufactured products like <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>. More and more people worked in more advanced jobs, like <a href="/wiki/High_technology" class="mw-redirect" title="High technology">high technology</a>. Also, many people went to universities to get a good education. Finland was one of the first countries where most people had <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> connections and mobile phones. A well-known company that makes <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a>, is from Finland. </p>
      <p>Finland joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in 1995. The Finnish currency was changed to the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> in 2002. </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Economy">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Change section: Economy">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Finland has a mixed economy. <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">Free market</a> controls most of production and sales of goods, but public sector is involved in services. In 2013, taxes were 44% of gross national product. This is 4th largest in Europe, after Denmark, France and Belgium.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2014 services were 70% of the gross national product.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The largest company in 2014 was oil refinery <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neste_Oil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Neste Oil (not yet started)">Neste Oil</a>. The second largest was <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a>. Two forest industries <a href="/wiki/Stora_Enso" title="Stora Enso">Stora Enso</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=UPM-Kymmene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="UPM-Kymmene (not yet started)">UPM-Kymmene</a>, were numbers three and four. Number five was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kesko&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kesko (not yet started)">Kesko</a> which sells everyday goods in K-supermarkets.<sup id="cite_ref-TE500_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TE500-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections">Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Change section: Elections">change</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Change section: Elections">change source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">Elections</a> are <a href="/wiki/Organize" class="mw-redirect" title="Organize">organized</a> to select 200 <a href="https://simple.wiktionary.org/wiki/member" class="extiw" title="wikt:member">members</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">Parliament</a> of Finland. Also selected are the <a href="/wiki/President" title="President">president</a> of Finland, members of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Town_council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Town council (not yet started)">town</a> and <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city councils</a> and Finnish members to the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Election" title="Election">elections</a> are <a href="/wiki/Secret" class="mw-redirect" title="Secret">secret</a> and direct. People <a href="/wiki/Vote" class="mw-redirect" title="Vote">vote</a> directly for the person they want to be elected. In presidential elections votes are only cast for a person, not for a <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a>. All the other elections are <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional</a>. The system is a combination of voting for individuals and parties.<sup id="cite_ref-CP_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CP-11">[11]</a></sup> The right to vote is universal and equal. In general elections everybody has one vote. </p>
